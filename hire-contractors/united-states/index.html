If you treat a US worker as a 1099 contractor when they should be a W-2 employee, you owe back tax. Not might. Will.

A single misclassified worker paid $80,000 a year for three years can cost you over $80,000 in federal back tax, FICA, and the wilful-misclassification penalty. State exposure (California, Massachusetts, New Jersey) lands on top.

Most founders have heard of 1099 versus W-2. Fewer understand that the IRS three-category test and the ABC tests run by around 30 states can reach opposite answers on the same worker.

This page covers the federal common-law test, the stricter state ABC tests, the cost of getting it wrong, and why an EOR cannot cure prior misclassification.

1099 versus W-2 is a tax-status call, not a contract call

A 1099-NEC contractor invoices you, gets paid gross, and files their own tax. A W-2 employee gets withholding at source, employer FICA of 7.65 percent, federal and state unemployment insurance, and any benefits in their offer letter.

Alex is a back-end developer in Austin. You pay him $9,000 a month against an invoice, he handles his own quarterly tax filings, he picks up other clients. That is a clean 1099.

Now Alex starts joining your daily stand-up, uses your laptop, and has a Slack handle on your team channel. The arrangement looks like employment. The IRS, not the contract, decides which one applies.

The difference shows up in three places: who carries the payroll tax, who carries the employment risk, and who controls the work day to day.

The classification matters because four agencies can look at the same arrangement and reach different answers. The IRS, the Department of Labor, the state revenue department, and the state labour department each run their own test. The federal common-law test is the baseline. States layer their own tests for income tax, unemployment insurance, wage-and-hour protection, and workers’ compensation eligibility. What is the IRS common-law test for independent contractors?

The IRS weighs three categories: behavioural control, financial control, and the relationship of the parties. No single factor decides. The arrangement, not the title, controls.

Priya is a brand designer in New York. She works from her own studio, uses her own tools, and pitches three other clients in the same month she ships your project. Behavioural control low, financial control high, relationship project-bounded. Clean contractor.

The IRS replaced its 20-factor test with this three-category framework in 1996. The current guidance lives in IRS Publication 15-A.

01 Contractor Classification Tests

Four overlapping frameworks decide whether a US worker is a 1099 contractor or a W-2 employee. The IRS common-law test is the federal baseline. The state ABC test sits on top for around 30 states. Section 530 of the Revenue Act of 1978 is a federal safe harbour that can preserve contractor status. The $600 annual aggregate triggers 1099-NEC filing per contractor.

The three categories work together. Each pushes the engagement toward either employment or self-employment, and the IRS weighs the whole picture. The detailed test is documented in IRS Publication 15-A and at IRS Topic 762.

1. Behavioural control

Does the business have the right to direct and control how the worker does the work, even if it doesn’t actually exercise that right? Key signals: detailed instructions about when, where, and how to work; required training; required attendance at meetings; required tools or systems. Strong behavioural control points to employment.

2. Financial control

Does the worker have a meaningful financial stake in the work? Signals: investment in equipment, the ability to make a profit or take a loss on the engagement, services advertised to the wider market, payment by the job rather than the hour, the right to send a substitute, unreimbursed business expenses. Strong financial autonomy points to self-employment.

3. Relationship of the parties

Does the worker look like part of the organisation? Signals: a written contract that genuinely reflects the working pattern, no employee benefits, services that are not a core part of the regular business, a defined engagement with a clear end date. A long, open-ended engagement that delivers core business services points to employment.

If the test fails, file an SS-8

Either you or the worker can file Form SS-8 (Determination of Worker Status) and ask the IRS to issue a binding classification determination. The IRS typically takes six months to respond. The determination binds federal tax purposes only. State authorities and the Department of Labor remain free to disagree.

The 2024 DOL economic reality test

For wage-and-hour purposes (overtime, minimum wage), the DOL’s Final Rule effective 11 March 2024 restored the multi-factor economic reality test. The DOL looks at six factors: opportunity for profit or loss, investment in the work, permanence of the relationship, nature and degree of control, work integral to the business, and skill and initiative. The DOL test runs alongside the IRS test, not in place of it. A worker can be a contractor under one and an employee under the other.

The ABC test trips engagements the IRS would pass

Around 30 states run an ABC test for at least one purpose, usually state unemployment insurance. California, Massachusetts, and New Jersey use the strictest version.

Marcus is a freelance copywriter you engage for a marketing agency in San Francisco. Under the IRS test, he is a clean contractor: he sets his own hours, works from his own laptop, takes on other clients. Under California’s AB5, he fails. Copywriting is the agency’s usual course of business.

You can pass the federal test and still owe back tax to a state. The arrangement gets graded twice.

The ABC test asks three questions. All must be answered “yes” for the worker to be a contractor:

  • A. The worker is free from the company’s control and direction in performing the work, both under contract and day-to-day.
  • B. The work is outside the usual course of the company’s business.
  • C. The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as the work performed.

The B prong is the trap. It is why Marcus the copywriter fails in California. It is why a fintech engaging a freelance React developer fails. The same arrangements pass the IRS three-category test.

California: AB5 and the Dynamex strict ABC test

California adopted the ABC test for wage-order purposes in the 2018 Dynamex decision and codified it for most state employment purposes in Assembly Bill 5 (AB5), effective 1 January 2020. AB5 has been amended several times since, adding statutory exemptions for specific occupations: licensed professionals, real estate agents, construction subcontractors meeting AB2257 criteria, and business-to-business contractors meeting 12 specific tests. Check the current exemption list before classifying.

Massachusetts runs the strictest ABC test in the country

Massachusetts applies the ABC test for all employment purposes, with no statutory exemption analogous to California’s. Massachusetts courts read the B prong more strictly than California, demanding the work be outside the usual course of the employer’s business even for short engagements.

New Jersey, Illinois, and the unemployment-only states

New Jersey applies the ABC test for state unemployment insurance and wage-payment purposes. Illinois applies it for the Illinois Wage Payment and Collection Act. Around 25 other states apply the ABC test only for state unemployment insurance eligibility, not for income tax withholding. That split matters. A worker can be a contractor for federal income tax and a state-UI employee at the same time.

New York Freelance Isn’t Free Act (statewide, August 2024)

New York’s Freelance Isn’t Free Act took effect statewide on 28 August 2024. It does not change the classification test. It requires a written contract for any freelance engagement of $800 or more, mandates payment within 30 days of work completion or by the contracted date, and provides double damages plus attorney’s fees for non-payment. The Act applies to genuine freelancers. Misclassified employees still recover under wage-and-hour law.

One misclassified worker costs around $80,000 in federal exposure alone

A worker reclassified as an employee triggers federal back tax and FICA at 15.3 percent. Add three years of unpaid overtime under federal wage-and-hour law, plus a 100 percent wilful-misclassification penalty.

State penalties add another 25 to 30 percent. The lookback window is three years standard, indefinite if intentional.

If you misclassified Alex the developer at $80,000 a year for three years, the federal stack alone lands at roughly $81,400. Add California, Massachusetts, or New Jersey and the number doubles.

The 100 percent wilful penalty applies when the IRS finds the misclassification was intentional, not when it was an honest error. Who chases the back tax

The IRS opens employment-tax audits through three doors. A worker files an SS-8 asking for a determination. A worker files self-employment tax and later switches to claiming employee status. Or a routine payroll-tax audit looks at 1099 spend in the prior three years. State revenue and labour authorities run parallel audits. California’s EDD audits trigger automatically when a 1099 contractor files for unemployment.

The three-year lookback

The federal statute of limitations runs three years from the return filing date. There is no statute of limitations for fraudulent or wilfully unfiled returns. Most enforcement actions reach back three years and stack penalties across all misclassified workers in that window.

What is Section 530 relief and when does it apply?

Section 530 is a federal safe harbour from the Revenue Act of 1978. It lets you keep treating workers as contractors even after the IRS finds they should have been employees. Three conditions: a reasonable basis, consistent treatment of similar workers, and timely 1099 filing.

State tax authorities ignore Section 530. California, Massachusetts, and New York will pursue their own back tax even if you win the federal argument.

Section 530 is the most-litigated provision in employment-tax practice. The IRS audit manual treats it as a threshold defence. The auditor has to apply Section 530 first, before reclassifying.

The three conditions, all of which must be met

  1. Reasonable basis. You had a reasonable basis for treating the worker as a contractor. Qualifying categories: a prior IRS audit that did not reclassify the same role; a published court case or IRS ruling supporting the classification; a long-standing recognised industry practice (sometimes interpreted as 10 years or more, though courts vary); or any other reasonable basis you can document.
  2. Consistent treatment. You treated all workers in substantially similar positions as contractors. If you classified some as employees and others as contractors for the same role, Section 530 fails.
  3. Filing consistency. You filed all required 1099-NECs on time for every contractor in the role for the years in question. A missed 1099 invalidates Section 530 for that worker.

What Section 530 does and doesn’t do

If Section 530 applies, you keep treating the worker as a contractor going forward, with no federal back tax, no penalty. The relief is prospective. The underpaid federal tax for the audit period is forgiven.

The relief is federal only. State revenue and labour authorities (especially California, Massachusetts, New York) routinely ignore Section 530 and pursue their own back tax and penalties. Section 530 also does not protect against federal wage-and-hour claims, which the Department of Labor administers on the economic reality test.

The voluntary classification settlement program

If Section 530 does not apply, the IRS offers the Voluntary Classification Settlement Program (VCSP) as an alternative. You voluntarily reclassify the workers going forward, pay 10 percent of the federal employment tax that would have been due on the most recent year (a sharp reduction from the open-market cost), and get immunity from federal back tax and interest. VCSP requires that you are under no current IRS audit on the classification issue. Most employers use VCSP only when Section 530 is clearly unavailable, because VCSP triggers a forward W-2 commitment and the state-tax exposure remains separate.

EOR does not cure prior misclassification. It can confirm it.

Putting an at-risk contractor through an EOR creates a textbook employment arrangement. The IRS reads that as confirmation the worker should have been an employee. The EOR transition can become the evidence used against you.

The reasoning mirrors UK IR35 logic. Classification asks whether the working arrangement looks like employment. Move a contractor onto an EOR and the arrangement becomes employment: same role, same client, same hours, routed through a new legal employer.

The federal lookback runs three years, indefinite for wilful underfiling. Moving someone to an EOR on 1 June does not prevent the IRS from opening an audit on the prior 18 months of 1099 engagement.

If the engagement is honestly assessed as employment from day one, the worker is a disguised employee, and you want to engage them as such, EOR is one of three legitimate paths:

  1. Direct W-2 employment via your own US entity (Teamed US Inc., your own US sub, or a PEO that runs payroll on your FEIN).

What EOR is not

EOR is not retroactive misclassification cover. Moving a contractor onto an EOR on 1 June does not cure the prior 18 months of 1099 treatment. For that, you need Section 530, the IRS settlement program, or to accept the federal back tax.

How do you onboard a US contractor properly?

Collect a signed W-9 before the first payment. Sign a written contract that documents independence. Pay against invoices, not payroll. File a 1099-NEC by 31 January for any contractor paid $600 or more the prior calendar year.

Priya the designer in New York invoices you for $15,000 across three projects in a year. You owe her a 1099 by the end of January. The IRS gets a copy.

Keep the engagement record for at least four years.

The operational sequence:

  1. Run the classification test before signing. IRS three-category common-law test plus the state ABC test if the contractor is resident in or working from California, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Illinois, Washington, or any other ABC-test state. Run the Contractor Classifier on every engagement.
  2. Collect a W-9. The contractor completes the W-9 providing their legal name, business name if different, taxpayer identification number (SSN or EIN), and address. Keep the form on file for four years.
  3. Sign a written contract that documents independence. Scope of work, fixed deliverables or output-based pricing, no required hours, no required tools, no required location, no exclusivity, right to send a substitute, right to take on other clients. A vague contract documenting hourly work and required attendance is misclassification evidence on its own.
  4. Pay against invoices, not payroll. The contractor invoices you. You pay in full, gross, no withholding. (The one exception: backup withholding at 24 percent if the contractor refuses to provide a TIN.) Pay through accounts payable, not payroll, to keep the audit trail clean.
  5. File the 1099-NEC by 31 January. For any contractor paid $600 or more in aggregate in the calendar year. File Copy A with the IRS by 31 January, and provide Copy B to the contractor by 31 January. The threshold is per contractor, not per engagement: $300 paid to one contractor across two engagements still adds up to $600 and triggers the form. Some states have lower thresholds.
  6. Track multi-state nexus. Paying a contractor based in a state where you have no other footprint can trigger nexus for state income tax or business registration. Run a nexus check before paying a contractor in any state where you have no other operations.

Backup withholding: the 24 percent trap

If a contractor refuses to provide a W-9, provides an obviously wrong TIN, or the IRS notifies you that a TIN you used is incorrect, you have to withhold federal tax at 24 percent of every payment to that contractor until the TIN issue is resolved. The rule: never make the first payment without the W-9 in hand.

Multi-state pay

A US contractor pays their own federal and state income tax. You do not withhold. But state nexus can trigger your own tax registration in the contractor’s state if their work creates business presence there. The risk is highest for sales-tax-relevant work, in-state physical presence, or any state with economic-nexus rules at low thresholds. Run the nexus check at engagement, not at year-end.
