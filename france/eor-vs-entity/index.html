The crossover lands at 8 to 12 employees

EOR cost scales linearly with headcount. Your own entity carries a fixed monthly overhead plus per-employee compliance. The lines cross at 8 to 12 French employees on average tech salaries.

What does it cost to set up a French SAS or SARL in 2026?

Realistic France entity setup runs €2,500 to €8,000 for an SAS or SARL through a lawyer or corporate-services firm. Statutory registration fees add €37 to €60. Timeline is 2 to 6 weeks from decision to first payslip if you go full-service.

The headline incorporation fee is cheap, around €53 for the registration and beneficial-ownership declaration, plus the mandatory legal-journal notice. The headline misleads. It excludes the operational setup that actually matters.

The bank account is the hidden bottleneck

French bank accounts for foreign-parented companies require a certificat de dépôt de capital issued by the bank before RCS registration completes. Traditional French banks ask for a working capital deposit of around €4,000 in practice, even though the legal minimum is €1. Digital-first banks like Qonto and Shine can open an account in 24 to 48 hours. Traditional banks often take 4 to 8 weeks. The bank is usually the step that turns a 2-week incorporation into a 6-week operational entity.

Ongoing entity cost runs €2,500 to €4,500 a month

A small French SAS or SARL doing payroll, accounting, statutory filings, and basic HR admin averages €2,500 to €4,500 a month. Below 5 employees the overhead dominates. Above 15, per-head overhead drops sharply as the fixed costs amortise.

The convention collective decides whether you pay 25% or 47%

France’s conventions collectives are binding sector agreements that sit on top of the statutory floor. They cover 98% of French employees. Which one applies to a hire is what moves your real employer cost from 40% closer to 47% of gross.

The convention follows your activity code, not your signature. If your job sits within a covered code the convention applies, whether or not you signed it directly. Syntec covers most engineering, consulting, and software firms. Two hires on the same €60,000 salary, one under Syntec, one under métallurgie, can carry employer costs three to five percentage points apart.

Hugo the Lyon engineer falls under Syntec. The convention adds a minimum salary grid (often above SMIC), a mandatory mutuelle health top-up with employer-paid share, and convention-specific bonuses such as Syntec’s 13th-month for managers. None of those show up in a generic "France employer cost" headline.

What are the hidden costs of a French entity, and what PE risk does EOR not fix?

A French SAS president or SARL gérant carries personal legal duties for URSSAF filings, unpaid social contributions, and the registre du personnel. These are not delegable.

Late filings trigger automatic URSSAF penalties. Repeat lateness on corporate tax can trigger criminal liability for the dirigeant. A CFO who signs DSN declarations without reviewing them is personally on the hook.

Permanent establishment risk runs in parallel. EOR does not eliminate it.

Most cost comparisons skip the director-liability dimension because it is hard to quantify. In France the criminal exposure is real, not theoretical, and worth flagging.

The compliance treadmill

  • DSN (Déclaration Sociale Nominative), monthly, by the 5th or 15th of the following month. Late means automatic URSSAF penalty.
  • Bilan annuel + liasse fiscale, within 3 to 4 months of year-end. Late means €150 to €1,500 penalty, escalating with delay.
  • Corporate tax return (CIT), within 3 months of year-end. Repeat lateness triggers criminal liability for the dirigeant.
  • Déclaration de bénéficiaires effectifs, within 30 days of any change in beneficial ownership.
  • CSE elections, once headcount crosses 11 employees for 12 consecutive months.
  • Mutuelle and prévoyance declarations, annually.

EOR does not solve permanent establishment exposure

France tax authorities read permanent establishment broadly. An EOR handles employee-level payroll, social contributions, and labour law. It does not eliminate the foreign-parent corporate tax exposure if your French employee signs contracts, negotiates deals, or holds out as your representative.

The OECD’s November 2025 Model Tax Convention update added a 50% working-time safe harbour for cross-border remote work and a commercial-reason test. The substantive PE analysis still applies. If your French hire is a sales lead, a country manager, or a dependent agent, PE is your tax team’s job, not your EOR’s.

When should you stay on an EOR in France?

Below 5 employees, on project hires, or while you are still validating the French market, the EOR is the right answer. The crossover is a maths threshold, not a strategic verdict.

Camille is hire one. She is the test. If the Paris launch works you can keep her on the EOR while you add Hugo and one more. The SAS or SARL is sticky once you open it. The EOR is reversible.

  • Under 5 French employees on average salaries: EOR is cheaper and faster every month, even after the 1 January 2026 severance contribution increase.
  • Market validation phase: you are hiring 1 to 2 people to test commercial fit, with an option to scale back if the bet does not pan out. SAS or SARL setup is sticky. The EOR is reversible.
  • Project-based hires: 6 to 12 month engagements where the upfront entity cost will not amortise.
  • No BSPCE pull yet: senior French hires are not expecting French-style stock options, or you are still pre-Series-A. The entity becomes valuable once BSPCE becomes a comp lever for French talent.
  • Cross-border remote roles under 50% French working time: the OECD November 2025 safe harbour reduces PE pressure, and the EOR cleanly handles French employment law without you needing a France presence.

Your own French entity beats EOR above 8 to 12 employees

Above 8 to 12 employees consistently, with a multi-year French growth plan, or with BSPCE or PE-substance needs, your own entity beats the EOR on cost and opens up capabilities the EOR cannot provide.

Hugo is hire eight. Camille and Margot are already on the books. The next three are sales reps with deal-signing authority. That is the moment to start your SAS. Open it now and it is operational by the time Hugo’s offer letter goes out.

  • French sales hires with deal-signing authority: dependent-agent PE risk in France is real, and an entity scopes the corporate tax exposure cleanly.
