If you hire your first UK employee on a US benefits playbook, you will lose the candidate in the final round. Not because you broke a law. Because someone else offered private medical and 5 percent pension.

A competitive UK benefits package adds £3,500 to £10,000 a year per employee on top of the statutory floor. Skip it and you compete on salary alone, against employers who have already done the maths.

Most US founders have heard that the UK is "cheap to hire in." Fewer understand where the talent actually compares offers.

This page covers the statutory floor every UK employer has to provide from day one, the competitive package that wins offers in 2026, and the pension and share-option moves that keep senior hires past year two.

The statutory floor is generous compared to the US

Every UK employee gets 5.6 weeks of paid annual leave (28 days for a full-time worker, bank holidays usually included), Statutory Sick Pay from day one, day-one paternity and parental leave, and a pension contribution of at least 3 percent of qualifying earnings.

The cash rates are modest. Statutory Sick Pay is £123.25 a week. Statutory Maternity, Paternity, and Adoption Pay all sit at £194.32 a week (after the first six maternity weeks at 90 percent of earnings).

Emma is a designer in Manchester earning £45,000. On day one she has 28 days of paid leave, the right to take Statutory Sick Pay if she catches flu, and an 8 percent combined pension contribution (3 percent from you, 5 percent from her salary).

The floor is generous next to the US. There is no equivalent of 28 days off, no equivalent of day-one paid parental leave, no equivalent of mandatory pension. The trap is treating "generous floor" as "competitive offer." It is not.

What does a competitive UK benefits package look like in 2026?

A competitive offer for a £45,000 to £75,000 professional role adds private medical insurance, a 5 to 8 percent pension match, life assurance at 4x salary, income protection, mental-health support, and a learning budget. For senior hires, EMI share options.

The total loaded cost runs £3,500 to £10,000 a year per employee, on top of base salary and statutory contributions.

James leads the shop floor for a Glasgow retailer and earns £38,000. His offer compares two packages: yours at statutory floor only, a competitor's at 5 percent pension match plus £900 of private medical. The competitor wins on perceived value, before salary is even discussed.

The pension match is where you signal seriousness

The legal floor is 3 percent employer. The mid-market average is 5 percent. Competitive tech and professional services offer 5 to 8 percent, usually as a matched contribution ("you put in 5, we match up to 5").

Senior roles regularly see 10 percent or more as a retention lever. The cost is modest. The signalling is strong.

Aisha runs HR for a 30-person Bristol startup. Her senior engineers compare offers from London tech firms paying 7 percent matched. Aisha lifts her match from 3 to 6 percent. The annual cost on a £80,000 salary is about £2,400 extra. The cost of losing the engineer is six months of recruiter fees and a missed product cycle.

Three common structures, in order of competitiveness:

  • Statutory minimum (3 percent employer plus 5 percent employee). Covers the legal obligation only. Below market for professional roles.
  • Fixed enhanced match (5 percent employer plus 5 percent employee). Competitive in the mid-market. Same contribution whatever the employee chooses.
  • Tiered match (up to 8 percent, matching what the employee puts in). Common in tech. Employee chooses contribution, you match pound-for-pound up to the ceiling.

Salary sacrifice fixes the £100k cliff

Employees earning between £100,000 and £125,140 hit a 60 percent effective marginal tax rate. The personal allowance tapers away in that band.

Sacrificing salary into pension reduces adjusted net income below the £100,000 line. The personal allowance is preserved. The 60 percent cliff disappears.

Salary sacrifice also saves National Insurance for both sides. A £5,000 sacrifice saves the employer about £750 and the employee about £400. Roughly £1,150 of combined saving on a £5,000 redirected payment, before the tax saving on the contribution itself.

EMI share options are the UK tech retention play

Enterprise Management Incentive options are the most tax-efficient way to grant share options in the UK. On exercise: usually no income tax. On disposal: capital gains rates of 10 or 20 percent, not income tax at 40 percent or more.

EMI requires a qualifying UK trading company with gross assets under £30 million and fewer than 250 full-time-equivalent employees. The employee has to work at least 25 hours a week (or 75 percent of their working time) and hold no more than 30 percent of the shares.

Daniel is a senior developer in Leeds, weighing a remote role at a US scale-up. The US company offers RSUs taxed as income at vesting. A UK competitor offers EMI options taxed at 10 percent CGT on exit. On a £150,000 gain, the difference is roughly £45,000 in his pocket.

EMI is the structural reason a UK Ltd (your own entity) beats EOR for senior hires you want to incentivise with options. Teamed Ltd, as the EOR, cannot grant EMI options to your employees. The qualifying company has to be your parent or a UK subsidiary you own.

EMI eligibility (the company)

  • Gross assets under £30 million
  • Fewer than 250 full-time-equivalent employees
  • Carrying on a qualifying trade (excluded: dealing in land, leasing, financial activities)
  • Independent (not 50 percent or more owned by another company)

EMI eligibility (the employee)

  • Employee of the granting company or a qualifying subsidiary
  • Working at least 25 hours a week, or 75 percent of working time
  • Holding no more than 30 percent of the company shares
  • Options within the £250,000 individual limit and £3 million company limit

The tax treatment

On exercise: usually no income tax and no National Insurance, provided the exercise price is at least the market value at grant. On disposal: capital gains tax at 10 percent if Business Asset Disposal Relief applies (lifetime £1m limit), otherwise standard CGT at 10 or 20 percent.

The HMRC sign-off process for EMI takes a few weeks and a valuation. Plan it ahead of an option grant cycle. Last-minute EMI does not exist.

Mental-health support stopped being a nice-to-have in 2024

Candidate surveys now place mental-health benefits in the top three differentiators alongside pension and flexible working. NHS waiting lists and Glassdoor-visible comparisons are pushing every competitive employer to add a baseline.

The minimum competitive offer is an Employee Assistance Programme plus mental-health cover in the private medical policy.

The cost is modest. £30 to £150 a year per employee for the full stack.

What competitive UK employers offer in 2026:

  • EAP. 24/7 confidential helpline, 4 to 8 counselling sessions per year, financial and legal advice.
  • Mental-health first aiders. Trained internal contacts, expanding in scope after 2023 government guidance.
  • Private medical mental-health cover. Therapy sessions, psychiatry consultations, sometimes inpatient cover.
  • Subscription apps. Headspace for Work, Calm, Spill, MyMynd.
  • Menstruation and menopause leave. Emerging benefit, particularly in tech.
  • Mental-health days. Separate from sick leave, no fit note required.
  • Compassionate or bereavement leave. Typically 3 to 5 days fully paid, separate from Parental Bereavement Leave.

Aisha's Bristol startup runs a £40 per head EAP plus mental-health cover bolted onto the Bupa policy. Total annual spend: under £4,000 for 30 employees. Two engineers used the counselling sessions in year one. Both stayed.
