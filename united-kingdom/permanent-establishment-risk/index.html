Hire a UK salesperson with quota and contract authority for your US parent, and HMRC will treat the parent as carrying on business in the UK. That triggers UK corporation tax on the profits attributed to that activity.

UK corporation tax runs at 25 percent on attributed profits, plus three years of back filings, plus a transfer-pricing memo. A late-discovered permanent establishment, or PE, on a single UK sales hire can land the parent a six-figure bill before legal fees.

Most founders have heard of PE risk. Fewer realise it depends on what the UK person does, not where the desk is.

This page covers the two HMRC tests, why a US sales hire in London usually triggers and a US engineer in Manchester usually doesn’t, and how EOR sidesteps PE because Teamed UK Ltd is the legal employer.

What is a UK permanent establishment?

A permanent establishment, or PE, is the legal hook that lets the UK tax a foreign company on UK-sourced profits.

A foreign company has a UK PE in two situations. Either it has a fixed place of business in the UK through which its business is carried on. Or it has a dependent agent in the UK who habitually plays the principal role in concluding contracts.

Hit either test, and the UK taxes the profits attributable to that activity.

The headline cost is the tax bill. The hidden cost is the operating load: UK statutory accounts for the PE, transfer-pricing analysis between the UK PE and the rest of the group, three back years of CT600 returns if the trigger is found late, and the HMRC enquiry that opens once the file is on a desk in Liverpool.

Most PE risk is foreseeable from the job description. The trigger is rarely surprise. The cost is the surprise.

The fixed-place test catches the rented London office

A fixed place of business is a physical location at the parent’s disposal, with permanence, through which the parent’s business is carried on. Rent a London office in the parent’s name for a UK sales team, and you have a textbook PE.

Three elements have to line up: a place, a degree of permanence, and the parent’s business carried on through it.

The bar for “at the parent’s disposal” is lower than founders expect. A regularly-used home office can qualify. A co-working desk used four days a week can qualify. A hotel room used systematically for the same purpose can qualify.

Sarah leads backend engineering for a Delaware-incorporated SaaS company. She lives in Manchester and writes code from her spare bedroom. She talks to product managers in Austin, ships against a US roadmap, and never speaks to a UK customer. Her work is internal to the global business.

That is not a fixed place of business for the US parent. The activity carried on through Sarah’s home is engineering for the group, not commercial activity selling to UK customers. HMRC reads this as preparatory and auxiliary, on the right side of the line.

Daniel runs UK sales for the same Delaware company. He works four days a week from a WeWork in Holborn, branded with the parent’s logo on the desk, and meets prospects there. That setup is a fixed place of business. The parent has a UK PE the moment Daniel signs the lease.

The activity exemption used to cover more ground. The 2017 anti-fragmentation rules narrowed it. HMRC now reads preparatory and auxiliary restrictively, so the old defence of “our UK office is just an information desk” rarely survives an enquiry.

The dependent-agent test catches the sales hire

A foreign company has a UK PE through a dependent agent if a UK-based person habitually concludes contracts, or plays the principal role in contracts the parent routinely signs without changing the terms.

Sales roles are the canonical risk pattern. Country managers and customer-success leads with renewal authority are the close second.

The 2017 update closed the old loophole. The argument used to be “our UK person doesn’t sign, HQ signs.” HMRC now looks at who did the work, not who held the pen.

Daniel is the UK sales lead for that same Delaware parent. He pitches UK prospects, walks them through the master service agreement, negotiates the security schedule, agrees the price, and emails the executed contract back to the buyer. The Austin-based VP signs the PDF, never reads it.

Daniel is a dependent agent. The parent has a UK PE through him. It does not matter that his employment contract is with Teamed UK Ltd. It does not matter that the Austin VP is the legal signatory. The PE test is about the activity, and the activity sits in the UK.

Four signals that move a UK role into dependent-agent territory:

  • Pitching UK prospects, presenting commercials, leading negotiation.
  • Setting terms or material commercial provisions HQ does not change.
  • Holding out as the customer’s contact for contract-related questions.
  • A customer-facing title like “UK Country Manager”, “Head of UK Sales”, “VP UK”.

The independent-agent carve-out is narrower than people hope

The rules do not apply to agents acting in the ordinary course of their independent business. A third-party UK distributor with its own customers is not a dependent agent.

An EOR is a different animal. Teamed UK Ltd is independent commercially, but the working arrangement between Daniel and the US parent is the thing HMRC examines. The carve-out helps with the legal-employer question. It does not change what Daniel does day to day.

How EOR sidesteps PE for the foreign parent

When you hire through Teamed UK Ltd, the legal employer of Sarah and Daniel is a UK company. The US parent is not the employer of record. That breaks the most common PE chain at the employment-contract layer.

For roles like Sarah’s, that is the whole answer. Engineering work for the global business does not give the parent a UK PE, and EOR removes the employment-side complication. The parent sees one fixed invoice line and no UK corporation-tax filing.

For roles like Daniel’s, EOR is necessary but not sufficient. The dependent-agent test looks at activity, not at whose payroll runs the salary.

What EOR fixes:

  • The legal employer is a UK Ltd. PAYE, National Insurance, pension auto-enrolment, and employee-side tax all flow through Teamed UK Ltd, not the parent.
  • The contract chain is parent to EOR to employee. The parent has no employment relationship with the UK person.
  • The EOR’s UK employees do not hold director or officer titles on the parent. They cannot bind the parent as agents of its constitution.

What EOR does not fix:

  • If the UK employee functionally concludes contracts for the parent, the dependent-agent test still fires.
  • If the parent rents UK office space in its own name and operates from there, the fixed-place test still fires.
  • If customer-facing material describes the setup as “our UK office”, HMRC reads the marketing copy as evidence of PE.

EOR is good cover for engineering, design, marketing, support, finance, operations, and any role internal to the global business. EOR is weak cover for UK sales, business development, country management, and customer-success roles with renewal or expansion authority.

Five UK hiring patterns that trip PE

Most PE exposures look like one of five things on a hiring brief. Read them before you write the job description, not after the offer goes out.

04 UK PE Trigger Map

Four conditions HMRC reads as evidence of a UK permanent establishment for a foreign-parented employer. Hit any one with sufficient weight and the dependent-agent or fixed-place test fires; the corporation-tax filing follows.

Fixed place of business Dependent agent · contract authority Server / IT presence Employee acting for foreign parent
  1. UK sales hire with quota and commission selling to UK customers. Daniel-pattern. Almost always triggers the dependent-agent test.
  2. UK office with the parent’s name on the door. Fixed-place trigger, even on a six-month short lease.
  3. Country manager, VP UK, or Head of UK title. The title alone is evidence of authority. HMRC reads the org chart.
  4. UK customer-success or account management with authority to renew or expand contracts. Increasingly read as dependent-agent activity, even without the “sales” label.
  5. UK marketing director hosting in-person events that present the parent’s offerings to UK prospects. Fixed-place and dependent-agent overlap.

Low-risk patterns in our portfolio: UK engineers building product for the global business (Sarah-pattern), UK designers contributing to global product, UK support handling tickets globally rather than just for UK customers, UK finance and operations roles internal to the company.

The pattern is not headcount. You can have twenty UK engineers and no PE. You can have one UK salesperson and a PE filing on day 91.

What to do if you think you have PE risk

Three steps. Assess the working arrangement honestly. Get a tax memo. Structure to avoid the trigger, or incorporate a UK entity on your own terms.

Doing nothing is the most expensive option. The cost of a PE found by HMRC after three years runs to back tax plus interest plus penalties plus the bill for a transfer-pricing memo you should have written on day one.

Step 1. The five-minute hiring-brief test

For every UK hire, walk through four questions. Does the role have customer-facing commercial authority? Does it work from a fixed UK location named in customer materials? Would the job description look like a sales role to an HMRC inspector? Would the LinkedIn bio?

If any answer is yes, you are looking at PE risk before the offer goes out. Sarah’s engineering brief survives every question. Daniel’s sales brief fails on the first.

Step 2. A short tax memo from a UK adviser

A PE-risk memo from a UK-qualified tax adviser costs £1,500 to £5,000, depending on complexity. The memo does not bind HMRC. It is strong evidence of reasonable care if HMRC challenges, which matters for penalty position. Get the memo before the first commercial hire, not after.

Step 3a. Structure to avoid

If the activities can be done without triggering PE, and most operational and engineering activities can, structure the engagement that way. EOR through Teamed UK Ltd, no UK office in the parent’s name, no UK customer-facing commercial authority, working arrangements consistent with the role being internal to the global business.

Step 3b. Incorporate a UK entity

If the activities materially benefit from a UK presence (commercial perception, EMI share options, a UK BD team), or cannot be reshaped to avoid the trigger, the right answer is your own UK Ltd. The PE becomes explicit rather than accidental. You control the attribution and the transfer-pricing position.
