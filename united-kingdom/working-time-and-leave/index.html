The 48-hour week is a cap on average, not on any single week

UK workers cannot be required to work more than 48 hours a week, averaged over a 17-week reference period. A 60-hour week is fine if a quieter stretch brings the average back down.

Every worker can sign a voluntary opt-out and remove the cap entirely. Most professional employees do. The opt-out is in Teamed’s standard UK contract and can be revoked on seven days’ notice.

James manages a retail floor in Glasgow. Through November and December he runs 55-hour weeks. He can keep doing that as long as January and February drop back below 41. If they don’t, his employer is in breach.

Three things catch US employers:

  • You cannot make signing the opt-out a condition of the job. The contract can include it; the worker must be free to refuse without prejudice.
  • On-call time counts when the worker is actively working. A developer paged at 2am for a 40-minute incident response logs 40 minutes of working time. A nurse asleep on-site between calls logs all of it.
  • The 17-week clock resets only at the end of each reference period. A new hire is averaged over the weeks they have worked, not a calendar window.

What rest breaks are UK workers entitled to?

Any shift over 6 hours gets a 20-minute break, uninterrupted, away from the workstation. Anything less and you owe the worker compensatory rest.

Between shifts, every worker gets 11 hours of uninterrupted daily rest. Every week, 24 hours of weekly rest, or 48 hours per fortnight.

James, the Glasgow retail manager, closes at 10pm Thursday. He cannot legally open at 8am Friday. The earliest start is 9am. Schedule the shift any earlier and a tribunal will read the rota against you.

The 20-minute break has three rules. It must be continuous, not four five-minute coffees. The worker must be free of work duties. And it has to fall during the shift, not bolted on at the start or end.

Paid or unpaid is your call. The statutory minimum allows unpaid. Most office employers pay it. Retail and hospitality usually don’t.

Annual leave bundles the bank holidays. That trips up most US employers.

Every UK worker gets 5.6 weeks of paid annual leave. For a five-day worker that is 28 days. Bank holidays are inside that 28, not on top.

Emma designs apps for a Manchester agency on a five-day week. Her statutory entitlement is 28 days. England has 8 bank holidays. If your contract bundles them, Emma has 20 days she can book plus the 8 fixed days off. If your contract grants 28 days on top of the bank holidays, she has 36 days off in total.

Both are legal. Mixing them up is the most common new-hire dispute in the first 90 days.

5.6 UK Statutory Leave

Statutory minimum is 5.6 weeks per year for a full-time worker, 28 days including bank holidays. Part-timers prorate by working pattern. The 4 EU-origin weeks behave differently from the 1.6 UK-origin top-up: only the first can carry over for sickness or statutory leave, with 18 months to take.

The bundled pattern is the most common UK arrangement. Teamed’s standard contract uses it by default. The “plus bank holidays” variant is available on request for clients who want a more competitive package.

The carry-over rule has teeth

Emma takes 14 days off in her first leave year. She is signed off sick for the last quarter and cannot use the remaining 6 days. The 4 EU-origin weeks she has not used will carry to the next year. She has 18 months from year-end to take them. The 1.6 UK-origin weeks are lost.

Get this wrong and you owe back-pay plus interest. Employment Appeal Tribunal awards for unpaid carry-over settle at 2 to 3 years of accrued holiday for long-tenure workers.

Holiday pay includes regular commission and overtime

Holiday pay must reflect what the worker normally earns. Base salary plus regular overtime, regular commission, regular allowances. The reference period is 52 weeks of pay history. Quarterly commission counts.

For Emma on £45,000 plus a quarterly £1,500 performance bonus, holiday pay is calculated on the blended weekly figure, not just the base salary. Pay the base alone and you owe the difference plus tribunal costs.

How many UK bank holidays are there?

Bank holiday counts vary by nation: 8 in England and Wales, 9 in Scotland (adds 2 January and 30 November), 10 in Northern Ireland (adds St Patrick’s Day and Battle of the Boyne).

When a bank holiday falls on a weekend, the following Monday is granted in lieu.

If Emma in Manchester gets 8 bank holidays a year, James in Glasgow gets 9, and Aisha’s Belfast hire gets 10. Same UK contract, different leave totals. Payroll has to know the work location.

There is no statutory premium for working a bank holiday

Time-and-a-half and double time on bank holidays are contractual, not statutory. Most office sectors pay base. Retail and hospitality usually pay a premium because the labour market expects it.

If you trade on bank holidays, write the premium into the contract. Quietly paying base on Christmas Day because the law allows it will cost you the hire within six months.

Parental leave became a day-one right on 6 April 2026

From 6 April 2026, paternity leave and unpaid parental leave are day-one rights. The 26-week and 1-year qualifying periods are gone.

Aisha joins a Bristol fintech startup on 1 July 2026. Her partner gives birth three weeks later. She qualifies for two weeks of paid paternity leave immediately. Pre-April 2026 she would have needed 26 weeks of service first.

Statutory family-leave pay sits at £194.32 per week (or 90% of average weekly earnings, whichever is lower). The first six weeks of maternity pay run at 90% of earnings before the rate kicks in.

What changed on 6 April 2026

Three rights moved to day one:

  • Paternity leave. Was 26 weeks of service. Now day one.
  • Unpaid parental leave. Was 1 year of service. Now day one.
  • Statutory Sick Pay. Was a 3-day waiting period and a lower earnings limit. Both abolished.

The rate uplifts are smaller. SSP moved from £118.75 to £123.25 per week. Family-leave pay sits at £194.32.

Competitive UK employers enhance well above the statutory floor

Most mid-sized UK employers pay above statutory. Common patterns:

  • Enhanced maternity: 16 weeks at full pay, then drop to SMP.
  • Enhanced paternity: 2 weeks at full pay. Replaces £194.32.
  • Equalised parental leave: the same enhanced package whoever takes the leave.

For Aisha’s Bristol startup hiring at the senior-engineer level, a 2-week paternity-leave package at full pay (around £3,500 gross) costs roughly £3,300 more than statutory. That is the going rate to keep her.

The new Statutory Sick Pay rules from April 2026

Statutory Sick Pay became a day-one right on 6 April 2026. The three waiting days are abolished. The lower earnings limit is removed.

The rate is £123.25 per week, paid for up to 28 weeks per period of incapacity for work.

When James in Glasgow is off sick with flu for four days, you owe him four days of SSP at the daily rate. Pre-April 2026 you would have owed nothing because the first three days were unpaid.

Three things to plan for:

  • SSP is your cost. You cannot reclaim it from HMRC. Build it into the total employer cost for every UK hire.
  • Self-certification covers the first 7 days. The employee signs a form. From day 8 you need a fit note from a GP, hospital doctor, nurse, occupational therapist, pharmacist, or physiotherapist.
  • Most professional UK employers enhance SSP. Common: 4 to 13 weeks of full pay on top of SSP. The legal floor is £123.25 per week. The market floor for office hires is closer to full salary for the first month.
