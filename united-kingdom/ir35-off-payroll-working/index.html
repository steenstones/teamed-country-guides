What is IR35 and what does it actually do?

IR35 is HMRC’s name for the off-payroll working rules. It is a tax test, not a contract test.

It asks one question. Ignore the contractor’s limited company for a moment. Would the worker look like an employee of the client? If yes, the engagement is “inside IR35”, and you pay the employment taxes.

Olivia is a limited-company contractor in London. She bills you £500 a day through her own Ltd. Inside IR35, you operate Pay As You Earn (PAYE) on her invoice, deduct income tax and employee National Insurance Contributions (NIC), and pay employer NIC on top. Outside IR35, she invoices and you pay gross.

The rules bite when three things are true at the same time:

  • A worker personally provides services to a client.
  • They do it through an intermediary, usually their own personal service company (PSC).
  • The working arrangement, looked at without the PSC, would be employment under the common-law tests.

The original anti-avoidance objective was to stop workers disguising employment income as dividends and skipping employer National Insurance. The 2021 reform changed who carries the risk of getting it wrong, not the underlying test.

Since 2021, the engager carries the risk

Until 2017, the contractor assessed IR35 and paid the tax if they got it wrong.

The 2017 reform moved that responsibility to public-sector clients. The 2021 private-sector reform did the same for every medium and large private client in the UK.

Today, the engager (you) is the “deemed employer” for IR35 purposes. If the assessment is wrong, you owe back PAYE, employer NIC, interest, and penalties. HMRC can reach back up to 6 tax years, longer for deliberate behaviour.

Who counts as the deemed employer

Ben is a personal service company (PSC) consultant in Edinburgh, contracted to you directly. You are the deemed employer for Ben’s engagement, full stop.

Add an agency in the middle and the fee-payer position usually shifts to the agency closest to Ben’s PSC. You still issue the Status Determination Statement and you can still carry the liability if that determination is wrong.

The Small Company Exemption keeps some hirers out of scope

End clients that meet the “small company” definition stay out of the 2021 reform. To qualify you need two of the following three: turnover at or under £10.2m, balance sheet total at or under £5.1m, employees at or under 50.

If you qualify, the assessment and the tax risk stay with the contractor’s PSC, as they did under the original 2000 rules. Cross either threshold for two consecutive years and the reform applies to your next contractor engagement.

What is a Status Determination Statement (SDS)?

The Status Determination Statement (SDS) is the written IR35 verdict you give the contractor and every party between you and them in the chain.

It says inside or outside, with reasons. Issue no SDS and the law puts you in the deemed-employer seat by default. Whatever the working arrangement actually looks like, you owe the tax.

01 Status Determination Statement

Four things HMRC checks if it opens an enquiry: the SDS is in writing, the conclusion is stated, the reasons are recorded, and reasonable care was taken. Miss any one and you become the deemed employer by default, regardless of what the working arrangement looks like. Your in-house UK employment specialist reviews each SDS edge case before it lands with HMRC, not a ticket queue.

In Writing Inside / Outside Reasons Recorded Reasonable Care

Priya is a Bristol freelance designer who works for you through her own Ltd. Before her first invoice, you issue an SDS that says “outside IR35” with reasons. You send it to Priya. If an agency sits in the chain, you send it to them too, and they pass it on. The SDS travels with the engagement.

Four things every SDS has to do:

  • Be in writing.
  • State the conclusion: inside or outside.
  • Set out the reasons.
  • Be reached with reasonable care, not lifted from a template.

HMRC’s own Check Employment Status for Tax (CEST) tool can support the SDS. But only if the answers reflect the actual working arrangement, not the one you wish you had.

The 45-day disagreement clock

If Priya disagrees with the SDS, she has a statutory right to challenge it. You have 45 days to consider the challenge and respond.

Miss the deadline and you inherit the deemed-employer position by default, even if your original determination was correct on the facts. Calendar the response date the day the challenge lands.

CEST is a starting point, not a safe harbour

HMRC will stand behind CEST determinations on one condition: the answers reflect the true working arrangement.

Tribunals routinely overturn CEST verdicts when the inputs were optimistic. The tool gives you a written answer in fifteen minutes. It does not give you cover if you fed it the wrong picture.

Three blind spots show up in the case law:

  • Mutuality of Obligation (MOO). Tribunals often treat MOO as decisive. CEST treats it as background.
  • Substitution. CEST asks a yes/no on substitution. Tribunals look for genuine, unfettered substitution, which is much harder to evidence.
  • Snapshot risk. CEST runs short. The same engagement, asked about on day 30 and day 300, can produce two honest answers.

Ben’s engagement is a useful test. He started on a 6-week piece of work with a defined deliverable. By month nine he is on a rolling extension, attending Monday stand-ups, and the project manager allocates him work like a permanent. CEST run on day one might have said outside. The day-300 reality looks inside.

The defensible move is to run CEST, then put the contract and the working pattern in front of an experienced IR35 specialist. Run the Contractor Classifier for a structured second opinion. If the specialist disagrees with CEST, weight their view higher.

An EOR move can be HMRC’s evidence, not your defence

Move an at-risk PSC contractor onto an Employer of Record and you create a textbook PAYE arrangement. Same role, same client, same hours, now plainly an employee of the EOR.

HMRC’s reading of that move is rarely flattering. The working arrangement looked like employment all along. You just made it official.

The lookback for an IR35 enquiry is 6 tax years. Switching Olivia to an EOR next Monday does nothing about the prior six years of PSC invoices. It can, however, focus HMRC’s attention on them.

When EOR is the right answer

Once you have honestly assessed an engagement as inside IR35, three structures are available:

  1. Direct employment on your own UK entity.
  2. EOR employment via Teamed Ltd as the legal employer.
  3. Continue the PSC engagement, but operate PAYE through the deemed-employer mechanism.

EOR fits cleanest for engagements under twelve months, or where you do not want to add the headcount to your own UK payroll. It does not retroactively cure historical IR35 exposure, and nothing else does either.

Four common-law tests do the real work

HMRC and the tribunals weigh four pillars: personal service, mutuality of obligation, control, and integration.

The first two have to be present for an employment finding. The second two carry weight but are not decisive on their own. The contract matters. The day-to-day reality matters more.

1. Personal service

Can the worker send a genuine substitute? Priya, the Bristol designer, runs a two-person studio and routinely sends her partner to client review meetings. That is real substitution and it cuts strongly toward self-employment. A clause that says “the client must approve any substitute” or “the substitute must hold the same accreditation” weakens that defence on contact with HMRC.

2. Mutuality of Obligation (MOO)

Are you obliged to offer work and is the contractor obliged to accept it? Continuous flow, even by habit rather than contract, points to employment. The freedom to refuse the next piece of work, or to end the engagement, points the other way. Tribunals often treat this pillar as decisive.

3. Control

Who decides what gets done, where, when, and how? A contractor is engaged for an outcome. An employee is directed on method. Set hours, mandatory attendance at internal stand-ups, and a line manager allocating Ben’s tickets all push the engagement toward employment.

4. Integration

Is the contractor part of your organisation or genuinely external? Internal email addresses, performance reviews, attendance at all-hands, and a place on the org chart point inside. Olivia listed on your “Meet the Team” page with a staff headshot is the example most often cited in tribunal judgments.
