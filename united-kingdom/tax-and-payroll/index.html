How does UK payroll tax work in 2026?

Employer NIC at 15%. Four income tax bands. A 60% marginal cliff between £100k and £125k that ambushes first-time UK employers. The headline rates haven’t moved — the frozen thresholds are doing the work.

If you budget a UK hire at the salary number on the offer letter, you have under-costed it by 15%. Employer National Insurance on every pound above £5,000 a year, no upper limit, with no employee deduction to offset it.

On an £80,000 salary, that is £11,250 a year of employer NIC sitting on top of gross. From 6 April 2026 the rate stays at 15% and the £5,000 threshold stays frozen until 2030–31, so every pay rise lands fully exposed.

Most US founders have heard that the UK has “payroll taxes”. Fewer understand the 60% marginal cliff between £100k and £125k that catches every senior hire.

This page covers employer NIC, employee NIC, the four income tax bands, the £100k personal-allowance taper, PAYE Real-Time Information, and the three documents every UK payroll has to produce.

Employer NIC is the line US founders miss

You pay 15% on every pound of gross salary above £5,000 a year. No upper ceiling. No deduction at the employee end to soften it.

Hire Emma, a designer in Manchester, on £60,000. Your employer NIC bill on her is £8,250 a year. Her gross cost to you is £68,250 before pension, before the Apprenticeship Levy, before anything else.

Eligible employers offset up to £10,500 a year via the Employment Allowance. The £100,000 prior-year eligibility cap was removed in 2025, so most small and mid-sized UK Ltds now qualify. Single-director limited companies with no other employee do not.

The Apprenticeship Levy threshold

Cross £3 million of annual pay bill and you pay 0.5% Apprenticeship Levy on everything above £15,000 of liability. For a mid-market business hiring through Teamed’s EOR, the Levy typically triggers around 40 to 50 UK employees on tech-sector salaries.

The Levy can be drawn down to fund apprenticeship training within 24 months. After that the funds expire to the Treasury.

What does the employee pay?

Employee Class 1 NIC is 8% between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2% above that. The drop to 2% in the higher band is deliberate — it keeps marginal NIC light at the top so that the income-tax cliff stands out on its own.

Take Aisha, a basic-rate hire at a Bristol startup on £35,000. Her employee NIC is 8% on the £22,430 above the primary threshold, or £1,794 a year. Her income tax on the same band is £4,486. Combined deduction: about £6,280, before pension and student loan.

NIC is calculated per pay period, not annually. That means a one-off £20,000 bonus is taxed differently from the same amount spread evenly across twelve months. Bonus-heavy roles need payroll that runs NIC on the actual period each pay event lands in.

Self-employed NIC (Class 2 and Class 4) sits outside this page. Class 4 currently runs at 6% between the relevant thresholds.

UK income tax bands for 2025–26

Four bands: 0% to £12,570, 20% to £50,270, 40% to £125,140, 45% above. Frozen at these levels until 2027–28.

Frozen bands plus wage inflation pull more workers into the higher bands every year. The Office for Budget Responsibility calls it “fiscal drag”. Your senior hires feel it as a tax rise every April even though no rate moved.

Scotland is on its own track

Scottish-resident taxpayers pay different bands and different rates under the Scottish Rate of Income Tax. The divergence is real at the top end — Scotland adds an “advanced” band at 45% from £75,000 and a “top” rate of 48% above £125,140.

James runs retail for a national chain out of Glasgow on £105,000. His marginal income tax in Scotland is 45%, not 40%. Add the 2% employee NIC and the personal-allowance taper, and his real marginal rate on the next pound earned is north of 67%.

Residence drives the tax code, not where the employer sits. Teamed’s payroll runs the right band based on the HMRC-issued code.

The £100k cliff: where the 60% marginal rate lives

Earn over £100,000 and your personal allowance starts to disappear. You lose £1 of allowance for every £2 of income above £100,000. By £125,140 it is gone.

The maths: every £100 you earn between £100k and £125k taxes £100 at 40% (£40), and reclaims £50 of personal allowance — meaning another £50 of income now sits in the 40% band (£20). Total tax on that £100: £60. A 60% marginal rate.

James the Glasgow head of retail at £105,000 is in that cliff right now. A £5,000 pay rise nets him £2,000. A £5,000 salary-sacrifice pension contribution costs him the same £2,000 in take-home but adds the full £5,000 to his pension.

Salary sacrifice is the standard fix

Pension contributions made by salary sacrifice reduce adjusted net income pound for pound. They also reduce employer NIC, which means a well-designed scheme is cheaper for you than paying the same amount as salary.

For James, a salary sacrifice that drops his adjusted net income from £105,000 to £100,000 keeps the full personal allowance, kills the 60% band, and saves both him and you NIC on the sacrificed amount. Set it up at offer stage. Retrofitting it after the tax year starts is harder.

If you offer a hire above £100,000 and don’t flag the cliff, expect a difficult conversation with them six months later when they read their P60.

How does PAYE Real-Time Information work?

You submit a Full Payment Submission (FPS) electronically to HMRC on or before each pay date. Not after. Late or missing FPS filings trigger automatic monthly penalties starting at £100 for the smallest employers.

PAYE stands for Pay As You Earn. RTI stands for Real-Time Information. Together they mean HMRC sees the wage data the same day the employee does — nothing batched up for an annual return.

01 HMRC RTI Filing Cadence

The FPS files electronically through the HMRC RTI gateway on or before each pay date, never after. The EPS files monthly when you’re claiming Employment Allowance or when no FPS was due that month. Penalties stack monthly under the automated regime — no warning letter precedes them.

FPS · on or before payday EPS · monthly if claims P60 · 31 May P11D · 6 July

Three submissions cover most UK payrolls:

  • FPS (Full Payment Submission) — on or before each pay date, for every employee paid.
  • EPS (Employer Payment Summary) — monthly, when you’re claiming Employment Allowance or when no FPS was due.
  • EYU (Earlier Year Update) — correction submissions for prior tax years where needed.

What do P45, P60 and P11D actually do?

A P45 goes to an employee when they leave. A P60 goes to every current employee by 31 May, summarising the tax year that ended on 5 April. A P11D reports any benefits in kind by 6 July.

Get the timing wrong on the P60 or P11D and you trigger automatic HMRC penalties. The P45 has no direct penalty, but the new employer needs it to set the right tax code — without it, the employee likely lands on an emergency code and overpays for months.

Pension auto-enrolment sits inside payroll

Pension contributions are 3% employer + 5% employee minimum on qualifying earnings between £6,240 and £50,270.

Every employee aged 22 or over earning more than £10,000 a year has to be auto-enrolled into a qualifying scheme from day one of eligibility. The employee can opt out within the first month and get their contributions back. After month one, it’s a one-way change until the three-year re-enrolment cycle resets it.

For Emma at £60,000 in Manchester, the qualifying-earnings band runs from £6,240 to £50,270 — so the employer 3% lands on £44,030 of earnings. That is £1,321 a year, on top of her gross salary and the £8,250 of employer NIC.

How do student loan deductions work in payroll?

UK graduates repay their student loans through PAYE once their earnings cross the relevant plan threshold. There are five plans, each with its own trigger.

For Aisha at £35,000 in Bristol on Plan 5 (post-2023 starters in England and Wales), the threshold is £25,000. She repays 9% on the £10,000 above it, or £900 a year, deducted from each payslip.
