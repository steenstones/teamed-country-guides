If you hired in the UK before 6 April 2026 and have not rewritten your contracts and handbook, you are out of compliance today. Not in January. Today.

The collective-redundancy award doubled from 90 to 180 days’ pay per affected employee. On a 20-person UK team facing restructure, that is £430,000 of statutory exposure before the underlying redundancy bill, up from £215,000 a year ago.

Most US founders who hired in the UK in 2024 have heard "day-one rights are coming". Fewer have read the actual schedule.

This page covers the five April 2026 changes that are live now, the January 2027 unfair-dismissal cliff that compresses your safe-dismissal window from two years to six months, and what to put in your handbook this quarter.

Five things changed on 6 April 2026

On 6 April 2026, five UK employment rules changed at once. Paternity leave became a day-one right. Unpaid parental leave became a day-one right. Statutory Sick Pay became a day-one right (no more three waiting days, no lower earnings limit). The weekly rates went up. The collective-redundancy protective award doubled to 180 days’ pay per affected employee.

Emma is a designer you hired in Manchester three weeks ago. Her partner has just had a baby. Under the old rules she had to wait until her 26-week anniversary to take paternity leave. Under the new rules she takes it now, and you pay the statutory rate from her first day.

If your UK handbook still says "26 weeks’ service required" for paternity, or "three waiting days" for sick pay, you are quoting the old law. Every new hire who reads it has a claim.

01 Day-One Rights since 6 April 2026

Four protections now kick in from day one of employment: statutory sick pay (waiting days abolished, lower earnings limit removed), paternity leave, unpaid parental leave, and the existing day-one unfair-dismissal protection in specific protected-reason cases. Discrimination has always been day one. Flexible working requests have been day one since April 2024.

January 2027 compresses the safe-dismissal window from two years to six months

From January 2027, an employee can bring an unfair-dismissal claim after six months of service. The current bar is two years. This is the biggest single change in the package, and the one most US founders have not priced in.

The press calls this "day-one rights". It is not. There is still a qualifying period. It is just shorter, by a factor of four.

James runs a 12-person retail team in Glasgow. He hired a store manager who is not working out at month seven. Today, he can dismiss with notice and pay, no tribunal risk. From January 2027, the same dismissal needs a documented fair procedure (a written warning, a hearing, a right of appeal), or James loses the claim and pays up to a year’s wages in compensation.

The safe-dismissal window goes from 24 months to six. The cost of getting it wrong stays the same.

Four things you do now to be ready for January:

  • Substantive probation reviews. Not a tick-box at month five. A documented assessment with named concerns, evidence, and an action plan.
  • Performance management starts earlier. A capability concern surfaced at month four gets a formal warning by month five, not "let’s wait and see" until month eight.
  • Settlement agreements get more common. A clean exit with finality replaces a contested dismissal, often for less than the legal fees of defending one.
  • Probation extensions become standard. A six-month probation extended to nine or twelve months where performance is unclear, signed by both sides, is your best protection.

Discrimination is uncapped, and starts at the job advert

Discrimination protection has always been a day-one right in the UK. There is no qualifying period, no compensation cap, and the protection covers nine characteristics.

Unfair-dismissal compensation maxes out at £105,707 in 2025/26. Discrimination compensation has no cap. A six-figure discrimination award is not unusual. The number is set by the loss the claimant proves, plus injury to feelings.

Aisha runs HR at a Bristol startup. A manager makes a comment in a one-to-one about an employee’s pregnancy and her "commitment to the team". That one comment, if pleaded as discrimination, is uncapped, has no qualifying period, and can be cited even if the employee was never dismissed.

Every UK HR process, recruitment, performance review, redundancy selection, promotion, termination, needs to be auditable against those nine characteristics. The records you keep on hiring decisions matter more than the decisions themselves when a tribunal looks back two years later.

Whistleblowing is broader than the headlines suggest

A UK employee who makes a "qualifying disclosure" about wrongdoing has day-one protection from being dismissed or disciplined for it. No qualifying period. No compensation cap.

The protection is wider than the classic image of a whistleblower with documents. A grievance about working time, a complaint about cash-in-hand pay practices, a report about health and safety, all qualify if framed as a disclosure rather than a personal complaint.

A "qualifying disclosure" needs four things: it has to be a disclosure of information (not a complaint about treatment), it has to be in the public interest, it has to relate to a recognised category of wrongdoing, and it has to go to an appropriate recipient. The employee is usually the recipient. A regulator can also qualify.

What is the UK GDPR, and what does it mean for employee data?

UK GDPR is the British version of the EU General Data Protection Regulation, kept in UK law after Brexit. It treats employee records as personal data and gives every employee a set of rights over them.

As an employer you need a lawful basis to process employee data, a written privacy notice issued at hire, and a 72-hour clock to report any data breach to the regulator. Subject access requests get a written response within one month, free of charge.

Aisha’s startup gets a subject access request from an ex-employee asking for every email mentioning her, plus her full HR file. Aisha has 30 days. She can extend by two months if the request is genuinely complex, but she has to tell the employee within the first 30 days. Miss the clock and the regulator can fine up to 4% of global turnover.

TUPE is the reason switching EOR providers is never free

TUPE stands for the Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations. It protects employees when their employer changes, by transferring them automatically to the new employer on the same terms, with their full continuity of service.

TUPE applies to business sales. It also applies to service-provision changes, like switching an outsourced service from Provider A to Provider B. And it applies when you move UK employees from one Employer of Record to another.

James decides to switch his 12-person Glasgow team from his current EOR to Teamed. TUPE applies. Teamed inherits the existing contracts, the existing salaries, the existing notice periods, and the existing service dates. James cannot use the move to vary terms; the new employer is bound by what the old one signed.

Most EOR-to-EOR moves run by mutual consent without anyone contesting TUPE. But the legal framework is real, and the paperwork has to match.
