If you spin up your own UK Ltd for your first two or three London hires, you will burn six weeks and £15,000 before you write the first payslip. The maths does not work yet.

A UK entity costs £3,200 to £4,500 per month to run, on top of £3,000 to £15,000 in setup. An EOR costs £479 per employee per month. Below 8 UK staff, the EOR is cheaper every month of the year.

Most US companies have heard the crossover lands at five employees. In practice it lands at 15 to 20. The UK threshold sits higher than France or Germany because employer National Insurance Contributions, or NIC, are lower here.

This page covers the crossover maths, the entity setup and ongoing cost, the director liability hidden in your own UK Ltd, and how Teamed's GEMO graduation model carries you from EOR to entity on one platform.

The crossover lands later than people think

EOR cost scales linearly with headcount. Entity cost has a fixed monthly overhead, plus a smaller variable cost per employee. The two lines cross at a headcount that depends on salary band.

For most US companies hiring average UK tech salaries, the crossover starts at 8 employees and settles fully at 15 to 20. Below that, the EOR is cheaper.

Emma runs a US software company with 10 UK staff in Manchester at £60,000 each. On EOR that is £4,790 a month. Her own UK Ltd would cost about £4,600 a month. The lines are within £200 of each other.

At Emma's headcount the maths is a wash. The decision is no longer about cost. It is about what an entity unlocks: EMI options, UK tax substance, and a UK MD on the payroll.

The underlying calculation is straightforward. Teamed's UK EOR fee is £479 per employee per month, flat. Your own UK Ltd has a roughly fixed monthly overhead, covering payroll, accounting, statutory filings, and a registered address, of £3,200 to £4,500.

What a UK entity actually costs to set up

A UK Ltd costs £3,000 to £15,000 to set up, depending on share structure, registered office, professional fees, and bank account difficulty.

Timeline runs roughly 6 weeks from decision to first payroll, if you go full-service through a corporate-services firm or through Teamed's GEMO offering.

A UK Ltd is one of the cheapest entities in Europe to incorporate. The headline £12 incorporation fee at Companies House is misleading, though. It excludes everything operational that actually matters.

The bank account is the hidden bottleneck

UK bank accounts for foreign-parented companies have tightened sharply since 2024. Expect 3 to 8 weeks from application to opened account with most high-street banks. It runs longer if the directors are not UK-resident.

This is usually the gating step that turns a 4-week incorporation into a 10-week operational entity. A US founder filing for the Ltd in week one will not be running payroll out of it until week ten.

UK entity ongoing cost: £3,200 to £4,500 a month

Ongoing compliance averages £3,200 to £4,500 a month for a small UK Ltd doing payroll, accounting, statutory filings, and basic HR admin.

Below 5 employees this overhead dominates the per-head cost. Above 15 employees, per-employee overhead drops sharply because the fixed costs amortise across more people.

James leads people for a US fintech with 25 UK staff in Glasgow. At that headcount his fixed entity overhead spreads across enough salaries that the per-head cost drops below the EOR fee. He is in entity territory.

The cost band widens above 15 employees. You need dedicated UK HR capacity, formal payroll processing rather than a bureau, and often a UK-based finance function. The fixed costs scale up, but the per-head cost still falls.

Director liability is the cost no one writes down

UK directors carry personal legal duties under the Companies Act, plus tax obligations under HMRC's "senior accounting officer" rules for larger entities.

The cost of getting this wrong is personal. Fines. Disqualification. Reputational damage that EOR clients never face. Most cost comparisons skip this dimension because it is hard to quantify.

Personal director duties

Under the Companies Act 2006, every UK director must promote the success of the company, exercise reasonable care and skill, declare personal interests, and avoid conflicts. These duties apply personally. They cannot be delegated to advisors.

A finance director who signs accounts they have not read is personally on the hook for any misstatement.

Statutory filings, the compliance treadmill

  • Confirmation statement, annually, with a £13 to £40 filing fee. Late filing triggers an automatic penalty.
  • Statutory accounts, within 9 months of year-end for small companies. Late filing costs £150 to £1,500, escalating with delay.
  • Corporation Tax return (CT600), within 12 months of year-end. The penalty regime is severe for repeat offences.
  • PSC register updates, within 14 days of any change in "people with significant control".
  • PAYE Real-Time Information, on or before each payday.
  • Pension regulator declarations, every 3 years.

Each filing on its own is small. Stacked together, they consume meaningful management attention. An EOR carries all of these for you on the EOR's own entity. Your own Ltd means the director carries them on the company's, and on theirs.

When you should stay on EOR

Below 8 employees, with project-flavoured hires, or while you are still validating the UK market, the EOR is the right answer.

The crossover is a maths threshold, not a strategic verdict. Five conditions point you to stay on EOR for longer than the headline cost number suggests.

  • Under 8 UK employees on average salaries. EOR is cheaper and faster every month.
  • Market validation phase. You are hiring 1 to 2 people to test commercial fit, with an option to scale back if the bet does not pan out. Entity setup is sticky. EOR is reversible.
  • Project-based hires. 6 to 12 month engagements where the upfront entity cost will not amortise.
  • No EMI options needed yet. Senior hires are not expecting share options, or you are still pre-Series-A. The entity becomes valuable once options become a comp lever.
  • Acquired team you may divest. Post-acquisition holding patterns where you do not want to add an entity that will need wind-up later.

When you should switch to your own entity

Above 15 employees consistently, with a multi-year UK growth plan, or with EMI and share-scheme needs, your own entity beats EOR on cost and unlocks capabilities the EOR cannot provide.

James, with 25 UK staff in Glasgow, is firmly in entity territory. Emma at 10 in Manchester is in the wash zone where the maths is even and the structural triggers carry more weight than cost.

02 GEMO Crossover Triggers

Four operational signals push a UK EOR setup toward its own UK Ltd. The crossover lands at 15 to 20 employees when entity overhead fully amortises across salary, NIC, and pension lines. The maths starts to flip around 8 employees, and any one of these triggers combined with sustained headcount tips the call. Track them against your hiring plan.

15+ UK hires sustained EMI options needed UK MD on payroll Tax-treaty substance
  • Sustained headcount above 15 UK employees on average salaries. The entity overhead amortises across enough people that per-head cost drops well below EOR.
  • EMI share option scheme. The Enterprise Management Incentive scheme requires a UK trading company. An EOR cannot issue EMI options on your behalf. The options have to sit in your own entity. This is the single biggest structural pull toward incorporation for venture-backed UK businesses.
  • Substance for tax-treaty purposes. A few cross-border tax structures need actual UK substance (employees, address, banking). An EOR does not count as your own substance.
  • Customer or investor expectation. Enterprise UK customers occasionally prefer contracting with a UK-incorporated supplier. Worth flagging early if relevant.

How Teamed's GEMO graduation model handles the transition

Teamed graduates customers from EOR to their own entity on one platform, with the same named UK country specialist, the same contracts of employment (novated to the new entity), and no break in tenure or benefits.
