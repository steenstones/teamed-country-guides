If you fire a UK employee the way you fire a Texan, you will lose at tribunal. There is no at-will employment in the UK. Every dismissal needs a fair reason and a documented process.

Skip the procedure on a two-year employee and a tribunal can award up to £118,223 in compensation, plus a 25% uplift if you ignored the Acas Code. A botched collective redundancy of 30 staff now risks roughly £4 to £6 million in protective awards from April 2026.

Most US and European founders have heard the UK is “hard to fire”. Fewer understand how it actually works.

This page covers the statutory notice schedule, the £751 weekly redundancy cap, the five fair reasons for dismissal, the doubled collective-redundancy penalty, and the January 2027 unfair-dismissal change.

How much notice must you give a UK employee?

Statutory minimum notice runs none under 1 month of service, 1 week between 1 month and 2 years, then 1 week per year, capped at 12 weeks at 12+ years.

The contract sets the floor for the employer. The statute sets the floor for the employee. Whichever is longer applies.

Senior roles routinely carry 3 to 6 months of contractual notice. C-suite contracts often run to 12 months with garden leave clauses attached.

Pay in Lieu of Notice (PILON)

You can pay the notice period rather than work it, if the contract has a PILON clause. PILON is the lump sum that buys the employee out of the working notice.

PILON payments are fully taxable as earnings. The historic tax-free treatment was abolished in 2018.

A separate ex gratia termination payment is different. The first £30,000 of an ex gratia payment sits outside income tax, distinct from PILON, contractual bonuses, and accrued holiday.

UK dismissal is procedure-driven, not at-will

UK dismissal needs fair procedure from day one of employment. The formal protection against unfair dismissal kicks in after 2 years today, dropping to 6 months from January 2027.

Procedure means a written warning, a chance to improve, a documented review meeting, and a right of appeal. Skip any of those steps and you carry the risk.

The Acas Code is the operational guide. Ignore it and a tribunal can lift the award by up to 25%.

Emma is a 28-year-old designer at a Manchester software business. She has 2 years and 3 months of service. Performance has slipped over the last quarter and her manager wants her out by month-end.

The clean route runs four meetings over six to eight weeks. First, an informal performance conversation with notes shared in writing. Second, a formal capability review with a written warning and a clear improvement plan. Third, a follow-up review after four weeks. Fourth, the dismissal meeting if performance has not moved, with the right of appeal offered in writing.

Skip the second meeting and Emma can claim unfair dismissal. The base award sits at her statutory redundancy figure. The compensatory award stacks on top, capped at the lower of one year’s pay or £118,223. Add the 25% Acas uplift and the bill on a £42,000 designer reaches roughly £52,500 before legal fees.

The five fair reasons for dismissal

  1. Conduct, gross misconduct or sustained misconduct after warning.
  2. Capability, performance below the required standard with reasonable support to improve.
  3. Redundancy, a genuine role redundancy with fair selection.
  4. Statutory bar, e.g. loss of right to work or a lost driving licence for a driver role.
  5. Some other substantial reason (SOSR), a catch-all that tribunals read narrowly.

Any dismissal outside these five is presumed unfair once the employee has qualifying service.

The January 2027 change you have not priced in yet

From January 2027 the unfair-dismissal qualifying period drops from 2 years to 6 months under the Employment Rights Act 2025.

This is not the “day-one rights” widely reported in the press. The Act introduced day-one rights for paternity leave, parental leave, and SSP from 6 April 2026. Unfair dismissal still has a qualifying period. The period is just shorter.

The practical shift is real. From January 2027 you carry meaningful unfair-dismissal risk on any employee past 6 months. The window for “easy” dismissal compresses from 24 months down to 6.

Your six-month review meeting now needs to carry the same weight as a year-one review does today.

How is UK redundancy pay calculated?

Redundancy pay starts at 2 years’ continuous service. The formula runs 0.5 week per year under 22, 1 week per year between 22 and 40, 1.5 weeks per year at 41 and above.

The weekly figure is capped at £751 from 6 April 2026. Service is capped at 20 years. The lifetime maximum statutory redundancy payout sits at £22,530.

A high salary does not push the statutory figure up. The cap binds.

James, a Glasgow retail manager

James is 45. He has run a Glasgow electronics shop for 8 years on a £48,000 salary. His role is made redundant when the chain consolidates two stores.

His age band sets the multiplier at 1.5 weeks per year of service. His 8 years of service produce 12 weeks of statutory pay. His salary divides to roughly £923 a week.

The cap pulls that £923 down to £751. His statutory redundancy works out at 12 × £751 = £9,012. The whole figure sits inside the £30,000 tax-free ceiling, so James takes it home in full.

Many UK employers run enhanced schemes that pay more than the statutory minimum, often uncapped weekly pay or higher multipliers. Enhanced terms can be contractual and binding, or discretionary and not binding. Saying which it is in writing avoids a custom-and-practice argument later.

Collective redundancy: the doubled penalty from April 2026

Collective redundancy rules trigger at 20 or more redundancies at one establishment within a 90-day window.

Consultation runs 30 days for 20 to 99 redundancies, 45 days for 100 or more. The protective award for failure to consult doubled to 180 days’ full pay per affected employee from April 2026.

This is the single biggest change in UK termination risk in 2026.

The collective-redundancy penalty, in one line
180 days’ full pay per affected employee, if you skip consultation.
Doubled from 90 days under the Employment Rights Act 2025, effective April 2026.

Aisha, a Bristol startup founder

Aisha runs a 110-person Bristol software business. Funding tightens and she plans to cut 22 roles across engineering and customer success. That sits over the 20-employee threshold.

Skip the formal consultation and the protective award maths runs 22 × 180 days at full pay. On an average gross salary of £55,000, that is roughly £220 a day. The exposure lands at 22 × 180 × £220 = £871,200, before individual unfair-dismissal claims stack on top.

Run the same maths at 30 redundancies and the figure clears £1.18 million. Push it to 100 and you are into the £4 to £6 million bracket. The change from 90 to 180 days is not marginal.

Five things the consultation has to cover

  • HR1 filed with the Insolvency Service before any redundancies are announced.
  • Employee representatives elected if there is no recognised trade union.
  • Information shared and consultation opened 30 or 45 days before the first dismissal date.
  • Alternatives genuinely considered: redeployment, voluntary redundancy, reduced hours.
  • Selection criteria applied objectively, with a paper trail for each scored employee.

Settlement agreements: the clean exit

A settlement agreement is the standard way to end a UK employment with finality. The employee gets compensation and signs away the right to bring claims. The employer gets certainty.

Independent legal advice for the employee is required by law. The employer typically pays for it at £500 to £1,500.

Settlement agreements suit contested performance, redundancy with enhanced terms, senior exits, and any termination where you want a clean stop rather than a process.

The agreement must be in writing, it must identify the specific claims being waived, and it must confirm the employee took independent advice from a named qualified adviser.
