How does Alaska wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

Alaska is one of only two US states with mandatory daily overtime. AS 23.10.060 requires 1.5 times the regular rate after 8 hours in a day OR 40 hours in a week, whichever yields more. State minimum wage is $13.00 per hour from 1 January 2026, rising to $14.00 from 1 July. Alaska prohibits tip credits entirely, so tipped workers earn the full minimum wage before tips.

Alaska wage and hour law in 2026 has more state-level texture than most US payroll teams expect. Minimum wage is $13.00 per hour from 1 January 2026 (raised by 2024 Ballot Measure 1), with scheduled jumps to $14.00 on 1 July 2026 and $15.00 on 1 July 2027. Overtime is the unusual rule: AS 23.10.060 requires 1.5 times the regular rate for hours over 8 in a day OR over 40 in a week, whichever yields more for the employee, with no double-counting between the two thresholds. Alaska prohibits the federal tip credit, so tipped workers must be paid the full minimum wage in cash before tips. Adult meal and rest breaks are not required. The one carve-out: AS 23.10.350 mandates a 30-minute break for any worker under 18 who works 5+ consecutive hours, with documentation and minimum-wage liability for missed breaks. The small-employer exemption: employers with fewer than 4 employees in the regular course of business are exempt from the overtime rules entirely.

What is Alaska’s minimum wage in 2026?

Alaska’s minimum wage in 2026 is $13.00 per hour from 1 January, rising to $14.00 from 1 July 2026 and $15.00 from 1 July 2027. The schedule was set by Ballot Measure 1, approved by Alaska voters in November 2024. From 2028 onwards, the minimum is indexed annually to the Consumer Price Index for Anchorage. Alaska’s rate already sits well above the federal $7.25 floor.

Three practical consequences for any employer paying Alaska wages in 2026:

  • Two rate changes inside a single calendar year. Payroll has to apply $13.00 from 1 January and switch cleanly to $14.00 on 1 July. Salary letters and offer paperwork tied to the minimum need the 1 July date written in.
  • No tip credit. Alaska law expressly bars the federal Fair Labor Standards Act tip credit (FLSA Section 3(m)). Every tipped worker, in every Alaska restaurant, bar, taxi, or hotel, is owed the full minimum wage in cash before any gratuity. Tips top up; they don’t substitute. This is a sharp departure from the federal $2.13 tipped cash wage that 43 US states permit in some form.
  • No subminimum for trainees, learners, or students under federal law. Alaska adopts the federal subminimum rules for learners (75 percent of minimum wage, with a Wage and Hour Division certificate) but applies them rarely. Assume full minimum wage unless you hold a specific certificate.

For multi-state employers, Alaska is now a mid-band state on minimum wage. As of January 2026 the headline rate sits between Florida ($14.00) and Maryland ($15.00), with the 1 July jump pushing Alaska to the same $14.00 level as Florida and several others. The Ballot Measure 1 rewrite

Ballot Measure 1 did three things in November 2024. It raised the minimum on a three-step schedule. It introduced statewide mandatory paid sick leave from 1 July 2025 (1 hour per 30 worked, up to 40 hours per year at small employers and 56 at large). And it banned mandatory captive-audience meetings on political and religious topics. The minimum-wage piece is the cleanest to comply with; the paid sick leave piece is covered on the Alaska paid leave page.

When does an Alaska employer have to pay overtime?

An Alaska employer pays overtime under AS 23.10.060: 1.5 times the regular rate for hours worked over 8 in a day OR over 40 in a week, whichever pays the employee more. Alaska is one of only two US states (with California) where daily overtime is mandatory. Hours that already triggered daily overtime are excluded from the 40-hour weekly calculation to prevent double-counting.

The mechanics catch most US payroll teams off guard. An Alaska employee who works 10 hours on Monday and 6 hours each on Tuesday through Friday hits 34 hours in the week but is still owed 2 hours of overtime, for the two hours over 8 on Monday. Same employee working 9 hours per day Monday through Friday hits 45 hours and is owed 5 hours of overtime, calculated as 5 daily overtime hours (one per day over 8) rather than 5 weekly overtime hours; the rule produces the same dollar number in this case but only because the math lines up cleanly.

The small-employer exemption

An employer that regularly employs fewer than 4 people is exempt from overtime under AS 23.10.060. Three employees, no overtime obligation, straight time for every hour worked. A fourth hire turns on the overtime clock for the whole workforce, going forward, and the Alaska Department of Labor takes the position that “regularly” means in the normal course of business, not on a one-off seasonal spike. Most growing US companies cross the 4-employee line within the first quarter of operations; budget for the overtime obligation from day one if you expect to grow past three.

Exempt vs non-exempt classification

To be exempt from both the federal weekly and the Alaska daily overtime rules, an employee must meet both a salary basis test and a duties test:

  • Salary basis: at least $684 per week ($35,568 per year), paid as a fixed salary not reduced by quality or quantity of work. Alaska defers to the federal floor; no state-level salary top-up applies.
  • Duties test: primary duty must be executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales as defined in 29 CFR Part 541. Job title alone doesn’t establish exemption.

A salaried “manager” making $50,000 per year who spends 90 percent of the day stocking shelves alongside hourly staff fails the duties test and is owed overtime. In Alaska, that includes the daily 8-hour trigger for every shift over 8 hours. Voluntary flexible work-hour plans

AS 23.10.060(15) allows an employer with 4+ employees to apply for a voluntary flexible work-hour plan that swaps daily overtime for a fixed 40-hour week with shifts up to 10 hours. The employee and employer file a signed written agreement with the Alaska Department of Labor; the Department issues a certificate; the employer can then schedule 4 x 10-hour days without daily overtime liability. The flex plan must be voluntary (no coercion, no condition of employment) and the schedule has to actually be followed, with deviations limited to one in five weeks. Are tipped employees paid differently in Alaska?

No. Alaska prohibits the FLSA tip credit. Every tipped worker is owed the full state minimum wage ($13.00 from 1 January 2026, $14.00 from 1 July) in cash before any gratuity is counted. Tips are the employee’s property on top of wages; they cannot be applied as a credit against the employer’s minimum-wage obligation. Alaska is one of only seven US states with this rule (joining California, Oregon, Washington, Nevada, Minnesota, and Montana).

Operational implications for restaurant, hospitality, and tourism employers:

  • Posted hourly wage matches what gets paid. A bartender hired at $13.00 per hour earns $13.00 per hour from the employer, full stop. Tips are over and above. By contrast, a federal-FLSA-floor state (Texas, for example) can pay the same bartender a $2.13 cash wage as long as tips bring total earnings to $7.25.
  • Tip pools are still permitted. Alaska employers can run a tip-pool arrangement that distributes pooled tips among customarily tipped employees (servers, bussers, bartenders). The 2018 federal amendment to the FLSA prohibits the employer or managers from sharing in any tip pool, regardless of whether the employer takes a tip credit. Alaska defers to that federal rule.
  • Service charges are not tips. A mandatory service charge added to a bill by the employer is employer revenue, not employee property. If the employer chooses to pass some or all of a service charge to staff, that distribution counts as wages (subject to payroll tax) rather than tips. Many Alaska tour operators and hotels structure compensation this way to provide stable income through the summer season.

The no-tip-credit rule is the second-biggest budgeting surprise for US employers expanding into Alaska, after the daily overtime rule. A $13.00 server with 25 hours per week earns $338 per week from the employer plus tips, compared with $53.25 plus tips in a $2.13-tipped-wage state. Build the rate into the unit economics; don’t price an Alaska restaurant on a Texas labour model. Source: Alaska Department of Labor and Workforce Development, Wage and Hour Frequently Asked Questions (labor.alaska.gov/lss/whfaq.htm). Accessed 21 May 2026.

Do Alaska employers have to give meal or rest breaks to adults?

No. Alaska law does not require employers to provide meal breaks or rest breaks to adult employees (age 18 and over). Federal FLSA does not mandate breaks either. If you do offer rest breaks of less than 20 minutes, federal law (29 CFR 785.18) counts them as paid hours worked. Meal periods of 30 minutes or more, where the employee is fully relieved of duty, can be unpaid.

The federal default rules govern what happens when you do choose to offer breaks:

  • Short rest breaks (5 to 20 minutes): 29 CFR 785.18 treats these as compensable working time. If you give an Alaska employee a 15-minute paid coffee break, that 15 minutes counts toward the 40-hour weekly and 8-hour daily overtime thresholds.
  • Meal periods (30+ minutes): 29 CFR 785.19 allows these to be unpaid, provided the employee is fully relieved from duty for the purpose of eating. An employee required to stay at their desk and answer the phone during a 30-minute lunch is “engaged to wait” and the time is compensable, which would also bring it back into the daily 8-hour count.
  • Nursing mothers: the federal PUMP Act (Section 7(r) of FLSA, extended by the 2022 act) requires reasonable break time and a private non-bathroom space to express breast milk, for one year after the child’s birth. This rule applies in Alaska and is non-negotiable for any FLSA-covered employer.

Most Alaska employers offer a 30-minute unpaid lunch and one or two 10-15 minute paid breaks per 8-hour shift, by policy not statute. The structure has to be documented in the employee handbook and applied consistently; ad-hoc “take a break when you need one” phrasing creates wage-and-hour exposure when an employee later claims they worked through unpaid time and breached the 8-hour daily overtime threshold as a result. The under-18 rule sits in a separate section

Adults have no statutory break entitlement, but minors do. AS 23.10.350 gives 30-minute breaks to anyone under 18 who works 5+ consecutive hours; the full rule is in the next section.

What is Alaska’s 30-minute break rule for workers under 18?

AS 23.10.350 requires a 30-minute break for any employee under 18 years of age who works 5 or more consecutive hours without a break. If a minor is scheduled for 6 or more consecutive hours, the 30-minute break must fall after the first 1.5 hours and before the last hour of the shift. Failure to provide the break creates a minimum-wage liability for the missed break time, enforceable as a wage claim by the Alaska Department of Labor.

Practical implementation for minor hires

Alaska’s tourism and fishing seasons hire heavily from the under-18 pool through the summer. Three operational rules cover most exposure:

  • Schedule the break, document the break. A 30-minute unpaid meal break should appear on the schedule for any shift of 5+ hours when the worker is under 18. The break has to be taken; the time card has to show it; the employer’s schedule note has to match.
  • Place the break inside the 1.5-to-last-hour window for 6+ hour shifts. A 10am-to-6pm shift cannot put the break at 10:30am (too early) or 5:30pm (too late). Aim for the middle third of the shift.
  • If you miss it, owe the minimum wage for the break length. Alaska treats a missed 30-minute break as a 30-minute minimum-wage claim. At $13.00 per hour through 1 July 2026, that’s $6.50 per missed break per minor; at $14.00 from 1 July, $7.00. The number is small; the audit exposure across a whole summer-season workforce is not.

Is on-call or waiting time paid in Alaska?

Federal FLSA controls this in Alaska. The test is “engaged to wait” (paid) vs “waiting to be engaged” (not paid). An employee at the workplace waiting for the next task is on the clock, and that time counts toward both the 8-hour daily and 40-hour weekly overtime triggers. An employee at home, free to use the time, who carries a phone in case they’re called in, is off the clock. The factual line is whether the employee can effectively use the time for their own purposes.

Three patterns Alaska employers see most often:

  • On-premises wait time: a delivery driver between runs reading a book in the warehouse breakroom. Engaged to wait. Compensable. Counts toward the 8-hour daily overtime trigger.
  • On-call at home with restrictions: an IT engineer required to stay within 15 minutes of the office, sober, and answer calls within 5 minutes. The restriction effectively prevents personal use. Likely compensable, depending on the call frequency and response window.
  • On-call at home, free to live normally: a service technician carrying a phone and expected to take one or two calls per week. Free to socialise, sleep, run errands. Not compensable for the wait time, only for the actual call response.

The federal Wage and Hour Division’s Fact Sheet #22 lists the controlling tests; the Alaska Department of Labor adopts them. The daily 8-hour overtime trigger gives the engaged-to-wait classification more bite in Alaska than in federal-only states. A driver who clocks in at 9am, completes a 7-hour run, and waits engaged at the warehouse for an extra 90 minutes before being released has worked 8.5 hours and is owed 30 minutes of overtime. Teamed’s payroll engine codes each on-call entry with the engaged-vs-waiting flag at clock-in, so the FLSA classification is set before the timesheet hits the pay run, not litigated after. Travel time, training time, meeting time

Three more FLSA-controlled patterns that catch employers out, with extra Alaska bite because of the daily trigger:

  • Travel time: ordinary commute is not compensable. Travel between job sites during the workday is compensable. Out-of-town overnight travel is compensable during normal working hours, even on weekends, if the travel cuts across the workday.
  • Training time: compensable unless all four of these are true: outside normal hours, voluntary, not directly job-related, no productive work performed.
  • Meeting time: attendance is compensable if mandatory or held during normal hours. Voluntary off-hours meetings with no job-related agenda are not compensable.

How should multi-state employers handle Alaska wage-and-hour law?

Apply the highest applicable standard for each employee, by employee location. Alaska sits above the federal floor on minimum wage, daily overtime, and tipped wages; California adds meal-and-rest-break premiums and a higher minimum wage; the federal floor states (most of the South and Midwest) sit below Alaska on all three counts. A single national handbook that promises Alaska-style daily overtime to every US employee is fine. A handbook that promises only the federal floor to an Alaska employee is a wage-and-hour claim waiting to happen.

Three operational rules for multi-state US employers with Alaska headcount:

  1. Maintain a state matrix, not a single policy. Document each state where you employ someone, with that state’s minimum wage, daily and weekly overtime triggers, tip-credit treatment, meal-break rule, rest-break rule, and sick-leave accrual. Re-check semi-annually; Alaska resets on 1 July as well as 1 January from 2026 onwards.
  2. Configure payroll for daily overtime per employee, not globally. A US-wide payroll provider has to be told that the Alaska employee’s daily 8-hour trigger is live. The default in most multi-state payroll systems is weekly-only; flipping the Alaska flag is a one-time setup that prevents 12 months of missed daily-overtime liability.
  3. Tag each employee’s work location. The wage-and-hour rule that applies is the rule of the state where the employee physically performs the work, not the state of incorporation. A New York-headquartered company with a remote employee living and working in Anchorage follows Alaska wage-and-hour law (and the Alaska no-tip-credit rule if the employee is in a tipped role). The cross-state rule rarely matters for salaried remote workers but always matters for hourly Alaska headcount.

For most early-stage US employers, the practical move is a single national handbook that defaults to the strictest applicable state for any benefit, plus a state-specific addendum for jurisdictions with hard mandates (California, New York, Massachusetts, Washington, Oregon, Colorado, Illinois, and Alaska for daily overtime). Alaska’s addendum is short: state the daily 8-hour overtime trigger, state the no-tip-credit rule, state the AS 23.10.350 minor-break rule, and reference the flex-plan option for compressed schedules.
