How does Arizona state income tax and unemployment insurance work in 2026?

A single flat 2.5 percent state income tax (one of the lowest in the country), a $8,000 SUTA wage base that caps employer unemployment cost early in the year, and a Form A-4 percentage election that simplifies withholding to one number per employee.

Arizona state payroll in 2026: state income tax at a single flat 2.5 percent for every filer regardless of income (ARS § 43-1011). State Unemployment Insurance (SUTA) costs 2.0 percent on the first $8,000 of wages for new employers, then 0.03 to 8.36 percent on experience under the Arizona DES UIT-0603A FY2026 rate chart. Every new hire completes Form A-4 and chooses a withholding percentage from seven options (0.5 to 3.5 percent); the default is 2.0 percent if no form is filed within 5 days of hire. Employers file Form A1-QRT quarterly, transmit deposits via Form A1-WP (or EFT), and reconcile annually on Form A1-R by 31 January. Arizona has no city or county payroll tax, which puts it among the lightest US states for combined statutory payroll burden.

What is the Arizona state income tax rate in 2026?

Arizona charges a single flat 2.5 percent on all taxable income, every filer, every dollar (ARS § 43-1011). The flat rate has applied since tax year 2023 after SB1828 (2021) replaced the prior 2.59 to 4.5 percent graduated ladder. Among states that levy income tax at all, only Indiana, North Dakota, and Pennsylvania run a comparably low single rate.

The flat-rate operational win

Flat-rate withholding removes the marginal-rate maths from every payslip. An Arizona employee earning $60,000 pays the same effective state rate as one earning $200,000, namely 2.5 percent of taxable income after federal adjustments and Arizona-specific deductions. There is no bracket creep, no annual indexation argument, and no employee asking why the marginal rate on a bonus looks different from their normal paycheque. The withholding side is built around a percentage election rather than a bracket lookup. Source: Arizona Department of Revenue and Arizona Legislature SB1828 (2021), accessed 21 May 2026.

What this means for offer letters

State tax is genuinely small at the headline. A $90,000 base in Arizona pays roughly $2,250 a year in state income tax, against $4,500 in Alabama at the 5 percent top rate or $8,370 in California at the 9.3 percent marginal bracket. The dollars saved show up in the take-home line of the offer letter and in employer-paid relocation maths.

What does an Arizona employer pay in unemployment insurance (SUTA)?

New Arizona employers pay 2.0 percent on the first $8,000 of each employee’s annual wages, capped at $160 per employee per year. After enough quarters to be experience-rated, actual employer cost lands in the 0.03 percent to 8.36 percent band per the Arizona DES UIT-0603A FY2026 chart. The $8,000 wage base is one of the lowest in the United States and means SUTA cost effectively stops at the end of an employee’s second pay period of the year.

Arizona DES sets each employer’s rate annually based on the reserve-ratio formula in ARS Title 23, Chapter 4: cumulative contributions paid in, minus benefits paid out to former employees, divided by average taxable payroll. A high-turnover employer drifts toward the top of the band; a stable employer with few claims settles near the floor. Authority: Arizona Revised Statutes Title 23, Chapter 4 (Employment Security).

The early-year cash-flow point

Because the $8,000 wage base is so low, a full-time Arizona employee earning $60,000 will hit the SUTA cap in late February. After that, SUTA is zero on every paycheque for the rest of the year. The annual UI bill is concentrated in Q1, not spread evenly. FUTA credit interaction

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) is 6 percent on the first $7,000. An Arizona employer that pays state SUTA in full and on time receives a FUTA credit of 5.4 percent, leaving an effective FUTA of 0.6 percent ($42 per employee per year). Late or partial SUTA payment erodes the credit. Teamed’s payroll books both lines together so the credit never gets lost. Source: IRS Topic 759, accessed 21 May 2026.

What is Form A-4 and how does the percentage election work?

Form A-4 is Arizona’s Employee’s Arizona Withholding Election, equivalent to the federal W-4 but built around a percentage choice instead of allowances. Every new Arizona employee completes one within 5 days of hire. The 2026 form offers seven options: 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0, or 3.5 percent of gross taxable wages. If the employee files no form, the employer withholds at the 2.0 percent default until they elect otherwise.

The percentage chosen on A-4 is the withholding rate applied to every paycheque, full stop. There is no bracket lookup, no allowance calculation, no married-jointly versus single grid. The employee is choosing how aggressively to over- or under-withhold relative to the 2.5 percent flat rate:

  • 0.5 to 2.0 percent: under-withholding. Employee will owe Arizona tax at filing time. Useful for employees with large itemised deductions, dependent credits, or other tax-reducing positions.
  • 2.5 percent: exact match to the flat rate. Withholding lands at roughly zero balance due, zero refund.
  • 3.0 to 3.5 percent: over-withholding. Employee will receive an Arizona refund. Useful for employees who treat withholding as forced savings.
  • Additional dollar withholding: an employee can also add a flat dollar amount on top of the percentage, on the same form.

The annual refresh requirement

Arizona is one of the states where the employee’s withholding election does not roll over automatically. Employers must provide each employee with a new A-4 by 31 January every year so the employee can confirm or change their percentage. If the employee keeps the prior election, that’s fine, but the employer’s legal obligation is to offer the choice annually. Source: Arizona Department of Revenue and Bloomberg Tax, accessed 21 May 2026.

The 5-day default trap

The 2.0 percent default is lower than what most full-time employees actually owe. A new hire who skips A-4 in their first week ends up under-withheld for the months it takes to notice, and then owes Arizona at filing. How does an Arizona employer file withholding (A1-QRT, A1-WP, A1-R)?

Three forms cover the lifecycle: Form A1-QRT reconciles each calendar quarter and is filed by every employer regardless of deposit cadence. Form A1-WP is the deposit voucher used by employers not on EFT. Form A1-R is the annual reconciliation, due 31 January after the tax year. All Arizona withholding returns must be filed electronically for tax years from and after 31 December 2019, through AZTaxes.gov.

Deposit cadence is federal-aligned

Arizona uses the federal four-quarter look-back to set deposit cadence. Employers with average quarterly liability under $1,500 deposit quarterly. Average liability $1,500 to $50,000 in the look-back period puts the employer on monthly. Above $50,000, semi-weekly. A single deposit obligation of $100,000 or more on any one payday triggers the next-banking-day rule, same as federal. The A1-QRT still reports the quarter even when an employer is on a semi-weekly deposit schedule.

Zero-filing rule

A quarterly filer with zero Arizona withholding for the period still files a zero A1-QRT, otherwise AZTaxes flags a missing return and starts the delinquency clock. Annual A1-R is required even in a zero year if the employer was active for any part of the year. Source: Arizona Department of Revenue, Employer Withholding Filing Obligations, accessed 21 May 2026.

Do Arizona cities or counties charge a local payroll tax?

No. Arizona has no city or county tax on payroll. There is no Arizona equivalent of Birmingham’s occupational tax, Philadelphia’s wage tax, or NYC’s local income tax. Withholding registration is state-only via the Arizona Joint Tax Application (JT-1) on AZTaxes.gov. Cities collect Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT), which is a sales-tax variant on business activity, not a payroll deduction.

Three things to keep straight on Arizona local tax:

  1. TPT is not payroll. Transaction Privilege Tax is levied on the seller’s gross receipts from taxable business activity. Phoenix charges 8.6 percent combined state-plus-city, Tucson 2 percent at city level. It has no relationship to employee wages and does not appear on a payslip.
  2. Local minimum wages do exist. Flagstaff sets its own minimum wage above the state floor under the Minimum Wage Act adopted by ballot initiative. Tucson followed with its own ordinance. Phoenix has not adopted a local minimum and uses the state figure. See the wages-and-overtime page for the city-by-city table.
  3. State-only registration. One application (JT-1) registers an employer for ADOR withholding tax and DES unemployment insurance simultaneously. There is no separate city payroll registration to chase.

For Teamed clients, the absence of local payroll tax is a genuine operational simplification. Multi-state employers running payroll in Pennsylvania (where dozens of municipalities run local Earned Income Tax) or Alabama (where Birmingham, Bessemer, and Gadsden all levy city occupational tax) feel the difference on the operations side. Arizona payroll is one state return, one state UI filing, one federal stack. Source: Arizona Department of Revenue, accessed 21 May 2026.

What is the Arizona minimum wage and overtime rule in 2026?

Arizona’s state minimum wage in 2026 is $14.70 per hour, set under the Fair Wages and Healthy Families Act (Proposition 206, 2016) with annual CPI indexation. Two cities run higher floors: Flagstaff at $17.85 and Tucson at $15.00. Overtime follows the federal FLSA: 1.5 times regular rate for non-exempt hours over 40 in a workweek. Arizona adds no state daily-overtime premium.

Overtime defers entirely to federal FLSA

Arizona has no state overtime statute. Non-exempt employees earn the FLSA 1.5x premium for hours over 40 in a workweek; exempt employees are exempt under the federal test (executive, administrative, professional, computer, outside-sales). No daily-overtime rule, no double-time rule, no California-style alternative workweek schedules. Source: Industrial Commission of Arizona and 29 USC § 207 (FLSA), accessed 21 May 2026.
