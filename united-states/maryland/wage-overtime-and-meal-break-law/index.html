Maryland sets a $15.00/hour minimum wage statewide under the Fair Wage Act, with Montgomery County at $17.65/hr (51+ employees) and Howard County at $16.00/hr (15+ employees, January 2026). Overtime kicks in at 40 hours per week only — Maryland has no daily overtime trigger. Most adult employees have no statutory right to a meal break. Retail employers with 50 or more employees must comply with the Healthy Retail Employee Act (Maryland Labor and Employment § 3-710): a 15-minute break for 4–6-hour shifts and a 30-minute break for shifts over 6 hours. First-offence penalties reach $300 per employee; repeat violations open the door to treble damages in circuit court.

What is the minimum wage in Maryland in 2026?

Maryland's statewide minimum wage is $15.00 per hour, effective 1st January 2024 under the Maryland Fair Wage Act. Montgomery County and Howard County both sit above the state floor in 2026.

The statewide floor hasn't changed since January 2024. Tipped employees can be paid a cash wage of $3.63/hr, provided tips bring the total to $15.00/hr. If they don't, the employer makes up the shortfall.

Does Maryland require overtime for working more than 8 hours in a day?

No. Maryland overtime triggers at 40 hours per week only under Labor and Employment § 3-415. A 9-hour day inside a 40-hour week carries no overtime obligation.

Maryland overtime — weekly only

Maryland follows the federal FLSA weekly overtime model. There is no daily overtime requirement. An employee who works 9 hours on Monday and 6 hours each other day totals 33 hours — zero overtime owed, regardless of the long Monday.

Where federal FLSA and Maryland state law conflict, the rule more favourable to the employee applies. In practice the two are identical for most hires. A misclassification that incorrectly exempts a worker under FLSA can still leave the employer exposed under the Maryland Wage Payment and Collection Law, which carries its own enforcement teeth.

Are Maryland employers required to provide meal breaks?

Maryland state law does not require meal or rest breaks for employees aged 18 and over in most industries. There is no general statutory right to a lunch break under the Maryland Guide to Wage Payment and Employment Standards.

This surprises most out-of-state employers used to California or New York standards. Maryland's Employment Standards Service confirms it explicitly: no statute mandates break periods for adult workers outside of retail. If a contract or employee handbook promises breaks, that agreement is enforceable — but the baseline is silence.

Two obligations exist regardless of industry:

  • Minors under 18: all employers must provide a 30-minute break for every 5 consecutive hours worked (Maryland Labor and Employment § 3-210). Violating this carries penalties up to $16,035 per standard violation and $72,876 per willful or repeat violation under 2026 child labour rules.
  • Federal FLSA paid-break rule: any break shorter than 20 minutes must be counted as paid work time. If your Maryland policy offers short rest breaks, those minutes go on the timesheet and into the overtime calculation.

The practical risk sits with large retail employers. If your Maryland hire is at a retail establishment with 50 or more employees, the Healthy Retail Employee Act applies and the compliance picture changes entirely.

What is the Maryland Healthy Retail Employee Act?

The Healthy Retail Employee Act (Maryland Labor and Employment § 3-710) requires retail employers with 50 or more Maryland employees to provide shift breaks: 15 minutes for a 4–6-hour shift and 30 minutes for shifts over 6 hours.

The Act covers retail establishments whose primary purpose is selling goods to consumers present at the time of sale, where more than 50% of gross sales come from in-store transactions. Multiple franchise locations with the same trade name aggregate their employee counts toward the 50-employee threshold — so a 30-employee Baltimore location and a 25-employee Rockville location of the same franchise chain are both covered.

A working break is permitted where the nature of the role prevents the employee being fully relieved — if the employee eats while working, the break counts as paid work time, and both parties agree in writing.

Several categories are exempt: FLSA-exempt workers (executive, administrative, professional, outside sales), commissioned sales staff earning more than 1.5 times the minimum wage from commissions, state and county government employees, and employees at single locations with five or fewer staff.

Penalties for non-compliance

The Commissioner of Labor and Industry can issue a compliance order on the first offence and fine the employer up to $300 per employee. A repeat violation within 3 years carries up to $600 per employee. On a second or subsequent offence the affected employee can also sue in circuit court for treble damages — three times their hourly rate per missed break — plus attorney fees. On a 50-person retail workforce, that exposure compounds fast.

Frequently asked questions: Maryland wage and break law

Does Maryland have daily overtime?

No. Maryland overtime triggers at 40 hours per week only under Labor and Employment § 3-415. There is no 8th-hour daily overtime rule. Agricultural workers reach their threshold at 60 hours per week; care facility and bowling alley employees at 48 hours. Everyone else: 40 hours weekly.

Do employers have to give meal breaks in Maryland?

Not for most adult employees. Maryland state law doesn't mandate meal or rest breaks for workers aged 18 and over outside of retail. If you offer short breaks (under 20 minutes), those minutes are paid work time under FLSA. Minors under 18 get a 30-minute break every 5 consecutive hours regardless of industry.

What is the Healthy Retail Employee Act?

Maryland Labor and Employment § 3-710 requires retail employers with 50 or more employees to provide a 15-minute break for 4–6-hour shifts and a 30-minute break for shifts over 6 hours. Penalties: $300 per employee (first offence), $600 per employee (repeat), plus treble damages in circuit court.

What is the minimum wage in Maryland in 2026?

$15.00/hr statewide. Montgomery County: $17.65/hr (51+ employees), $16.00/hr (11–50), $15.50/hr (10 or fewer) — effective July 2025, adjusted annually on 1st July. Howard County: $16.00/hr for 15+ employees from 1st January 2026.

What are the penalties for breaking Maryland's break laws?

Under the Healthy Retail Employee Act: $300 per employee (first offence), $600 per employee (repeat within 3 years), plus the right to sue in circuit court for treble damages and attorney fees. Child labour break violations carry fines up to $16,035 per standard violation.
