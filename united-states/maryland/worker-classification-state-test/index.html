Maryland's two-test framework — May 2026

What does the Maryland Workplace Fraud Act require in construction and landscaping?

Workers are presumed to be your employees. You must overcome that presumption to treat anyone as an independent contractor in construction or landscaping.

The Workplace Fraud Act, enacted 1st October 2009, applies to construction and landscaping. It creates a presumption of employment that four statutory exceptions can rebut.

The first exception is the 'exempt person': a sole proprietor with no employees other than an immediate family member. The second is the ABC test, which requires all three: the worker is free from direction and control in performing the work; the worker is customarily engaged in an independent business of the same nature; and the work is either outside the usual course of your business or performed outside your place of business.

The third and fourth exceptions cover business-to-business subcontracting arrangements at the same location, and regulated subcontracting with a written contract, an affidavit of independent contractor status, department registration in good standing, and all required occupational licences.

How does Maryland classify independent contractors outside construction and landscaping?

For all other industries, Maryland courts apply the common-law right-to-control test. The right to control (not the actual exercise of it) is the decisive factor.

Maryland's Court of Special Appeals set the controlling principle in L.M.T. Steel Products v. Pierson (47 Md. App. 633): it's the right to control and direct the work, both in terms of results and method, that determines the relationship. Not whether you actively exercise that right.

Five factors feed the analysis. First, who has the right to control how the work is done. Second, who selects and hires the worker. Third, who has the power to discharge. Fourth, whether you provide tools, materials, or a workspace. Fifth, whether the work is part of your regular business operations. None of these factors is individually decisive, but control is the closest thing to a trump card.

Maryland also applies COMAR 09.32.01.18 in regulatory contexts. For unemployment insurance specifically, Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 8-205 governs. For workers' compensation, § 9-402 is the operative provision. These are Maryland state tests, separate from the IRS's three-category framework for federal employment tax purposes.

Classification disputes in general employment go back to court precedent. There's no single published state checklist equivalent to the WFA's four exceptions. That ambiguity is why borderline cases need a qualified review, not just a form.

How does Maryland's classification test differ from the federal IRS test?

They're separate. A worker classified as an independent contractor under IRS rules can still be an employee under Maryland law, and vice versa. You need to satisfy both.

The IRS condensed its original 20-factor analysis into three categories: behavioural control (does your business direct how work gets done?), financial control (do you control the economic aspects of the job?), and type of relationship (written contracts, benefits, permanency, integration). Maryland's common-law right-to-control test uses similar logic but is applied independently by state courts and state agencies.

The Workplace Fraud Act's ABC test in construction and landscaping is stricter than the IRS standard in most cases, because it starts with a presumption of employment that you must actively rebut. Satisfying the IRS test doesn't protect you from Maryland DOL enforcement on UI contributions or workers' compensation claims.

What are the penalties for misclassifying workers in Maryland?

Up to $20,000 per misclassified worker for repeat violations under the Workplace Fraud Act. Plus treble economic damages owed to the worker.

The Workplace Fraud Act (Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 3-903 and § 3-904) sets a graduated penalty schedule. Unintentional misclassification carries up to $1,000 per worker , but only after the employer fails to remediate within a 45-day cure window following a finding.

Knowing misclassification starts at $5,000 per worker on the first offence, rises to $10,000 on the second, and reaches $20,000 per worker for employers with three or more violations. On top of those penalties, a court can award treble damages: three times the actual economic damages the worker suffered.

Outside construction and landscaping, misclassification exposure comes through back UI contributions, the employer share of FICA and FUTA, workers' compensation premium shortfalls, and wage-payment violations. Each agency runs its own enforcement mechanism. The Commissioner of Labor and Industry enforces the Workplace Fraud Act; the Maryland Division of Unemployment Insurance enforces back UI through BEACON.

What do out-of-state companies miss about Maryland worker classification?

The dual-test structure is the most common gap. Most out-of-state companies assume one test applies everywhere and run the IRS checklist alone.

Three things that catch first-time Maryland employers off guard. First, the Workplace Fraud Act's presumption of employment in construction and landscaping is stricter than the equivalent in most states. The burden sits with you to disprove employee status, not with the state to prove it.

Second, Maryland has no single codified multi-factor list for the common-law test outside those two industries. Classification disputes in general employment go back to court precedent (L.M.T. Steel and its progeny), which creates more ambiguity than a state that publishes an explicit factor checklist. That ambiguity increases the cost of a wrong call.

Third, the federal IRS test and the Maryland state tests are entirely independent enforcement tracks. Frequently asked questions about Maryland worker classification

Does Maryland use the ABC test for worker classification?

Maryland uses an ABC-style test only for construction and landscaping under the Workplace Fraud Act (Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. §§ 3-901 through 3-916). All other industries use the common-law right-to-control test. The federal IRS three-category framework applies separately for federal tax purposes.

What is the Maryland Workplace Fraud Act?

The Maryland Workplace Fraud Act, enacted 1st October 2009, prohibits misclassification of workers in the construction and landscaping industries. It creates a presumption that workers are employees, which employers must rebut through one of four statutory exceptions. Penalties range from $1,000 to $20,000 per misclassified worker plus treble damages.

What factors does Maryland use to classify workers outside construction?

Maryland courts apply the common-law right-to-control test: (1) the right to control how work is done, (2) who selects and hires the worker, (3) the power to discharge, (4) whether the employer provides tools and workspace, and (5) whether the work is part of the employer's regular business. Control is the decisive factor.

What are the penalties for worker misclassification in Maryland?

Under the Workplace Fraud Act: up to $1,000 per worker for unintentional misclassification (after a 45-day cure window), up to $5,000 for knowing misclassification on the first offence, up to $10,000 on the second offence, and up to $20,000 for employers with three or more violations. Courts can also award treble economic damages.
