Is Maryland an at-will employment state?

Yes. Maryland employers can terminate for any reason (or no reason) without advance notice, unless a contract or one of seven statutory exceptions applies.

At-will employment is the default in Maryland. You don't need to document cause, provide written notice, or follow a formal process before ending employment. That flexibility is real. It's also bounded, clearly.

Maryland's Fair Employment Practices Act (FEPA), Md. Code Ann., State Gov't § 20-601 et seq., federal civil rights statutes, and a set of common-law protections carved out by Maryland courts all put hard limits on when at-will termination is lawful. Getting that boundary wrong is expensive: a successful wrongful-termination plaintiff can recover back pay, compensatory damages, and legal fees.

What are Maryland's at-will employment exceptions?

Seven categories break at-will protection in Maryland: discrimination, workers' comp retaliation, wage-law retaliation, jury/military duty, public-policy violations, contract-based protections, and HWFA retaliation.

Maryland courts established the public-policy exception in Adler v. American Standard Corp. (291 Md. 31, 1981): if a termination violates a clear statutory or constitutional mandate, the employee has a wrongful-discharge claim regardless of at-will status.

In practice, that covers terminating someone for filing a workers' compensation claim, for asserting overtime rights under Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 3-415, for reporting for jury duty, or for refusing to commit a criminal act.

The FEPA adds a separate discrimination layer. Employers with 15 or more employees cannot terminate on the basis of race, colour, sex, age (18+, broader than federal), religion, national origin, marital status, sexual orientation, gender identity, disability, or genetic information. Harassment protections apply to all employers regardless of size.

Retaliation claims (for reporting discrimination, filing a wage complaint, or requesting FMLA leave) are the fastest-growing category of wrongful-termination filings in Maryland.

When must Maryland employers pay final wages after termination?

Final wages are due on the next scheduled pay date after separation, whether you fire the employee or they resign. No special 'termination day' deadline exists.

Maryland's final-pay rule is intentionally simple: pay on the regular pay date the employee would have received anyway. That applies whether the separation is voluntary or involuntary.

What counts as 'wages' is broader than base pay. It includes determinable commissions, overtime, and, if your written policy treats accrued PTO as earned compensation, that too. Forfeiture clauses in PTO policies are valid in Maryland, but only if you disclosed them in writing at the time of hire.

Sick leave accrued under the Healthy Working Families Act (HWFA) is not payable on separation. If an employee is rehired within 37 weeks, you must reinstate their unused HWFA balance unless you already paid it out voluntarily.

Non-payment carries a real penalty: Maryland courts can order 3× unpaid wages plus attorney fees and court costs.

Does Maryland require severance pay?

No. Maryland law does not mandate severance. It becomes an obligation only when your contract, written policy, or established practice says so.

There's no statute in Maryland that forces an employer to pay severance upon termination. The obligation arises from what you've already put in writing: an employment agreement, a separation policy, or a pattern of paying severance that created a reasonable expectation.

When severance is offered, Maryland courts treat it as wages if it's earned and determinable; it must be paid on the same timeline as final wages. Severance agreements that include a release of claims must specifically cover Maryland-law claims (FEPA, Equal Pay for Equal Work Act, HWFA) to be enforceable.

What are Maryland's mass layoff notification rules?

Maryland's Economic Stabilization Act (ESA) requires 60 days' notice for layoffs affecting 15 or more workers or 25% of the workforce at companies with 50 or more employees.

Maryland's ESA came into full effect on 13th October 2025. It's now a live obligation for any employer with 50 or more employees planning a 'reduction in operations' that affects at least 15 workers or 25% of the workforce (whichever is greater) over any 3-month window.

Notice goes to: the Maryland Department of Labor's Dislocation Services Unit, affected employees, employee representatives, and the chief local elected official. The federal WARN Act runs alongside it: companies with 100 or more employees that lay off 50 or more workers (or 33%+ of the workforce) must also give 60 days' federal notice.

A third tier applies to any employer with advance knowledge of a layoff affecting 25 or more workers. A Dislocation Event Form must reach Maryland DOL at least 48 hours before the layoff begins.

What should you know before terminating an employee in Maryland?

Maryland's employment protections are broader than the federal baseline in two ways most employers overlook. Age discrimination coverage starts at 18, not 40 as under federal ADEA. so a 25-year-old can bring an age claim if you let them go and cite their age.

Sexual orientation and gender identity have been protected since 2001, predating federal clarity by two decades. The 'constructive discharge' doctrine is active: courts look at whether a reasonable person would feel compelled to resign, not whether they technically chose to.

Frequently asked questions: Maryland termination law

Is Maryland an at-will employment state?

Yes. Maryland is an at-will employment state, meaning employers can terminate employment for any reason or no reason without advance notice, unless a statutory or contractual exception applies.

What are the exceptions to at-will employment in Maryland?

Maryland recognises seven exceptions: discrimination under FEPA (race, colour, sex, age 18+, religion, national origin, marital status, sexual orientation, gender identity, disability, genetic information), workers' comp retaliation, wage-law retaliation, jury and military duty, public-policy violations, contract-based protections, and HWFA retaliation.

When must employers pay final wages in Maryland?

Maryland requires employers to pay all wages due on or before the next scheduled pay date after separation. Accrued PTO is payable only if the employer's written policy provides for it. Non-payment exposes the employer to 3 times unpaid wages plus attorney fees.

Does Maryland require severance pay?

No. Maryland law does not mandate severance pay. An obligation to pay severance arises only if an employment contract, written company policy, or established practice creates one.

What is the Maryland Economic Stabilization Act?

The Maryland Economic Stabilization Act (ESA), effective 13th October 2025, requires employers with 50 or more employees to give 60 days' advance notice before layoffs affecting 15 or more workers or 25% of the workforce. Notice goes to Maryland DOL, affected employees, and local officials.
