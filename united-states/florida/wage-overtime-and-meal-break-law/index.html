Three practical consequences for any employer running Florida payroll in 2026:

  • The rate steps on 30 September, not 1 January. Most CPI-indexed states (Arizona, Colorado, Washington, New York) adjust on 1 January. Florida adjusts on 30 September because the 2004 Amendment 5 fixed that calendar date and Amendment 2 kept it. Payroll engines configured to expect a calendar-year minimum-wage update will lag the Florida law by a full quarter every year.
  • The 2026 step takes Florida to the top of the regional band. The 30 September 2026 step to $15.00 puts Florida above every other state in the US Southeast region (Alabama, Georgia, Mississippi, South Carolina, Tennessee all default to the federal $7.25 floor; North Carolina also at $7.25). The 2026 step lands Florida alongside the highest-minimum US jurisdictions (Washington, California, Connecticut, Massachusetts, Delaware).
  • After 2026 the rate stops being a fixed-schedule number and becomes a CPI-W result. The constitutional indexation calculates on the August-over-August change in the Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners and Clerical Workers (CPI-W) for the southern region, announced by the Florida Department of Commerce in mid-October each year, effective 30 September of the following year. Multi-state payroll teams configured for Florida need a forward-looking process that watches the Department of Commerce notice in October, not a static schedule.

The constitutional protection is unusual

Florida voters wrote the state minimum wage into Article X § 24 of the Florida Constitution through Amendment 5 in November 2004 (71 percent yes vote), at a baseline of $6.15 per hour with annual CPI-W adjustment. In November 2020, Amendment 2 layered the $10.00–$15.00 phased schedule on top, retaining the CPI-W mechanism for years after the final $15.00 step. Most US states keep the minimum wage in statute, which a future legislative session can amend by simple majority. Florida’s constitutional placement means any rollback or freeze of the minimum-wage rule requires a fresh constitutional amendment, with a 60 percent voter threshold. For multi-state payroll teams, that means the Florida wage rule is structurally more stable than most state minimums; the rate moves by mechanism, not by political cycle.

For multi-state employers, Florida moved from a low-floor state in the early 2010s to a top-band state by the end of 2026. The 30 September 2026 step lands Florida above Maryland ($15.00 effective 1 January 2024 with no further index), Illinois ($15.00 from 1 January 2025), and below only the city-overlay jurisdictions in Washington State and California. How does Florida’s tipped minimum wage and tip credit work?

Florida sets the tipped cash wage at the state minimum wage minus a constitutionally fixed $3.02 per hour tip credit. The 2026 tipped cash wage is $10.98 per hour from 30 September 2025, stepping to $11.98 per hour from 30 September 2026. The employer must guarantee that combined cash wage plus tips equals or exceeds the state minimum each pay period. Any shortfall has to be topped up by the employer in the same pay run. Florida’s $3.02 tip-credit ceiling is tighter than the federal $5.12 ceiling and looser than the seven no-tip-credit states (Alaska, California, Nevada, Oregon, Washington, Minnesota, Montana).

Three operational rules for restaurant, bar, and hospitality employers in Florida:

  • Make-up calculation runs each pay period, not weekly or monthly. A server paid the $10.98 cash wage who books only $2.00 in tips on a 40-hour week earned $12.98 per hour total. The employer must top up by $1.02 per hour for that week to reach the $14.00 state minimum. Track tip totals per shift and per pay period; do not net out high-tip and low-tip weeks.
  • Tip pools are permitted with federal limits. Florida employers can run a tip-pool arrangement that distributes pooled tips among customarily tipped employees (servers, bussers, bartenders). The 2018 federal amendment to the FLSA bars the employer or any manager or supervisor from sharing in any tip pool, regardless of whether the employer takes a tip credit. Florida defers to that federal rule.
  • Service charges are not tips. A mandatory service charge added to a bill by the employer is employer revenue, not employee property. If the employer passes some or all of a service charge to staff, that distribution counts as wages (subject to payroll tax) rather than tips and cannot count against the $3.02 tip-credit ceiling.

The constitutional placement of the $3.02 tip credit matters operationally. The federal $5.12 tip credit moves with future federal minimum-wage legislation; the Florida $3.02 credit is locked at the constitutional level and only moves if voters amend Article X § 24 by a 60 percent supermajority. For a national restaurant operator running Florida and federal-floor states side by side, the Florida tipped cash wage moves predictably alongside the state minimum (always $3.02 below), while the federal tipped wage and tip-credit ceiling can shift by act of Congress. Does Florida have a state overtime law?

No. Florida does not have a state overtime statute. Overtime in Florida is governed exclusively by the federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA): 1.5 times the regular rate for any hours worked over 40 in a workweek under 29 U.S.C. § 207(a)(1). There is no daily overtime trigger, no consecutive-shift trigger, and no 7th-day premium. Florida sits in the operationally lightest tier of US states on overtime, alongside Alabama, Georgia, and Tennessee, and lighter than Colorado (daily-12 trigger) or California (daily-8). Exempt classification follows the federal salary basis of $684 per week and the duties tests in 29 CFR Part 541.

Because Florida has no state overtime layer, the calculation is the standard FLSA workweek rule. Define a fixed 168-hour workweek (for example Sunday 00:00 to Saturday 23:59), total the non-exempt hours, and pay 1.5 times the regular rate on every hour over 40. The regular rate includes non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, and commission earned in the same week; it excludes discretionary bonuses, gifts, and reimbursement of business expenses. Florida wage-claim enforcement runs through the US Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division or as a private FLSA action; there is no state Labor Department equivalent to the Industrial Commission of Arizona or the Connecticut DOL Wage and Workplace Standards Division.

Exempt vs non-exempt classification

To be exempt from FLSA overtime in Florida, an employee must meet both a salary basis test and a duties test:

  • Salary basis: at least $684 per week ($35,568 per year), paid as a fixed salary not reduced by quality or quantity of work. Florida defers to the federal floor; no state-level salary top-up applies.
  • Duties test: primary duty must be executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales as defined in 29 CFR Part 541. Job title alone never establishes exemption.

A salaried “manager” making $48,000 per year in a Florida restaurant who spends 80 percent of the day cooking on the line alongside hourly staff fails the duties test and is owed overtime on every hour over 40 in the workweek. The 2024 federal rule increase that would have raised the salary basis to $1,128 per week was vacated by the US District Court for the Eastern District of Texas in November 2024, so the operative floor in May 2026 is $684 per week. The regular-rate calculation matters

Florida courts follow federal FLSA case law on the regular-rate calculation: all non-discretionary remuneration earned in the workweek (cash wages, non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, commission, on-call pay) divided by total hours worked. A non-discretionary attendance bonus that pushes a $20-per-hour employee’s effective rate to $21 in a given week means the overtime rate for that week is $31.50, not $30. Payroll engines that calculate overtime on the base rate only without recapture of bonus and commission allocations under-pay by 5 to 10 percent on weeks with bonus payouts. The fix is automated regular-rate recalculation at workweek close, not at base-rate setup.

Does Florida require meal or rest breaks for adult employees?

No. Florida law does not require employers to provide meal breaks or rest breaks to adult employees (age 18 and over). Federal FLSA does not mandate breaks either. If you do offer rest breaks of less than 20 minutes, federal law (29 CFR 785.18) counts them as paid hours worked. Meal periods of 30 minutes or more, where the employee is fully relieved of duty, can be unpaid. Florida is one of about 30 US states with no adult break mandate at all, sitting in the lightest tier alongside Alabama, Georgia, Texas, and Arizona.

The federal default rules govern what happens when you do choose to offer breaks:

  • Short rest breaks (5 to 20 minutes): 29 CFR 785.18 treats these as compensable working time. A 15-minute paid coffee break counts toward the 40-hour weekly overtime threshold.
  • Meal periods (30+ minutes): 29 CFR 785.19 allows these to be unpaid, provided the employee is fully relieved from duty for the purpose of eating. An employee required to stay at the counter and ring up customers during a 30-minute lunch is “engaged to wait” and the time is compensable, which would bring it back into the 40-hour weekly count.
  • Nursing mothers: the federal PUMP Act (Section 7(r) of FLSA, extended by the 2022 act) requires reasonable break time and a private non-bathroom space to express breast milk, for one year after the child’s birth. This rule applies in Florida and is non-negotiable for any FLSA-covered employer.

Most Florida employers offer a 30-minute unpaid lunch and one or two 10-15 minute paid breaks per 8-hour shift, by policy not statute. The structure has to be documented in the employee handbook and applied consistently; ad-hoc “take a break when you need one” phrasing creates wage-and-hour exposure when an employee later claims they worked through unpaid time. Minors get a different rule under FL Stat § 450.081

Florida does carry a state-level meal-break rule for workers under 18 under Florida Statute § 450.081(4): a 30-minute uninterrupted meal break for any minor who works more than 4 consecutive hours. The rule sits in the broader child-labor framework of Chapter 450, which also caps hours and restricts hazardous occupations under FL Stat § 450.081(1) and (2). Multi-state employers running shifts that include 14- to 17-year-old workers (common in food-service, retail, lifeguard, and amusement-park roles in Florida) need the minor break configured at the schedule level, not relying on adult-pattern defaults.

How often must Florida employers pay wages?

Florida has no state statute mandating pay frequency for private-sector employers. Federal FLSA recordkeeping rules apply, but federal law does not dictate cadence either. Florida employers set the schedule (weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly, monthly) by employment contract or policy, then must pay each employee for time worked on the schedule they established. Bi-weekly is the operational default for most Florida employers because it aligns with federal Form 941 deposit cadence and the Florida Department of Revenue Reemployment Tax quarterly cycle. Once a payday is established, federal FLSA case law treats a sustained late or missed payday as a wage-payment violation actionable in federal court.

Florida is one of about 15 US states with no state-level pay-frequency mandate. Most US states require at least semi-monthly or weekly (Connecticut, Massachusetts, New York), or set a 16-day maximum gap between paydays (Arizona). The absence of a Florida statute means the employer has full discretion to set the cycle, but also that an arbitrary mid-stream cadence change carries breach-of-contract exposure if the employment agreement names a specific pay schedule.

Four operational rules for Florida payroll cadence:

  • Bi-weekly is the operational default. Most Florida employers run bi-weekly (every 14 days, 26 pay periods per year) rather than weekly or monthly, because it aligns cleanly with the federal Form 941 deposit cadence and the Florida Department of Revenue RT-6 quarterly Reemployment Tax cycle. Bi-weekly is fully compliant with both Florida and federal law without any DOR filing or notice.
  • Semi-monthly and monthly are also permitted. An employer may legitimately run a semi-monthly cycle (1-15 / 16-end-of-month) or a monthly cycle without any state filing. This is a meaningful operational simplification for executive payrolls, professional services partnerships, and any Florida business where bi-weekly creates payroll-cycle complexity.
  • Direct deposit is permitted with employee consent. Florida defers to federal labor-law guidance on direct-deposit consent. Most Florida employers collect written direct-deposit authorisation on the day-one onboarding form. An employer cannot mandate direct deposit as a condition of hire under federal law, and cannot force the employee onto a specific bank.
  • The pay day should be in the offer letter. Without a state-statute requirement for a posted pay-day notice, the offer letter or employee handbook is the operative documentation. A Florida offer letter that names the pay cadence (for example “bi-weekly, paid by direct deposit on alternate Fridays”) sets the contractual baseline against which any future late or missed pay is judged. Multi-state offer-letter templates that name only national policy without a Florida-specific cadence clause create ambiguity at the first wage-claim test.

For multi-state employers, the operational pattern that works in Florida is the same as most states: run Florida headcount on bi-weekly from day one, name the cadence in the offer letter, collect written direct-deposit consent on the onboarding form, and document any cadence change in writing before it takes effect. Florida sits alongside Alabama, Georgia, and South Carolina in the no-state-cadence-mandate group; the same handbook clause that satisfies one of those states satisfies all four. When is final pay due in Florida?

Florida has no state statute setting an accelerated final-pay deadline. Final wages on separation (voluntary quit, involuntary discharge, layoff) are due on the next regular payday under federal FLSA and common-law contract principles. There are no Florida waiting-time penalties (no California 30-day-wages clock, no Arizona 7-working-day rule, no Connecticut next-business-day rule, no Massachusetts day-of-discharge rule). Accrued unused vacation is owed at separation only if the employer’s policy, handbook, or employment contract treats vacation as earned wages. A late final paycheck still creates federal FLSA wage-claim exposure and breach-of-contract exposure under Florida common law, but the timeline is “next regular payday,” not an accelerated state-statutory clock.

The absence of an accelerated Florida final-pay statute creates two distinct operational realities. For employers, it removes the sharp-edged risk that runs in states like Arizona (7-working-day clock) or Delaware (10-percent-per-business-day damages). For employees, it means the practical remedy on a delayed final paycheque is a wage claim under federal FLSA or a Section 448.110 action on any unpaid constitutional minimum, both of which take months to resolve through the federal court system rather than an immediate state DOL administrative process.

Three operational rules for Florida final pay

  • Pay on the next regular payday. The standard “final pay on next regular payday” default works in Florida without exception. There is no off-cycle wire requirement equivalent to Arizona or Delaware. A bi-weekly Florida employee terminated on a Tuesday is paid all earned wages on the next regular Friday payday in the normal cycle.
  • Document accrued-vacation policy explicitly. Florida doesn’t require employers to treat vacation as earned wages, but if the handbook, offer letter, or employment contract says vacation is earned, then it is, and it is owed at separation alongside earned wages. The fix is a Florida-specific handbook clause that names the policy choice (vacation is a benefit not earned wages, or vacation is earned wages payable at separation) and is consistent with practice across all Florida separations.
  • Issue a final pay statement showing earned wages, hours, and deductions. Florida has no statutory pay-statement requirement equivalent to Delaware’s 19 Del. C. § 1108, but federal FLSA recordkeeping and a clean audit trail on the final pay run protect against later breach-of-contract claims. The same payroll engine that produces a bi-weekly statement should produce the final-pay statement on the same template.

For multi-state employers, the most common failure pattern in Florida is the opposite of the Arizona or Delaware pattern. Where those states catch employers who default to next-payday final pay (out-of-cycle wire required), Florida catches employers who pull a Florida employee onto a national policy that pays final wages within 24 hours (a state-California or state-Massachusetts pattern) and then forgets to roll back to the standard payroll-cycle process. The Florida rule is simply “next regular payday,” and that is the documented expectation in any compliant Florida offer letter or handbook. How is the Florida constitutional minimum wage enforced under Section 448.110, F.S.?

An employee who claims an unpaid constitutional minimum-wage shortfall must first send the employer a written notice identifying the dates and amount of the alleged underpayment under Section 448.110(6)(a), F.S.. The employer has 15 calendar days to pay the unpaid wages in full. If the employer fails to cure within the 15-day window, the employee may bring a civil action for the unpaid wages, an equal amount in liquidated damages, and reasonable attorney’s fees and costs. The statute of limitations is 4 years for general wage claims and 5 years for wilful violations. The action runs in state or federal court; Florida has no equivalent of a state Department of Labor wage-claim administrative track.

The 15-day cure window is the operationally distinctive part of the Florida wage-enforcement track. Most US states either let an employee file directly with the state DOL (Arizona, Connecticut, Delaware, California) or require a pre-suit notice with a much shorter window (10 days in some jurisdictions). The Florida 15-day cure window is a meaningful opportunity for an employer to correct a documented underpayment before litigation exposure attaches, but it requires a process that escalates the notice to payroll within 24-48 hours of receipt so the cure pay run lands within the statutory window.

Four practical rules for Florida wage-claim handling:

  • Route Section 448.110 notices to payroll immediately. A 15-day cure window starts the day the notice is received. A Florida employer that routes wage-claim correspondence through a slow legal-review process risks missing the cure deadline and converting a $500 underpayment into a $1,000 liquidated-damages liability plus attorney’s fees.
  • Pay the disputed amount in full. A partial cure (paying half of the disputed amount) does not stop the liquidated-damages clock. If the underpayment claim is wrong, the employer should document the position in writing and decline to pay; if the claim is right or even arguably right, paying the full amount within 15 days is the cleanest defensive move.
  • Liquidated damages run on top of the wages. The statute provides for the unpaid wages plus an equal amount as liquidated damages. A $1,200 underpayment over a 6-month period becomes a $2,400 liability after the cure window closes, before attorney’s fees. The fees can dwarf the underpayment itself in a successful claim, which is why most disputed Florida wage matters resolve through the cure window rather than litigation.
  • The 4- or 5-year limitations period is longer than federal FLSA. Florida’s 4-year general statute of limitations (5 years for wilful violations) under FL Stat § 95.11(2)(d) is longer than the federal FLSA 2-year window (3 years for wilful). A multi-state employer that records-retains for the federal 2-year FLSA period only is exposed on Florida constitutional minimum-wage claims that look back 4 or 5 years. Florida wage records should be retained for at least 5 years.

The constitutional placement of the wage right matters for the enforcement track too. A claim under Section 448.110 is functionally a constitutional-rights enforcement claim, not a statutory wage-payment claim. Florida courts have read the cure-window strictly: missing the 15-day window is enough to convert the underpayment into the full damages package, even if the employer’s payroll-system error was inadvertent. The defensive posture is procedural: receive notice, immediately calculate the alleged underpayment, pay in full within 15 days if right or arguably right, document the position in writing if wrong. Set Florida-specific retention on payroll, time records, and offer letters to 5 years minimum.
