Does Florida require paid family or sick leave in 2026?

No. Florida has no state paid family leave law, no state paid sick leave law, and Florida Statute § 218.077 preempts cities and counties from creating their own. Federal FMLA gives 12 weeks unpaid at 50+ employees. Everything else is the policy you write.

Florida in 2026 has no state paid family leave law, no state paid sick leave law, and Florida Statute § 218.077 expressly preempts cities and counties from mandating either. The only mandatory leave floor is federal FMLA (29 U.S.C. § 2601), which provides 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period for employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. An employee qualifies after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked. Below the 50-employee threshold, federal FMLA does not apply and Florida has no equivalent state floor. The preemption statute is distinctive: most large states allow localities to add leave on top of state law, while Florida blocks the local route entirely. SB 220 (2026 Session) proposes paid parental leave for state-government employees only; nothing for the private sector. Everything else (paid parental, paid sick, paid bereavement, paid PTO) is voluntary, designed by the employer. Federal Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA, June 2023), USERRA for military duty, Florida Statute § 250.482 for state-active-duty leave, and Florida Statute § 40.271 jury duty job protection apply on top regardless of headcount.

Does Florida require paid family leave?

No. Florida has no state paid family leave law. Nine US states plus DC do require it (California, Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Washington, and Washington DC); Florida is not one of them. There is no Florida payroll deduction for PFL, no state PFL programme to fund, and no state PFL claim portal for employees.

One Florida-specific point worth noting. SB 220 (2026 Session) proposes paid parental leave for state-government employees only (Florida Department of Management Services would administer six weeks of paid leave for state agency staff). The bill, as filed, does not extend to private employers and would change nothing for a private-sector Florida hire. As of May 2026 the legislation has not been enacted; even if it passes in the current session, the private-sector employer obligation remains zero.

For a private Florida employer, hiring in Florida removes the state-level PFL withholding line that adds 0.5 to 1.0 percent of wages in California, New York, or Washington. There is no equivalent payroll deduction, no state PFL bond schedule, and no state agency claim portal to maintain. The cost line simply does not exist on a Florida payslip, and unlike most large states there is no city or county PFL to track either (see the preemption section below).

What this means for talent: a Florida employee comparing job offers against friends in California or New York will not have access to a state PFL programme to fall back on for the birth of a child or the care of a parent. The employer’s voluntary parental-leave policy carries the full weight of that retention conversation, and the gap between what your offer letter says and what a California or Bay Area employer offers is fully visible to the candidate.

Does Florida require paid sick leave?

No. Florida has no state paid sick leave law and no city or county may create one. Eighteen US states and dozens of cities require accrued paid sick time (California, Colorado, Connecticut, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Washington, plus DC); Florida is not one of them, and Florida Statute § 218.077 preempts municipalities and counties from filling the gap. Sick days at a Florida job are whatever the offer letter says they are.

The preemption rule is distinctive. Florida Statute § 218.077 (enacted 2013, amended 2017) expressly prohibits political subdivisions of the state from enacting employment benefits ordinances that require an employer to provide wages, benefits, or leave above those required by state or federal law. Miami-Dade County passed an Earned Sick Time ordinance in 2013 that was effectively neutralised by the statute; Orlando’s 2012 paid-sick-leave petition campaign was halted before reaching a vote for the same reason. No Florida city or county has an operative paid sick leave ordinance as of May 2026, and the preemption forecloses the possibility.

This makes the offer-letter sick-day line load-bearing for retention, and it makes Florida administratively simpler than California or Washington (where a multi-city patchwork of local ordinances adds tracking overhead). SHRM’s 2025 benefits survey shows the median Florida private-sector employer offers 5 paid sick days per year, accrued at roughly one hour per 30 hours worked. Competitive employers in knowledge-work sectors (tech in Miami and Tampa, professional services in Jacksonville, healthcare administration in Orlando) typically go to 7 to 10 sick days, often consolidated into a single PTO bank with vacation rather than tracked separately.

One nuance: although there is no Florida state PSL, a Florida employee may still qualify for unpaid leave under federal FMLA if the absence is for a serious health condition (defined as inpatient care or continuing treatment by a healthcare provider, lasting more than three consecutive days) and the employer is FMLA-covered. A bad case of flu generally does not qualify; pneumonia hospitalisation typically does. Job protection during a serious-health-condition absence is the point, not the pay.

Why can’t Florida cities pass their own paid leave laws?

Florida Statute § 218.077 preempts political subdivisions of the state from mandating employment benefits, wages, or leave above state or federal floors. The statute was enacted in 2013 (House Bill 655) and tightened in 2017. It is the reason Miami-Dade’s 2013 Earned Sick Time ordinance never took practical effect and Orlando’s 2012 paid-sick-leave petition was blocked. Florida sits with Alabama, Tennessee, and a handful of other states with strong leave preemption; most large states (California, New York, Illinois) allow cities to layer on top.

01 F.S. § 218.077 · Leave preemption

Florida Statute § 218.077 expressly forecloses local employment-benefits ordinances. A city cannot require an employer within its borders to provide paid sick leave, paid parental leave, or any other leave benefit above what state or federal law already requires. This is the single most distinctive feature of Florida leave law and the reason the no-state-statute frame is uniquely simple to administer here.

For an employer with workers across multiple Florida cities, there is no city-by-city tracking. A Tampa employee, a Miami employee, an Orlando employee, and a Jacksonville employee all sit under the same Florida and federal rules with no local overlay. Compare that with California, where San Francisco, Oakland, Berkeley, Emeryville, Los Angeles, San Diego, and Santa Monica all run different paid sick leave ordinances, each with its own accrual rates, caps, carry-over rules, and notice-poster requirements. Florida’s preemption removes that overhead entirely.

The flip side: a Florida employer who wants to differentiate cannot rely on a city ordinance to set a floor for everyone. The voluntary policy you write is the entire policy your workforce gets, in every Florida county. There is no patchwork that nudges the small employer up toward the larger employer’s benchmark, the way a San Francisco ordinance forces a 10-person tech startup to match what Salesforce offers on accrued sick time. In Florida, the small employer is competing against the larger employer entirely on the offer letter.

What Miami-Dade and Orlando tried

Miami-Dade County passed an Earned Sick Time ordinance in October 2013, six months after the state preemption statute took effect. The ordinance was effectively neutralised by § 218.077; the County repealed it in 2017. Orlando qualified a paid-sick-leave petition for the November 2012 ballot, then suspended the vote after the Florida Legislature signalled the preemption bill that became § 218.077. Several other Florida cities considered ordinances in the 2012-2014 window; none survived. Source: Florida Statute § 218.077; Miami-Dade County Ordinance 13-94 (repealed); Orlando City Council records, accessed 26 May 2026.

What does federal FMLA give Florida employees?

Federal FMLA gives qualifying Florida employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period, with group health coverage continued at the employer’s normal contribution. It applies only to employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. The employee qualifies after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked in the preceding 12 months.

02 29 U.S.C. § 2601 · FMLA

The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 is the federal floor for unpaid, job-protected leave. It overrides nothing in Florida because Florida has no competing state floor for private employers. The 50-employee threshold counts every US employee, not just Florida ones, which catches multi-state employers off guard.

Five qualifying reasons trigger FMLA leave:

  • Birth and care of a newborn, within 12 months of birth
  • Placement of a child with the employee for adoption or for a placement under state child-placement law, within 12 months of placement
  • Care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition
  • The employee’s own serious health condition that makes them unable to perform the job
  • Qualifying military exigency arising from a family member’s active duty in the Armed Forces; up to 26 weeks for military caregiver leave

The 50-employee threshold counts your whole US workforce

An employer with 30 Florida employees and 25 Texas employees crosses the 50-employee FMLA threshold even though neither state alone gets there. The Department of Labor counts every employee on the US payroll for any 20 or more weeks of the current or preceding year. The 75-mile-radius rule then determines which specific worksites are FMLA-covered, which matters for fully remote employers whose employees are geographically dispersed across the southeastern states.

What FMLA does not require

  • Pay during leave. FMLA leave is unpaid by statute. An employee may substitute accrued PTO; the employer may require it. There is no FMLA wage-replacement benefit.
  • FMLA leave for employers under 50. A 25-employee Florida company has no FMLA obligation and Florida has no mini-FMLA. Below the threshold, parental leave and serious-illness leave are entirely voluntary.
  • FMLA leave for the first 12 months. A 3-month tenure Florida employee with a new baby has no FMLA right to job-protected leave; the employer may grant unpaid leave voluntarily and may terminate at any time, subject only to the federal anti-discrimination floor and Florida’s at-will doctrine.

Source: US Department of Labor, FMLA Final Rule (29 CFR Part 825), accessed 26 May 2026.

What protections apply for pregnancy and disability in Florida?

Federal law applies; Florida adds the Florida Civil Rights Act of 1992 (mirrors federal anti-discrimination at 15+ employees) and recognises pregnancy as a protected class under the FCRA (2014 Florida Supreme Court decision in Delva v. Continental Group). The federal Pregnancy Discrimination Act (PDA, 1978) treats pregnancy like any other temporary disability for employer leave and benefits. The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA), effective 27 June 2023, requires reasonable accommodation for pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions, applying to employers with 15 or more employees. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) covers pregnancy-related disabilities such as gestational diabetes or severe preeclampsia.

The practical reading for a Florida employer at 15 to 50 employees: PWFA covers reasonable accommodation (modified duties, schedule changes, time off for medical appointments, lactation breaks), but FMLA does not yet cover the 12 weeks of job-protected leave. The accommodation conversation under PWFA may include voluntary unpaid leave as the accommodation, but the employer has no statutory obligation to hold the job open beyond the accommodation period.

At 50+ employees the package stacks: PWFA accommodation during the pregnancy, FMLA for the birth and bonding, ADA for any post-delivery serious complications, plus the Florida Civil Rights Act layered on the federal anti-discrimination floor. The four laws together resemble what a single state PFL programme covers in California or New York, except every line is unpaid unless the employer voluntarily layers pay on top.

What this means for offer letters

The most retention-critical voluntary line on a Florida offer letter is paid parental leave. The federal floor (12 weeks unpaid at FMLA-covered employers, accommodation under PWFA at 15+) leaves a wide gap that voluntary policy fills. Mid-market Florida employers commonly offer 6 to 8 weeks of paid maternity leave and 2 to 4 weeks of paid paternity or partner leave; top-quartile knowledge-work employers in Miami, Tampa, Orlando, and Jacksonville offer 12 to 16 weeks paid for the primary caregiver regardless of gender. Source: SHRM 2025 Employee Benefits Survey, US EEOC PWFA guidance, Florida Civil Rights Act of 1992, accessed 26 May 2026.

What about military leave and jury duty in Florida?

Federal USERRA protects civilian jobs for service members. Florida Statute § 250.482 grants Florida private-sector employees called by the Governor to state active duty the same job-protected leave plus a 30-day reinstatement window. Jury duty is mandatory; Florida Statute § 40.271 prohibits discharge or threat for jury service, and § 40.24(3) requires employers with 10 or more full-time employees to pay regular wages for the first three days of jury service when the juror is a regular full-time employee not paid by the court for those days.

USERRA and Florida military leave

The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA, 38 U.S.C. § 4301 et seq.) provides civilian reemployment rights for service members called to active duty, with a cumulative cap of five years of military absence. The employer pays nothing during the leave itself, but health-plan coverage must continue (employee may pay up to 102 percent of the full premium for COBRA-style continuation). On return, the employee is reinstated to the position they would have held had they not left, the so-called escalator principle.

Florida private-sector employees pick up matching protections under Florida Statute § 250.482 when the Governor calls members of the Florida National Guard to state active duty. The statute grants job-protected leave for the duration of state-active service and a reinstatement window after release. State and federal protections stack; the employee gets whichever is more favourable for the specific call-up. Public-sector employees fall under more generous separate regimes: state and county employees may receive paid military leave under chapter-specific personnel rules.

Jury duty in Florida

Florida Statute § 40.271 prohibits an employer from discharging, threatening, or coercing an employee because of jury service. The statute makes the prohibition a civil violation with reinstatement and back-pay remedies. Florida Statute § 40.24(3) goes further for employers with 10 or more full-time employees: the employer must pay regular wages for the first three days of jury service to a regular full-time employee who is not paid by the court for those days. Beyond the third day, the court’s per-diem payment is the only compensation unless the employer voluntarily continues pay.

The watch-out: the § 40.24(3) pay obligation triggers at 10 full-time employees, not at FMLA’s 50, so a small Florida employer that crosses the 10-employee threshold picks up the jury-pay obligation well before FMLA enters the picture. Most Florida knowledge-work employers voluntarily continue full pay during jury duty as a retention signal regardless of headcount, treating it as a low-cost benefit because jury service is infrequent and the alternative leaves the employee mildly penalised for civic participation.

Source: Florida Statutes, Sections 250.482, 40.271, and 40.24(3); USERRA at 38 U.S.C. § 4301 et seq.; Florida Senate primary text; accessed 26 May 2026.

What about employers with fewer than 50 employees in Florida?

Below the federal FMLA threshold, a Florida employer has very limited mandatory leave law to comply with, with four narrow exceptions: PWFA accommodation and Florida Civil Rights Act anti-discrimination at 15+ employees, USERRA reemployment for service members (plus the § 250.482 state-activation mirror), § 40.271 jury duty job protection at all sizes, and § 40.24(3) jury duty pay at 10+ full-time employees. Everything else is voluntary, including paid parental leave, paid sick leave, paid bereavement, and unpaid time off for a child’s school visit.

The good news is the absence of compliance friction: no state PFL claim portal, no accrued-PSL ledger to maintain, no city or county leave ordinance overlay (preemption blocks them), no termination clock to manage during a protected leave at single-digit headcount. The bad news is the absence of employer cover: the small Florida employer is exposed to the full retention cost of a competitor offering a meaningful voluntary leave package.

The pattern we see at Teamed for clients hiring their first three to ten people in Florida is to write a short, clear voluntary leave policy that punches above the median: 8 weeks paid parental leave, 8 paid sick days per year, 3 paid bereavement days, full pay during jury duty (regardless of the 10-employee threshold). The total cost is roughly 1.5 to 2.5 percent of payroll spread across the workforce, and it makes the difference between a Miami, Tampa, or Orlando candidate accepting your offer and accepting an offer from a California-headquartered competitor with state PFL benefits stacked on top.

The headcount tipping points

A Florida employer crosses leave-relevant thresholds at three points. At 10 full-time employees, § 40.24(3) jury-duty pay turns on. At 15 employees, the federal PWFA accommodation duty and the Florida Civil Rights Act anti-discrimination floor turn on. At 50 employees, the federal FMLA 12-weeks-unpaid duty turns on; that trigger uses the 20-or-more-weeks test in either the current or preceding calendar year, and once tripped the FMLA obligation continues through the rest of the current and full following calendar years even if headcount drops back below 50. The lookback rule catches employers who hire seasonally or scale through funding rounds; the FMLA obligation often outlasts the headcount that triggered it.
