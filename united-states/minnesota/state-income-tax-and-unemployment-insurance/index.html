How does Minnesota state income tax work for employers?

Minnesota has four income tax brackets running from 5.35% to 9.85%. You withhold at the employee's marginal rate using the 2026 tables from the Minnesota Department of Revenue.

The entry rate of 5.35% applies to the first $33,310 of income for single filers in 2026. The top marginal rate of 9.85% kicks in above $203,151 for singles and $337,931 for married filers, placing Minnesota among the ten highest top-rate states in the country. Brackets are recalculated each year against the US Chained CPI, rounded to the nearest $10.

Minnesota has no city or county income tax. You manage one withholding account, not multiple local jurisdictions. What is Minnesota's Paid Family and Medical Leave law?

Minnesota PFML went live on 1 January 2026. The total premium is 0.88% of wages, split 50/50 between employer and employee by default, against a $185,000 wage base.

Employees can take up to 12 weeks of family leave and up to 12 weeks of medical leave in a benefit year, with a hard cap of 20 weeks combined. The maximum weekly benefit equals the state average weekly wage: $1,423 in 2026.

What sick leave must Minnesota employers provide?

Minnesota's Earned Sick and Safe Time (ESST) law requires all employers to provide paid sick leave. Employees accrue one hour for every 30 hours worked, up to 48 hours per year.

ESST has been in force since 1 January 2024 and applies to every Minnesota employer with no size threshold. Accrued hours carry over year-to-year until the employee's balance reaches 80 hours.

From 1 January 2026, you may advance ESST hours to employees at the start of the year based on estimated accrual (useful for streamlining administration). If your advance falls short of actual accrual, you must true it up during the year.

ESST covers the employee's own illness, a family member's care needs, preventive care appointments, and safety-related absences (domestic abuse, stalking, sexual assault). Minneapolis and other cities had pre-existing sick time ordinances; ESST sets the state floor but local ordinances may be more generous. Source: Minnesota DLI, Earned Sick and Safe Time.

How does Minnesota define independent contractors?

Minnesota uses separate statutory tests for construction workers (14 factors under Minn. Stat. § 181.723) and general workers (§ 181.722). Misclassification carries penalties of up to $10,000 per worker.

For non-construction workers, Minn. Stat. § 181.722, subd. 3 governs the general misclassification test. The Minnesota Department of Revenue runs a separate test for income tax withholding purposes at revenue.state.mn.us/classifying-worker: two agencies, two tests, one worker.

For construction, § 181.723, subd. 4 (effective 1 March 2025) expanded the test from nine to 14 factors. The worker must operate as a registered business entity, own or lease their own equipment, invoice under a business name, and serve multiple clients. All 14 factors must be met. Source: MN DLI Misclassification FAQs.

Penalties: up to $10,000 per violation, $10,000 per misclassified employee, plus back wages, overtime, benefits, and health insurance contributions. The Minnesota Attorney General actively prosecutes construction-sector misclassification.

What else should you know before hiring in Minnesota?

Minnesota's 9.85% top marginal rate is among the ten highest in the US. PFML went live in January 2026 and most payroll guides don't yet reflect it. The construction IC test grew to 14 factors in March 2025.

Three things that catch first-time Minnesota employers off guard. First, Minnesota's top rate of 9.85% affects any employee earning above $203,151 (single) or $337,931 (married). For competitive tech or finance roles, this matters for net take-home modelling and offer-stage conversations.

Second, PFML is live as of 1 January 2026 and the first quarterly premium payment was due 30 April 2026. If you hired a Minnesota employee in Q1 2026 without PFML in your payroll setup, you're behind. Get that corrected before the Q2 filing (due 31 July 2026).

Third, Minnesota has no state-level disability insurance (SDI) beyond the new PFML programme. Unlike California, New York, or New Jersey, your total statutory burden is more predictable as a result.

What are Minnesota state income tax withholding rates for 2026?

Minnesota has four brackets: 5.35% on the first $33,310, 6.80% up to $109,430, 7.85% up to $203,150, and 9.85% above $203,151 for single filers. Supplemental wages are withheld at a flat 6.25%. Source: Minnesota Department of Revenue.

What is the Minnesota unemployment insurance taxable wage base in 2026?

The Minnesota UI taxable wage base is $44,000 per employee in 2026. New employers in most non-construction industries pay a 1.00% rate. Experience-rated employers range from 0.00% to 8.90%. UI is an employer-only tax. Source: UIMN (uimn.org).

Does Minnesota have paid family and medical leave in 2026?

What sick leave is required for Minnesota employees?

Minnesota Earned Sick and Safe Time (ESST) requires all employers to provide one hour of paid sick leave per 30 hours worked, up to 48 hours per year with an 80-hour accrual cap. ESST has applied to all Minnesota employers since 1 January 2024.
