How does Minnesota’s worker classification state test work in 2026?

Two separate state tests. A 5-factor general test under § 181.722. A 14-factor construction test under § 181.723, expanded on 1 March 2025. Civil penalties up to $10,000 per worker. And a Minnesota Misclassification Act enforcement track that runs independently of the IRS.

Stat. § 181.722, which applies a 5-factor test, control, integration into usual business, customary independent occupation, profit-or-loss risk, and investment in tools or premises. Both tests run under the Minnesota Misclassification Act, enforced by the Minnesota Department of Labor and Industry (DLI), independently of any federal IRS analysis. Civil penalties reach up to $10,000 per misclassified worker plus back wages, back SUI premiums, and back PFML contributions. Which worker classification test does Minnesota use?

Minnesota uses two state-specific statutory tests, not the federal IRS 20-factor common-law analysis. The test you apply depends on the industry the work falls into.

For general (non-construction) workers, Minn. Stat. § 181.722 sets a 5-factor test. For construction workers, § 181.723 applies a stricter 14-factor test with a rebuttable employee presumption. Both sit under the Minnesota Misclassification Act and are enforced by the Minnesota DLI.

Marcus is a software developer in Minneapolis, working remotely for a Chicago-based fintech on a 12-month contract. He owns his own equipment, sets his own hours, has two other active clients, and operates through his LLC. The § 181.722 5-factor reading comes out contractor. The federal IRS analysis independently agrees. Both are needed; a pass on one does not satisfy the other.

Minnesota is a multi-test state because the DLI has specific legislative authority to run its own classification determinations. A federal IRS ruling that treats a worker as a contractor does not bind Minnesota DLI. And a Minnesota DLI finding of employment does not automatically trigger IRS reclassification, though it frequently leads to one in practice through inter-agency data sharing.

The practical risk is highest for construction, where the presumption reversal means any hiring contractor has to prove all 14 factors affirmatively before the first day of work. For general workers, the 5-factor test is more predictable, but the penalty exposure, up to $10,000 per worker, makes the analysis worth doing carefully.

In practice, the DLI test is the harder one for general workers; construction is harder still.

The 5-factor general test under § 181.722

For non-construction workers, Minnesota applies the 5-factor test from Minn. Stat. § 181.722, subd. 3. A worker qualifies as an independent contractor only if all five factors are satisfied.

The five factors are: (1) the worker maintains a separate business with the worker’s own office, equipment, materials, and other facilities; (2) the worker holds or has applied for a federal employer identification number or has filed business or self-employment income tax returns with the US Internal Revenue Service based on that work or services; (3) the worker operates under contracts to perform specific services for a specific amount of money and controls the means of performing the services; (4) the worker incurs the main expenses related to the service that the worker provides under contract; and (5) the worker is responsible for satisfactory completion of the services the worker contracts to provide and is liable for a failure to satisfactorily complete the work.

Marcus holds an EIN, has his own workstation at home, operates under a fixed-fee contract, pays his own software subscriptions and hardware costs, and carries professional-liability insurance against delivery failures. He passes all five. Change one variable: he works exclusively on the fintech’s infrastructure stack, uses their cloud credentials, bills hourly with no fixed scope, and has no other active clients. Now multiple factors fail simultaneously.

All five factors must be met to qualify as a contractor under § 181.722. Minnesota courts have found that one or two missing factors is enough to reach employee status. The test is conjunctive, not a balancing exercise.

Three patterns produce most misclassification exposure under the general test:

  • Long-tenure single-client arrangements. A contractor who has worked exclusively for one company for 18 months, billing hourly, using their laptop, lacks the “separate business” character factor 1 asks for.
  • Scope-creep from project to retainer. An engagement that starts as a fixed deliverable and drifts into an open-ended monthly retainer loses the fixed-scope element in factor 3.
  • Client bears all tools and expenses. When the client’s AWS account, Slack licence, and MacBook are the sole working environment, factor 4 collapses.

Construction uses a 14-factor test with a presumption of employment

Construction work is governed by Minn. Stat. § 181.723, subd. 4. Effective 1 March 2025, the test expanded from its prior version to 14 enumerated factors. The default is employee: a construction worker is presumed to be an employee unless the hiring contractor proves all 14 factors are met.

The 14 factors require, among other things, that the worker holds an active state contractor licence or journeyperson card (where required), carries general liability insurance in the worker’s own name, maintains a principal place of business outside the hiring contractor’s premises, provides their own tools and equipment, and has multiple simultaneous clients.

Carlos is a roofing subcontractor working for a Minneapolis GC. He is licensed through the GC’s blanket policy, works exclusively on the GC’s projects, uses the GC’s nail guns and scaffolding, and operates out of the GC’s yard. Three or more of the 14 factors fail immediately. Under § 181.723, Carlos is an employee from the first day. Using a 1099 exposes the GC to up to $10,000 per worker in civil penalties plus back wages, back SUI, and back workers’ comp premium.

The March 2025 expansion: what changed

Before 1 March 2025, the Minnesota construction test ran on fewer factors and lacked explicit requirements around advertising, business records, and the specifics of insurance coverage. The 2025 expansion added those elements and tightened the presumption language, making it harder for single-client construction workers to cross the contractor threshold. Penalty exposure also increased: the maximum civil penalty per worker increased to $10,000 as part of the same legislative package.

Construction employers who classified crews before March 2025 should re-run the analysis under the expanded 14-factor test. A contractor classification that was defensible in 2024 may not survive the 2025 factors.

The Minnesota Misclassification Act: a separate civil enforcement track

The Minnesota Misclassification Act creates a dedicated civil enforcement mechanism that runs independently of the federal IRS analysis. A Minnesota DLI investigator can open a Misclassification Act determination on any worker without any federal proceeding pending or concluded.

When DLI finds a worker misclassified under the Misclassification Act, the civil exposure includes: up to $10,000 per misclassified worker in civil penalties (as expanded effective 1 March 2025), back wages for any period of misclassification, the employee benefits the worker would have received (including Earned Sick and Safe Time and PFML contributions), and back SUI premiums on DEED’s account. DLI coordinates with DEED and the Department of Revenue for back-premium and back-withholding recovery.

The Act also gives workers a private right of action. A misclassified worker can file a complaint with DLI or sue directly. Attorney’s fees are available on a successful claim.

The Misclassification Act enforcement track is entirely separate from any IRS audit. A Section 530 reasonable-basis defence may limit federal payroll-tax exposure but does not affect the Minnesota DLI civil penalty or back-wage liability. Employers who relied on a prior IRS treatment or industry-practice defence cannot carry that defence into a Minnesota DLI proceeding.

Key structural point
A federal IRS Section 530 safe harbour does not protect against Minnesota DLI civil penalties or Misclassification Act back-wage claims.
Both analyses run on separate tracks, enforced by separate agencies, on separate legal standards.

Minnesota sits between the ABC states and the pure common-law states

Minnesota is a mixed-test state. For general workers, the 5-factor § 181.722 test is less aggressive than California’s ABC test but more prescriptive than a pure IRS common-law reading. For construction, the 14-factor presumption-of-employment test is among the strictest construction-specific tests in the country.

California, Massachusetts, and Delaware run strict ABC tests across most industries. Texas, Georgia, and Idaho run pure common-law. Minnesota sits in the middle for general workers and shifts firmly toward the ABC presumption end for construction.

Priya is a UX researcher on a single-client engagement with a Minneapolis healthcare company. She works 30 hours a week on their design system, uses her own laptop, bills a monthly retainer, and has no other active clients. Under California’s ABC test, prong B (work outside the usual course of business) fails immediately. Under Minnesota § 181.722, the same facts reach employee status more slowly, through factor 3 (fixed scope versus open-ended retainer) and factor 1 (separate business character).   • The March 2025 construction expansion brought Minnesota’s construction test in line with the strictest in the country, with penalties that now match or exceed those in major ABC states.

What does Minnesota misclassification actually cost?

Stacked liability across five categories, running independently of any federal exposure.

A misclassified $90,000-a-year Minnesota worker, reclassified after two years, generates $15,000 to $35,000 per worker in aggregate state exposure before any federal piece is added. The components: back Minnesota income tax withholding at the applicable bracket rate (up to 9.85%); back SUI premiums on the $44,000 wage base; back ESST accrual; back PFML contributions at 0.88% of wages; and DLI civil penalties of up to $10,000.

Construction misclassification is worse. The 14-factor presumption means the burden of proof runs against the employer from the first day, and a crew of 10 misclassified workers produces up to $100,000 in DLI civil penalties alone, plus the back-wage and back-premium stack across all 10 workers.

What an audit typically looks like in Minnesota

Most Minnesota Misclassification Act audits begin with a SUI benefit claim when a worker files for unemployment after an engagement ends, a workers’ comp claim after a work-related injury, an ESST complaint for unpaid sick leave, or a private civil complaint. DLI receives the referral, opens an investigation, and runs the § 181.722 or § 181.723 analysis against the actual facts of the engagement.

DLI then shares findings with DEED (for back SUI), the Department of Revenue (for back withholding), and the PFML administrator. All back-payment demands land within weeks of each other. The stacked exposure arrives in four separate agency letters, not one.
