You can fire without cause. You cannot fire for the wrong reason. Four statutes draw the line.

Fire a Minnesota employee the wrong way and the at-will rule does not protect you. Four statutes carve out situations where a dismissed employee can sue regardless of how your offer letter reads.

The strictest exposure: final wages on an involuntary dismissal are due within 24 hours of the employee’s written demand. Miss that window and the state can order back wages plus penalties. Minneapolis and St Paul add their own wage-theft fines on top.

Most US companies hiring in Minnesota have heard of at-will employment. Fewer know the Whistleblower Act covers internal reports to management, not just tip-offs to regulators.

This page covers the four statutory exceptions, the 24-hour final-pay rule, drug-testing obligations that constrain dismissal, and what Teamed handles when you exit an employee.

Minnesota is at-will, but “at-will” is not a blank cheque

Pricing is Zero FX, no currency mark-up, no hidden conversion margin.You can terminate any employee at any time, with or without cause and with no required notice period, unless a contract or statute says otherwise.

The phrase “at-will” describes the default rule. It does not override the four statutory exceptions below.

Jordan is a sales manager in Edina. There is no employment agreement, no union, no handbook policy requiring cause for dismissal. His employer can let him go on a Tuesday with no explanation. That is the at-will default working exactly as written.

But if Jordan had reported a suspected fraud to his manager three weeks earlier, the timing creates a Whistleblower Act exposure. The employer now carries the burden of showing the dismissal was unconnected to the report. “At-will” does not erase that burden.

The Whistleblower Act protects internal complaints, not just regulatory tip-offs

The Minnesota Whistleblower Act (Minn. Stat. § 181.932) prohibits dismissing or retaliating against an employee who in good faith reports a suspected legal violation or refuses to participate in an illegal act.

The report can be internal. It does not need to go to a government agency to trigger protection.

Maya works in accounts payable in St Paul. She emails her controller flagging what she believes is incorrect sales-tax remittance. Three weeks later she is let go as part of a “restructuring.”

The proximity and the internal nature of the complaint combine to create a Whistleblower Act claim. A successful case can produce reinstatement, full back wages, and attorneys’ fees. The employer shoulders the burden of proving the dismissal was genuinely unconnected to the report once the employee establishes the protected activity happened and the timing is close.

The good-faith requirement matters. A deliberately false or malicious report is not protected. But honest, mistaken belief is enough. Courts do not require the employee to be right about the violation.

The Minnesota Human Rights Act covers more protected classes than federal law

The Minnesota Human Rights Act (MHRA), Minn. Stat. § 363A, prohibits dismissing an employee because of race, colour, national origin, sex, marital status, disability, age, sexual orientation, familial status, or public-assistance status.

Several of those classes, including marital status and public-assistance status, are not protected under federal Title VII. Minnesota goes further.

Carlos is a 55-year-old engineer in Rochester. His team is restructured and his role is eliminated. Three months later the company posts a nearly identical role and hires a 29-year-old. The MHRA’s age protection covers workers at any age above 18, unlike the federal Age Discrimination in Employment Act that only covers workers 40 and older.

The MHRA enforcement route runs through the Department of Human Rights or the courts. Successful claimants can recover compensatory damages, injunctive relief, and attorneys’ fees. There is no statutory cap on compensatory damages for intentional discrimination.

Drug-testing rules constrain when and how you can dismiss for a positive result

Minnesota drug-testing law (Minn. Stat. §§ 181.950–181.957) requires a confirmatory test before any adverse action, a written explanation of results, and a chance for the employee to present a medical explanation.

For a first positive, Minnesota requires you to offer a rehabilitation evaluation before dismissal, unless the role is safety-sensitive.

A Minneapolis tech company tests 12 employees as part of a random programme. One result comes back positive for cannabis. The company terminates immediately. That is a procedural violation. The required sequence is: positive initial test, confirmatory re-test, written notice, 3-day window for the employee to provide a medical explanation, and then, before dismissal on a first offence for non-safety roles, an offer of a rehabilitation referral.

Skip any step and the employee has a wrongful-termination claim even though Minnesota is an at-will state. The drug-testing statute overrides the at-will default on process, not on the substance of what you can test for.

Note that Minnesota cannabis law changed from 1 August 2023. Employers may still test, but cannot discipline employees solely for off-duty cannabis use in many circumstances. Safety-sensitive roles (drivers, heavy-machine operators) are an exception. This intersection is actively developing and should be confirmed with your Teamed specialist before any dismissal for cannabis-related reasons.

You cannot fire someone for smoking on their own time

The Lawful Consumable Products Act (Minn. Stat. § 181.938) bars employers from refusing to hire or terminating an employee solely because they use lawful consumable products, including tobacco and alcohol, outside work hours.

This is not a wellness carve-out. The restriction applies even if the employer wants to lower health-insurance costs.

A Minneapolis benefits manager decides the company will only hire non-smokers to keep group-health premiums down. She screens out three finalists for off-hours tobacco use. That screening violates the LCPA before a single person is hired, let alone fired.

The employer can still prohibit tobacco use during working hours and on company premises. The LCPA only protects off-duty, off-premises use of legal products. It does not override a drug-testing regime for safety-sensitive roles where impairment at work can be demonstrated.

Final pay after involuntary dismissal is due within 24 hours of written demand

Minnesota Statutes section 181.13 sets the clock: for involuntary termination, the employer must pay all earned wages within 24 hours of the employee’s demand.

For voluntary resignation with at least 5 days’ notice, final pay is due on the last day of work. Without 5 days’ notice, pay is due on the next regular payday.

Dana is let go from a St Paul marketing agency on a Friday afternoon. She sends a written demand for final wages by email at 5pm. The 24-hour clock starts. If her employer does not pay by 5pm Saturday, the employer is in default. The state can order penalties on top of the unpaid wages.

Minneapolis and St Paul both have wage-theft ordinances that layer additional penalties and enforcement mechanisms on top of the state baseline. A St Paul employer who delays a $4,000 final payment by two weeks faces state penalties plus city-level wage-theft liability.

02 Final Pay § 181.13

The 24-hour-demand rule is one of the strictest final-pay timelines in the United States. Most states allow one to three pay periods. Minnesota’s written-demand trigger means the clock can start the same day as the termination.
