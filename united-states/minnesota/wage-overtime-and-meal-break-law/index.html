How does Minnesota wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

One overtime trigger, not four. A 30-minute meal break for shifts over 8 hours. And two city wage floors that stack above state. Minnesota’s regime is simpler than California’s, but the Minneapolis and St Paul ordinances catch out-of-state employers every time.

What is Minnesota’s minimum wage in 2026?

Minnesota has one minimum wage rate, adjusted annually for inflation by the Department of Labor and Industry (DLI). From 1 January 2025, every employer pays the same rate regardless of size. The large/small employer distinction was eliminated by Chapter 116 of 2023 Laws (effective 1 August 2024).

The 2026 rate carries a factual uncertainty: two sources in this sprint’s research returned different figures ($11.13/hr and $11.41/hr). The MN DLI minimum wage page is the primary authority and you should confirm the current rate at dli.mn.gov before committing to any specific number in an offer letter or payroll model.

Three things catch out-of-state employers on Minnesota minimum wage:

  • City floors update annually. Minneapolis and St Paul both index their floors and publish rate changes, often on different schedules from the state. An employee who works one shift inside Minneapolis city limits earns the Minneapolis rate for those hours.
  • No tip credit under state law. Minnesota is one of seven states that do not allow employers to pay tipped workers a cash wage below the standard minimum. Tips supplement wages; they do not substitute for them. Source: MN Stat. § 177.24, subd. 4.
  • Work-location tracking matters. For a distributed team or a mobile worker, the rate that applies depends on where they perform work, not where the company is registered.

Exempt salary floor in Minnesota

To classify an employee as exempt from overtime under FLSA, you need to meet both a salary basis test and a duties test. The federal FLSA salary floor is $684 per week ($35,568 per year) as of 2025. Minnesota has not enacted a separate, higher state-level salary basis threshold.

If your national pay band falls below $35,568 per year, any employee classified as exempt is a misclassification risk regardless of their job title. Minnesota employees are covered by FLSA overtime rules for most roles.

Does Minnesota have its own overtime rule?

For most employers, no. Minnesota does not have a separate state overtime threshold above the federal FLSA standard. Overtime (1.5 times the regular rate of pay) applies after 40 hours in a workweek.

There is no daily overtime trigger in Minnesota. An employee who works a 12-hour day earns no overtime premium for those extra hours unless their total for the week exceeds 40.

Minnesota’s weekly-only overtime rule is straightforward compared to California or Alaska. What catches multi-state employers is not the overtime calculation itself. It is the interaction between the workweek definition and payroll cadence.

A fixed workweek (for example, Sunday through Saturday) has to be established in writing and applied consistently. If your payroll system defaults to a calendar-week or pay-period workweek that differs from the designated workweek, overtime calculations can drift. An employee who works 45 hours in a Sunday-to-Saturday workweek is owed 5 hours of overtime, even if those 45 hours straddle two bi-weekly pay periods.

Exempt employees and the duties test

Minnesota does not have a state-specific duties test. FLSA duties tests (executive, administrative, professional, outside sales, highly compensated employee) apply in Minnesota as they do federally. The practical consequences:

  • The salary floor matters. A "manager" paid below $684 per week is non-exempt regardless of title and is owed overtime on every hour above 40.
  • Primary duty, not time percentage. The executive exemption requires the employee’s primary duty to be managing. A shift supervisor who spends 80% of their time on the floor doing non-managerial work is likely non-exempt.
  • Computer employee exemption. The FLSA computer employee exemption applies in Minnesota. Employees earning at least $684/week (salary) or $27.63/hour (hourly) in qualifying computer-related roles can be exempt if they meet the duties test. This covers most software engineering roles.

Employees not covered by FLSA

A small number of employees fall outside FLSA coverage entirely: employees of very small firms not engaged in interstate commerce, domestic workers in private homes below certain thresholds, and some agricultural workers. For these employees, Minnesota’s own minimum wage law (MN Stat. § 177.21–177.35) covers minimum wage but does not separately require overtime premium above the federal standard. In practice, the vast majority of employees hired through Teamed’s EOR in Minnesota are FLSA-covered.

Minnesota’s meal break and rest break rules

For shifts of 8 or more consecutive hours, Minnesota law requires employers to give employees a sufficient unpaid period for a meal. The break must be long enough for the employee to eat a meal. Source: MN Stat. § 177.253.

On top of the meal requirement, employees working 4 or more consecutive hours must be given adequate time to use the nearest restroom. The rest break is paid time and must occur at least once in each 4-hour stretch. Source: MN Stat. § 177.254.

Minneapolis imposes a stricter rule: a 30-minute meal period for shifts of 6 or more hours under the Minneapolis Workplace Health and Safety program. If you have employees working inside Minneapolis city limits, the city standard governs, not the state floor.

Two points that trip up multi-state employers:

  • The 8-hour threshold is consecutive, not total. An employee who works a split shift (4 hours in the morning, a 2-hour gap, 4 hours in the afternoon) has not worked 8 consecutive hours. The state meal break obligation may not apply to that specific shift pattern. The Minneapolis 6-hour standard does not use the ‘consecutive’ qualifier in the same way, so check the city ordinance for Minneapolis employees.
  • Nursing break time does not expire at the federal 12-month mark. FLSA required nursing break time for up to one year after birth (under the Providing Urgent Maternal Protections Act / PUMP Act). Minnesota law has its own one-year provision under MN Stat. § 177.253, subd. 2, plus additional protections under the Minnesota Human Rights Act. They run in parallel; the more protective rule governs.

Wage theft and break-time enforcement

Minnesota’s Wage Theft Prevention Act (effective July 2019) expanded DLI’s enforcement authority and introduced criminal penalties for wage theft. Failing to provide required break time, or failing to pay for a required paid break, is a wage violation subject to civil penalties.

Employers must provide a written notice to employees at the start of employment covering pay rate, pay period, and employer information. A missing or inaccurate notice is a separate violation from an unpaid wage claim. The DLI Labour Standards Division handles complaints. Minneapolis and St Paul both operate their own enforcement offices for violations of their city ordinances.

Minneapolis and St Paul stack above state minimums

Both Minneapolis and St Paul maintain city-level minimum wage ordinances that set floors above the state minimum. If an employee performs work inside either city, the city rate applies for those hours, not the state floor.

The most common mistake is treating Minnesota as a single-rate state. You need to track work location at the city level, not just the state level.

The tip-credit point matters. If you run national payroll with tipped workers at a reduced cash wage (the federal cash wage is $2.13/hr with full tip credit), that model is unlawful in Minnesota for state-covered wages. Every Minnesota tipped employee earns the full state minimum wage in cash, with tips on top.

Wage theft ordinances in Minneapolis and St Paul

Both cities have Wage Theft Prevention Ordinances that go beyond the state’s 2019 Act. Key differences:

  • Written wage notices. Both cities require an employer to give the employee a written notice at hire (and within 24 hours of any change) covering pay rate, overtime policy, pay period, pay day, and employer contact details. The state requires the same, but the city notices must be in the employee’s primary language.
  • Paystub requirements. Both cities require paystubs that are more detailed than the federal minimum. Itemising each type of pay (regular, overtime, any premium) is required.
  • Retaliation prohibition. Retaliation against an employee for exercising rights under city wage law is independently actionable under city ordinance, in addition to the state and federal protections.

For a distributed team that includes remote employees in Minneapolis or St Paul, configure city-level rules in payroll before the first hire, not after. The city ordinances set out penalties per violation, per pay period. The dollar amounts update periodically; confirm current penalty figures directly with the Minneapolis or St Paul Labour Standards offices.

How often do you have to pay Minnesota employees?

Minnesota requires employers to pay wages at least once every 31 days for most employees. Source: MN Stat. § 181.101.

Employees who are paid on a commission basis must be paid at least once every 15 days for commissions earned in the prior 15-day period, unless a written agreement provides for a different frequency approved by the Commissioner of Labour and Industry.

Three pay-timing rules catch out-of-state payroll teams:

  1. The 24-hour final-pay rule. A terminated employee who makes a written demand for their wages is owed every dollar within 24 hours. Late final pay creates a penalty of one day’s wages for each day beyond 24 hours, up to 15 days (MN Stat. § 181.13). If your payroll runs weekly, the next cycle is too late.
  2. Paystub accuracy matters at the city level. Minneapolis and St Paul require detailed paystubs. Running a generic national paystub template for Minnesota employees without adding hours-by-rate breakdowns or ensuring primary-language availability can create a wage-theft violation independent of the underlying wages being accurate.
  3. Wage deductions require written consent. Health insurance premiums, parking, equipment deposits: every voluntary deduction requires a written employee authorisation. No authorisation, no deduction, regardless of what the employment agreement says.
