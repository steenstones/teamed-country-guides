How does Arizona wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

Arizona is one of the few US states whose minimum wage is indexed to inflation every January. The state floor is $14.70 per hour for 2026 under Proposition 206 (ARS § 23-363), more than double the federal $7.25. Overtime defers to federal FLSA (1.5x after 40 hours per week, no daily trigger). Adult meal and rest breaks are not required. Flagstaff and Tucson set city-level minimums above the state floor. The Arizona Wage Act (ARS § 23-350 et seq.) controls pay frequency and final-pay timing.

Arizona wage and hour law in 2026 leads with the inflation-indexed state minimum wage. The state floor is $14.70 per hour for calendar year 2026 under Proposition 206 (ARS § 23-363), with annual 1 January indexation to the August-over-August CPI-U. Two cities sit above the state floor: Flagstaff at $17.85 and Tucson at $15.00. The tip credit is $3.00 below the state minimum, with the gap recoverable in tips; the tipped cash wage is $11.70 per hour for 2026. Overtime defers to federal FLSA only: 1.5 times regular rate after 40 hours per workweek, no daily overtime trigger, $684 per week salary basis for exempt classification. Arizona has no state mandate on adult meal or rest breaks. The Arizona Wage Act (ARS § 23-350 et seq.) requires pay at least semi-monthly (max 16 days between paydays) and sets the final-pay clock at the sooner of the next regular payday or 7 working days after an involuntary separation.

What is Arizona’s minimum wage in 2026?

Arizona’s state minimum wage in 2026 is $14.70 per hour, set by Proposition 206 and indexed annually under ARS § 23-363(B). The rate adjusts every 1 January to the August-over-August change in the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U, US city average). Two Arizona cities are higher: Flagstaff at $17.85 and Tucson at $15.00 for 2026. The federal floor of $7.25 has no practical effect in Arizona; the state rate has run above $7.25 every year since the first Prop 206 step in 2017.

Three practical consequences for any employer paying Arizona wages in 2026:

  • The rate moves every 1 January. Payroll systems set up with a static state minimum wage will lag the law each year. The Industrial Commission of Arizona announces the next-year rate around 30 September (after the August CPI release), giving employers roughly 90 days to update offer letters and salary letters tied to the minimum.
  • Two city overlays. Flagstaff and Tucson set higher local floors. An employer with an Arizona payroll but employees physically working in Flagstaff or Tucson follows the city rate, not the state. Each city publishes its own annual notice; verify both city pages each January.
  • No subminimum for trainees or learners under Arizona law. Arizona adopts the federal subminimum rules for learners and full-time students by reference, but they require a US Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division certificate to apply. Assume the full state minimum wage unless you hold one of those certificates.

For multi-state employers, Arizona is now a mid-band state on minimum wage. The 2026 rate of $14.70 sits between Florida ($14.00) and Maryland ($15.00), and well above the 21 US states that still default to the federal $7.25 floor. The Proposition 206 origin

Voters approved Proposition 206, the Fair Wages and Healthy Families Act, in November 2016. It set a four-step minimum-wage schedule from 2017 to 2020 and wrote in the annual CPI indexation that has driven every 1 January raise since 2021. The same proposition introduced statewide Earned Paid Sick Time (1 hour per 30 worked, capped at 40 hours per year for employers with 15+ employees and 24 hours per year for smaller employers) under ARS § 23-373. The Arizona Legislature passed HB 2579 in 2016 to preempt cities from setting paid-leave rules above the Prop 206 floor, but the minimum-wage layer was not preempted, which is why Flagstaff and Tucson lawfully set their own city floors above the state.

When does an Arizona employer have to pay overtime?

An Arizona employer pays overtime under the federal Fair Labor Standards Act only. The rule is 1.5 times the regular rate for hours worked over 40 in a workweek. Arizona has no state overtime statute and no daily overtime trigger. Exempt classification follows the federal salary basis of $684 per week and the duties tests in 29 CFR Part 541.

Because Arizona has no state overtime layer, the calculation is the standard FLSA workweek rule. Define a fixed 168-hour workweek (e.g. Sunday 00:00 to Saturday 23:59), total the non-exempt hours, and pay 1.5 times the regular rate on every hour over 40. The regular rate includes non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, and commission earned in the same week; it excludes discretionary bonuses, gifts, and reimbursement of expenses. The Arizona Industrial Commission’s Labor Department does not enforce a separate overtime regime; FLSA wage-claim enforcement runs through the US Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division or as a private FLSA action.

Exempt vs non-exempt classification

To be exempt from FLSA overtime in Arizona, an employee must meet both a salary basis test and a duties test:

  • Salary basis: at least $684 per week ($35,568 per year), paid as a fixed salary not reduced by quality or quantity of work. Arizona defers to the federal floor; no state-level salary top-up applies.
  • Duties test: primary duty must be executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales as defined in 29 CFR Part 541. Job title alone never establishes exemption.

A salaried “manager” making $48,000 per year who spends 80 percent of the day stocking shelves alongside hourly staff fails the duties test and is owed overtime on every hour over 40 in the workweek. The 2024 federal rule increase that would have raised the salary basis to $1,128 per week was vacated by the US District Court for the Eastern District of Texas in November 2024, so the operative floor in May 2026 is $684 per week. How does Arizona’s tip credit work?

Arizona allows a tip credit of up to $3.00 below the state minimum wage under ARS § 23-363(C). The tipped cash wage is $11.70 per hour for 2026 ($14.70 minus $3.00). The employer must guarantee that combined cash wage plus tips equals or exceeds the state minimum each pay period; any shortfall has to be made up by the employer. Arizona’s rule is more generous to employers than the seven no-tip-credit states (Alaska, California, Nevada, Oregon, Washington, Minnesota, Montana), and tighter than the federal $2.13 cash wage with $5.12 tip credit.

Three operational rules for restaurant, bar, and hospitality employers in Arizona:

  • Make-up calculation runs each pay period. A server paid $11.70 per hour cash wage who books only $2.00 in tips on a 40-hour week earned $13.70 per hour total. The employer must top up by $1.00 per hour for that week to reach the $14.70 state minimum. Track tip totals per shift and per pay period; do not net-out high-tip and low-tip weeks.
  • Tip pools are permitted with limits. Arizona employers can run a tip-pool arrangement that distributes pooled tips among customarily tipped employees (servers, bussers, bartenders). The 2018 federal amendment to the FLSA bars the employer or any manager or supervisor from sharing in any tip pool, regardless of whether the employer takes a tip credit. Arizona defers to that federal rule.
  • Service charges are not tips. A mandatory service charge added to a bill by the employer is employer revenue, not employee property. If the employer chooses to pass some or all of a service charge to staff, that distribution counts as wages (subject to payroll tax) rather than tips and cannot count against the tip-credit ceiling.

Flagstaff applies a smaller $2.00 maximum tip credit (city tipped wage $15.85 for 2026); Tucson’s tip credit matches the state at $3.00 (city tipped wage $12.00). An employer running operations in all three jurisdictions has three different tipped cash wages to set per location. Do Arizona employers have to give meal or rest breaks to adults?

No. Arizona law does not require employers to provide meal breaks or rest breaks to adult employees (age 18 and over). Federal FLSA does not mandate breaks either. If you do offer rest breaks of less than 20 minutes, federal law (29 CFR 785.18) counts them as paid hours worked. Meal periods of 30 minutes or more, where the employee is fully relieved of duty, can be unpaid.

The federal default rules govern what happens when you do choose to offer breaks:

  • Short rest breaks (5 to 20 minutes): 29 CFR 785.18 treats these as compensable working time. A 15-minute paid coffee break counts toward the 40-hour weekly overtime threshold.
  • Meal periods (30+ minutes): 29 CFR 785.19 allows these to be unpaid, provided the employee is fully relieved from duty for the purpose of eating. An employee required to stay at their desk and answer the phone during a 30-minute lunch is “engaged to wait” and the time is compensable, which would bring it back into the 40-hour weekly count.
  • Nursing mothers: the federal PUMP Act (Section 7(r) of FLSA, extended by the 2022 act) requires reasonable break time and a private non-bathroom space to express breast milk, for one year after the child’s birth. This rule applies in Arizona and is non-negotiable for any FLSA-covered employer.

Most Arizona employers offer a 30-minute unpaid lunch and one or two 10-15 minute paid breaks per 8-hour shift, by policy not statute. The structure has to be documented in the employee handbook and applied consistently; ad-hoc “take a break when you need one” phrasing creates wage-and-hour exposure when an employee later claims they worked through unpaid time. Minors under federal child-labor rules

Arizona does not add a state-level meal-break rule for workers under 18, but federal child-labor rules (29 CFR Part 570) cap hours, restrict hazardous occupations, and require specific working-time limits for 14- and 15-year-olds. Schedule a 30-minute unpaid meal break for any minor working a 5+ hour shift as a defensive practice; document it on the timesheet.

How do the Arizona Wage Act rules on pay frequency and final pay work?

The Arizona Wage Act (ARS § 23-350 to § 23-355) sets two operational rules. Pay frequency must be at least semi-monthly, with no more than 16 days between paydays (ARS § 23-351). Final pay on involuntary separation is due the sooner of the next regular payday or 7 working days after the termination date (ARS § 23-353(A)). A voluntary quit triggers final pay on the next regular payday (ARS § 23-353(B)). Violations expose the employer to treble damages plus the unpaid wages under ARS § 23-355.

The two operational implications for inbound payroll:

  • Bi-weekly is compliant; weekly is over-compliant; monthly is non-compliant. Most multi-state employers default to bi-weekly (every 14 days), which satisfies the 16-day max comfortably. Monthly payroll is illegal for most employee types in Arizona, with limited carve-outs for executive, administrative, and professional employees paid on a salary basis (ARS § 23-351(C)(3)).
  • Involuntary terminations need next-day-or-near-next-day payroll capability. The 7-working-day clock is sharper than the federal default. An Arizona employee terminated on Monday must be paid by the following Wednesday (7 working days excluding the weekend), or on the next regular payday, whichever comes first. Treble damages under ARS § 23-355 turn a small late-pay claim into a meaningful settlement. A single $3,000 unpaid final paycheck paid 14 days late, after the 7-working-day window closed, exposes the employer to $9,000 in damages plus the original $3,000, plus attorney fees if litigated. Is on-call or waiting time paid in Arizona?

Federal FLSA controls this in Arizona. The test is “engaged to wait” (paid) vs “waiting to be engaged” (not paid). An employee at the workplace waiting for the next task is on the clock, and that time counts toward the 40-hour weekly overtime trigger. An employee at home, free to use the time, who carries a phone in case they’re called in, is off the clock. The factual line is whether the employee can effectively use the time for their own purposes.

Three patterns Arizona employers see most often:

  • On-premises wait time: a delivery driver between runs reading a book in the warehouse breakroom. Engaged to wait. Compensable. Counts toward the 40-hour weekly overtime trigger.
  • On-call at home with restrictions: an IT engineer required to stay within 15 minutes of the office, sober, and answer calls within 5 minutes. The restriction effectively prevents personal use. Likely compensable, depending on the call frequency and response window.
  • On-call at home, free to live normally: a service technician carrying a phone and expected to take one or two calls per week. Free to socialise, sleep, run errands. Not compensable for the wait time, only for the actual call response.

The federal Wage and Hour Division’s Fact Sheet #22 lists the controlling tests; the Industrial Commission of Arizona’s Labor Department adopts them by reference. Travel between job sites during the workday is compensable; the ordinary commute to and from the workplace is not; out-of-town overnight travel is compensable during normal working hours, even on weekends. Training time is compensable unless all four of the following are true: outside normal hours, voluntary, not directly job-related, and no productive work is performed. Source: US Department of Labor, Fact Sheet #22, Hours Worked Under the Fair Labor Standards Act. Accessed 21 May 2026.

How should multi-state employers handle Arizona wage-and-hour law?

Apply the highest applicable standard per employee, by physical work location. Arizona sits above the federal floor on minimum wage and tip credit, defers to federal on overtime and breaks, and adds the Arizona Wage Act’s pay-frequency and final-pay rules on top. An employee working remotely in Flagstaff follows the $17.85 Flagstaff minimum; an employee in Tucson follows the $15.00 Tucson minimum; the rest of the state follows $14.70.

Four operational rules for multi-state US employers with Arizona headcount:

  1. Maintain a state matrix with city overlays. Document each state where you employ someone, plus each Arizona city with a local minimum wage (Flagstaff and Tucson). Re-check on 1 January each year when the state and city rates step.
  2. Tag each employee’s work location, not the company’s headquarters. The wage-and-hour rule that applies is the rule of the location where the employee physically performs the work. A Delaware-incorporated company with a remote employee living and working in Flagstaff follows the Flagstaff city minimum. The cross-state rule rarely matters for salaried remote workers but always matters for hourly Arizona headcount.
  3. Configure final-pay runs for the 7-working-day clock. Most multi-state payroll systems are set up for the federal default of next-payday final pay. The Arizona 7-working-day rule (involuntary separation) needs a discrete flag on each Arizona employee record to trigger the out-of-cycle pay run. Configure once, on the first Arizona hire.
  4. Set the Arizona tip-credit calculation per location. Phoenix, Mesa, Scottsdale, Tempe and most of the state apply the $3.00 state tip credit (cash wage $11.70). Flagstaff applies a $2.00 city tip credit (cash wage $15.85). Tucson applies a $3.00 city tip credit (cash wage $12.00). Three configurations, not one.

For most early-stage US employers, the practical move is a single national handbook that defaults to the strictest applicable state for any benefit, plus a state-specific addendum for jurisdictions with hard mandates (California, New York, Massachusetts, Washington, Oregon, Colorado, Illinois, and Arizona for the Wage Act final-pay rule and the indexed minimum wage). Arizona’s addendum is short: state the current Arizona minimum wage with the Flagstaff and Tucson overlays, state the $3.00 state tip credit, state the FLSA-only overtime rule, state the 7-working-day final-pay clock, and reference the Earned Paid Sick Time accrual under Prop 206.
