Which worker classification test does Arizona use?

Arizona uses the IRS common-law test for state unemployment insurance (administered by the Arizona Department of Economic Security) and state income tax withholding (administered by the Arizona Department of Revenue). Arizona has no state-specific ABC test and no equivalent of Alabama’s Act 2021-226 codifying a 20-factor list. Workers’ compensation uses a separate right-of-control test.

This makes Arizona one of the friendlier US states for genuine contractor engagements. California’s strict ABC test under AB5 presumes employment until the hiring party proves all three factors (no control, work outside the usual course of business, independently established trade). Arizona has no such presumption. The default is a facts-and-circumstances analysis under the same factors the IRS applies on a Form SS-8 determination, weighted across behavioural control, financial control, and the nature of the relationship.

What is Arizona’s Declaration of Independent Business Status (DIBS)?

DIBS is a codified safe-harbour under ARS § 23-1601, enacted by HB 2114 in 2016 and effective 6 August 2016. A signed declaration creates a rebuttable presumption of independent-contractor status for state-law purposes when the hiring party acts substantially consistent with its terms. Most US states have no codified safe-harbour. Arizona does.

The declaration is voluntary. Not signing one does not create any negative presumption, and DIBS is not admissible to deny the existence of an independent-contractor relationship if the parties chose not to use it. But signing a valid DIBS and operating consistently with it shifts the burden of proof to the party challenging the classification, which is a meaningful procedural advantage in an audit.

What the declaration must include

The contractor must sign a written declaration affirming:

  1. They operate their own independent business
  2. They are not an employee and have no right to unemployment benefits from the hiring party
  3. They are responsible for all tax liability on payments received from the hiring party
  4. They are responsible for obtaining and maintaining all necessary registrations and licenses
  5. At least six of ten enumerated factors apply to them

The ten DIBS factors (at least six must apply)

#Factor
1The contractor has no health insurance or workers’ compensation coverage through the hiring party
2The contractor is free to work for other businesses without restriction
3The contractor has the right to accept or decline service requests
4The contractor has a realistic expectation of performing work for multiple parties
5The contractor is economically independent from this single engagement
6The contractor controls the methods and processes used to perform the work
7The hiring party may set quality and deadline standards, but the contractor controls the work schedule
8Payment is based on contracted work, not salary or a regular minimum payment
9The contractor provides and maintains their own tools and equipment
10The contractor bears all performance-related business expenses

Three limitations every hiring party should know

  • State-law only. DIBS is a defence against state-level classification challenges. It has no presumptive effect on federal classification by the IRS, the US Department of Labor, or the NLRB. A worker who is a contractor under DIBS for Arizona purposes can still be re-classified as an employee under federal law.
  • Substantial consistency required. Signing the form and then treating the worker as an employee in practice voids the presumption. The hiring party must actually operate the engagement consistently with the declaration. A DIBS in the file and W-2-style supervision in the office is worse than no DIBS at all.
  • Does not cover workers’ comp. DIBS protects against UI and state tax classification challenges. For workers’ comp, the parallel safe-harbour at ARS § 23-902 requires a separate written agreement with its own disclosure language.

How does Arizona workers’ compensation classification work?

Workers’ comp is the carve-out. Arizona’s Workers’ Compensation Act under ARS Title 23 Chapter 6, administered by the Industrial Commission of Arizona (ICA), uses a right-of-control test. A parallel safe-harbour at ARS § 23-902 lets a hiring party and contractor sign a written agreement that creates a rebuttable presumption of independent-contractor status, but only if it contains a specific disclosure that the contractor is not entitled to workers’ comp benefits from the business.

02 Two Safe-Harbours, One Hire

Arizona runs two parallel safe-harbours on the same contractor relationship. The DIBS declaration (ARS § 23-1601) covers unemployment insurance and state tax. The ARS § 23-902 written agreement covers workers’ comp. A 1099 backed by one without the other is half-protected, with full exposure on the missing track.

The right-of-control test under ICA case law asks one core question: did the hiring party reserve the right to direct the means and method of the work, even if it did not exercise that right? It is narrower than the multi-factor common-law analysis, but Arizona appellate courts read it generously toward coverage. A workers’ comp claim filed by an injured contractor lands in front of a judge who applies a different rulebook than the Arizona DES auditor.

What the ARS § 23-902 written agreement must contain

To trigger the rebuttable presumption for workers’ comp purposes, the written agreement between the business and the contractor must include all eight of the following conditions plus the required disclosure:

  1. The business does not supervise or control the actual work
  2. The business does not require exclusive service
  3. The contractor obtains their own necessary licences and registrations
  4. The business does not pay salary or hourly wages
  5. The contractor provides their own tools
  6. The contractor controls the timing of the work
  7. Payment is made via the name on the agreement (not the individual)
  8. The contractor maintains separate business operations

The required disclosure: ‘The independent contractor is not entitled to workers’ compensation benefits from the business.’ The agreement is null and void if either party’s consent was obtained through misrepresentation, false statements, fraud, intimidation, coercion, or duress. When voided, the carrier may collect premiums for the period.

What it costs an Arizona employer who gets workers’ comp classification wrong

  • Missed premium, plus penalty and interest, calculated back to the start of the engagement
  • Personal liability for the injury itself if the worker was hurt on the job during the period and never carried under a policy
  • Loss of statutory immunity from civil suit, because the workers’ comp bargain (premium for tort immunity) was never paid

A single uncovered head injury can blow past six figures, even for a small Arizona firm. What does misclassifying an Arizona worker actually cost?

Stacked liability across six categories. A misclassified $60,000-per-year Arizona worker, audited and re-classified after 3 years on 1099, typically exposes the employer to roughly $25,000 to $80,000 per worker in back taxes, FLSA double damages, and workers’ comp gap, before legal fees. Wilful misrepresentation in unemployment matters is a Class VI Felony with criminal exposure on top.

The criminal threshold is wilful misrepresentation, not honest error, but the line between optimistic adviser language and wilful conduct can look uncomfortably thin in an aggressive audit.

Why the Arizona numbers look smaller than other states

Two structural reasons. First, Arizona’s state income tax is a flat 2.5 percent, one of the lowest single-rate income taxes in the US, so the state withholding exposure on a misclassification is materially lower than in a state like California or New York. Second, the SUTA wage base is $8,000, also among the lowest in the country, so the unemployment-tax exposure caps out earlier. The federal floor (FICA, FUTA, FLSA) dominates the Arizona stack. State-level exposure adds 5 to 15 percent on top of the federal cost.

How does the 2026 federal rule change interact with Arizona’s test?

On 27 February 2026, the federal Department of Labor issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking to rescind the 2024 ‘totality of the circumstances’ FLSA test and restore the 2021 economic reality test. The proposal affects federal FLSA wage and hour analysis only. Arizona’s state-level common-law test is unaffected, because it sits in state statute and case law, not federal regulation.

The federal proposal matters for FLSA overtime exposure, which is one of the six stacked liability categories above. If the proposed rule lands, the threshold for finding a worker is an ‘employee’ for FLSA overtime purposes tightens slightly compared with the 2024 rule. The state-level Arizona analysis under the IRS common-law test runs on its own track, so a worker who passes DIBS for Arizona purposes can still be analysed under the new federal economic-reality test for FLSA, and reach a different conclusion in the edge cases.

Practical effect for an Arizona hire

For typical full-time-equivalent roles, both tests reach the same answer: employee. The divergence sits in the edge cases, the highly-specialised consultant who works from home, uses their own tools, sets their own hours, and is paid by deliverable. That profile passes DIBS and passes economic-reality. The role that fails one usually fails the other too. Where they diverge, the conservative approach is the answer with the broader coverage, which is usually ‘employee’.
