## Verified data (sources at bottom)

### Arizona's test for worker classification — the distinctive angle

Arizona has **no state-specific ABC test**. Instead, the state uses the **IRS common-law test** for unemployment insurance and state tax withholding purposes — same factors as the federal Form SS-8 analysis. What makes Arizona distinctive is the **codified safe-harbour**: **ARS § 23-1601**, the **Declaration of Independent Business Status (DIBS)**, enacted by **HB 2114 in 2016** and effective **6 August 2016**.

DIBS lets a hiring party and a contractor sign a structured declaration that creates a **rebuttable presumption** of independent-contractor status. Most US states do not have a codified safe-harbour at all; Arizona does.

### DIBS — what the declaration must contain (ARS § 23-1601)

The contractor must sign a declaration affirming:
1. They operate their own independent business
2. They are not an employee and have no right to unemployment benefits
3. They are responsible for all tax liability on payments received
4. They are responsible for obtaining and maintaining all necessary registrations and licenses
5. **At least six of the following ten factors apply to them:**
   - No health or workers' comp coverage through the hiring party
   - Freedom to work for other businesses
   - Right to accept or decline service requests
   - Expectation of performing work for multiple parties
   - Economic independence from this single engagement
   - Control over performance methods and processes
   - Hiring party may set quality/deadline standards but contractor controls the schedule
   - Payment based on contracted work, not salary or hourly wage
   - Contractor provides and maintains their own tools and equipment
   - Contractor bears all performance-related business expenses

The declaration must apply to the contractor, their employees, and any subcontractors.

**Critical limitations of DIBS:**
- It is a **state-law safe-harbour only** — has no presumptive effect on federal classification (IRS, US DOL, NLRB)
- The hiring party must **act substantially consistent with the declaration** — signing the form then treating the worker as an employee in practice voids the presumption
- A construction industry licensee (Title 32, Chapter 10) has additional restrictions
- DIBS does NOT substitute for the written agreement required under ARS § 23-902 for workers' compensation purposes

### Workers' compensation — the parallel safe-harbour (ARS § 23-902)

Arizona's Workers' Compensation Act is administered by the **Industrial Commission of Arizona (ICA)** under ARS Title 23, Chapter 6. The classification test for workers' comp is **right-of-control** — case-law-derived, applied as a totality-of-circumstances analysis.

ARS § 23-902 provides a parallel safe-harbour for workers' comp: a signed **written agreement** between the business and the independent contractor that includes:
1. The business does not supervise or control the actual work
2. The business does not require exclusive service
3. The contractor obtains their own licenses and registrations
4. The business does not pay salary or hourly wages
5. The contractor provides their own tools
6. The contractor controls work timing
7. Payment is made via the name on the agreement
8. The contractor maintains separate business operations

Plus a **disclosure statement that the independent contractor is not entitled to workers' compensation benefits from the business**.

This creates a **rebuttable presumption** of independent contractor status for WC purposes. The agreement is null and void if obtained through misrepresentation, fraud, or coercion.

The two safe-harbours (DIBS + § 23-902 written agreement) are independent — a hiring party who wants full coverage on a 1099 needs both signed and acted-on consistently.

### Cost of getting it wrong in Arizona

Misclassification exposure stacks across multiple categories:

1. **Federal payroll tax (FICA, FUTA)** — same federal exposure as any state: unpaid 7.65 percent employer FICA, FUTA shortfall, penalty under IRC § 3509
2. **Arizona state income tax withholding** — ARS Title 43. Unpaid withholding at the **2.5 percent flat rate** (the lowest single-rate income tax in the US that levies one at all) plus penalty and interest under Arizona Department of Revenue rules
3. **Arizona unemployment insurance (SUTA)** — DES UIT-0603A FY2026 rate chart. New-employer rate **2.0 percent** on first **$8,000** wages = up to **$160 per worker per year** new-employer rate; up to **$668.80** at the ceiling rate of 8.36 percent. Plus penalty, interest, and loss of FUTA credit (potentially another $432 = $8,000 × 5.4 percent)
4. **SUTA dumping penalty** — if the misclassification looks like an attempt to obtain a lower UI rate (e.g. moving workers between related entities), Arizona imposes either **the highest rate provided by law OR the current rate plus an additional 2 percent**, whichever is greater. Tax-evasion classification triggers separate criminal exposure
5. **Federal FLSA back wages and overtime** — 2-year lookback, 3 years if wilful; plus **liquidated damages equal to back wages** (double damages by default)
6. **Workers' compensation premium and uncovered injury** — premium gap on the period, plus personal liability for any injury sustained during the look-back period
7. **Criminal exposure** — Arizona treats wilful misrepresentation in unemployment matters as a **Class VI Felony**, up to **2 years prison** and **$150,000 per false statement** in fines

Conservative back-of-envelope on a misclassified Arizona worker earning $60,000 per year over 3 years, audited and re-classified: **roughly $25,000 to $80,000 in stacked back taxes, FLSA exposure, and workers' comp gap** before legal fees. Lower than the comparable Alabama exposure because Arizona's state income tax rate (2.5 percent flat) and SUTA wage base ($8,000) are among the lowest in the US — the federal floor dominates.

### Recent changes that matter

- **DIBS has been live since 6 August 2016** (HB 2114). It is well-established case-law now; no major recent amendments. Worth surfacing as the distinctive Arizona structure because most US states do not have codified safe-harbours.
- **27 February 2026 — federal DOL Notice of Proposed Rulemaking** to rescind the 2024 totality-of-circumstances rule and restore the 2021 economic-reality test for FLSA. Federal-level only; Arizona's common-law test for state UI and state tax is unaffected.
