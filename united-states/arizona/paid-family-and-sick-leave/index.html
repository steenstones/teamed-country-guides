What leave must Arizona employers provide in 2026?

Paid sick time has been mandatory in Arizona since 1 July 2017 under Proposition 206 (the Arizona Earned Paid Sick Time Act): 40 hours/year at 15 or more employees, 24 hours/year under 15. No state paid family leave; federal FMLA at 50+ employees. Out-of-state employers commonly miss this statute.

Arizona in 2026 does require paid sick time, and has done since 1 July 2017 under the Arizona Earned Paid Sick Time Act (created by Proposition 206 in November 2016 and codified at Arizona Revised Statutes Title 23, Chapter 2, Article 8.1, §§ 23-371 to 23-381). Employees accrue paid sick time at 1 hour per 30 hours worked, capped at 40 hours per year for employers with 15 or more employees and 24 hours per year for employers with fewer than 15. Time is available to use after 90 days of employment, carries over up to the annual cap (or the employer may front-load the annual entitlement and decline carry-over), and covers the employee’s own illness, a family member’s illness, public health emergency closures, and safe leave (domestic violence, sexual violence, abuse, or stalking). Arizona has no state paid family leave law; the only family-leave floor is federal FMLA (29 U.S.C. § 2601), which provides 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave at employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. PWFA, USERRA, and Arizona’s jury duty job-protection rules stack on top.

Does Arizona require paid sick leave?

Yes, since 1 July 2017. Arizona voters passed Proposition 206 (the Fair Wages and Healthy Families Act) in November 2016. The sick-leave half of the proposition took effect halfway through 2017 and is codified as the Arizona Earned Paid Sick Time Act at ARS §§ 23-371 to 23-381. Most out-of-state employers have never updated their Arizona handbook since the statute landed.

The accrual mechanic is the same every Arizona employee runs on. Every Arizona employee earns 1 hour of paid sick time for every 30 hours worked. The annual cap depends on employer size: 40 hours per year at employers with 15 or more employees, and 24 hours per year at employers with fewer than 15. Accrual starts on the first day of employment, but the employee cannot use the time until they have completed 90 days of employment (or earlier if the employer chooses to allow it). Unused hours carry over to the next year, capped at the annual entitlement. The employer may instead front-load the full annual entitlement at the start of each year and decline carry-over; that is a common shortcut for cleaner ledger maths.

The qualifying reasons are broader than a typical sick-day policy. Sick time under ARS 23-373 covers the employee’s own physical or mental illness, the care of a covered family member with an illness, closure of a workplace or a child’s school by order of a public official because of a public health emergency, and what the statute calls safe leave: absences related to domestic violence, sexual violence, abuse, or stalking, whether the victim is the employee or a covered family member. Safe leave covers court appearances, relocation, counselling, and obtaining services from a victim-services organisation.

What this means for an Arizona handbook in 2026

If your Arizona employee handbook was last updated before mid-2017, it almost certainly needs a rewrite. Three things must appear: (1) the accrual rate of 1 hour per 30 hours worked, (2) the correct annual cap for your headcount band (24 or 40 hours), and (3) the broader scope of sick time (own illness, family illness, public health closure, and safe leave). A handbook that still describes paid sick days as “discretionary” or “at manager approval” is not compliant in Arizona and exposes the employer to the private right of action under ARS 23-376.

For employers with established PTO policies that already exceed the statutory floor, the rule is that the existing PTO bank can satisfy the entitlement as long as it can be used for the same qualifying reasons (including safe leave) and the accrual rate is no slower than 1 per 30. Most out-of-state handbooks need updating to cover safe leave specifically, even if their generous PTO totals already exceed 40 hours.

Enforcement sits with the Industrial Commission of Arizona (ICA) Labor Department. Employees may file a complaint with the ICA or sue in state court directly; ARS 23-376 allows recovery of unpaid sick time, an additional sum equal to twice the unpaid amount as liquidated damages, and reasonable attorney fees. Anti-retaliation provisions create a rebuttable presumption of retaliation for any adverse action taken within 90 days of an employee exercising sick-time rights.

Does Arizona require paid family leave?

No. Arizona has no state paid family leave law. Nine US states plus DC do require it (California, Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Washington, and Washington DC); Arizona is not one of them. There is no Arizona state PFL insurance tax, no state PFL programme to fund, and no state PFL claim process for employees. Proposition 206 created paid sick time only; it did not create a paid family leave entitlement.

For an Arizona employer, hiring in Arizona removes the state-level PFL withholding line that adds 0.5 to 1.0 percent of wages in California, New York, or Washington. There is no equivalent payroll deduction for PFL, no state PFL bond schedule, and no state agency PFL claim portal to maintain. The cost line simply does not exist on an Arizona payslip. Combined with Arizona’s 2.5 percent flat income tax and the $8,000 state unemployment wage base, Arizona is one of the lighter-burden states for an employer’s total payroll cost stack.

What this means for talent: an Arizona employee comparing job offers against friends in California or Washington will not have access to a state PFL programme to fall back on for the birth of a child or the care of a parent. The employer’s voluntary parental-leave policy carries the full weight of that retention conversation, especially for Phoenix and Tucson knowledge workers who compare against Bay Area and Seattle roles where state PFL is layered onto company benefits.

What does federal FMLA give Arizona employees?

Federal FMLA gives qualifying Arizona employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period, with group health coverage continued at the employer’s normal contribution. It applies only to employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. The employee qualifies after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked in the preceding 12 months.

The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 is the federal floor for unpaid, job-protected family leave. It sits on top of Arizona’s Earned Paid Sick Time Act: FMLA covers parental bonding and serious health conditions, the Arizona statute covers shorter sick-day absences and safe leave. The 50-employee threshold counts every US employee, not just Arizona ones, which catches multi-state employers off guard.

Five qualifying reasons trigger FMLA leave:

  • Birth and care of a newborn, within 12 months of birth
  • Placement of a child with the employee for adoption or for a placement under state child-placement law, within 12 months of placement
  • Care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition
  • The employee’s own serious health condition that makes them unable to perform the job
  • Qualifying military exigency arising from a family member’s active duty in the Armed Forces; up to 26 weeks for military caregiver leave

The 50-employee threshold counts your whole US workforce

An employer with 12 Arizona employees and 45 Texas employees crosses the 50-employee FMLA threshold even though Arizona alone does not get there. The Department of Labor counts every employee on the US payroll for any 20 or more weeks of the current or preceding year. The 75-mile-radius rule then determines which specific worksites are FMLA-covered, which matters for fully remote employers whose employees are geographically dispersed across the southwest.

What FMLA does not require

  • Pay during leave. FMLA leave is unpaid by statute. An employee may substitute accrued PTO or accrued Arizona Earned Paid Sick Time; the employer may require substitution of accrued paid time. There is no FMLA wage-replacement benefit.
  • FMLA leave for employers under 50. A 25-employee Arizona company has no FMLA obligation and Arizona has no mini-FMLA. Below the threshold, parental leave and serious-illness leave longer than the sick-time accrual are entirely voluntary.
  • FMLA leave for the first 12 months. A 3-month tenure Arizona employee with a new baby has no FMLA right to job-protected leave; the employer may grant unpaid leave voluntarily.

Source: US Department of Labor, FMLA Final Rule (29 CFR Part 825), accessed 21 May 2026.

What protections apply for pregnancy and disability in Arizona?

Federal law applies; Arizona adds nothing specific beyond the sick-time act. The Pregnancy Discrimination Act (PDA, 1978) treats pregnancy like any other temporary disability for employer leave and benefits. The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA), effective 27 June 2023, requires reasonable accommodation for pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions, applying to employers with 15 or more employees. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) covers pregnancy-related disabilities such as gestational diabetes or severe preeclampsia.

The practical reading for an Arizona employer at 15 to 50 employees: PWFA covers reasonable accommodation (modified duties, schedule changes, time off for medical appointments, lactation breaks), but FMLA does not yet cover the 12 weeks of job-protected leave. The accommodation conversation under PWFA may include voluntary unpaid leave as the accommodation, but the employer has no statutory obligation to hold the job open beyond the accommodation period. An employee may still draw on the 40-hour Arizona sick-time bank for prenatal appointments.

At 50+ employees the package stacks: PWFA accommodation during the pregnancy, FMLA for the birth and bonding, ADA for any post-delivery serious complications, plus the 40-hour Arizona sick-time bank that the employee can use during routine prenatal appointments and recovery. The three federal laws together resemble what a single state PFL programme covers in California or New York, except every line is unpaid (the Arizona Earned Paid Sick Time is paid but capped at 40 hours).

What this means for offer letters

The most retention-critical voluntary line on an Arizona offer letter is paid parental leave. The federal floor (12 weeks unpaid at FMLA-covered employers, accommodation under PWFA at 15+) plus 40 hours of paid sick time still leaves a wide gap that voluntary policy fills. Mid-market Arizona employers commonly offer 6 to 8 weeks of paid maternity leave and 2 to 4 weeks of paid paternity or partner leave; top-quartile knowledge-work employers in Phoenix and Tucson offer 12 to 16 weeks paid for the primary caregiver regardless of gender. What about military leave and jury duty in Arizona?

Federal USERRA protects civilian jobs for service members; Arizona Revised Statutes § 26-168 mirrors USERRA for Arizona National Guard members called to state active duty. Jury service is mandatory and ARS § 21-236 protects an employee’s job during service. Arizona, like Alaska and unlike Alabama, does not require employers to continue pay during jury duty; it only protects the job. Neither USERRA nor jury duty is a state PFL or PSL programme; these are narrow, separate carve-outs.

USERRA and Arizona military leave

The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA, 38 U.S.C. § 4301 et seq.) provides civilian reemployment rights for service members called to active duty, with a cumulative cap of five years of military absence. The employer pays nothing during the leave itself, but health-plan coverage must continue (the employee may pay up to 102 percent of the full premium for COBRA-style continuation). On return, the employee is reinstated to the position they would have held had they not left, the so-called escalator principle.

Arizona Revised Statutes § 26-168 adds state-active-duty protection (Arizona National Guard members called by the Governor): same job-protection logic, no statutory pay during state-active-duty absence, no length cap for state active duty specifically. State and federal protections stack; the employee gets whichever framework is more favourable for the specific call-up. Arizona is a heavy National Guard state because of Luke Air Force Base and Davis-Monthan Air Force Base support roles, so this comes up more often than in many other southwestern states.

Jury duty in Arizona

ARS § 21-236 prohibits an employer from discharging, threatening, intimidating, or coercing an employee because of jury service or a jury summons. Unlike Alabama’s pay-continuation rule, Arizona does not require the employer to pay regular wages during jury service. The employee may use accrued PTO or accrued Arizona Earned Paid Sick Time (some employers permit this; the statute does not require it), and the court pays a small per-diem.

Most Arizona employers in knowledge-work sectors voluntarily continue full pay during jury duty as a retention signal, treating it as a low-cost benefit because jury service is infrequent. Source: Arizona Revised Statutes § 26-168 and § 21-236, accessed 21 May 2026.

What about employers with fewer than 15 employees in Arizona?

Below 15 employees, an Arizona employer must still provide 24 hours/year of paid sick time under ARS 23-372 and remains exempt from PWFA accommodation and federal FMLA. USERRA reemployment and Arizona’s jury-duty job protection apply regardless of size. Everything else is voluntary, including paid parental leave, paid bereavement, and additional paid sick days beyond the 24-hour floor.

The compliance burden at single-digit Arizona headcount is lighter than at California or New York equivalents, but it is no longer zero. The Earned Paid Sick Time accrual ledger has to be maintained: every Arizona employee earns 1 hour per 30 worked, available after 90 days, capped at 24 per year, carrying over to the next year up to the cap (or front-loaded). The absence does count against any FMLA tracking if you later cross the 50-employee threshold.

The 15-employee threshold tipping point

An Arizona employer hits the 15-employee threshold under ARS 23-372 when the Arizona headcount reaches 15 (the count is by individuals employed for compensation in each working day of any twenty or more calendar weeks in the current or preceding year, per ARS 23-371(F)). At the 15-employee point, the annual sick-time cap rises from 24 to 40 hours per year per employee. The employer’s accrual ledger needs to switch over for new hires on the day the threshold trips, and existing employees’ caps move up at the start of the next benefit year (or on the trip date, depending on the policy).

The 50-employee threshold tipping point

An Arizona employer crosses the FMLA 50-employee threshold gradually as the total US headcount grows, often without noticing. The trigger is 20 or more weeks of 50+ US employees in either the current or preceding calendar year. Once tripped, the FMLA obligation continues for the rest of the current calendar year and the full following calendar year, even if headcount drops back below 50. The lookback rule catches employers who hire seasonally or scale through funding rounds; the FMLA obligation often outlasts the headcount that triggered it.
