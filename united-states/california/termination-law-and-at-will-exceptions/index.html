How does California termination law and at-will exceptions actually work?

California is statutorily at-will under Cal. Lab. Code § 2922, but the bench has built three judicial exceptions on top of it: the Tameny public-policy TORT (1980), the Foley implied-in-fact contract from a totality of circumstances (1988), and the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing as narrowed in Guz v. Bechtel (2000). Layered on top sit FEHA (broader than Title VII), CFRA (broader than FMLA), and Cal-WARN (75 employees, not 100). The hard part is the cash mechanic: § 201 requires the final cheque in hand at the termination meeting, with a § 203 waiting-time penalty of up to 30 days of wages for late payment. The strictest final-pay rule in the United States.

California is the most plaintiff-friendly termination jurisdiction in the United States. The at-will baseline is statutory under Cal. Lab. Code § 2922, but the California Supreme Court has built three judicial exceptions on top of it: public-policy TORT (Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co., 27 Cal.3d 167 (1980)) with punitive-damages exposure; implied-in-fact contract from a totality-of-circumstances test (Foley v. Interactive Data Corp., 47 Cal.3d 654 (1988)); and the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, capped at contract damages only after Guz v. Bechtel Nat'l, Inc., 24 Cal.4th 317 (2000). Federal anti-discrimination law (Title VII, ADA, ADEA, USERRA, FMLA) layers underneath FEHA (Cal. Gov. Code § 12940, broader than Title VII, no statutory damage caps) and CFRA (Cal. Gov. Code § 12945.2). California is a 300-day deferral state for EEOC charges, and the CRD filing window is 3 years after SB 807. Final pay is due immediately at the termination meeting under § 201; quits under § 202 within 72 hours; late payment triggers a § 203 waiting-time penalty of up to 30 days of wages. Cal-WARN (Cal. Lab. Code §§ 1400–1408) applies at 75 employees (not 100), and SB 617 expanded the mandatory notice content effective 1 January 2026.

Is California really an at-will employment state?

Yes, but it is the at-will state with the most judicial exceptions in the United States. The baseline rule lives in Cal. Lab. Code § 2922, which says employment without a specified term may be terminated at the will of either party on notice to the other. Then the California Supreme Court built three judicial exceptions on top of it over 20 years, and the legislature stacked FEHA, CFRA, and Cal-WARN on top of those. The statute is one sentence; the rest of the chapter runs hundreds of pages.

The baseline statute reads, in full: "An employment, having no specified term, may be terminated at the will of either party on notice to the other. Employment for a specified term means an employment for a period greater than one month." Cal. Lab. Code § 2922. That is the entire at-will rule. Everything else is what the courts and the legislature have done with it.

What this means in practice for a US-or-international company hiring into California:

  • You do not need “cause” under § 2922 to terminate an indefinite at-will hire. A reduction in force, a strategic pivot, a personality fit, all sufficient on the statutory baseline before the exceptions bite.
  • The state-law exceptions are the largest in the United States. Tameny, Foley, and Guz (the “at-will trilogy”) plus FEHA, CFRA, the Labor Code retaliation provisions, and the PAGA enforcement mechanic that lets private plaintiffs sue on behalf of the state for civil penalties.
  • The federal toolbox sits on top, not underneath. Title VII, ADA, ADEA, USERRA, FMLA, FLSA retaliation, NLRA Section 7. None of it goes away because the state is at-will; it stacks with the state claims.
  • Final pay is the rule a non-California employer is most likely to break in week one. § 201 requires the cheque in hand at the termination meeting; § 203 doubles a few hours of delay into 30 days of wages as a statutory penalty.

California sits at the opposite end of the spectrum from Alabama (which rejects the public-policy exception altogether) and even from Arkansas (two narrow exceptions under Sterling Drug). California adopts every common-law exception that has ever appeared in a US jurisdiction, then layers a uniquely employee-favourable statutory apparatus on top. The at-will headline is real; the at-will defence at trial is much narrower than the headline suggests.

What the Tameny / Foley / Guz trilogy actually says

Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co., 27 Cal.3d 167 (1980). An at-will employer cannot fire an employee for refusing to commit an illegal act (price-fixing on the facts of Tameny). The court held this is a TORT, not a contract claim, because the duty arises from public policy rather than the parties' agreement. Punitive damages are available. The plaintiff must point to a public policy that is (a) delineated in a constitutional or statutory provision, (b) public in the sense of affecting society at large (not just the parties), (c) well established at the time of discharge, and (d) substantial and fundamental. Gantt v. Sentry Insurance (1992) and Stevenson v. Superior Court (1997) tightened the “public” element. The whistleblower statute at Cal. Lab. Code § 1102.5 codifies one major category.

Foley v. Interactive Data Corp., 47 Cal.3d 654 (1988). An at-will presumption can be overcome by an implied-in-fact contract drawn from the “totality of circumstances”: the employee's tenure, the employer's personnel policies, oral assurances of continued employment, raises and promotions, industry custom, and any handbook language that promises termination only for cause. Foley himself had seven years' service, written termination guidelines that mandated a seven-step pre-termination procedure, and oral assurances. That combination defeated the at-will presumption. The court denied tort damages for breach of this implied contract; the remedy is contract damages.

Guz v. Bechtel Nat'l, Inc., 24 Cal.4th 317 (2000). The California Supreme Court narrowed both the Foley implied-contract claim and the implied-covenant claim. Length of service, raises, and good performance reviews alone are not enough to defeat the at-will presumption without an “agreement, express or implied, that limits the employer's otherwise unfettered authority.” The court confirmed that the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in employment is bounded by the specific terms of the agreement: where the agreement allows at-will termination, the employer's motive is irrelevant to the covenant claim. No tort damages for breach of the covenant. The case is the modern benchmark for at-will employer-side defence.

The handbook lever

A clean at-will disclaimer, repeated in the offer letter and the handbook, signed at hire and on every handbook update, is the single biggest state-law lever an employer has. A handbook that promises “progressive discipline”, lists an exhaustive set of dismissal grounds, or includes language suggesting termination only for cause without a controlling disclaimer creates the exact Foley-style record. The 2020 California Court of Appeal decision in Hassell v. Lawn Care Corp. reaffirmed that an at-will disclaimer in an offer letter and a signed acknowledgement of at-will status in a handbook will defeat an implied-contract claim absent specific oral assurances to the contrary, but the disclaimer must be conspicuous and consistent across documents.

Which federal and FEHA claims can a fired California employee still bring?

All of them, on a longer clock and with no damage caps. California is a 300-day deferral state for EEOC charges because the CRD enforces FEHA under a work-sharing agreement, so the federal Title VII / ADA / ADEA clock runs at 300 days instead of the 180-day non-deferral baseline. The CRD's own filing window is 3 years from the alleged violation under SB 807. Once the right-to-sue letter issues, the employee has 1 year to file a FEHA lawsuit. FEHA has no statutory damage caps, unlike Title VII's $50,000–$300,000 caps tied to employer size.

Why the 300-day window matters

Most US states with a state civil-rights enforcement agency that has a work-sharing agreement with the EEOC are deferral states, and the federal charge window extends from 180 days to 300 days. California has the CRD (Civil Rights Department, formerly DFEH until 1 July 2022), which enforces FEHA and has a long-standing work-sharing agreement with the EEOC. A California plaintiff therefore gets 300 days on the federal clock. This is the opposite of Alabama and Arkansas (both 180-day non-deferral) and the same as New York, Illinois, and most other large states with active state civil-rights agencies.

The CRD filing path

A California FEHA plaintiff typically files with the CRD first (online at calcivilrights.ca.gov), requests an immediate right-to-sue letter, and proceeds to civil court in state or federal court within 1 year of the right-to-sue notice. After SB 807 (Stats. 2021, ch. 278), the CRD filing window itself extended from 1 year to 3 years from the alleged violation, and the CRD's own investigatory tolling further protects late-discovered claims. The right-to-sue letter is administrative, not substantive: it doesn't decide the merits, just opens the courthouse door.

No statutory damage caps under FEHA

Federal Title VII caps compensatory and punitive damages on a sliding scale by employer size: $50,000 for 15–100 employees, up to $300,000 for 500+ employees. FEHA has no statutory cap. A FEHA jury verdict can include unlimited compensatory damages (including emotional distress), unlimited punitive damages subject to due-process review, full lost-wages back pay, and attorney's fees on top. This is one of two structural reasons a California termination is the most expensive in the United States to defend (the other being the § 203 waiting-time penalty mechanic below).

CFRA broader than FMLA

CFRA covers smaller employers (5+ employees after SB 1383, effective 1 January 2021, down from 50+) and broader family relationships (parent-in-law, grandparent, grandchild, sibling, and a designated person of the employee's choice). FMLA covers 50+ employees within a 75-mile radius and the narrower spouse / child / parent. A California employee with a sick sibling can take CFRA leave that FMLA wouldn't cover; firing them in retaliation triggers a CFRA wrongful-termination claim that has no federal counterpart.

When is the final paycheck due in California, and what is the 30-day waiting-time penalty?

For an involuntary discharge, immediately at the termination meeting under Cal. Lab. Code § 201. For a voluntary quit, within 72 hours under § 202 (or on the last day worked if the employee gave 72 hours' notice). Late payment triggers the § 203 waiting-time penalty: a full day of wages for each day late, up to 30 days. This is the strictest final-pay rule in the United States and the single largest cash-management risk for a multi-state employer hiring in California.

What “wages due” actually includes

California reads “wages” broadly. The final payment must cover:

  • Regular hourly or salary pay through the last moment worked, including the termination meeting itself.
  • All earned and unpaid vacation as a vested wage under Suastez v. Plastic Dress-Up Co., 31 Cal.3d 774 (1982). California does not permit “use-it-or-lose-it” PTO; once vacation accrues, it cannot be forfeited at termination and must be paid out in cash at the employee's final rate.
  • Commissions and bonuses already earned under the plan documents at separation, even if not yet calculated. The calculation must be reasonable and complete by the § 201 / § 202 deadline.
  • Earned but unpaid overtime premiums under California's daily overtime rule (over 8 hours/day, over 40 hours/week, and seventh-consecutive-day premiums), not just the federal weekly threshold.
  • Reporting-time pay, split-shift premiums, and any other wage line owed under the applicable IWC Wage Order.
  • Note: sick leave under California's paid sick leave law is NOT vested and is NOT payable on termination.

How the § 203 waiting-time penalty actually works

The mechanic in § 203 is automatic and brutal. If the employer wilfully fails to pay any wages due under § 201 or § 202, the employee's wages continue at the same daily rate as a penalty for every day the wages remain unpaid, up to a maximum of 30 days. “Wilful” in § 203 means “intentional”, not malicious; an honest dispute about the amount owed can be a defence, but it must be genuinely good-faith and the employer carries the burden of proof. A $400/day employee paid one day late on $4,000 of accrued wages is exposed to up to $12,000 in penalty wages on top of the $4,000 underlying amount. A $1,500/day senior engineer paid two weeks late is exposed to $21,000 in § 203 penalties before fees, with the California Labor Commissioner or a private suit on the other side.

Why the cash mechanic matters more than the headline rate

Other US states use “next regular payday” (Alabama, Florida, federal FLSA default), “7 days of demand” (Arkansas), or treble damages (Arizona). California's § 201 requires the cheque in hand at the termination meeting itself, before the employee leaves the room. A central HR team running a multi-state payroll cycle two weeks out cannot rely on its normal cadence for a California discharge; it has to cut a manual cheque on the day. Teamed's payroll engine flags California separations at the point of decision and produces a same-day cheque ready to hand over at the termination meeting, alongside the wage statement that itemises vacation pay-out, commission accrual, and final overtime.

What protects you

Four things. First, decide the termination date and the time of day in advance, and have payroll cut the cheque the morning of, not the evening before. Second, run a contemporaneous calculation against your written vacation, commission, and bonus policy, signed off internally before the cheque cuts. Third, your written vacation policy itself: California permits accrual caps (no further accrual once a ceiling is reached, then resumes after time off is taken) but does NOT permit forfeiture at termination, so the policy needs to be drafted around the right lever. Fourth, document the moment of payment: many employers hand the cheque over with a wage statement, get a signed receipt at the termination meeting, and timestamp it. The receipt is not a release of claims, just proof of delivery against the § 201 clock.

How should you document a termination in California?

Five documents do most of the work: a handbook with a conspicuous at-will disclaimer in the offer letter and signed acknowledgement, a contemporaneous performance file, a termination letter with an independent specific stated reason, a same-day final-pay calculation hitting the § 201 deadline, and an itemised wage statement meeting the Cal. Lab. Code § 226 line-item requirements. The goal is to defeat the Foley / Guz implied-contract claim, give the court a clean non-pretextual record on summary judgment under McDonnell Douglas, and stay out of § 203 penalty territory.

The handbook disclaimer (Foley / Guz defence)

The disclaimer that defeats a Foley implied-contract claim under Guz is a clear, conspicuous statement that nothing in the handbook is an express or implied contract of employment, that nothing alters at-will status, that the employer reserves the right to modify any policy unilaterally, that any list of disciplinary grounds is non-exhaustive, and that no manager other than a specified senior executive has authority to bind the company to a contract for a specified term. Put it on the front page, repeat it on the signature page, repeat it in the offer letter, and obtain a signed acknowledgement at hire and on every handbook update. The Guz opinion is explicit that an at-will employee with long tenure, raises, and good performance reviews who lacks an “agreement, express or implied, that limits the employer's otherwise unfettered authority” cannot defeat the at-will presumption. The disclaimer is the “express agreement that confirms unfettered authority”.

Contemporaneous performance documentation

FEHA and federal anti-discrimination law live or die on the question of pretext. The plaintiff's argument is that your stated reason for termination is a cover for discriminatory motive (race, sex, age, disability, sexual orientation, etc.) or a Tameny-protected activity (whistleblowing, refusing to violate the law). The defence is contemporaneous documentation: performance reviews with dated entries, written warnings, PIPs with signed acknowledgements, customer complaints, attendance records. Documents created the day of the event carry far more weight in a California jury than reconstructed narratives written after the CRD complaint arrives.

The termination letter

State the reason clearly and precisely. “Position eliminated as part of the May 2026 reduction in force” or “continued failure to meet the documented sales quota despite the 30-day PIP that ended on 14 April 2026” both work. Vague reasons (“business needs”, “not the right fit”) invite the plaintiff to fill in the blank with a protected-class motive. California state and federal courts apply the McDonnell Douglas burden-shifting framework, and the test turns on whether the stated reason was the actual reason and whether the employer believed it at the time. Guz also requires the employer to articulate the reason for the at-will termination if the employee makes a prima facie showing under the implied-contract theory; vague answers help the plaintiff.

Same-day final pay and the § 226 wage statement

The final pay must hit the § 201 deadline (immediately at the termination meeting) and the wage statement that accompanies it must comply with Cal. Lab. Code § 226's nine itemised line-item requirements: gross wages, total hours worked, all deductions, net wages, pay period start and end dates, name and last 4 of SSN, employer's legal name and address, all hourly rates and corresponding hours, and any piece rate / commission detail. A wage-statement violation is a separate cause of action under § 226(e) with damages up to $4,000 per employee plus attorney's fees, and is a routine PAGA add-on to any termination claim. Get the wage statement right or the § 201 victory becomes a § 226 loss.

Independent grounds for protected-activity terminations

For terminations close in time to a workers' comp claim under § 132a, a whistleblower disclosure under § 1102.5, a CFRA / FMLA leave request, an EEOC charge, or a complaint about wage-and-hour conditions, California courts apply a stringent causation test. SB 497 (Stats. 2023, ch. 285) added a rebuttable presumption of retaliation if an adverse action occurs within 90 days of the protected activity, with a civil penalty up to $10,000 per employee per violation under Cal. Lab. Code § 1102.5(j). If you have an independently sufficient ground for the termination, document it before the protected activity is on the table, and rely on it as the stated reason, you cut off the retaliation theory at the root.

What about mass layoffs in California (Cal-WARN and SB 617)?

California has a state mini-WARN that is stricter than federal WARN. The California WARN Act at Cal. Lab. Code §§ 1400–1408 requires 60 days' written notice for a plant closing, mass layoff, or relocation at a 75-employee site (not 100), with broader coverage than federal WARN. As of 1 January 2026, SB 617 (Stats. 2025, ch. 401) expanded the mandatory notice content: each Cal-WARN notice must now state whether the employer will coordinate workforce-development-board services, include CRD contact information, and include CalFresh food-assistance program information. A timely but content-deficient notice is treated the same as no notice: per-day back pay and benefits liability up to 60 days.

SB 617 and the 1 January 2026 content expansion

SB 617 (Stats. 2025, ch. 401) signed by Governor Newsom in October 2025 amended Cal. Lab. Code § 1401 to add three new mandatory notice elements. Every Cal-WARN notice issued on or after 1 January 2026 must include:

  1. An affirmative statement of whether the employer plans to coordinate rapid-response orientation services through the local workforce development board, through a different entity, or not at all. If coordination is planned, the rapid-response services must be arranged within 30 days after the notice issues.
  2. A functioning email and telephone number for the local workforce development board, with a specified description of America's Job Center of California services.
  3. A short description of the CalFresh food-assistance program, the CalFresh helpline number, and a link to the program website.

The statute applies to every Cal-WARN notice issued on or after 1 January 2026, regardless of when the employer began planning the workforce reduction. The 60-day notice period, the 75-employee threshold, the 50-employee mass-layoff trigger, and the definitions of “mass layoff”, “relocation”, and “termination” did not change.

Damages: timely-but-deficient is treated like no notice

The most underrated risk in SB 617 is that a timely but content-deficient notice does not satisfy Cal. Lab. Code § 1401 as amended. Each day of deficient notice is a separate violation. An employer that issued a 60-day notice on 15 January 2026 without the SB 617 content additions has violated the statute for 60 days, exposing the company to up to 60 days of back pay and benefits for every affected employee, plus the $500/day civil penalty to the local government. The penalty arithmetic is the same as if the employer had given no notice at all. Re-draft the template; do not assume an old template clears the 2026 bar.

EDD Rapid Response and the local workforce development board

Separately from the notice itself, the EDD Rapid Response programme co-ordinates workforce-transition support, on-site briefings, and UI processing for affected employees. The team contacts both employer and affected employees as soon as a Cal-WARN notice is on file. SB 617 elevates the local workforce development board to a co-equal partner in this co-ordination, which is why the new notice content requires explicit WDB contact details. This is operational, not a separate statutory obligation, but it tends to surface fast and should be folded into the communication plan.

Where does the real California termination lawsuit risk sit?

Four places. The Tameny public-policy tort claim with punitive-damages exposure (whistleblowers, refusal-to-violate-the-law, lawful off-duty conduct). The FEHA discrimination / harassment / retaliation suit with no statutory damage cap and a 3-year CRD filing window. § 203 waiting-time penalties on a final-pay disagreement, especially when the dispute is over accrued vacation under Suastez. And the PAGA representative action that converts any individual Labor Code violation into a state-amplified class-style claim with attorney's fees.

What we see in the case docket and in client matters:

  • Wrongful-discharge tort under Tameny. An employer terminates an at-will employee shortly after the employee complained to HR about a wage-and-hour issue, reported a safety violation, refused to participate in something the employee believed was unlawful, or invoked CFRA leave for a sibling. The plaintiff frames it as a Tameny-style public-policy discharge with punitive-damages exposure, and a 90-day SB 497 rebuttable presumption of retaliation if the termination falls inside the window. The fix is documenting the independent business reason before the protected activity is on the table.
  • FEHA discrimination claim with no damage cap. An employee files a CRD complaint within 3 years of the termination alleging a protected-class motive (race, sex, age, disability, sexual orientation, gender identity, military / veteran status, reproductive health decisionmaking). The CRD issues a right-to-sue letter; the employee has 1 year to file in state or federal court. Settlement values run higher than federal Title VII analogues because there is no “500+ employees, $300,000 cap” arithmetic capping the jury's exposure on the employer side.
  • § 201 / § 203 final-pay disputes. An involuntarily-terminated employee's final cheque arrives one day late, or omits an accrued vacation balance, or omits a commission that the employee argues vested before termination. The § 203 penalty doubles a few hours of delay or a small principal underpayment into 30 days of full daily wages, plus the wage-statement § 226 add-on, plus attorney's fees under § 218.5 and § 226(e). The fix is calendaring the termination meeting against a same-day-cheque workflow and a wage-statement template that meets all nine § 226 line items.
  • PAGA representative action. Any Labor Code violation an individual plaintiff can plead becomes a PAGA representative action where the named plaintiff sues on behalf of the State of California for civil penalties on all aggrieved employees. 75% of the recovery goes to the Labor and Workforce Development Agency, 25% to the aggrieved employees, plus attorney's fees. The 2024 PAGA reform (AB 2288 and SB 92) constrained PAGA somewhat (enhanced cure mechanism, capped penalties in some scenarios, standing limited to recent personal experience) but it remains the single largest plaintiff-side amplifier in California employment law.
  • Cal-WARN deficient-notice exposure under SB 617. An employer issues a 60-day notice in February 2026 using a 2025 template that doesn't include the workforce-board coordination statement and CalFresh information. Even though the timing is right, the content fails, and the employer is on the hook for 60 days of back pay and benefits per affected employee. The fix is updating the template the week SB 617 became effective, not the week the first 2026 notice has to go out.

The lesson, repeated in every California employment-defence briefing: the Guz shield is your handbook disclaimer, the § 203 trap is your same-day cheque, the FEHA exposure is your contemporaneous file, and the PAGA amplifier is the reason every individual Labor Code violation matters.

Teamed Legal Operations
California is the state where every termination has to clear three gates at once: the Foley / Guz implied-contract gate on the legal side, the FEHA / Tameny protected-activity gate on the motive side, and the § 201 / § 203 same-day-cheque gate on the cash side. The handbook disclaimer defeats the first. The contemporaneous performance file defeats the second. A calendared termination meeting with the cheque cut the morning of defeats the third.
