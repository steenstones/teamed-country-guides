How does California’s ABC test under AB5 work in 2026?

One presumption flips every classification analysis in California. Every worker is an employee until you prove all three prongs A, B, and C. Prong B is the prong that kills most contractor engagements: the work must sit outside the usual course of your business.

California’s worker classification test in 2026: the strict ABC test, codified by AB 5 (Stats. 2019, ch. 296) at Cal. Labor Code § 2775, presumes every worker is an employee unless the hiring entity proves all three prongs A, B, and C. The test was drawn from Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court (2018) 4 Cal.5th 903, applies across the Labor Code, Unemployment Insurance Code, and IWC Wage Orders, and was upheld by the Ninth Circuit in Olson v. California (2024) with SCOTUS certiorari denied 13 January 2025. Civil penalties under Cal. Labor Code § 226.8 run $5,000 to $15,000 per violation, rising to $10,000 to $25,000 for a pattern or practice, and PAGA actions under § 2698 et seq. let private plaintiffs sue on the State’s behalf. Most multi-state employers walk into California assuming the IRS 20-factor test still applies. It doesn’t. Run the ABC analysis before the first invoice.

Which worker classification test does California use?

California uses the strict ABC test, codified by AB 5 at Cal. Labor Code § 2775. The test was drawn from Dynamex Operations West v. Superior Court (2018) and replaced the older Borello multi-factor test as the default standard across the Labor Code, Unemployment Insurance Code, and IWC Wage Orders.

Before AB5 took effect on 1 January 2020, California applied the Borello balancing test, a flexible multi-factor analysis similar in spirit to the IRS 20-factor common-law test used in most other US states. The California Supreme Court ended that regime in Dynamex by adopting the ABC test for IWC wage-order purposes, and the Legislature codified the ruling and extended it across the Labor Code in AB5. The structural shift matters: under Borello, the analysis weighed factors and reached a probabilistic answer. Under ABC, the worker is presumed an employee, and the hiring entity carries the burden of proving all three prongs. Fail any one prong, the worker is an employee.

What are the three prongs of California’s ABC test?

All three must be met for contractor status. Prong A: the worker is free from control and direction in performing the work. Prong B: the worker performs work outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business. Prong C: the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade or business of the same nature.

ProngStatutory textWhat it actually tests
A The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity in connection with the performance of the work, both under the contract and in fact. Who controls how the work is done. Both the written contract AND the day-to-day reality have to read ‘independent’.
B The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business. The killer. A software company hiring contractor developers fails B every time, because the developers ARE the usual course of business.
C The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as the work performed. Real, ongoing, public-facing business of their own. Other clients, marketing, business licence, separate insurance.

Why Prong B catches almost every software, marketing, and design hire

Prong B is the prong that ended the 2010s California gig economy and is the headline reason out-of-state employers misclassify their first California hire. If you are a software company and you hire a contractor to write software, the work is the usual course of your business. Prong B fails. If you are a marketing agency and you hire a contractor copywriter, Prong B fails. If you are an architecture firm and you hire a contractor draftsperson, Prong B fails. The narrow path through Prong B runs through work that genuinely sits outside what you sell: a plumber called to fix your office bathroom passes Prong B; an electrician wiring your new conference room passes Prong B; a janitorial service passes Prong B (assuming Prongs A and C also pass).

The corollary: an out-of-state employer who used 1099 contractors comfortably in Texas or Florida and assumes the same pattern works in California has misclassified from day one. The Dynamex court was explicit about the policy goal: reduce employer incentive to misclassify by removing the multi-factor wiggle room. Run the analysis before you sign. Source: Cal. Lab Code § 2775(b)(1); DLSE Independent Contractor versus Employee FAQ, accessed 21 May 2026.

Who is exempt from the ABC test under AB5?

A long but narrow list. AB5 (as amended by AB2257) carves out specific occupations and bona-fide business-to-business contracts under Cal. Lab Code §§ 2776 to 2787. Exempt occupations fall back to the older Borello multi-factor test. Everything not on the list defaults to ABC.

The exemption list is not a contractor playground. Each carve-out has its own qualifying conditions, and missing any single condition pushes the relationship back into ABC. The major categories at 2026:

How the bona-fide B2B exemption actually works

The B2B exemption under § 2776 is the route most multi-state employers reach for first, because it does not require the contractor to be on a specific occupational list. It also fails most often. The exemption requires the service provider to be a separate business entity (typically an LLC or corporation, not a sole proprietor), to maintain a separate business location, to hold its own business licence and contractor licence where required, to carry its own insurance, to advertise to the general public, to set its own rates, to negotiate and contract on its own behalf, and to perform similar services for other clients. Failing any one of the 12 conditions kicks the relationship back to ABC and Prong B then determines the answer for most engagements.

Prop 22 (Bus. & Prof. Code § 7448 et seq.), passed by California voters in November 2020, carves out app-based rideshare and delivery drivers from AB5. The measure survived state and federal legal challenges through 2024 and remains in force at 2026. It does not extend to other gig-economy categories. Source: Cal. Lab Code §§ 2776 to 2787; Holland & Knight analysis of AB5 codifying ABC test, accessed 21 May 2026.

How is California’s ABC test different from the IRS 20-factor test?

Two structural differences. Presumption: ABC presumes employee; IRS 20-factor weighs neutral factors. Burden: under ABC the hiring entity must prove all three prongs; under IRS the auditor reaches a balance-of-factors conclusion. A worker who passes the IRS test as a contractor in Texas will routinely fail California’s Prong B.

Most US states (Alabama, Arkansas, Texas, Florida, Indiana, and many others) use some version of the IRS 20-factor common-law test for state unemployment and tax purposes, often codified into state law. The 20-factor analysis groups facts into three buckets (behavioural control, financial control, relationship of the parties) and lets the auditor weigh them. No single factor decides; the pattern decides. A contractor who scores 14 of 20 toward employee is usually an employee; a contractor who scores 14 of 20 toward independent is usually a contractor. The test reflects an underlying balance.

California is structurally different on two axes. First, the worker is presumed an employee from the start. The auditor does not balance factors; the hiring entity bears the affirmative burden to prove each of A, B, and C. Second, Prong B is binary and has no escape valve through facts: the question is whether the work is in the usual course of the hiring entity’s business. There is no ‘the contractor signs their own NDA’ or ‘the contractor provides their own laptop’ factor that can offset a Prong B fail.

The conversion trap for multi-state employers

A common audit pattern: a software company in Texas hires a contractor developer, renews the engagement annually for three years, then opens a small California office and re-engages the same person at the California address. The Texas engagement passes the IRS 20-factor analysis comfortably. The California re-engagement fails Prong B from day one. When the worker files an unemployment claim or a wage-hour complaint, the EDD or Labor Commissioner reaches back over the California portion of the engagement and reclassifies. The reclassification triggers UI back-contributions, SDI back-contributions, ETT back-contributions, state income-tax withholding back, IWC Wage Order back wages (including overtime and meal-break premiums), and exposure under § 226.8 for civil penalties.

What does AB5 misclassification actually cost in California?

Stacked liability across five categories. A misclassified $80,000-per-year California worker, reclassified after 3 years on 1099, exposes the hiring entity to roughly $80,000 to $140,000 per worker in back UI/SDI/ETT contributions, back state income-tax withholding, IWC Wage Order back wages, Cal Lab Code § 226.8 civil penalties, and PAGA exposure, before legal fees.

Why California has no Section 530 safe harbour

Unlike most IRS-aligned states, California does NOT recognise the federal Section 530 reasonable-basis safe harbour for ABC test purposes. A hiring entity that believed in good faith its contractor classification was correct still owes the back contributions and back wages when EDD or the Labor Commissioner reclassifies. Section 530 may still cap the federal payroll-tax piece of the exposure, but it does not touch the state-side UI, SDI, ETT, withholding, IWC Wage Order back-wage, § 226.8 penalty, or PAGA exposure. The California exposure is the dominant exposure, and there is no defence on intent.

Plaintiffs' firms in California track the EDD audit pattern and file follow-on PAGA suits within the one-year limitations window. A single misclassified role can produce a five-figure EDD assessment, a six-figure PAGA exposure, and a class wage-hour suit that hits every contractor on the same job description across the workforce. How does Proposition 22 fit California classification in 2026?

Prop 22 (Bus. & Prof. Code § 7448 et seq.), passed in November 2020, exempts app-based rideshare and delivery drivers from the ABC test and treats them as independent contractors subject to a defined set of pay and benefit minimums. The measure survived state and federal legal challenges and remains in force at 2026.

Prop 22 is the most prominent exception to AB5 and the only carve-out reached by ballot rather than statute. It applies narrowly to drivers for app-based transportation and delivery network companies, defined as companies that match drivers to consumers via an online-enabled app for prearranged transportation or delivery. The exemption does not extend to other gig categories. Same-day couriers who work without an app are not exempt. Field-service contractors who use their own dispatch system are not exempt. The cleaning marketplaces that briefly tried to read Prop 22 broadly were forced back into ABC compliance.

What the carve-out includes

  • Minimum compensation floor of 120 percent of the applicable minimum wage on engaged time, plus 30 cents per mile (indexed)
  • Health-care subsidy for drivers working at least 15 engaged hours per week (full subsidy at 25 hours)
  • Occupational accident insurance for engaged time
  • Anti-discrimination and sexual-harassment protections aligned with employee-level protections
  • No employer obligation for unemployment insurance, paid sick leave, paid family leave, or workers’ comp on the app-based driver work

The 2024-2025 legal certainty

The California Supreme Court upheld Prop 22 in Castellanos v. State of California (2024), settling state constitutional challenges. The Ninth Circuit upheld AB5 itself against constitutional challenge in Olson v. California (2024), and the US Supreme Court denied certiorari on 13 January 2025. The combined effect: AB5 ABC test is the default; Prop 22 is a narrow voter-approved exception for app-based drivers; both are legally settled for 2026. Source: Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 7448 et seq.; Castellanos v. State of California (2024) S279622; Olson v. California (9th Cir. 2024).
