How does California wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

California is the most wage-and-hour-heavy state in the United States. Cal Lab Code § 510 layers daily overtime over 8 hours, double-time over 12 hours, and a separate 7th-consecutive-day rule on top of the federal 40-hour week. Cal Lab Code § 226.7 then charges an extra hour of pay every time a meal or rest break is missed. Four ways the same shift can owe premium, before PAGA exposure gets added on top.

California wage and hour law in 2026 has more state-level texture than any other US jurisdiction. State minimum wage is $16.50 per hour from 1 January 2026, set by Cal. Lab. Code § 1182.12 with annual CPI escalation under SB 3 (2016). Many California cities pay higher (West Hollywood at $20.22, San Francisco at $19.18, Berkeley at $19.18). Industry minimums sit higher still: fast-food workers at $20.00 per hour under AB 1228, healthcare workers tiered from $18.00 to $25.00 per hour under SB 525. Overtime under Cal. Lab. Code § 510 runs 1.5x over 8 hours per day or 40 per week, 2x (double-time) over 12 hours per day or over 8 hours on the 7th consecutive workday. Meal breaks under Cal. Lab. Code § 512 must be 30 minutes unpaid before the 5th hour for shifts over 5 hours, with a second meal for shifts over 10 hours. IWC Wage Orders mandate a 10-minute paid rest break every 4 hours. Missed meal or rest break = one extra hour of pay at the regular rate per missed break per day under Cal. Lab. Code § 226.7. The Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA, Cal. Lab. Code § 2698 et seq.) lets employees sue on behalf of the state at $100 per pay period for initial violations, $200 per pay period for subsequent violations, multiplied across the workforce. That is the reason California wage-and-hour misses generate eight-figure settlements where the same mistake in Texas costs an apology and a back-pay cheque.

What is California’s minimum wage in 2026?

California’s state minimum wage in 2026 is $16.50 per hour, effective 1 January, set by Cal. Lab. Code § 1182.12 with annual CPI-U escalation under SB 3 (Stats. 2016, ch. 4). Industry-specific minimums sit higher: fast-food workers earn $20.00 per hour under AB 1228 (Cal. Lab. Many California cities pay above the state floor, with West Hollywood the highest in the US at $20.22 per hour from 1 July 2025.

Three practical consequences for any employer paying California wages in 2026:

  • Two CPI rate-change dates inside a single calendar year. The state minimum updates on 1 January under SB 3. Most California cities update on 1 July. Payroll has to ingest both schedules, applied per employee’s work location. A San Francisco employee on $19.18 cannot be paid the state $16.50 floor between 1 July and 31 December.
  • No tip credit. California prohibits the federal Fair Labor Standards Act tip credit (FLSA Section 3(m)) entirely. Every tipped worker, in every California restaurant, bar, taxi, or hotel, is owed the full applicable minimum wage (state or local, whichever is higher) in cash before any gratuity. Tips top up; they don’t substitute. California is one of seven US states with this rule (joining Alaska, Oregon, Washington, Nevada, Minnesota, and Montana).
  • Highest applicable rate always wins. An employee working a single shift in West Hollywood gets the $20.22 city rate. An employee working remotely from home in Berkeley gets the $19.18 city rate. An employee working in unincorporated Los Angeles County gets the LA County unincorporated minimum (currently $17.81), not the state $16.50. Payroll tags each hour to the location where it was physically performed.

The exempt salary threshold tracks the state minimum

Under Cal. Lab. Code § 515(a), an exempt salaried employee must earn at least two times the state minimum wage for full-time employment. Two times $16.50 times 40 hours times 52 weeks equals $68,640 per year in 2026. The federal floor at $35,568 per year is irrelevant in California; the higher of the two applies, and California is the higher number for every salaried role. An employee paid $50,000 per year on a "manager" title is automatically non-exempt in California, owed overtime on every shift over 8 hours and the 7th-day rule on every consecutive seven-day stretch. Multi-state employers with a $48,000 minimum salary band for entry-level managers nationally have a California exposure on every California hire in that band. Source: Cal. Lab. Code § 515; California Department of Industrial Relations, Division of Labor Standards Enforcement, Wage and Hour FAQs (dir.ca.gov/dlse/FAQ_Overtime.htm). Accessed 26 May 2026.

When does a California employer have to pay overtime?

A California employer pays overtime under Cal. Lab. Code § 510(a): 1.5x the regular rate for hours over 8 in a workday OR 40 in a workweek, plus 1.5x for the first 8 hours on the 7th consecutive workday. Double-time (2x) kicks in over 12 hours in a workday OR over 8 hours on the 7th consecutive workday. California is one of only two US states with mandatory daily overtime (with Alaska), and the only state with mandatory double-time and a 7th-day premium.

The 7th-day rule catches most US payroll teams unfamiliar with California. An employee who works seven days in a row, in any single workweek as defined by the employer’s established schedule, is owed 1.5x for every hour of the first 8 hours of day seven and 2x for every hour over 8. The employer cannot avoid the premium by rescheduling around it after the fact; the trigger is the actual work performed, not the calendar planning. A retail manager who works Monday through Sunday during a busy holiday week is owed an entire premium day’s pay for that Sunday, regardless of total weekly hours. Combine the 7th-day rule with the daily 12-hour double-time trigger and an over-scheduled employee can earn the equivalent of two and a half days’ straight time on a single Sunday shift.

The four overtime triggers in one shift

Take an employee scheduled seven days in a row, including a 14-hour shift on day seven. The overtime calculation runs:

  • Hours 1-8 on day seven: 1.5x regular rate (7th-day premium for the first 8 hours).
  • Hours 9-12 on day seven: 2x regular rate (over 8 hours on the 7th consecutive workday).
  • Hours 13-14 on day seven: 2x regular rate (over 12 hours in a workday). Same 2x rate; the trigger is the higher of the two rules.
  • Workweek total: the same shift counts toward the 40-hour weekly trigger, but anti-pyramiding prevents double-payment.

For multi-state employers, the operational rule is simple: every California-based hourly employee needs a payroll engine that runs all four triggers in parallel and applies the highest applicable premium per hour, with anti-pyramiding logic to prevent stacking. Most national payroll providers ship with weekly-only as the default and an opt-in California configuration. Switching that flag at hire time is a one-minute setup that prevents 12 months of missed-premium liability. Source: Cal. Lab. Code § 510(a); California Department of Industrial Relations, Wage and Hour FAQs Overtime (dir.ca.gov/dlse/FAQ_Overtime.htm). Accessed 26 May 2026.

Alternative workweek schedules

Cal. Lab. Code § 511 lets a California employer adopt an alternative workweek schedule (AWS) with a 4-day, 10-hours-per-day or 3-day, 12-hours-per-day structure, replacing the daily 8-hour overtime trigger with the AWS schedule. The schedule has to be proposed in writing to the affected work unit, approved by a two-thirds secret-ballot vote of employees in the unit, and reported to the DIR. Daily overtime then triggers above the AWS-scheduled hours (10 or 12), and the weekly 40-hour and 7th-day rules still apply. How do California meal and rest breaks work?

Cal. Lab. Code § 512 requires a 30-minute unpaid meal break for any shift over 5 hours, taken before the end of the 5th hour. A second 30-minute meal break is required for shifts over 10 hours. IWC Wage Orders § 12 require a 10-minute paid rest break for every 4 hours worked (or major fraction), taken as close as practicable to the middle of each work period. Cal. Lab. Code § 226.7(c) requires an employer to pay one additional hour at the employee’s regular rate for each missed meal break and each missed rest break, per workday. A single shift with one missed meal and one missed rest break owes the employee two extra hours of pay on top of regular wages.

The "before the end of the 5th hour" rule, in practice

The meal break has to start before the employee crosses the 5-hour mark. A shift that begins at 9:00 must give the meal break by 1:59. Schedule the break at 2:30 and the employer owes the premium hour, even if the employee actually took a 30-minute break later. The same rule applies to the second meal break: shift starts at 9:00, the second meal must begin by 6:59 if total scheduled hours exceed 10. Most California payroll incidents start here: a manager defers the break because the shift gets busy, the employee works through, and a year later a wage-and-hour complaint cites the entire restaurant’s timesheet history for matching violations.

The premium-pay rate matters

The California Supreme Court ruled in Ferra v. Loews Hollywood Hotel (2021) that the "regular rate of compensation" in Cal. Lab. Code § 226.7(c) means the same calculated regular rate used for overtime, which includes non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, and commissions divided across the workweek hours. A $20-an-hour line cook earning a $200 non-discretionary weekend bonus and missing one meal break that week is owed a premium of more than $20 per missed break, because the regular rate goes up for that week. The arithmetic is harmless on a single shift; the compounded look-back across a 4-year claim period is what makes California meal-break litigation so expensive.

Provide the opportunity, document the result

The employer’s legal obligation is to provide the opportunity to take the meal break on time, not to force the employee off the floor. Brinker Restaurant Corp. v. Superior Court (2012) 53 Cal.4th 1004 framed the standard: relieve the employee of duty, give a reasonable opportunity to take a 30-minute break, and don’t impede or discourage the break. Employees can waive a properly-offered meal break by working through (with mutual consent and signed waiver paperwork), but the burden of proving the offer fell on the employer. Three operational rules cover most exposure:

  • Schedule the break before the 5th hour. The employee handbook and the schedule both have to put the meal break before hour 5:00. A shift template that defaults the meal break to "mid-shift" is fine for 8-hour shifts, problematic for 10-hour shifts (because the meal would land at hour 5:00, on the edge of compliance).
  • Auto-deduct only for full 30 minutes off the clock. A 30-minute meal-break deduction can only run when the employee is fully relieved of duty for 30 consecutive minutes. An interrupted meal break (employee asked to take a customer order during their lunch) reverts to paid time and may also trigger the premium.
  • Print the meal-break offer on the schedule. Many California employers add a one-line acknowledgement to the schedule: "Meal break scheduled at [time]. By signing this schedule the employee acknowledges the opportunity was offered." This puts the burden of refusing or working through onto the employee, not the employer.

What goes on a California wage statement?

Cal. Lab. Code § 226(a) requires every California wage statement (paystub) to show 9 specific items on every pay period: gross wages, total hours worked, all piece-rate units and rates, all deductions, net wages, pay period dates, the employee’s name and ID, the employer’s full legal name and address, and all applicable hourly rates with corresponding hours worked at each rate. Missing any single item triggers $50 in statutory damages for the first violation and $100 per pay period for each subsequent violation up to $4,000 per employee, plus attorney’s fees and costs.

Three patterns produce most California wage-statement liability:

  • Multi-rate omissions. Item 9 trips up payroll systems built for single-rate hourly employees. A California employee who earned regular hours at $18.50, overtime hours at $27.75, and double-time hours at $37.00 in the same period needs all three rates and the corresponding hours-worked at each rate listed separately. Lumping them into a single "regular wages" line violates § 226(a)(9).
  • Wrong employer name. Item 8 requires the legal name, not the trade name or DBA. A statement showing "California Burrito Inc." when the legal entity is "CalBurrito LLC" is a per-pay-period violation, even though the trade name is what the employee knows. Payroll has to be configured with the registered legal entity name.
  • No accrued sick leave balance shown. Cal. Lab. Code § 246(i) layers a separate requirement: every wage statement (or a separate document issued at the same time) must show accrued paid sick leave available. This is on top of the § 226(a) items, not a substitute for any of them.

Why the statutory damages compound

California pays statutory damages even when the employee suffered no actual harm. The violation itself triggers the $50/$100 penalty. A 30-employee workforce missing a single § 226(a) item across 24 bi-monthly pay periods over a year produces 30 × (50 + 23 × 100) = $70,500 in penalties before attorney’s fees, which are mandatory under § 226(e). Add in PAGA exposure (§ 2698 et seq.) and the same defect across the same workforce can multiply into a six-figure settlement. The fix is one-time payroll configuration, not eight figures of litigation. Source: Cal. Lab. Code § 226; California DLSE Wage Statement FAQ (dir.ca.gov/dlse/FAQ_Paystubs.htm). Accessed 26 May 2026.

How often must California employers pay wages?

Under Cal. Lab. Code § 204, California employers must pay wages at least twice per calendar month (semi-monthly), on regular established paydays. Work performed between the 1st and 15th of any month must be paid between the 16th and 26th of the same month. Work performed between the 16th and last day must be paid between the 1st and 10th of the following month. Weekly and bi-weekly schedules are also permitted; some industries (notably agriculture and entertainment) have more frequent requirements.

Three practical rules:

  • Monthly pay schedules don’t satisfy § 204. A "one paycheque per month" model is non-compliant for all non-exempt California employees. Exempt salaried employees can be paid monthly under § 204(c), but only if the regular payday is set on or before the 26th of the month and the full month’s salary is paid by that date.
  • Bi-weekly is fine; semi-monthly is the floor. A bi-weekly (every 14 days) schedule is the most common California payroll cadence and exceeds the § 204 minimum. Both bi-weekly and semi-monthly cadences trigger 24-26 payroll runs per year, which interlocks with the deposit-threshold cadence on Form DE 88 once California PIT withholding crosses $350 cumulative per quarter.
  • Final-pay rules are stricter than ongoing-pay rules. Cal. Lab. Code §§ 201-203 govern final wages: an employee fired or laid off is owed all wages immediately upon termination; an employee who quits with 72+ hours’ notice is owed final wages on the last day; an employee who quits with less notice is owed final wages within 72 hours. Late final-pay creates "waiting-time penalties" of up to 30 days of wages under § 203, regardless of the size of the missed amount. Final-pay handling is covered in detail on the California termination page.

What is PAGA and why does it matter for California wage-and-hour?

The Private Attorneys General Act (Cal. Lab. Code § 2698 et seq., PAGA) lets a California employee sue the employer on behalf of the State of California for Labor Code violations, recovering civil penalties that would otherwise be assessed by the Labor and Workforce Development Agency (LWDA). Default penalties are $100 per employee per pay period for initial violations and $200 per employee per pay period for subsequent violations. The State takes 65% of recovered penalties; the employee plaintiff and their counsel take 35%. PAGA claims are not waivable by arbitration agreements (the US Supreme Court’s 2022 Viking River Cruises decision partially limited PAGA, but the California Supreme Court’s Adolph v. Uber response restored most of the practical impact).

Why PAGA changes the calculus on every wage-and-hour decision

In a 50-employee California workforce on bi-weekly pay (26 pay periods per year) with a single recurring wage-statement defect, the unreformed PAGA exposure is 50 × (1 × 100 + 25 × 200) = $255,000 per year before LWDA’s share, attorney’s fees, costs, and the underlying § 226(e) statutory damages. With the 15% "reasonable steps" cap from AB 2288, the same exposure drops to roughly $38,250 before the same add-ons. The cap is the reason California employers now treat wage-and-hour compliance as a budgeted ongoing function, not an annual review item. How should multi-state employers handle California wage-and-hour law?

Treat California as a separate compliance regime, not a state-level overlay on federal rules. Apply the highest applicable standard for each employee, by employee work location, with California-specific configuration on every payroll engine, every employee handbook, and every offer letter. A single national policy that defaults to federal floors will create California wage-and-hour and PAGA exposure on every California employee, every pay period, for the duration of employment.

Five operational rules for multi-state US employers with California headcount:

  1. Configure payroll for all four California overtime triggers per employee. A US-wide payroll provider has to be told that each California employee gets daily-8-hour, daily-12-hour-double-time, weekly-40-hour, and 7th-consecutive-day premiums calculated in parallel with anti-pyramiding. The default in most multi-state systems is weekly-only; flipping the California flag is a one-time setup that prevents 12 months of missed-premium liability.
  2. Maintain a California-specific section in the employee handbook. Document meal-break offer process, rest-break schedule, premium-pay treatment for missed breaks, alternative workweek schedules if applicable, and the wage-statement nine-item disclosure. California-specific addendum, not a footnote in the national handbook.
  3. Tag work location to the city, not just the state. A California employee in West Hollywood follows the $20.22 minimum; the same employee in unincorporated LA County follows the $17.81 unincorporated minimum; the same employee remote in Berkeley follows the $19.18 Berkeley minimum. Payroll has to know the city, not just California.
  4. Set the exempt salary band at the California floor for any role with California headcount. $68,640 per year minimum for full-time exempt in 2026, scaling with the state minimum each January. Below that number, the employee is non-exempt regardless of duties, owed overtime on every shift over 8 hours and every 7th-day stretch. Many multi-state employers set a single national exempt-minimum band at the California floor to simplify the matrix.
  5. Audit wage statements quarterly, not annually. The § 226(a) nine-item list compounds at $100 per pay period; a 6-month audit cycle catches drift before PAGA liability accumulates. Build the audit into the payroll calendar, document the audit, and the documentation seeds the "reasonable steps" defence under AB 2288.

For most early-stage US employers, the practical move is a single national handbook that defaults to the strictest applicable state for any benefit, plus a California-specific addendum that covers all four overtime triggers, the meal-and-rest-break premium, the § 226 wage-statement disclosure, and the PAGA "reasonable steps" compliance language. California’s addendum is long because the surface area is large; a 2-page summary covers the operational rules and references the source statutes for any audit follow-up. A client schedules a perfectly normal 8-hour retail shift with the lunch break at 1:30 thinking they’ve given a full 30 minutes off the clock, and because the shift started at 8:00 they’ve put the break past the end of the 5th hour. One hour of premium pay owed. Repeat that across a 40-employee floor for a year and the audit number gets ugly fast. We’ll either fix the shift template at onboarding or run premium-pay automation that pays it cleanly on the next paycheque. Either is fine. Ignoring it is the expensive option.
A note from Tom Price-Daniel

California has four overtime triggers, a 30-minute meal break before the 5th hour, and PAGA hanging over every payroll defect.
Get all four triggers live in payroll, schedule the meal break before hour 5:00, audit wage statements quarterly, and document the audit.
That covers 95 percent of the wage-and-hour risk in this state.
