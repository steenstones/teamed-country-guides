How does California wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

California is the most wage-and-hour-heavy state in the United States. Cal Lab Code section 510 layers daily overtime over 8 hours, double-time over 12 hours, and a separate 7th-consecutive-day rule on top of the federal 40-hour week. Cal Lab Code section 226.7 then charges an extra hour of pay every time a meal or rest break is missed. Four ways the same shift can owe premium, before PAGA exposure gets added on top.

California has the toughest wage rules in the US. It also has the biggest bill if you get them wrong.

The state minimum is $16.50 an hour from January 2026. Some cities go higher. Some industries go higher still.

The bigger trap is the overtime maths. After 8 hours in a day, you pay time-and-a-half. After 12, you pay double. The seventh day in a row gets a premium too.

Miss a meal break and you owe an extra hour of pay. Miss a wage-statement line and one employee can sue on behalf of the state. The penalty runs $100 to $200 per pay period per violation, multiplied across your whole workforce. That is how a single payroll mistake becomes an eight-figure settlement.

What is California’s minimum wage in 2026?

California’s state minimum is $16.50 an hour from 1 January 2026. The rate goes up every year with inflation.

Two industries pay more. Fast-food workers earn at least $20 an hour. Healthcare workers earn $18 to $25, depending on the size of the facility.

California cities can set their own higher minimums. West Hollywood is the highest in the US at $20.22 an hour.

Three things to know:

  • Two rate changes a year. The state minimum changes on 1 January. Most California cities change on 1 July. Your payroll has to know both. A San Francisco employee gets the San Francisco rate, not the state rate, from 1 July onwards.
  • No tip credit. Tipped workers get the full minimum wage in cash, before any tips. Tips top up; they don’t substitute. California is one of seven states that ban the tip credit (with Alaska, Oregon, Washington, Nevada, Minnesota, and Montana).
  • The highest rate always wins. An employee working in West Hollywood gets $20.22 an hour. The same employee working from home in Berkeley gets $19.18. Payroll has to track where each shift was actually worked.

The salary line for exempt employees

To classify an employee as exempt (salary only, no overtime), you have to pay at least twice the state minimum wage for a full-time role. In 2026 that’s $68,640 a year.

The federal floor is $35,568, but in California the higher number wins. If you pay a "manager" $50,000 and call them exempt, you’re wrong. They’re owed overtime on every shift over 8 hours, and the seventh-day premium on every consecutive seven-day stretch.

If you run a single national pay band that sits below $68,640, every California hire in that band is a problem.

When does a California employer have to pay overtime?

California has four overtime triggers, not one.

Time-and-a-half kicks in after 8 hours in a day, after 40 in a week, and on the first 8 hours of the seventh consecutive workday.

Double-time kicks in after 12 hours in a day, or above 8 hours on day seven.

Alaska also mandates daily overtime, but California is the only state with double-time and a seventh-day premium.

The seventh-day rule catches every US payroll team new to California. An employee who works seven days in a row is owed a premium on the seventh day, no matter how many hours they worked that week.

You can’t fix it by rescheduling after the fact. The trigger is the actual work, not the schedule.

A retail manager who works Monday through Sunday in a busy holiday week is owed a full premium day’s pay for Sunday. Combine the seventh-day rule with the 12-hour double-time trigger, and an over-scheduled employee can earn the equivalent of two-and-a-half days’ pay in one Sunday shift.

Four triggers, one shift

Take an employee scheduled seven days in a row, with a 14-hour shift on day seven. The overtime adds up like this:

  • Hours 1–8: time-and-a-half (the seventh-day premium).
  • Hours 9–12: double-time (over 8 hours on the seventh day).
  • Hours 13–14: still double-time (over 12 hours in a day). The higher rule applies.
  • The same hours also count toward the 40-hour weekly trigger, but the rules don’t stack. The employee gets paid once per hour, at the highest applicable rate.

Every California-based hourly employee needs payroll that runs all four triggers at once and applies the highest rate per hour. Most national payroll systems default to weekly-only. Flipping the California flag at hire time is a one-minute job that prevents 12 months of missed premium.

Compressed workweeks

You can replace the daily 8-hour trigger with a longer day, but only by following a specific process. A 4-day, 10-hour-per-day schedule, or a 3-day, 12-hour schedule, is allowed if:

  • The schedule is proposed in writing to the affected work unit.
  • It’s approved by a two-thirds secret-ballot vote of those employees.
  • It’s reported to the Department of Industrial Relations.

Daily overtime then kicks in only above the scheduled hours (10 or 12). The weekly 40-hour rule and the seventh-day rule still apply. Teamed runs this process for clients on compressed schedules.

How do California meal and rest breaks work?

Any shift over 5 hours gets a 30-minute unpaid meal break. It must start before the end of the 5th hour.

Any shift over 10 hours gets a second 30-minute meal break.

On top of meals, employees get a 10-minute paid rest break for every 4 hours worked.

Miss either type of break and you owe one extra hour of pay at the regular rate, per missed break, per day. A shift with one missed meal and one missed rest break costs two extra hours.

The 5th-hour rule, in practice

The meal break has to start before the employee crosses the 5-hour mark. A shift that begins at 9:00 has to give the meal break by 1:59. Schedule it at 2:30 and you owe the premium hour, even if the employee took a full 30 minutes later.

Most California payroll trouble starts here. A manager defers the break because the shift gets busy. The employee works through. A year later a complaint cites the whole restaurant’s timesheet history for matching violations.

The premium rate isn’t just the hourly rate

The California Supreme Court ruled in Ferra v. Loews Hollywood Hotel (2021) that the "regular rate" for missed-break premiums includes non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, and commissions.

A $20-an-hour line cook earning a $200 weekend bonus and missing one meal break that week is owed a premium of more than $20. The regular rate for that week is higher.

The arithmetic is small on one shift. Compound it across a four-year look-back and California meal-break cases get expensive fast.

Offer the break. Document it.

Your obligation is to offer the meal break on time, not to force the employee off the floor. The Brinker case (2012) set the standard: relieve the employee of duty, give a reasonable chance to take 30 minutes, and don’t discourage them from taking it.

Employees can waive a properly-offered break by working through, with signed paperwork. But the burden of proving the offer was made falls on you.

Three operational rules cover most of the risk:

  • Schedule the break before the 5th hour. Put it in the handbook and on the shift schedule.
  • Only auto-deduct for a full 30 minutes off the clock. An interrupted meal break (an employee asked to take a customer order during lunch) reverts to paid time and may trigger the premium.
  • Print the offer on the schedule. Add a one-line acknowledgement: "Meal break scheduled at [time]." When the employee signs the schedule, they’ve acknowledged the offer.

What goes on a California wage statement?

Every California wage statement (paystub) has to show nine specific items: gross wages, total hours, piece-rate units and rates, deductions, net wages, pay period dates, employee name and ID, the employer’s full legal name and address, and every hourly rate with hours worked at each rate.

Miss any single item and you owe $50 in damages for the first violation, then $100 per pay period, up to $4,000 per employee, plus the employee’s legal fees.

Three patterns produce most California wage-statement liability:

  • Multi-rate problems. Item 9 trips up payroll systems built for single-rate workers. An employee who earned regular hours at $18.50, overtime at $27.75, and double-time at $37.00 in the same period needs all three rates and the matching hours listed separately. Lumping them into one "regular wages" line is a violation.
  • Wrong employer name. Item 8 requires the legal name, not the trade name or DBA. A statement showing "California Burrito Inc." when the legal entity is "CalBurrito LLC" is a per-pay-period violation, even though the trade name is what the employee knows. Payroll has to be configured with the registered legal entity name.
  • No accrued sick leave balance shown. Every wage statement (or a separate document issued the same day) has to show the accrued paid sick leave available. This is on top of the nine items, not one of them.

Why the statutory damages compound

California pays damages even when the employee suffered no actual harm. The violation itself triggers the penalty.

Take a 30-employee workforce missing one wage-statement item across 24 pay periods in a year. The penalty maths: 30 × (50 + 23 × 100) = $70,500, before legal fees (which are mandatory). Add PAGA on top and the same defect can become a six-figure settlement.

The fix is one-time payroll configuration. The cost is eight figures of litigation if you skip it.

How often must California employers pay wages?

California employers have to pay wages at least twice a month, on set paydays.

Work from the 1st to the 15th has to be paid between the 16th and the 26th of the same month. Work from the 16th to month-end has to be paid between the 1st and the 10th of the next month.

Weekly and bi-weekly schedules are also fine. A few industries (agriculture, entertainment) have stricter requirements.

Three rules:

  • Monthly is not enough. A "one paycheque per month" model is illegal for non-exempt California employees. Exempt salaried employees can be paid monthly, but only if the payday is on or before the 26th and the full month is paid by then.
  • Bi-weekly is the most common cadence. Every 14 days, 26 payrolls a year. It clears the twice-a-month rule and lines up well with California’s tax deposit thresholds.
  • Final-pay rules are stricter than ongoing pay. An employee fired or laid off is owed everything immediately. An employee who quits with 72 hours’ notice is owed everything on their last day. An employee who quits with less notice is owed everything within 72 hours. Late final pay creates "waiting-time penalties" of up to 30 days of wages, no matter how small the amount you missed.

What is PAGA and why does it matter for California wage-and-hour?

PAGA stands for the Private Attorneys General Act. It lets one California employee sue the employer on behalf of the state for any Labor Code violation.

Default penalties are $100 per employee per pay period for the first violation, $200 per pay period after that. The state takes 65% of what’s recovered. The employee and their lawyer take 35%.

PAGA can’t be waived by an arbitration clause. The US Supreme Court tried to limit it in 2022, but the California Supreme Court found a way to restore most of the original bite a year later.

The 2024 PAGA reform tilted the playing field back toward employers who act fast.

If you can show you took "all reasonable steps" to comply before the PAGA notice landed, the penalty caps at 15% of the default. If you cure the problem within 60 days of getting the notice, the cap is 30%.

Both caps depend on an actual compliance programme: written policy, training, regular audits, documented responses to prior complaints. A boilerplate handbook with no paper trail won’t qualify.

Why PAGA changes everything

Take a 50-employee California workforce on bi-weekly pay (26 pay periods a year) with one recurring wage-statement defect.

Without the reform: 50 × (100 + 25 × 200) = $255,000 a year, before the state’s 65% cut, legal fees, and the underlying statutory damages.

With the 15% "reasonable steps" cap: about $38,250 before the same add-ons.

That difference is why California employers now treat wage-and-hour as a budgeted ongoing function, not an annual review. How should multi-state employers handle California wage-and-hour law?

Treat California as its own country, not a state-level overlay on federal rules.

Apply the highest standard per employee, by where they work. Configure California specifics into every payroll engine, handbook, and offer letter.

A single national policy that defaults to federal floors creates wage-and-hour and PAGA exposure on every California employee, every pay period, for the whole time they’re employed.

Five things to get right:

  1. Set payroll to run all four California overtime triggers per employee. Daily 8-hour, daily 12-hour double-time, weekly 40-hour, and seventh-day. Most US payroll systems default to weekly-only. Flipping the California flag is a one-minute job that prevents 12 months of missed premium.
  2. Add a California section to your employee handbook. Cover the meal-break offer process, the rest-break schedule, premium pay for missed breaks, any compressed workweek schedules, and the nine-item wage statement. Treat it as a proper addendum, not a footnote.
  3. Tag work location to the city, not just the state. An employee in West Hollywood gets $20.22. The same employee in unincorporated LA County gets $17.81. The same employee remote in Berkeley gets $19.18. Payroll has to know the city.
  4. Set the exempt salary band at the California floor. $68,640 a year for full-time exempt in 2026, going up each January with the state minimum. Below that number, the employee is non-exempt no matter what their title says. Many multi-state employers set the California floor as their national minimum to simplify the matrix.
  5. Audit wage statements quarterly, not annually. The penalty compounds at $100 per pay period. A quarterly audit catches drift before the PAGA liability piles up. Build it into the payroll calendar, document it, and the paper trail seeds your "reasonable steps" defence.

For most early-stage US employers, the cleanest move is a single national handbook that defaults to the strictest state for any benefit, plus a California addendum that covers all the points above.
