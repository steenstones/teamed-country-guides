What paid family and sick leave does California require in 2026?

Four stacked programmes apply: 40 hours Paid Sick Leave, 8 weeks wage-replacement PFL, 12 weeks CFRA job protection, and up to 4 months Pregnancy Disability Leave. CFRA and PDL kick in at 5 employees, not 50.

California has the deepest paid-leave statute stack in the United States. Four programmes apply on top of federal FMLA: Paid Sick Leave (PSL) at 40 hours per year, accrued at 1 hour per 30 worked, covering every employer regardless of size (Cal Lab Code §§ 245-249, as amended by SB 616 effective 1 January 2024); Paid Family Leave (PFL) giving up to 8 weeks of SDI-funded wage replacement at 70-90% of wages, capped at $1,765 per week in 2026 (Cal Unemp Ins Code § 3300 et seq., funded by the 1.3% employee SDI deduction on uncapped wages); the California Family Rights Act (CFRA) at 12 weeks of job-protected leave for employers with 5 or more employees, not the 50 of federal FMLA (Cal Gov Code § 12945.2, expanded by SB 1383 effective 1 January 2021); and Pregnancy Disability Leave (PDL) at up to 4 months per pregnancy, again at the 5-employee threshold (Cal Gov Code § 12945). Local ordinances in San Francisco, Los Angeles, Oakland, Berkeley, San Diego, and Emeryville add more sick leave on top — employer applies whichever rule is most generous. Multi-state employers underestimate California because their FMLA mental model assumes the 50-employee threshold; CFRA and PDL flip the calculation at hire number five.

Which paid-leave laws apply to California employees in 2026?

Four California statutes apply on top of federal law: PSL (sick), PFL (family wage replacement), CFRA (job protection), and PDL (pregnancy disability). A single California pregnancy can trigger all four at once. The compliance question is not which law applies; it is how they stack and which threshold flips when.

Federal FMLA still applies at 50+ employees and gives 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave. California then layers four separate statutes on top, each with its own threshold, duration, eligibility test, and funding mechanism. A multi-state employer who only ships an FMLA policy into California is short on every dimension that matters: too few employer thresholds tracked, no wage replacement assumed where state law guarantees it, and no pregnancy disability framework at all.

The headline mistake we see at Teamed is the 50-employee assumption. An employer with 5 to 49 California employees has no federal FMLA obligation but a full CFRA and PDL obligation. A 7-person Bay Area engineering team is operating under the same job-protection regime as a 700-person enterprise. Once you cross the 5-employee threshold in California, every pregnancy and every serious health condition becomes a statutory case to administer, not an HR policy choice.

How much paid sick leave does California require in 2026?

40 hours or 5 days per year, accrued at 1 hour per 30 hours worked. SB 616 raised the floor from 24 hours to 40 hours effective 1 January 2024. Every California employer must comply once an employee works 30 days in the state in a year; there is no headcount threshold.

The Healthy Workplaces Healthy Families Act of 2014 (Cal Lab Code §§ 245-249), as amended by SB 616, sets the framework. Employees accrue at one hour of paid sick leave per 30 hours worked. Employers may use an alternative accrual schedule, but it must deliver at least 24 hours by day 120 of employment and 40 hours by day 200 — the safe harbour is to use 1-per-30 from day one.

Caps, carryover, and front-loading

  • Accrual cap: 80 hours or 10 days — once an employee has banked 80 hours, accrual pauses until they use some.
  • Annual use cap: 40 hours or 5 days per benefit year — an employee cannot consume more than 40 hours in a 12-month window even if they have banked more.
  • Carryover: unused hours carry over year to year up to the 80-hour accrual cap. Employers cannot zero out the balance at year-end.
  • Front-loading alternative: employer may grant 40 hours up front at the start of each year and skip accrual tracking. Front-loaded leave does not have to carry over — this is the simpler administration path.
  • Pay-out at separation: California does not require pay-out of unused PSL at termination (unlike vacation under Cal Lab Code § 227.3). Unused PSL is forfeited, unless employer policy or PTO bank rules say otherwise.

What PSL covers

  • Employee’s own diagnosis, care, treatment, or preventive care
  • Care for a family member — California’s definition is broad: parent, child, spouse, registered domestic partner, grandparent, grandchild, sibling, and (since AB 1041, effective 1 January 2023) a designated person the employee identifies at the time of leave
  • Qualifying acts of violence: domestic violence, sexual assault, stalking, and (effective 1 January 2025) injury, death, or threat of either to the employee or a family member — employers with 25+ employees
  • Jury duty and court appearances for protected matters (effective 1 January 2025)
  • Agricultural employees: smoke, heat, or flooding conditions during declared emergencies

PTO bank as substitute

An employer may use a single PTO plan covering vacation, sick, and personal time in place of separate PSL, provided the PTO plan meets or exceeds every statutory minimum: accrual at 1-per-30 or front-load at 40 hours, 80-hour cap, no use restrictions beyond what PSL permits. The PTO bank approach simplifies tracking but loses the California-specific carve-out that PSL is not paid out at separation — PTO under § 227.3 is wages and must be paid out, so a combined bank converts forfeitable sick hours into payable wages on every exit. Most employers separate them for this reason.

How does California Paid Family Leave (PFL) work in 2026?

PFL gives eligible employees up to 8 weeks of wage replacement at 70 to 90 percent of regular wages, capped at $1,765 per week in 2026. It is a state insurance benefit funded by the 1.3% employee SDI deduction, administered by the EDD. PFL does not provide job protection — that comes from CFRA, FMLA, or PDL stacked on top.

Cal Unemp Ins Code § 3300 et seq. created Paid Family Leave in 2002, the first state PFL programme in the United States. Twenty-four years on, it is the most-used: 8 weeks of partial wage replacement when an employee needs to bond with a new child, care for a seriously ill family member, or handle qualifying military exigency. The benefit is paid by the State Disability Insurance fund, not the employer, which means employer payroll cash flow is undisturbed when an employee goes on PFL.

Wage-replacement rate, 2026

SB 951 (Stats. 2022, ch. 878) restructured the wage-replacement formula effective 1 January 2025. Lower earners now receive up to 90 percent of their regular weekly wage; higher earners receive 70 percent, subject to the weekly maximum. The formula is progressive: an employee earning at or below one-third of the State Average Weekly Wage gets 90 percent; an employee earning above gets 70 percent; the benefit is capped at the maximum weekly amount regardless of how much higher the wage runs.

Eligibility

An employee qualifies for PFL if they have earned at least $300 in wages with SDI contributions during the 18-month base period before filing. Most W-2 California workers clear this on their first paycheck. Self-employed individuals can opt in to SDI separately (Disability Insurance Elective Coverage). PFL benefits are available in increments — an employee can take 8 weeks in one block or split it across the 12-month window.

Three claim types

  • Bonding — new child by birth, adoption, or placement in care, within 12 months of arrival
  • Care — for a seriously ill family member (broad California definition: spouse, registered domestic partner, child, parent, parent-in-law, grandparent, grandchild, sibling, and, since AB 1041, a designated person)
  • Military assist — qualifying exigency for a family member on active duty or call to active duty

The job-protection gap

PFL pays the wages. PFL does not hold the job. An employee who takes 8 weeks of PFL bonding leave at an employer with 4 California employees has no CFRA right (5-employee threshold), no FMLA right (50-employee threshold), and no PDL right (different statute). The employer may legally fill the role during the leave and decline to reinstate the employee on return. This is the gap candidates do not realise exists until they need it; it is the gap that competitive parental-leave policies are designed to close.

What is the California Family Rights Act (CFRA) and why does the 5-employee threshold matter?

CFRA gives eligible employees 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period for the same family-care reasons as federal FMLA, but at the 5-employee threshold — ten times lower than FMLA’s 50. SB 1383 dropped the threshold from 50 to 5 effective 1 January 2021. Employers with 5 to 49 employees in California carry full job-protection obligations that do not exist under federal law.

Cal Gov Code § 12945.2 is the operative statute. It tracks federal FMLA on the basic mechanics — 12 weeks per 12-month period, 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked in the preceding year for eligibility, group health insurance continued at employer’s normal contribution — and then diverges in three ways that catch multi-state employers off guard.

Three CFRA divergences from federal FMLA

  1. Employer threshold of 5, not 50. SB 1383 (Stats. 2020, ch. 86) expanded CFRA to employers with 5 or more employees effective 1 January 2021. State and local government employers are covered at any size. The vast majority of California small businesses crossed into CFRA coverage on that date without realising it, and many still operate on outdated FMLA mental models.
  2. Broader family definition. CFRA covers care for a parent, child, spouse, registered domestic partner, parent-in-law, grandparent, grandchild, sibling, and (since AB 1041, effective 1 January 2023) a designated person the employee identifies. Federal FMLA covers only parent, child, and spouse — CFRA reaches further into actual family structures.
  3. CFRA and FMLA run concurrently when both apply, but separately when one does not. An employer with 75 employees gives 12 weeks under both laws, used concurrently — the employee gets 12 weeks total, not 24. An employer with 10 California employees gives 12 weeks under CFRA alone (no FMLA), and the employee still gets 12 weeks — the employer just cannot rely on FMLA at all.

The PDL-then-CFRA stacking case

For pregnancy, PDL and CFRA do not run concurrently — they stack. An employee disabled by pregnancy takes up to 4 months of PDL for the disability itself (the medically certified pregnancy and recovery period). When the PDL period ends, she may then take up to 12 weeks of CFRA for baby bonding. The total protected leave for a single pregnancy can therefore reach roughly 7 months at an employer with 5+ California employees. Federal FMLA, by contrast, gives the same employee a single 12-week block (covering both the disability and the bonding combined) at employers with 50+ employees.

Paired with PFL for wage replacement

CFRA itself is unpaid; an employee on CFRA leave typically files a PFL claim with EDD to receive the 70-90% wage replacement during the same period. CFRA holds the job; PFL pays the wages; the two are designed to work together. The employer’s payroll obligation during CFRA is health-insurance continuation only — the state insurance fund handles the wage replacement, so cash-flow impact is minimal.

Source: Cal Gov Code § 12945.2 (leginfo.legislature.ca.gov); SB 1383 (Stats. 2020, ch. 86); AB 1041 (Stats. 2022, ch. 748); 2 Cal Code Regs § 11087 (CFRA implementing regulations). Accessed 26 May 2026.

What is California Pregnancy Disability Leave (PDL)?

PDL gives an employee who is disabled by pregnancy, childbirth, or a related medical condition up to 4 months of job-protected leave per pregnancy. It applies to employers with 5 or more employees and has no minimum-service eligibility. The employer must continue paying group health coverage at the same level throughout.

Cal Gov Code § 12945 and the implementing regulation at 2 Cal Code Regs § 11042 create PDL as a separate statute from CFRA. It is not a parental-leave programme — it is a disability-leave programme tied to the medically certified period during which the employee is unable to perform job functions because of pregnancy or a related condition. Severe morning sickness, gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, bed rest, postpartum recovery, and any other certified disability period all count.

Four key features

  • Up to 4 months (approximately 17.33 weeks at a 40-hour week) per pregnancy. The duration runs with the medical certification — the employee gets the time they are medically disabled, up to the 4-month cap.
  • No tenure or hours-worked requirement. An employee on day three of the job is eligible for PDL if she becomes disabled by pregnancy. Unique among California leave statutes.
  • 5-employee threshold — same as CFRA, dramatically lower than federal FMLA.
  • Mandatory health-insurance continuation. The employer must maintain and pay for group health coverage for the duration of PDL (up to 4 months) at the same level and conditions as if the employee were working. This is a stricter rule than CFRA, which only requires continuation at the employer’s normal contribution.

PDL pairs with SDI for wage replacement

PDL itself is unpaid leave. The employee typically files for State Disability Insurance (DI) benefits during the PDL period — SDI provides wage replacement at the same 70-90% formula as PFL, capped at the same $1,765 weekly maximum in 2026. SDI for pregnancy disability is funded by the same 1.3% employee deduction that funds PFL. Effectively: the employer holds the job (PDL), the state pays the wages (SDI), and the employee uses health benefits at the employer’s expense throughout.

Reinstatement

On return from PDL, the employer must reinstate the employee to the same position, or to a comparable position if the same role no longer exists for legitimate, non-PDL business reasons (e.g. a layoff that would have eliminated her position regardless). The employer bears the burden of showing the non-PDL reason — refusal to reinstate is presumed unlawful and the employee can sue under the Fair Employment and Housing Act for back pay, reinstatement, and punitive damages.

Source: Cal Gov Code § 12945; 2 Cal Code Regs § 11042 (Pregnancy Disability Leave); California Civil Rights Department (CRD) pregnancy and PDL guidance. Accessed 26 May 2026.

Which California cities require more paid sick leave than the state?

At least six California cities — San Francisco, Los Angeles, Oakland, Berkeley, San Diego, and Emeryville — have their own paid sick leave ordinances that exceed the state’s 40-hour floor. The employer must apply whichever rule is most generous to the employee on each specific point.

State law preempts local rules in a small set of administrative areas (paystub format, calculation methodology, notice timing, separation pay-out), but on the substantive question — how much leave the employee gets and how fast they accrue it — the local rule wins if it gives more. A San Francisco employer who runs a state-compliant 40-hour PSL plan is non-compliant with the SF Paid Sick Leave Ordinance, which sets a higher accrual cap.

The most-generous test in practice

An employee working in Los Angeles for a 200-employee national employer with a 40-hour-per-year sick leave policy is short on three dimensions: the LA annual use cap is 48 hours, not 40; the LA accrual cap is 72 hours, not the state’s 80 (but the state’s higher cap wins on this point); and the LA notice requirements differ from the state’s. Compliance requires layering the state, local, and federal rules and applying the employee-favourable answer on each line. This is why most California employers with multi-city workforces administer paid sick leave as a tiered policy by work location, not a single national policy.

Other jurisdictions to watch: Long Beach, Santa Monica, and the unincorporated areas of LA County all maintain their own sick-leave rules tied to specific industries (hotels, fast food). Sacramento, San Jose, and the broader Bay Area cities do not currently have their own ordinances — state law applies.
