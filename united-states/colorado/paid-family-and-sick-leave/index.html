What paid family and sick leave does Colorado require in 2026?

Two state statutes stack on top of federal FMLA: HFWA gives 48 hours paid sick leave per year (plus 80 hours during a public health emergency), and FAMLI gives 12 weeks paid family or medical leave (16 weeks if pregnancy or birth complications) at up to 90 percent wage replacement. Both apply at every employer, regardless of size.

Colorado runs two state-mandated paid-leave programmes on top of the federal FMLA floor. The Healthy Families and Workplaces Act (HFWA) (CRS § 8-13.3-401 et seq.) gives every Colorado employee 48 hours of paid sick leave per year, accrued at 1 hour per 30 hours worked, applicable to every employer regardless of size. During a declared public health emergency, HFWA grants an additional 80 hours of supplemental paid sick leave on top of the 48-hour bank. Colorado FAMLI (Family and Medical Leave Insurance, CRS § 8-13.3-501 et seq.), in force since 1 January 2024, gives eligible employees up to 12 weeks of paid family or medical leave per year, extending to 16 weeks for pregnancy or childbirth complications, at up to 90 percent wage replacement for lower earners scaling down for higher earners. The FAMLI 2026 premium is 0.88 percent of wages split 0.44 / 0.44 between employer and employee, with employers of fewer than 10 workers exempt from the employer share. FAMLI’s job-protection right only attaches after 180 days of employment; the wage-replacement benefit attaches earlier. Federal FMLA still applies at 50-plus employees and runs concurrently with FAMLI when both cover the same absence.

Which paid-leave laws apply to Colorado employees in 2026?

Three statutes apply: HFWA (paid sick), FAMLI (paid family and medical), and federal FMLA at 50-plus employees. A single Colorado pregnancy can trigger all three at once. The compliance question is not which law applies; it is how they stack and which employer-headcount threshold flips when.

Colorado is one of roughly 14 US states with both a mandated state-level paid sick leave statute and a state-mandated paid family and medical leave insurance programme on the books. HFWA covers every employer of any size; FAMLI covers every Colorado worker (with a $2,500 base-period earnings test) and is funded by a payroll premium that every employer collects and most employers split. Federal FMLA continues to apply at 50-plus employees and gives 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave for the federal qualifying reasons. A multi-state employer who only ships a generic FMLA policy into Colorado is short on three dimensions: no paid sick leave bank, no wage-replacement framework for family and medical leave, and no familiarity with the FAMLI 180-day job-protection threshold that creates a tenure trap for early-tenure employees.

The two state statutes do different work. HFWA covers the short, predictable absences — an employee’s flu, a child’s ear infection, a preventive-care appointment. FAMLI covers the longer, life-event absences — bonding with a new child, caring for a seriously ill family member, an employee’s own serious health condition. They are designed to stack, not to overlap. A new parent uses HFWA for the prenatal appointments and FAMLI for the post-birth bonding leave; an employee with a cancer diagnosis uses HFWA for the first chemotherapy half-day and FAMLI for the recovery weeks that follow.

How much paid sick leave does HFWA require in 2026?

48 hours per year, accrued at 1 hour per 30 hours worked, plus 80 supplemental hours during a declared public health emergency. HFWA applies to every Colorado employer regardless of size — there is no headcount carve-out. The first 48-hour bank is universal; the 80-hour PHE bank activates only when state or local authorities formally declare a public health emergency.

The Healthy Families and Workplaces Act (CRS § 8-13.3-401 et seq.), enacted 2020 and fully effective since 1 January 2022, sets the framework. Employees accrue one hour of paid sick leave per 30 hours worked, capped at 48 hours per benefit year. The accrual rule is the floor; an employer may grant the full 48 hours up front at the start of each benefit year and skip accrual tracking, which is the cleaner administration path for salaried workforces.

Caps, carryover, and the PHE 80-hour bonus

  • Annual accrual cap: 48 hours per benefit year. Once an employee has banked 48 hours, accrual pauses until they use some.
  • Carryover: unused hours carry over year to year, but the cap on accrued balance remains 48 hours. The employee never holds more than 48 paid sick hours at any moment under HFWA’s base programme.
  • Front-loading alternative: employer may grant 48 hours at the start of each year and skip accrual tracking. Front-loaded leave does not have to carry over — this simplifies administration but the employee’s mid-year balance can be higher than under accrual.
  • Public health emergency 80-hour bonus: when state or local authorities formally declare a public health emergency, every Colorado employee becomes immediately entitled to up to 80 additional hours of supplemental paid sick leave (40 hours for part-time employees). The supplemental bank is in addition to the 48-hour base bank, runs alongside it, and disappears four weeks after the emergency declaration ends. CRS § 8-13.3-405 sets the framework.
  • Pay-out at separation: HFWA does not require pay-out of unused paid sick leave at termination. Unused HFWA hours are forfeited unless the employer’s policy or PTO bank rules say otherwise.

What HFWA covers

  • Employee’s own mental or physical illness, injury, or health condition
  • Diagnosis, treatment, or preventive care for the employee or a family member
  • Care for a family member whose place of business or school is closed by a public official due to a public health emergency
  • Domestic abuse, sexual assault, or harassment leave — for the employee or a family member
  • Bereavement, funeral arrangements, and financial or legal affairs of a deceased family member (added 2024 amendments)
  • Care for a family member who has been ordered or recommended by a healthcare provider to quarantine or self-isolate
  • Evacuation due to an inclement-weather event, power loss, or other unexpected occurrence that results in the closure of the employee’s residence or workplace (added 2024 amendments)

Universal employer coverage

HFWA is one of the few US paid-sick-leave statutes with no headcount carve-out. A Colorado employer with a single employee is fully covered. There is no small-business exemption, no probationary-period exclusion (sick leave begins to accrue immediately on hire), and no salaried-exempt carve-out. The only ineligibility is for genuinely federal employees and certain railroad workers covered by federal labour statutes. The simplest mental model: every paying Colorado employer of any size owes every Colorado employee at least 48 paid sick hours per year.

How does Colorado FAMLI work in 2026?

FAMLI gives eligible Colorado workers up to 12 weeks of paid family or medical leave per year, extending to 16 weeks for pregnancy or birth complications, at up to 90 percent wage replacement for lower earners scaling down for higher earners. It is a state insurance benefit funded by a 0.88 percent payroll premium split between employer and employee, administered by the FAMLI Division of CDLE. Benefits have been payable since 1 January 2024.

CRS § 8-13.3-501 et seq. created FAMLI by voter initiative (Proposition 118, November 2020). Premium collection began 1 January 2023 to fund the trust; benefits became claimable on 1 January 2024. Two years on, FAMLI is the largest piece of new American paid-leave infrastructure since California PFL in 2002, and the only US programme to combine paid family leave, paid medical leave, and an embedded pregnancy-complication extension in a single statute.

The five qualifying reasons

  • Bonding — with a new child by birth, adoption, or placement in care, within 12 months of arrival
  • Family care — for a seriously ill family member (Colorado uses a broad definition: spouse, domestic partner, child, parent, grandparent, grandchild, sibling, plus any person whose close association with the employee is the equivalent of a family relationship)
  • Own serious health condition — including pregnancy and childbirth recovery, and the additional pregnancy-complication extension
  • Military exigency — qualifying need arising from a family member’s call to active military duty
  • Safe leave — for the employee or a family member subject to domestic violence, sexual assault, harassment, or stalking

Wage replacement: progressive, capped, paid by the state

FAMLI’s wage-replacement formula is progressive. An employee earning at or below 50 percent of the State Average Weekly Wage receives up to 90 percent of their average weekly wage; the rate scales down toward 50 percent for higher earners; the benefit is capped at the State Average Weekly Wage regardless of how much higher the wage runs. The cap is recalculated each year; check the FAMLI Division’s current rate notice for the 2026 maximum weekly benefit before publishing it to candidates.

Eligibility

An employee qualifies for the FAMLI wage-replacement benefit if they have earned at least $2,500 in FAMLI-covered wages in their base period (typically the four quarters preceding the quarter in which the claim is filed). Most full-time and substantial part-time Colorado workers clear this threshold within their first few months on payroll. The eligibility test for the wage-replacement benefit is separate from the job-protection threshold, which is covered in the next section.

How a FAMLI claim runs operationally

An employee files a claim through the FAMLI portal at famli.colorado.gov, providing medical or family-event documentation appropriate to the qualifying reason. The FAMLI Division adjudicates the claim and issues benefit payments directly to the employee’s bank account on a biweekly cycle. The employer’s payroll obligation during a FAMLI absence is benefit continuation only — the state insurance fund handles the wage replacement, so the employer’s cash flow during a leave is unaffected except for any voluntary employer top-up policy. The employer is notified of the claim and required to confirm the employee’s wage history and dates of employment, but does not adjudicate or deny the claim.

How does FAMLI provide job protection — and what is the 180-day gap?

FAMLI’s job-protection right attaches only after an employee has worked for the employer for 180 days. Before day 180, an eligible employee can still claim FAMLI wage-replacement benefits, but the employer is not statutorily required to reinstate them to the same position at the end of leave. This tenure trap is unique to Colorado and is the single feature most often missed by multi-state employers porting an FMLA mental model.

CRS § 8-13.3-509 sets the job-protection rule. Two tests have to be true before an employee is entitled to reinstatement at the end of a FAMLI leave: 180 days of employment with the current employer, and continuous employment for the period preceding the leave. The 180-day test is calendar days from start date, not days actually worked.

What the 180-day gap means in practice

  • Day 1 to Day 179: employee can file a FAMLI claim and receive wage-replacement benefits, but no statutory right to reinstatement to the same job at end of leave. Employer may legally fill the position, restructure the team, or decline to reinstate (subject to anti-discrimination law generally).
  • Day 180 onward: full FAMLI job-protection right attaches. Employer must restore the employee to the same or an equivalent position at end of leave; failure to do so is a statutory violation actionable through the FAMLI Division and through private right of action under CRS § 8-13.3-509(8).
  • Interaction with FMLA: at employers of 50-plus employees, federal FMLA’s job-protection right (12 months tenure / 1,250 hours) runs separately. An employee with 9 months of service at a 60-person employer has FMLA protection (assuming hours met) but not FAMLI protection until day 180 — the FAMLI wage replacement is available regardless of which job-protection track applies.

The new-hire pregnancy scenario

Hire date 1 February. Employee becomes pregnant in March. Baby due date in October. Employee gives notice of intent to take 12 weeks of FAMLI bonding leave starting at delivery. The FAMLI claim is approved (eligibility on the $2,500 base-period earnings test is clear by autumn). The job-protection test depends on what date the leave actually starts: if leave begins on day 240 (mid-October), the employee is past the 180-day threshold and reinstatement is guaranteed. If pregnancy complications trigger an earlier bedrest absence beginning on day 165, the early portion of the leave falls into the 180-day gap and reinstatement is not statutorily protected for that segment. Employers who treat FAMLI as equivalent to FMLA-with-pay miss this distinction and create avoidable risk.

Benefit continuation during FAMLI leave

An employer who maintained the employee’s group health insurance immediately before the leave must continue that coverage on the same terms (including the same employer-contribution share) for the duration of the FAMLI leave. The employee remains responsible for any employee-contribution share and must continue to pay it during the leave to keep coverage active. Failure to pay the employee share for more than 30 days can result in coverage termination, which is governed by the same rules as during active employment.

Can a Colorado employer use a private plan instead of state FAMLI?

Yes — an employer may opt out of state FAMLI by offering a CDLE-approved private plan that equals or exceeds every state-plan benefit on every dimension. The private plan must be approved annually by the FAMLI Division Director and renewed each year. The cost trade-off is real for stable workforces with predictable claim experience; the underwriting and adjudication overhead is the price.

CRS § 8-13.3-521 and 7 CCR 1107-2 (FAMLI Private Plans Rules) govern the opt-out. An employer who wants to skip the state programme must file a private-plan application with the FAMLI Division, demonstrating that the private plan’s benefits, eligibility, employee-cost share, and grievance process meet or exceed the state-plan equivalent on every line. Plans typically take the form of fully insured policies underwritten by carriers that have built FAMLI-compliant products (Aflac, MetLife, The Hartford, Unum, and several others write Colorado FAMLI private-plan policies as of 2026).

The opt-out cost trade-off

  • State plan: 0.88 percent of wages, split 0.44 / 0.44 (employer share waived <10 ee). Fixed rate, no underwriting, no claim-experience volatility, but the employer pays the full rate even if claims experience is below the state-pool average.
  • Private plan: underwritten premium based on the employer’s demographic and claims profile. For a young, healthy workforce with low expected claim volume, the underwritten rate can come in below 0.88 percent. For an older workforce or one with high expected pregnancy / serious-health-condition exposure, the underwritten rate can come in higher.
  • Administrative load: private plan requires the carrier’s claim-intake process, the employer’s coordination with the carrier for medical certification and reinstatement, and the annual reapproval filing with the FAMLI Division. State plan requires only quarterly premium remittance through My FAMLI+ Employer and confirmation of wage history when an employee files a claim.
  • Employee-experience risk: employees compare a private-plan claim experience against the state plan their friends at other Colorado employers are using. Slower or more restrictive claim adjudication on the private side becomes a recruiting friction.

When the opt-out makes sense

The private-plan route works best for larger, stable Colorado workforces (100-plus employees) with a multi-year track record of below-average claim incidence and the operational capacity to manage a carrier relationship. For smaller employers, the state plan’s simplicity and fixed rate almost always wins on total cost of administration. Multi-state employers running Colorado as one piece of a larger US payroll typically use the state plan to avoid building Colorado-specific carrier-administration tooling.

Annual reapproval discipline

Private-plan approval is annual. Each year the employer must file a renewal application demonstrating that the plan remains compliant with the current state-plan benefits (which can change as the FAMLI Division updates the maximum weekly benefit or the wage-replacement formula). Failure to renew on time pushes the employer back into the state plan and triggers premium-collection back to the date of lapsed approval. Build the renewal calendar into the payroll year early.

How do HFWA, FAMLI, and FMLA stack for a single pregnancy?

At a Colorado employer with 50-plus employees and a 12-month-tenure employee with pregnancy complications, the protected-leave stack can reach roughly seven months. HFWA covers the early appointments and acute episodes; FAMLI provides 16 weeks of paid leave for complicated pregnancy; FMLA runs concurrently with FAMLI for the federally protected portion. Knowing how the clocks layer is the difference between competitive policy and an avoidable lawsuit.

Three statutes, three eligibility tests, three duration clocks. The interaction is the compliance work.

The four phases of a complicated pregnancy in Colorado

  1. Prenatal appointments and early-pregnancy episodes (months 1-6): HFWA covers the routine prenatal-visit half-days, the early-trimester nausea episodes, the urgent-care visits. The 48-hour bank is typically enough for an uncomplicated pregnancy; it begins to feel tight in the second trimester for an employee with significant symptoms.
  2. Bedrest or hospitalisation before delivery (variable, e.g. weeks 28-40): if a healthcare provider certifies the employee as medically unable to perform job functions, the FAMLI medical-leave clock starts. For a complicated pregnancy, the 16-week FAMLI cap is the operating ceiling. At a 50-plus-employee employer, FMLA runs concurrently with FAMLI for the first 12 of those weeks, exhausting the federal leave entitlement.
  3. Delivery and immediate postpartum recovery (weeks 40-46): the medical-leave portion of FAMLI continues to run, covering the medically certified recovery period. HFWA continues to be available for episode-by-episode use as needed. The 16-week FAMLI cap may or may not be reached depending on the medical course.
  4. Bonding leave (post-recovery): if FAMLI weeks remain after medical recovery, they are available for bonding within the 12-month window from the qualifying event. If FMLA was exhausted concurrently with FAMLI medical leave, there is no separate FMLA-bonding entitlement left — the federal 12-week clock is one bank, not two. State-funded bonding through FAMLI continues to the FAMLI cap.

The maximum-stack scenario

An employer with 60 Colorado employees, an employee with 18 months of tenure, certified pregnancy complications, and the full 16-week FAMLI extension. Roughly: 48 hours of HFWA used episodically across the first two trimesters, then 16 weeks of FAMLI (12 of which run concurrently with FMLA) for medical leave and bonding combined. Total protected and largely paid leave: roughly 16 weeks of FAMLI plus the HFWA hours, against a federal FMLA backdrop that the FAMLI period exhausts in parallel. The employer’s payroll obligation during the FAMLI period is benefit continuation only; the state insurance fund pays the wages.
