How does Colorado wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

Colorado is the only US state with a 12-hour daily overtime trigger. COMPS Order 38 (effective 1 January 2026) pays 1.5x for every hour over 12 in a workday, or over 12 consecutive hours in any single shift regardless of when the shift starts. Layer on Denver’s $19.29 minimum wage, the salary-range mandate in every job posting, and a $15.16 state floor that resets each January, and the operational rules look nothing like the federal default most multi-state payroll systems ship with.

Colorado wage and hour law in 2026 sits at the top of the COMPS Order, the annual rule that the Colorado Department of Labor and Employment rewrites every December for the following calendar year. COMPS Order 38 took effect 1 January 2026 and sets a $15.16 state minimum (CPI-indexed under Colorado Constitution Art. XVIII § 15), with $12.14 tipped after the federal $3.02 tip credit. Local jurisdictions sit higher: Denver at $19.29, Edgewater at $18.17, Boulder city and Boulder County at $16.82. Overtime runs 1.5x over 40 hours in a workweek (federal FLSA), over 12 hours in a workday, OR over 12 consecutive hours in any single shift, whichever trigger gives the employee the most pay (COMPS Order 38, Rule 4.1.1). The 12-consecutive-hour rule is the unusual one: it bites across the seam of a graveyard shift even when neither workday on its own crosses the 12-hour line. Meal breaks: 30 minutes unpaid for shifts over 5 hours; rest breaks: 10 minutes paid per 4 hours worked (COMPS Order 38, Rules 5.1 and 5.2). Colorado has no California-style premium-hour penalty for missed breaks, but missed meal time reverts to paid working time. Pay frequency: at least monthly under CRS § 8-4-103; most employers run semi-monthly or bi-weekly. The Colorado Equal Pay for Equal Work Act (CRS § 8-5-201, as amended by SB 23-105) requires a salary range in every job posting plus a post-hire promotion announcement to all current employees. Different country, same surface area as California; different mechanics.

What is Colorado’s minimum wage in 2026?

Colorado’s state minimum wage in 2026 is $15.16 per hour, effective 1 January, set by Colorado Constitution Art. XVIII § 15 (annual CPI indexation) and codified for the year in COMPS Order 38. The state tipped minimum is $12.14 per hour after the federal $3.02 tip credit. Local jurisdictions pay higher, with Denver leading at $19.29 per hour ($16.27 tipped), followed by Edgewater at $18.17 and Boulder city and county at $16.82. Where an employee works determines which floor applies, not where the employer is registered.

Three rules that follow from the wage stack:

  • Work location governs, not employer registration. A remote employee living at a Denver address and reporting to a Boulder-headquartered employer earns the Denver $19.29 rate, not the Boulder $16.82 rate. Payroll tags the work-performed location on every shift. Get this wrong and the wage-claim look-back under CRS § 8-4-122 reaches two years (three if the violation was willful).
  • Annual reset on 1 January. Colorado’s constitutional CPI indexation means a new state floor takes effect every 1 January, with the rate published in the COMPS Order issued the previous December by the Colorado Department of Labor and Employment. Denver and the other local jurisdictions publish their own rates on a separate schedule. Payroll engines configured against last year’s minimum without an annual review will under-pay starting on the first January shift.
  • Tip credit is allowed (unlike California). Colorado permits the federal $3.02 tip credit, so tipped employees can be paid $12.14 cash plus tips that bring them to the applicable minimum. If tips don’t close the gap on a slow shift, the employer makes up the difference. Denver tipped employees earn $16.27 cash plus tips. The credit only applies if the employer can document that the employee was informed of the tip-credit arrangement in writing before the work started.

The local-jurisdiction patchwork is the operational pain point

Colorado is one of only a few US states where local minimum-wage ordinances meaningfully exceed the state floor (the other notable one is California). Denver’s ordinance has run ahead of the state since 2020; Edgewater joined the picture more recently. Multi-jurisdiction payroll has to tag the city, not just the state. An employee who works two days a week from a Denver coffee shop and three days a week from a home address in Aurora needs Denver $19.29 on the coffee-shop days and the state $15.16 on the Aurora days, because Aurora doesn’t have its own minimum wage. A national payroll engine that runs Colorado as a single $15.16 rate will under-pay every Denver shift. When does a Colorado employer have to pay overtime?

A Colorado employer pays overtime at 1.5x the regular rate whenever any of three triggers fire under COMPS Order 38, Rule 4.1.1: over 40 hours in a workweek (federal FLSA aligned), over 12 hours in a workday, OR over 12 consecutive hours in any single shift regardless of when the shift starts or which workday it crosses. Whichever rule produces the most overtime pay applies. Colorado is the only US state with the 12-consecutive-hour rule structured this way; California and Alaska both use a daily-8-hour trigger instead.

The 12-consecutive-hour rule is the unusual one and catches every multi-state payroll team that hasn’t run a Colorado shift before. The trigger looks at the shift itself, not the calendar workday. A graveyard shift that starts at 8pm on a Monday, runs through midnight, and ends at 9am Tuesday is a 13-hour shift that crosses the workday boundary. Neither Monday nor Tuesday on its own has 13 hours of work, so the daily-12 rule wouldn’t fire. The consecutive-hour rule fires anyway: hour 13 of the continuous shift is overtime, regardless of which workday it falls inside. Industries that run cross-midnight shifts (healthcare, hospitality, transport, manufacturing) hit this rule constantly.

The three triggers in one shift

Take an employee scheduled for a 14-hour shift starting at 6am on a Friday in a week that already includes 36 hours of regular time. The overtime calculation runs:

  • Hours 1-8 on Friday: regular rate. (Weekly running total reaches 44 hours after hour 4; daily-12 trigger has not yet fired.)
  • Hour 5-12 on Friday (running total 41-48): hours 5-8 are weekly overtime (over 40 in the workweek), so 1.5x. Hours 9-12 are still 1.5x under the weekly rule.
  • Hours 13-14 on Friday: 1.5x rate. The daily-12 trigger fires and the consecutive-12 trigger fires. Both rules give 1.5x; no stacking, the highest single rate applies.
  • Anti-pyramiding: an hour already paid at one overtime trigger is not counted again under a different trigger inside the same shift or week.

For a US-wide payroll provider running Colorado employees for the first time, the operational rule is: turn on daily-12 and consecutive-12 triggers per employee at hire time, set the workweek explicitly in writing, and configure anti-pyramiding so no hour is counted twice. Most national payroll engines ship with weekly-only as the default and an opt-in Colorado configuration. Flipping the flag at onboarding is a one-time setup that prevents two years of wage-claim look-back under CRS § 8-4-122. Source: Colorado COMPS Order 38, Rule 4 (overtime); Colorado Department of Labor and Employment, cdle.colorado.gov/dlss/wage-and-hour-law. Accessed 26 May 2026.

Exempt salary threshold

Colorado’s COMPS Order sets a state exempt-salary threshold that runs above the federal FLSA floor. For 2026, the COMPS Order 38 exempt-salary minimum is $1,128.46 per week ($58,679.92 per year) for executive, administrative, and professional exemptions. The federal FLSA floor sits at $684 per week ($35,568 per year) under 29 CFR Part 541. The higher of the two applies, and Colorado is the higher number for every salaried role. An employee paid $50,000 per year on a manager title is automatically non-exempt in Colorado, owed overtime on every shift over 40 hours, 12 hours daily, or 12 consecutive hours. Multi-state employers with a $48,000 minimum salary band for entry-level managers nationally have a Colorado exposure on every Colorado hire in that band. Source: COMPS Order 38, Rule 2.5; CDLE Wage and Hour FAQ. Accessed 26 May 2026.

How do Colorado meal and rest breaks work?

COMPS Order 38, Rule 5.1 (codified at 7 CCR 1103-1) requires a 30-minute unpaid meal break for any shift over 5 hours, with the employer obligated to allow the break and to relieve the employee of all duties. Rule 5.2 requires a 10-minute paid rest break for every 4 hours worked (or major fraction thereof), taken as close as practicable to the middle of each work period. If a meal break is interrupted or the employee is not fully relieved, the time reverts to paid working time and counts toward overtime triggers. Colorado does not impose California’s premium-hour penalty for missed breaks, but unpaid meal time that wasn’t actually taken becomes paid wages.

What “fully relieved of duty” means in practice

The 30-minute unpaid meal break only stays unpaid if the employee is completely off the clock and free to leave the work area. A receptionist who covers the phone “just in case” during their meal break is on-duty and the meal break reverts to paid time. A line cook asked to handle a single ticket during a 30-minute break is on-duty and the meal time reverts to paid. The reversion is automatic: payroll auto-deductions for the unpaid 30-minute slot get reversed at the end of the pay period, and the time gets counted toward the weekly 40-hour and daily-12 overtime triggers. CDLE’s interpretation since the COMPS Order 36 update treats any duty performed during a meal break, even a 1-minute interruption, as triggering the full 30-minute reversion.

Why Colorado is structurally lighter than California on this rule

Two states approach the same problem from opposite ends. California (Cal. Lab. Code § 226.7(c)) charges a 1-hour premium at the regular rate for each missed meal break and each missed rest break, per workday, recoverable as a wage claim for four years. Colorado simply reverts unpaid meal time to paid time, with no separate penalty hour, and a 2-year claim window. The practical impact on a single missed-break incident is much smaller in Colorado, which is why national payroll providers often pay close attention to California meal breaks and let Colorado run on autopilot. The autopilot strategy works until the timesheet history gets pulled for a wage claim or a CDLE investigation, at which point the unpaid-meal reversions compound across the workforce in the same way California premiums do. The exposure is smaller per incident, but the audit work is the same shape.

Three operational rules for clean compliance

  • Schedule the meal break before the 5-hour mark. COMPS Order 38 doesn’t set a strict timing requirement the way California does, but a meal break placed late in a long shift looks worse on audit and is more likely to get challenged as not actually offered. A meal break at hour 4 or hour 5 is unambiguously fine.
  • Document the offer, not just the deduction. Payroll auto-deductions for the unpaid 30-minute slot are not, on their own, evidence the break was offered. The schedule, the time-clock entry, or a written shift acknowledgement should show the meal break was scheduled and the employee took it.
  • Audit interrupted-break entries quarterly. If a time-clock system shows a meal break of less than 30 consecutive minutes, the auto-deduction should reverse. A pattern of 25-minute breaks across the workforce is a CDLE flag and a wage-claim risk under CRS § 8-4-122.

What does the Colorado Equal Pay for Equal Work Act require in job postings?

The Colorado Equal Pay for Equal Work Act (CRS § 8-5-201), as amended by SB 23-105 effective 1 January 2024, requires every employer to disclose the compensation range (or a range that the employer in good faith believes it will pay) and a general description of benefits in every job posting for a role that could be performed in Colorado, including remote roles. Additionally, the employer must notify all current employees when a job opportunity is posted, and announce internal promotions within 30 days. The law applies to any employer with even one Colorado employee, and the posting requirement applies to remote roles open to applicants in Colorado.

The Equal Pay for Equal Work Act is the rule that catches non-Colorado employers most often. A US-wide job posting that doesn’t list a salary range is non-compliant the moment a Colorado-resident candidate could apply, even if the employer never intends to hire in Colorado. CDLE’s INFO #9 guidance treats “could be performed in Colorado” as a broad standard: remote roles, hybrid roles, and even on-site roles in adjacent states that allow Colorado-resident workers all fall inside the disclosure requirement. The fix is to put the range on every posting that’s visible to a Colorado applicant, which in practice means every posting on every major job board.

The 2024 SB 23-105 amendment added two operational requirements that go beyond the original 2021 law:

  • The promotion announcement. When an internal promotion happens, all employees must be notified within 30 days of the new role, the successful candidate, and how other employees can become eligible for similar roles in the future. The rule was added to address criticism that the 2021 version let employers fill roles by quiet internal promotion to dodge the disclosure rule.
  • The notice to current employees of job opportunities. Before a hiring decision is made, all current employees must be notified that a job opportunity exists. This is the rule that triggers the most non-compliance in practice: companies fill roles by referral or by tapping the founder’s network without ever posting internally, and miss the notice requirement.

How often must Colorado employers pay wages?

Under CRS § 8-4-103, Colorado employers must pay wages at least once per calendar month, on regular established paydays no more than 10 days after the close of the pay period. Most Colorado employers run semi-monthly (twice a month) or bi-weekly (every 14 days) cadences in practice, both of which exceed the monthly minimum. Final pay rules are stricter: an involuntarily terminated employee is owed all wages immediately upon termination under CRS § 8-4-109(1)(a), while a voluntary quit is owed final wages on the next regular payday under CRS § 8-4-109(1)(b).

Three practical rules for any Colorado payroll:

  • Monthly is the floor, not the recommendation. A “once a month” cadence is legal but operationally unusual outside of executive payrolls. Most Colorado employers run semi-monthly or bi-weekly to align with the federal withholding deposit schedule and FAMLI quarterly reporting. A pay period that closes on the 15th must pay by the 25th; a pay period that closes on the 30th must pay by the 10th of the following month. The 10-day rule is the operative limit.
  • Final-pay timing depends on who initiated the separation. Involuntary termination owes immediate payment of all earned wages and accrued vacation, on the day of separation. Voluntary quit owes payment on the next regular payday. A misclassification at separation (recording an involuntary as a voluntary to delay payment) creates wage-claim exposure under CRS § 8-4-109, plus a willful-violation upgrade under § 8-4-122.
  • Accrued vacation is wages under Colorado law. CRS § 8-4-101(14)(a)(III) treats earned vacation as wages, payable at termination on the same timeline as regular wages. A vacation-use-it-or-lose-it policy isn’t enforceable in Colorado: Nieto v. Clark’s Market, Inc. (2021) 488 P.3d 1140 held that accrued vacation cannot be forfeited, even where the employee handbook says otherwise. Final-pay calculations have to include the full accrued balance.

Final-pay handling is covered in detail on the Colorado at-will termination page; the wage and hour interplay is the operationally important piece here. Source: CRS § 8-4-103 (pay frequency); CRS § 8-4-109 (final pay); CRS § 8-4-101(14) (vacation as wages); Nieto v. Clark’s Market, Inc. (2021) 488 P.3d 1140. Accessed 26 May 2026.

How should multi-state employers handle Colorado wage-and-hour law?

Treat Colorado as a separate compliance regime, not a state-level overlay on federal rules. Configure payroll for the daily-12 and consecutive-12 overtime triggers per Colorado employee, tag work location to the city (not just the state) so Denver, Edgewater, and Boulder ordinances apply correctly, and add a Colorado-specific section to the employee handbook covering meal-and-rest breaks, the COMPS Order 38 exempt salary floor, and the SB 23-105 job-posting disclosure rules. A national policy that defaults to FLSA-only will under-pay Colorado overtime on every cross-midnight shift and miss the salary-range disclosure on every Colorado-eligible posting.

Five operational rules for multi-state US employers with Colorado headcount:

  1. Configure payroll for both Colorado-specific overtime triggers. A US-wide payroll provider has to be told that each Colorado employee gets daily-12 and consecutive-12 calculated in parallel with weekly-40, with anti-pyramiding so no hour double-counts. The default in most multi-state systems is weekly-only; flipping the Colorado flag is a one-time setup that prevents the 2-year wage-claim look-back from accumulating.
  2. Maintain a Colorado-specific section in the employee handbook. Document the 30-minute meal break process, the 10-minute-per-4-hours rest break schedule, the on-duty meal-break consent requirement, the COMPS Order 38 exempt salary basis ($1,128.46 per week in 2026), and the salary-range disclosure rule for any Colorado-eligible posting. Colorado-specific addendum, not a footnote in the national handbook.
  3. Tag work location to the city, not just the state. A Colorado employee in Denver follows the $19.29 minimum; the same employee in unincorporated Jefferson County follows the state $15.16 minimum; the same employee in Boulder follows the $16.82 city minimum. Payroll has to know the city, not just Colorado.
  4. Set the exempt salary band at the Colorado COMPS Order floor for any role with Colorado headcount. $58,679.92 per year minimum for full-time exempt in 2026, with the floor resetting on 1 January each year under the annual COMPS Order. Below that number the employee is non-exempt regardless of duties, owed overtime on every shift over 40 hours weekly, 12 hours daily, or 12 hours consecutive.
  5. Audit every Colorado-eligible job posting for salary-range disclosure. Every posting on every major job board needs the compensation range, benefits description, application deadline, and notice-to-current-employees acknowledgement. Promotion announcements need to go out within 30 days under SB 23-105. Build a posting checklist into the recruitment workflow and audit it monthly; CDLE penalties run $500 to $10,000 per violation.

For most early-stage US employers, the practical move is a single national handbook that defaults to the strictest applicable state for any benefit, plus a Colorado-specific addendum that covers the daily-12 and consecutive-12 overtime triggers, the COMPS Order 38 exempt-salary floor, the meal-and-rest-break rules, and the SB 23-105 posting and promotion-announcement requirements. Colorado’s addendum is shorter than California’s because the surface area is smaller, but the COMPS Order resets annually and the salary-range rule is broader than most state pay-transparency laws.
