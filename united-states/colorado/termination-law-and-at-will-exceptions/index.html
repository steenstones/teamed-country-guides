How does Colorado termination law and at-will exceptions actually work?

Colorado is statutorily at-will, but Colorado courts have built a distinctive doctrine line: Continental Air Lines, Inc. v. Keenan (1987) recognises both the public-policy wrongful-discharge exception and the implied-contract-from-handbook exception, while Decker v. Browning-Ferris (1997) firmly rejected any implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in employment. Federal anti-discrimination law layers underneath the Colorado Anti-Discrimination Act (CADA, expanded by HB22-1367 and the 2023 POWR Act). The hard part is the cash mechanic: CRS § 8-4-109 requires the final cheque immediately on involuntary discharge, plus the CRS § 8-4-114 wage-theft statute makes wilful non-payment a criminal offence with felony exposure above $2,000. As strict as California on the cash side. No mini-WARN.

Is Colorado really an at-will employment state?

Yes, Colorado is at-will by judicial doctrine, but the same case that confirmed the at-will baseline also built two of the three available exceptions. The leading authority is Continental Air Lines, Inc. v. Keenan, 731 P.2d 708 (Colo. 1987). It recognised both the public-policy exception and the implied-contract exception in one opinion. A decade later, Decker v. Browning-Ferris closed the door on a third exception by refusing to imply a covenant of good faith and fair dealing in employment.

The doctrine line in Colorado runs through three Colorado Supreme Court decisions:

  • Continental Air Lines, Inc. v. Keenan, 731 P.2d 708 (Colo. 1987). The leading case. The court confirmed the at-will baseline, then recognised that an employer's distribution of a handbook or personnel manual containing specific termination procedures can, when relied on by an employee and supported by continued service, become contractually binding. Same opinion also confirmed that termination in violation of a clear and well-established Colorado public policy is actionable.
  • Martin Marietta Corp. v. Lorenz, 823 P.2d 100 (Colo. 1992). Refined the public-policy test. The plaintiff must show a clear expression of public policy in a constitutional, statutory, or regulatory provision; the discharge contravened that policy; and the employer was aware of, or should have been aware of, the protected conduct.
  • Decker v. Browning-Ferris Industries of Colorado, Inc., 931 P.2d 436 (Colo. 1997). Closed the third door. The court held there is no implied duty of good faith and fair dealing in employment contracts. If the parties want one, they must include it expressly. This is the material difference from Alaska, which recognises the covenant as a freestanding doctrine.

What this means in practice for a US-or-international company hiring into Colorado:

  • You do not need cause to terminate an indefinite at-will hire on the doctrinal baseline. A reduction in force, a strategic pivot, a personality fit, all sufficient before the exceptions bite.
  • The exceptions are narrower than California's. No Foley-style implied-contract claim from a totality of circumstances. No implied-covenant claim. The handbook trap from Keenan is real but it requires specific handbook language plus reliance.
  • The federal toolbox sits on top, not underneath. Title VII, ADA, ADEA, USERRA, FMLA, FLSA retaliation, NLRA Section 7. None of it goes away because the state is at-will.
  • The state statutory layer is thicker than people expect: CADA, the POWR Act, the Healthy Families and Workplaces Act, FAMLI, the Job Application Fairness Act, and the wage-theft amendments of HB19-1267.

Colorado sits in the middle of the spectrum. Tighter than California (which adopts every common-law exception). Broader than Alabama (which rejects the public-policy exception altogether). Same broad doctrine as Arkansas (two judicial exceptions), but the Colorado cash-mechanic is much stricter, and the criminal-theft exposure of CRS § 8-4-114 is unique to Colorado.

The statutory layer covers CADA (race, sex, age, disability, sexual orientation, gender identity, marital status, religion), CFEPA-style anti-retaliation for workers' comp, jury duty, sick leave under HFWA, FAMLI leave, and the POWR Act's lowered harassment threshold. Colorado does not recognise an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in employment.

What the Keenan / Lorenz / Decker doctrine line actually says

Continental Air Lines, Inc. v. Keenan, 731 P.2d 708 (Colo. 1987). The court held that an employer's distribution of a personnel manual containing specific procedures for termination of employment can, when relied on by an employee, become contractually binding. The traditional contract elements apply: offer (the manual's published terms), acceptance (continued service), and consideration (continued performance after the manual takes effect). Same opinion recognised the public-policy exception. The Colorado Law Review's 1987 commentary called Keenan “a watershed in the modification of at-will employment in Colorado”, and it remains the operative authority on both prongs.

Martin Marietta Corp. v. Lorenz, 823 P.2d 100 (Colo. 1992). The court refined the Keenan public-policy exception into a four-part test. The plaintiff must establish: (1) the employer directed the employee to perform an illegal act as part of work duties, or prohibited the employee from performing a public duty or exercising an important job-related right or privilege; (2) the action would violate a specific statute relating to public health, safety, or welfare, or undermine a clearly expressed public policy; (3) the employee was terminated as a result of refusing to perform the act or for performing the public duty; and (4) the employer was aware or reasonably should have been aware that the employee's conduct related to a clearly expressed public policy. Categories include: refusing to commit perjury, refusing to falsify safety reports, filing a workers' comp claim, serving on a jury, reporting unsafe working conditions, and reporting violations of law to regulators.

Decker v. Browning-Ferris Industries of Colorado, Inc., 931 P.2d 436 (Colo. 1997). The Colorado Supreme Court refused to extend the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing into employment law. The court reasoned that the public-policy wrongful-discharge tort already operates as the appropriate analog to the insurance bad-faith covenant; there is no need to imply a separate, freestanding good-faith duty in every employment relationship. Parties who want one must contract for it expressly. Decker is the case Colorado employer-side defence counsel cite to defeat any implied-covenant claim that survives the motion to dismiss.

The handbook lever

A clean at-will disclaimer, repeated in the offer letter and the handbook, signed at hire and on every handbook update, is the single biggest state-law lever an employer has under Keenan. A handbook that promises progressive discipline, lists an exhaustive set of dismissal grounds, or includes language suggesting termination only for cause without a controlling disclaimer creates the exact Keenan fact pattern. Colorado courts have consistently held that a conspicuous, repeated, unambiguous at-will disclaimer with signed acknowledgement defeats the implied-contract claim, but the disclaimer must be in both the offer letter and the handbook itself, not buried in a footer or limited to one document.

Which federal and CADA claims can a fired Colorado employee still bring?

All of them, on the 300-day federal clock. Colorado is a 300-day EEOC deferral state because the Colorado Civil Rights Division (CCRD) enforces CADA under a work-sharing agreement, so the federal Title VII / ADA / ADEA clock runs at 300 days instead of the 180-day non-deferral baseline. CADA's own filing window also became 300 days after HB22-1367 (2022), up from the pre-amendment 180 days. The 2023 POWR Act removed the “severe or pervasive” standard for harassment claims, lowering the threshold significantly. The CCRD complaint goes through investigation, conciliation, and either a probable-cause finding or a right-to-sue letter; the employee then has up to 90 days to file in district court.

Why the 300-day window matters

Most US states with an active state civil-rights enforcement agency that has a work-sharing agreement with the EEOC are deferral states, and the federal charge window extends from 180 days to 300 days. Colorado has the CCRD (Colorado Civil Rights Division), which enforces CADA and has a long-standing work-sharing agreement with the EEOC. A Colorado plaintiff therefore gets 300 days on the federal clock. After HB22-1367, the state CADA clock matches at 300 days. This is the opposite of Alabama and Arkansas (both 180-day non-deferral) and the same as California, New York, Illinois, and most other large states with active state civil-rights agencies.

The CCRD filing path

A Colorado CADA plaintiff typically files dual-filed with CCRD and EEOC, with the work-sharing agreement directing the lead investigation to one agency. CCRD investigates, attempts conciliation, and either issues a probable-cause determination or a right-to-sue letter. The employee then has 90 days to file a civil action in state or federal court after receiving the notice of right to sue. The CCRD complaint itself must be filed within 300 days of the alleged violation under HB22-1367 (the deadline was 180 days before the 2022 amendment). The CCRD does not have authority to extend these deadlines, even on a showing of good cause.

The 2023 POWR Act

HB23-1095, signed by Governor Polis on 6 June 2023 and effective 7 August 2023, made the most significant CADA amendments in decades. The headline change: the “severe or pervasive” harassment standard, drawn from federal Title VII jurisprudence, was removed and replaced with a substantially lower threshold (unwelcome conduct on the basis of a protected class that a reasonable person would find subjectively and objectively harmful when considered with the totality of circumstances). The Act also extended protections to several previously uncovered categories of workers, including domestic workers and independent contractors in some contexts, and required Colorado employers to maintain records of harassment complaints for at least five years. A 2024 Colorado termination playbook that uses pre-POWR harassment definitions is out of date and exposed.

No statutory damage caps under CADA

Unlike federal Title VII (which caps compensatory and punitive damages on a sliding scale by employer size, $50,000 for 15 to 100 employees up to $300,000 for 500+ employees), CADA imposes its own caps that mirror the federal sliding scale, but the back-pay and front-pay components have no statutory cap. A CADA jury verdict can therefore include unlimited back pay, front pay where reinstatement is not feasible, compensatory damages within the federal-mirror cap, and punitive damages subject to due-process review. Attorney's fees on top.

When is the final paycheck due in Colorado, and what does CRS § 8-4-109 actually require?

For an involuntary discharge, wages are due and payable immediately under CRS § 8-4-109. The default is that the cheque is handed over at the termination meeting. Two narrow exceptions: if the employer's accounting unit is not regularly scheduled to operate at the moment of discharge, payment is due within 6 hours of the start of the next regular workday (on-site accounting) or within 24 hours of the start of the next regular workday (off-site accounting). For a voluntary quit, payment is due on the next regular payday. Late payment after written demand triggers up to 300 percent of the wages due, or $3,000, whichever is greater. This is the strictest immediate-pay rule in the United States alongside California.

What “wages due” actually includes

Colorado reads wages broadly under CRS § 8-4-101(14). The final payment must cover:

  • Regular hourly or salary pay through the last moment worked, including the termination meeting itself.
  • Accrued and earned vacation as a vested wage under CRS § 8-4-101(14)(a)(III), confirmed by the Colorado Supreme Court in Nieto v. Clark's Market, Inc., 488 P.3d 1140 (Colo. 2021), which held that vested vacation cannot be forfeited at termination. “Use it or lose it” PTO policies that strip accrued vacation at separation are unenforceable in Colorado.
  • Commissions and bonuses already earned under the plan documents at separation, even if the formal calculation is not yet complete. The calculation must be completed and paid by the § 8-4-109 deadline.
  • Earned but unpaid overtime premiums under the federal FLSA weekly rule (40+) and the Colorado COMPS Order 38 daily rule (12+ in a workday or 12 consecutive hours in a single shift, whichever produces more overtime).
  • Any other wage line owed under COMPS Order 38, including required reporting-time pay or scheduled-shift pay.
  • Note: sick leave accrued under HFWA is NOT vested and is NOT payable on termination, unlike vacation.

How the § 8-4-109 demand-and-penalty mechanic actually works

The penalty under CRS § 8-4-109 and § 8-4-110 is triggered by written demand. If the employer wilfully fails to pay any wages due under § 8-4-109, and the employee sends written demand for payment by certified mail, the employer has a 14-day cure window to pay the full amount before the penalty attaches. If the employer pays within 14 days, no penalty. If the employer fails to pay within 14 days, the employee is entitled to recover the wages due plus the greater of (i) 300 percent of the wages due or (ii) $3,000, plus reasonable attorney's fees and costs under § 8-4-110. A $4,000 underpayment that is not cured within 14 days is exposure of $12,000 in penalty (300 percent of $4,000) plus the underlying $4,000 plus fees. A $500 unpaid commission that is not cured is exposure of $3,000 (the floor) plus the underlying $500 plus fees. The mechanic creates a strong incentive to cure within 14 days, but only if you know the demand has arrived.

Why the cash mechanic matters more than the headline rate

Other US states use “next regular payday” (Alabama, Florida, federal FLSA default), “7 days of demand” (Arkansas), or treble damages (Arizona). Colorado's § 8-4-109 puts Colorado in the same strictness band as California: the cheque in hand at the termination meeting itself, on the involuntary side. A central HR team running a multi-state payroll cycle two weeks out cannot rely on its normal cadence for a Colorado discharge; it has to cut a manual cheque on the day. Teamed's payroll engine flags Colorado separations at the point of decision and produces a same-day cheque ready to hand over at the termination meeting, alongside a wage statement that itemises vested vacation under Nieto, commission accrual, and final overtime.

What protects you

Four things. First, decide the termination date and time of day in advance, and have payroll cut the cheque the morning of, not the evening before. Second, run a contemporaneous calculation against your written vacation, commission, and bonus policy, signed off internally before the cheque cuts. Third, your written vacation policy itself: Colorado permits accrual caps (no further accrual once a ceiling is reached, then resumes after time off is taken) but does NOT permit forfeiture at termination after Nieto, so the policy needs to be drafted around the right lever. Fourth, document the moment of payment: many employers hand the cheque over with a wage statement, get a signed receipt at the termination meeting, and timestamp it. The receipt is not a release of claims, just proof of delivery against the § 8-4-109 clock and the 14-day cure window.

What is the CRS § 8-4-114 wage-theft criminal exposure?

Colorado has the most aggressive wage-theft statute in the United States. Under CRS § 8-4-114 and HB19-1267 (effective 1 January 2020), willful refusal to pay wages, falsely denying a wage claim, or intentionally failing to pay minimum wage is now classified as criminal theft under Colorado law. Theft of wages above $2,000 is a felony. Below $2,000 it ranges from petty offence to misdemeanour by amount. The 2020 amendments also removed the exemption for employers in bankruptcy and expanded personal liability for individuals who act on behalf of the employer.

Most out-of-state employers running their first Colorado payroll do not know the wage-theft statute is criminal. HB19-1267 collapsed the historical wall between civil wage claims and criminal theft prosecution. The statute now treats willful non-payment of wages exactly the same as taking property by deception or threat: a theft charge under Colorado's general criminal-theft statute (CRS § 18-4-401), with the same dollar-amount escalator.

How the criminal exposure interacts with a civil § 8-4-109 claim

The CDLE and the Colorado Attorney General have concurrent jurisdiction. An employee with an unpaid-wage claim can pursue a civil action under CRS § 8-4-110 (300 percent penalty plus fees), and file a complaint with the CDLE Division of Labor Standards and Statistics, and independently report the underlying conduct to the local district attorney as criminal wage theft. The criminal track is not common as a first move (most disputes resolve civilly), but the existence of the criminal track materially changes the calculus for a willful or repeat employer. The 2022 amendments under SB22-161 added employee-misclassification enforcement to the same enforcement chassis, which means a willful misclassification that withholds wages can be charged as both wage theft and a misclassification penalty.

Personal liability for individuals

HB19-1267 expanded personal liability for individuals who act on behalf of the employer. An owner, officer, manager, or agent who is “an employer or any agent or officer of an employer who knowingly permits the violation” can be personally named in a civil claim and personally charged in a criminal action. This is not a corporate-veil claim; it is direct individual liability built into the Wage Act itself. A multi-state HR director who signs off on a Colorado termination without final pay on the § 8-4-109 timeline is in the line of fire personally, separate from any company exposure.

The bankruptcy carve-out is gone

Prior to HB19-1267, an employer that failed to pay wages because of a Chapter 7 bankruptcy or other court action limiting control over assets had a statutory exemption from criminal exposure. That exemption was removed. The post-2020 statute treats inability to pay because of bankruptcy as no defence to the criminal theft charge, although it may still inform civil-side calculations and the willfulness analysis. The narrow good-faith dispute defence still exists: an honest, good-faith disagreement about the amount owed, supported by contemporaneous documentation, can defeat the willfulness element. But the burden of proof is on the employer.

How should you document a termination in Colorado?

Five documents do most of the work: a handbook with a conspicuous at-will disclaimer in the offer letter and signed acknowledgement, a contemporaneous performance file, a termination letter with an independent specific stated reason, a same-day final-pay calculation hitting the § 8-4-109 immediate-pay deadline, and an itemised wage statement meeting the COMPS Order 38 line-item requirements. The goal is to defeat the Keenan implied-contract claim, give the court a clean non-pretextual record on summary judgment under McDonnell Douglas, and stay out of the § 8-4-110 penalty and the § 8-4-114 criminal-exposure zone.

The handbook disclaimer (Keenan defence)

The disclaimer that defeats a Keenan implied-contract claim is a clear, conspicuous statement that nothing in the handbook is an express or implied contract of employment, that nothing alters at-will status, that the employer reserves the right to modify any policy unilaterally, that any list of disciplinary grounds is non-exhaustive, and that no manager other than a specified senior executive has authority to bind the company to a contract for a specified term. Put it on the front page, repeat it on the signature page, repeat it in the offer letter, and obtain a signed acknowledgement at hire and on every handbook update. The Colorado courts have followed the Tenth Circuit's reasoning in Vasey v. Martin Marietta Corp. and Tuttle v. ANR Freight Sys., Inc.: a conspicuous, repeated disclaimer with signed acknowledgement defeats the implied-contract claim absent specific oral assurances or written promises to the contrary.

Contemporaneous performance documentation

CADA and federal anti-discrimination law live or die on the question of pretext. The plaintiff's argument is that your stated reason for termination is a cover for discriminatory motive (race, sex, age, disability, sexual orientation, gender identity, etc.) or a Keenan-protected activity (whistleblowing, refusing to violate the law, filing a workers' comp claim, taking HFWA or FAMLI leave). The defence is contemporaneous documentation: performance reviews with dated entries, written warnings, PIPs with signed acknowledgements, customer complaints, attendance records. Documents created the day of the event carry far more weight in a Colorado jury than reconstructed narratives written after the CCRD complaint arrives.

The termination letter

State the reason clearly and precisely. “Position eliminated as part of the May 2026 reduction in force” or “continued failure to meet the documented sales quota despite the 30-day PIP that ended on 14 April 2026” both work. Vague reasons (“business needs”, “not the right fit”) invite the plaintiff to fill in the blank with a protected-class motive. Colorado state and federal courts apply the McDonnell Douglas burden-shifting framework, and the test turns on whether the stated reason was the actual reason and whether the employer believed it at the time. After Lorenz, a public-policy plaintiff also needs to show employer awareness of the protected conduct, so the timing and language of the letter directly bear on the public-policy defence.

Same-day final pay and the COMPS Order 38 wage statement

The final pay must hit the § 8-4-109 deadline (immediately at the termination meeting in the default case) and the wage statement that accompanies it must comply with COMPS Order 38's itemised line-item requirements: gross wages, total hours worked, all deductions, net wages, pay period start and end dates, all rates of pay, and any piece-rate or commission detail. Colorado wage-statement requirements are not as elaborate as California's Cal. Lab. Code § 226, but the COMPS Order 38 baseline applies to every Colorado pay event, and a non-compliant final statement opens a separate CDLE complaint route. Get the wage statement right or the § 8-4-109 victory becomes a COMPS-Order audit.

Independent grounds for protected-activity terminations

For terminations close in time to a workers' comp claim under CRS § 8-2-116, HFWA sick-leave use under CRS § 8-13.3, FAMLI leave under CRS § 8-13.3-509, a CADA discrimination charge, or any reporting of legal violations that could trigger the Keenan public-policy claim, Colorado courts apply a stringent causation test. The closer the temporal proximity, the heavier the employer's burden to show the independent business reason existed before the protected activity. If you have an independently sufficient ground for the termination, document it before the protected activity is on the table, and rely on it as the stated reason, you cut off the retaliation theory at the root.

What about mass layoffs in Colorado, and is there a state mini-WARN?

There is no Colorado mini-WARN Act. The federal WARN Act applies in full, but Colorado has not enacted a state-level WARN statute. The federal floor: 60 days' written notice for a plant closing affecting 50+ employees at a single site, or a mass layoff of 50 to 499 employees that affects at least 33 percent of the workforce, or any layoff of 500+ employees. Coverage threshold is 100+ full-time employees at the employer level. CDLE administers the EDD Rapid Response programme alongside federal WARN, which is why Colorado practitioners sometimes confuse Rapid Response with a state notice requirement.

What confuses out-of-state employers

Colorado does have several adjacent statutes that can be mistaken for a state-WARN:

  • CRS § 8-2-115 historically covered employment verification at hire (the Colorado Employment Verification Law), which was repealed in 2016. It was never a mass-layoff statute. The numbering is sometimes cited in legal-tech databases as if it were a Colorado WARN, but it is not.
  • CDLE Rapid Response programme. CDLE's Office of Just Transition and Rapid Response coordinates workforce-transition support for any employer facing a mass layoff, plant closing, or significant downsizing. This is an operational service, not a statutory notice requirement. The team contacts both employer and affected employees when a federal WARN notice is on file.
  • Colorado JUST Act (CRS § 8-83-501 et seq.) provides transition benefits for workers in fossil-fuel industries affected by industry decline, but it is also not a general WARN notice requirement.

The practical answer: if you are running a Colorado mass layoff, the only notice statute you need to comply with is federal WARN. The CDLE Rapid Response team will engage automatically once your federal notice is filed.

The single-site question

The federal WARN coverage test is calculated at the “single site of employment”, which is a 20 CFR § 639.3(i) concept. Colorado employers with multiple Front Range locations (Denver, Boulder, Fort Collins, Colorado Springs) typically treat each city as a separate single site. A 60-person layoff that affects 25 in Denver, 20 in Boulder, and 15 in Fort Collins does not trigger federal WARN at any single site, even though the aggregate is 60. The aggregation rule narrows in cases where the sites are nearby and operationally integrated; the analysis is fact-specific. A 75-person layoff at a single Denver site that crosses the 50-employee plant-closing threshold or the 33-percent mass-layoff threshold triggers federal WARN.

CDLE Rapid Response and unemployment-insurance interaction

Even without a state-WARN requirement, CDLE Rapid Response is automatically engaged once a federal WARN notice is filed. The team provides on-site briefings for affected employees, walks them through UI registration on MyUI Claimant, and coordinates Colorado Workforce Center services. This is operational rather than statutory, but it shows up fast and should be folded into the communication plan. Affected employees who file UI claims will see their CO SUI processing accelerated by the Rapid Response notification.

Where does the real Colorado termination lawsuit risk sit?

Four places. The Keenan public-policy wrongful-discharge claim (whistleblowers, refusal-to-violate-the-law, workers' comp filers, jury-duty servers, HFWA users, FAMLI leave-takers, lawful off-duty conduct under CRS § 24-34-402.5). The CADA discrimination / harassment / retaliation suit with the 300-day CCRD window and the lowered post-POWR harassment threshold. § 8-4-109 wage-claim disputes with the 300-percent-or-$3,000 penalty, particularly when vacation is at issue under Nieto v. Clark's Market. And the § 8-4-114 criminal-theft exposure with personal liability for managers and officers.

What we see in the case docket and in client matters:

  • Wrongful-discharge tort under Keenan / Lorenz. An employer terminates an at-will employee shortly after the employee complained to HR about a safety violation, reported a wage-and-hour issue, refused to participate in something the employee believed was unlawful, filed a workers' comp claim, or used HFWA sick leave. The plaintiff frames it as a Keenan public-policy discharge under the four-part Lorenz test. The fix is documenting the independent business reason before the protected activity is on the table.
  • CADA discrimination claim with the post-POWR harassment threshold. An employee files a CCRD complaint within 300 days alleging a protected-class motive (race, sex, age, disability, sexual orientation, gender identity, military status, pregnancy, religion). After the POWR Act, harassment claims no longer require “severe or pervasive” conduct; the new threshold is broader. Settlement values are running noticeably higher in Colorado post-POWR than pre-POWR, and the 300-day filing window means the claim arrives later than the federal 180-day baseline would suggest.
  • § 8-4-109 final-pay disputes. An involuntarily-terminated employee's final cheque arrives one day late, or omits accrued vacation that vested under Nieto, or omits a commission that the employee argues vested before termination. The 14-day demand-and-cure mechanic means an employer that responds quickly avoids the penalty; an employer that ignores the demand or disputes weakly is exposed to the 300-percent-or-$3,000 floor plus the underlying amount plus attorney's fees. The fix is calendaring the termination meeting against a same-day-cheque workflow and reading every demand letter the day it arrives.
  • § 8-4-114 criminal-theft escalation. Rare as a first move, but the existence of the criminal track changes the negotiating posture in any civil § 8-4-109 claim, particularly above the $2,000 felony threshold. An employer that is also facing a misclassification claim under SB22-161 is in a worse posture, because the same conduct can be charged under both statutes. Personal liability for the manager, officer, or HR director who authorised the non-payment is built into the statute, not added by piercing the corporate veil.
  • HFWA and FAMLI retaliation. Two relatively new statutory anti-retaliation claims with a clean private right of action. Colorado plaintiffs' counsel are pleading these alongside the Keenan public-policy claim and the CADA claim in any termination that follows leave use, which is most terminations of any employee who has been with the company more than a year.

The lesson, repeated in every Colorado employment-defence briefing: the Keenan shield is your handbook disclaimer, the § 8-4-109 trap is your same-day cheque, the § 8-4-114 amplifier is your criminal exposure, and the post-POWR CADA threshold is your harassment risk profile. None of them are new, but the combination has shifted noticeably towards the plaintiff since 2020.
