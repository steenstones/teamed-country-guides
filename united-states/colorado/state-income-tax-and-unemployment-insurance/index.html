How does Colorado state income tax and unemployment insurance work in 2026?

Colorado pairs the simplest state income tax in the United States (a TABOR-protected 4.40 percent flat rate with no brackets) with the most distinctive state-level payroll layer in the country: the FAMLI paid-family-leave premium, which stepped DOWN to 0.88 percent on 1 January 2026 and splits 0.44 / 0.44 between employer and employee for any employer with ten or more workers.

Colorado state payroll in 2026: state income tax is a 4.40 percent flat rate with no brackets and no phase-outs, protected by TABOR (Colorado Constitution Article X § 20). State Unemployment Insurance is charged on the first $30,600 of each employee’s wages (up from $27,200 in 2025), with the solvency surcharge in effect for 2026 after the reserve ratio came in at 0.649 percent against a 0.7 percent benchmark. The distinctive Colorado layer is FAMLI (Family and Medical Leave Insurance), which stepped DOWN to 0.88 percent of wages on 1 January 2026, split evenly between employer and employee (0.44 each), with employers under 10 employees exempt from the employer share. FAMLI is capped at the federal Social Security wage base of $184,500. The withholding form DR 0004 is optional, not mandatory: the default is to calculate withholding from the federal W-4 using the DR 1098 worksheet. State minimum wage is $15.16 per hour, with Denver at $19.29, Boulder city and county at $16.82, and Edgewater at $18.17.

What is the Colorado state income tax rate in 2026?

Colorado charges a single 4.40 percent flat rate on all individual taxable income in 2026. No brackets, no phase-outs, no marginal cliffs. The rate is set under Colorado Revised Statute Title 39, Article 22, and is constrained by TABOR (the Taxpayer’s Bill of Rights, Colorado Constitution Article X § 20), which requires voter approval for any tax increase. HB24-1065 retained the 4.40 percent rate for 2025 and forward.

One of fourteen flat-rate states

Colorado is one of the simplest US states to model payroll for, on the income-tax dimension. Compare with California’s nine-bracket graduated structure climbing to 13.3 percent at the top, or New York’s eight brackets to 10.9 percent. A Colorado employee earning $90,000 owes roughly $3,960 in state income tax for 2026 (4.40 percent of the full amount, with standard federal-aligned deductions stripped out). The same offer in California carries roughly $8,400 in state tax at the 9.3 percent marginal bracket. Source: Colorado Department of Revenue, tax.colorado.gov, accessed 26 May 2026.

Ballot measures still cycle the rate

Liberal advocacy groups have proposed a graduated income tax for Colorado’s 2026 ballot, and conservative groups have proposed further reductions to the flat rate. Neither has passed at the time of writing. Under TABOR, any increase requires statewide voter approval, and any rate change ratified at the ballot box becomes operative for the following tax year. Your offer-letter calculator should sit on 4.40 percent for 2026 with a quarterly check of Secretary of State ballot filings. What this means for offer letters

Flat rate means simple modelling. Take gross wages, subtract federal pre-tax deductions, multiply by 4.40 percent, and that is the state tax liability for the year. No bracket transitions to model, no top-rate cliff to manage at year-end bonus time, no spousal-income interaction. The complexity in Colorado payroll lives elsewhere — in FAMLI, the SUI wage base, and the local minimum wages in Denver and Boulder — not in income tax.

What does a Colorado employer pay in unemployment insurance (SUI premiums)?

Colorado SUI is calculated on the first $30,600 of each employee’s annual wages in 2026, up from $27,200 in 2025. New employers pay an industry-assigned introductory rate (the Colorado Department of Labor and Employment assigns a default by NAICS code) plus a Support Rate and the Solvency Surcharge, which remains in effect for 2026 because the 2025 reserve ratio came in at 0.649 percent against a 0.7 percent benchmark. After enough quarters of charged-experience, your account moves to an experience-rated band.

The wage-base jump matters for January cash flow

The $3,400 increase in the chargeable wage base means SUI runs longer through the year before any given employee hits the cap. A Colorado employee earning $60,000 hit the SUI cap in early June under the 2025 $27,200 base; under the 2026 $30,600 base, the same employee hits the cap in late June. Your Q2 and Q3 SUI payments will be larger than the equivalent quarters in 2025 if your headcount is stable. Teamed’s payroll engine forecasts the cap-date per employee at the start of the year so the higher Q2/Q3 charge is on your books before it lands as a variance on the Q3 close.

Industry-assigned new-employer rate

Colorado does not assign a single new-employer rate. Instead, CDLE looks up your North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) code and assigns the introductory rate that applies to your industry sector. Construction sectors typically carry higher introductory rates because of higher turnover and benefit charge history; professional services sectors typically carry lower rates. The introductory rate stays in effect until your account has accumulated enough quarters of charged-experience to compute a reserve-ratio based experience rate, usually three full calendar years from your first chargeable wages. Your specific rate appears on the annual rate notice in MyUI Employer+.

FUTA credit interaction

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) is 6.0 percent on the first $7,000 of each employee’s wages. A Colorado employer that pays state SUI in full and on time receives a FUTA credit of 5.4 percent, leaving an effective FUTA of 0.6 percent ($42 per employee per year). Late or partial Colorado SUI payment erodes the federal credit and pushes the effective FUTA rate up. Teamed’s payroll books both lines together so the credit never gets lost, and the auditable trail is itemised on every invoice. Source: IRS Topic 759 and Colorado CDLE, accessed 26 May 2026.

What is Colorado FAMLI and what does it cost in 2026?

FAMLI is the Family and Medical Leave Insurance programme, in force across Colorado since 1 January 2024. The 2026 premium is 0.88 percent of wages, DECREASED from 0.90 percent in 2024 and 2025 by the FAMLI Division Director’s annual recalculation. The split is 0.44 percent employer and 0.44 percent employee for any employer with ten or more employees. Employers with nine or fewer employees are exempt from the employer 0.44 percent share but must still withhold and remit the employee 0.44 percent. Premiums are capped at the federal Social Security wage base of $184,500 for 2026.

Quarterly remittance through My FAMLI+ Employer

FAMLI premiums are remitted quarterly through the My FAMLI+ Employer portal at famli.colorado.gov. Wage reports plus payment are due by the last day of the month after each quarter (30 April, 31 July, 31 October, 31 January). The quarterly wage report is reconciled against the SUI DR-quarterly report so a discrepancy between the two raises a flag inside CDLE for review. Teamed’s payroll engine files both filings off the same wage data, so the cross-check never returns a mismatch.

What employees can claim

FAMLI provides up to 12 weeks of paid leave per year for bonding with a new child, caring for a seriously ill family member, the employee’s own serious health condition, military exigency, or safe-leave reasons (domestic violence, sexual assault, harassment, or stalking). An additional 4 weeks is available for pregnancy or childbirth complications. Wage replacement is up to 90 percent of average weekly wages for low earners, scaling down for higher earners, capped at the state average weekly wage. Eligibility requires $2,500 in FAMLI-covered wages in the base period. The leave is job-protected after the employee has worked 180 days with the same employer. Source: Family and Medical Leave Insurance Division, famli.colorado.gov, accessed 26 May 2026.

The under-10 carve-out is operational, not optional

Employers under 10 employees do not pay the 0.44 percent employer share, but they are still required to withhold the 0.44 percent employee share and remit it through My FAMLI+ Employer. The 10-employee threshold is calculated as the average number of employees on payroll over the four quarters of the prior calendar year, with the 20-weeks-of-employment rule for counting. If you cross the threshold mid-year, your employer-share obligation begins the following 1 January, not retroactively. The cleanest read of the test is in 7 CCR 1107-1 Rule 4.

What is Form DR 0004 and how does Colorado withholding work?

Form DR 0004 is the Colorado Employee Withholding Certificate, an OPTIONAL state-level supplement to the federal W-4. It is not a mandatory parallel form. The default is for the employer to calculate Colorado withholding from the federal W-4 using the DR 1098 Colorado Withholding Worksheet for Employers. An employee files DR 0004 only when they want to fine-tune state withholding in a way the W-4 cannot capture — a higher annual standard deduction, additional withholding per pay period, or a specific dollar amount.

The 2020 federal W-4 redesign removed the allowances mechanic that older state forms relied on. Colorado solved that by building DR 1098 as a translation layer: enter the W-4 numbers, apply the DR 1098 lookup, and Colorado withholding emerges. An employee who is comfortable with W-4 withholding aligned to Colorado’s 4.40 percent flat rate does not need DR 0004 at all.

  • Line 1 (annual standard deduction) — an employee can override the default state standard deduction. Useful for itemising filers who want closer alignment between withholding and final liability.
  • Line 2 (annual allowance for child and dependent tax credits) — reduces taxable wage on which withholding is calculated.
  • Line 3 (additional withholding per pay period) — a flat-dollar over-withholding line, similar to the federal W-4 extra-withholding field.
  • Box at top (no withholding) — rarely used; only valid where the employee certifies under penalty of perjury that they will owe no Colorado tax for the year.

What employers must do when an employee files DR 0004

If an employee gives you a completed DR 0004, you are required to calculate their Colorado withholding using the amounts entered on the DR 0004, as prescribed in the DR 1098 worksheet. If an employee does not file DR 0004, you calculate withholding from the federal W-4 entries, also via DR 1098. Either way, DR 1098 is the canonical method. Employers do not pick their own method.

The retention rule

If an employee asks about adjusting their Colorado withholding, you are required to provide them with form DR 0004. You retain the completed form in the employee’s payroll file; you do not submit it to the Colorado Department of Revenue with regular filings. The form is produced only on audit request. Teamed’s onboarding flow offers DR 0004 alongside W-4 and I-9 on day one, captures any completed form in the employee record, and feeds the values into the DR 1098 calculation automatically. The employer-side discretion is none — just the calculation. Source: Colorado Department of Revenue, tax.colorado.gov/DR0004, accessed 26 May 2026.

How does a Colorado employer file withholding (DR 1094, DR 1093)?

Colorado runs a three-tier deposit schedule driven by annual withholding volume. Employers with annual Colorado withholding under $7,000 file DR 1094 quarterly. Employers between $7,000 and $50,000 file DR 1094 monthly by the 15th of the following month. Employers above $50,000 file DR 1094 weekly, with the week ending Friday. Everyone reconciles annually on Form DR 1093 by 31 January, the annual transmittal of state W-2 forms. Filing runs through Revenue Online at colorado.gov/revenueonline.

The cadence-switch rule

The Colorado Department of Revenue reviews each account during the 12-month period of 1 July to 30 June. If your withholding volume crosses a threshold, DOR notifies you before January of the next calendar year and your cadence changes from the first January payroll. You do not pick your own cadence; CDOR assigns it. Teamed’s payroll engine flags the crossover at the headcount addition that tips the math, so you are never caught filing quarterly when you should have moved to monthly. The same applies to the much larger $7,000 to $50,000 threshold for the move to weekly filing.

Revenue Online is the e-file portal

Revenue Online at colorado.gov/revenueonline is the canonical e-file portal for DR 1094, DR 1093, and the 1099 withholding return DR 1106. Employers with more than 250 W-2s are required to file electronically. Below that threshold, e-filing is optional but practically universal for any employer running modern payroll software. The portal also handles registration for new Colorado employers via the CR 0100AP combined application. Source: Colorado Department of Revenue, tax.colorado.gov, accessed 26 May 2026.

Zero-filing requirement

A quarterly, monthly, or weekly filer with zero Colorado withholding for the period still files a zero DR 1094, otherwise Revenue Online flags a missing return and starts the delinquency clock. DR 1093 is required even in a zero year if the employer was active for any part of the year. Source: Colorado DOR Filing Frequency & Due Dates, accessed 26 May 2026.

What is the Colorado minimum wage in 2026, including Denver and Boulder?

Colorado’s state minimum wage is $15.16 per hour from 1 January 2026 (up 35 cents from $14.81 in 2025), CPI-indexed annually under Colorado Constitution Article XVIII § 15. Local jurisdictions can set higher minima: Denver is the highest in the state at $19.29 per hour, Boulder city and county are $16.82, and Edgewater is $18.17. The tipped minimum statewide is $12.14 per hour ($15.16 minus the $3.02 federal tip credit). Overtime follows federal FLSA at 1.5x for hours over 40 in a workweek; Colorado adds a daily overtime rule at 1.5x for hours over 12 in a workday under COMPS Order 38.

The local-wage trap

A Denver-resident remote employee who reports to a Boulder-headquartered employer is paid at the rate of the jurisdiction where the work is performed, not where the employer is registered. If your Colorado payroll is a mix of Denver, Boulder, Aurora, and unincorporated state workers, you operate three or four minimum wages simultaneously. The compliance question is captured at onboarding (work address by jurisdiction) and re-checked at any address change. Source: CDLE PAY CALC Order, govdocs.com 2026 update, accessed 26 May 2026.

Daily overtime under COMPS

The Colorado Overtime & Minimum Pay Standards (COMPS) Order adds two state-specific overtime rules on top of FLSA: 1.5x for hours over 40 in a workweek (matches federal), 1.5x for hours over 12 in a workday (no federal equivalent), and 1.5x for hours over 12 consecutive in a single shift. Whichever rule produces more overtime pay applies. Most Colorado employees never trigger the daily rule, but warehouse, healthcare, and field-service operations do. Source: Colorado COMPS Order 38 (effective 1 January 2026), CDLE.

Does Colorado have a local payroll tax beyond minimum wage?

Yes, narrowly. Denver charges an Occupational Privilege Tax (OPT) of $5.75 per month per qualifying employee from the employee, plus $4.00 per month per qualifying employee from the employer, for any employee earning $500 or more in a month from a Denver-based job. Aurora, Glendale, Greenwood Village, and Sheridan operate smaller equivalent occupational taxes. No other Colorado municipality charges payroll tax. The Denver OPT is filed monthly via the Denver eBiz Tax Center.
