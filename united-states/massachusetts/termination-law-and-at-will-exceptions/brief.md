## Headline angle
Massachusetts is the strictest US state for final pay on involuntary termination: M.G.L. c. 149 § 148 mandates same-day final pay on firing/layoff. This is the page's lead fact. Most states allow 72 hours or the next payday; Massachusetts gives employers zero hours.

## Implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing
Massachusetts recognises this as a narrow but real exception to at-will. Triggered when an employer terminates an employee specifically to deprive them of an earned commission, benefit, or right that has accrued (e.g., firing before a bonus vests). Leading case: Fortune v. National Cash Register Co. (1977). Does not convert Massachusetts to a "for cause" state; it creates a damages remedy for bad-faith deprivation of earned compensation.

---

## Public-policy exception
Massachusetts recognises wrongful discharge claims where an employee is terminated for:
- Refusing to commit an illegal act
- Exercising a legal right (e.g., filing a workers' comp claim, jury duty)
- Reporting illegal activity (whistleblower)
Statute: M.G.L. c. 149 § 185 (whistleblower protection for public employees); broader public-policy protection developed through common law and M.G.L. c. 151B.

---

## MGL c. 151B anti-discrimination layer
M.G.L. c. 151B is Massachusetts's primary anti-discrimination statute. Applies to employers with 6+ employees. Covers all protected classes listed above. Creates wrongful-termination claims when dismissal is motivated by membership in a protected class. Enforced by the Massachusetts Commission Against Discrimination (MCAD).
