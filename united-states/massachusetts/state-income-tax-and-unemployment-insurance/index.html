What is the Massachusetts state income tax rate in 2026?

Massachusetts has a 5% flat income tax rate in 2026. Almost every dollar of wage income you pay to a Massachusetts employee will be withheld at exactly 5%.

The flat structure makes Massachusetts one of the simpler states to model for payroll purposes. Unlike Maryland's ten brackets or California's nine, Massachusetts applies 5% uniformly to wages, salaries, and most other income types. There are exceptions: short-term capital gains are taxed at 8.5%, and long-term capital gains on collectibles at 12%. But for payroll, you're almost always withholding at 5%.

The 5% rate has been in place since 2020, when a series of scheduled reductions brought it down from 5.85%. The state legislature has not proposed further reductions, and the flat rate is constitutionally embedded (a graduated income tax requires a constitutional amendment in Massachusetts, which voters rejected in 1994 and 2018).

Standard deduction in 2026: $2,200 for single filers, $4,400 for married filing jointly. Employees submit Form M-4 to declare their withholding exemptions. Without an M-4 on file, you withhold at zero exemptions (maximum withholding). Source: M.G.L. c. 62; Massachusetts DOR, Tax Rates 2026.

Your Massachusetts employees' effective rate will be below 5% once exemptions and the standard deduction are factored in. Build 5% into your cost models as the ceiling, not the floor. No county or city rate sits on top.

What is the Massachusetts millionaires surtax and does it affect payroll?

Massachusetts applies an additional 4% surtax on individual income above $1,107,750 in 2026, under M.G.L. c. 62, § 5C. This pushes the combined marginal rate to 9% for high earners. Most employees won't reach this level.

The surtax was enacted as Article 44 of the Massachusetts Constitution in November 2022 (effective January 2023). The $1M threshold is indexed annually for inflation; for the 2026 tax year, the adjusted threshold is approximately $1,107,750. This is an individual income tax matter, not an employer withholding requirement in the same sense as the base rate. Employers don't mechanically withhold the surtax via a separate line; instead, high-earning employees should adjust their M-4 elections or make estimated tax payments to cover it.

For employers, the practical impact: if you're hiring senior executives, engineering leaders, or other high-earners in Massachusetts at salaries approaching or exceeding $1 million, flag the surtax in compensation conversations. Some candidates will factor state tax exposure into relocation or remote-work decisions. Massachusetts is now one of only a handful of states with a combined marginal rate of 9% or higher (compared with 5% for the same person earning $900,000).

The surtax threshold will continue adjusting annually. The progression: $1,000,000 (2023) → $1,053,750 (2024) → $1,083,150 (2025) → $1,107,750 (2026). Source: VisaVerge, Massachusetts 2026 Income Tax.

How does Massachusetts employer withholding work?

Massachusetts employers withhold state income tax from each paycheck using the 5% flat rate applied to wages after subtracting personal exemptions. Payment frequency (annual, quarterly, monthly, or semi-weekly) depends on your total withheld liability.

Registration: employers register with the Massachusetts Department of Revenue (DOR) for a withholding account before the first payroll. You can register online via MassTaxConnect at mtc.dor.state.ma.us. Your federal EIN is required. Once registered, the DOR assigns your withholding account number and filing frequency.

Massachusetts assigns employers to one of four withholding payment frequency categories:

Annual reconciliation is filed on Form M-3 (Reconciliation of Massachusetts Income Taxes Withheld), due 31st January each year. Employers who file 50 or more W-2s or 1099s must file electronically. W-2s must be distributed to employees by 31st January. Source: Circular M, Massachusetts Income Tax Withholding 2026.

What is Massachusetts PFML and how much does it cost employers?

Massachusetts Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML) under M.G.L. c. 175M costs employers with 25 or more covered individuals a total of 0.88% of eligible wages in 2026. Employers must pay at least 0.42% of that directly; the rest can be deducted from employees.

Massachusetts PFML has been in effect since January 2021 and is administered by the Department of Family and Medical Leave (DFML). The total contribution rate for 2026 is unchanged from 2025. The rate applies to eligible wages up to the Social Security wage base ($176,100 in 2026).

The 0.88% breaks down as: medical leave (0.70%) split between employer (min 0.42%) and employee (max 0.28%), plus family leave (0.18%) which employers can pass entirely to employees. The DFML publishes a contribution calculator at mass.gov for exact per-employee amounts.

Benefits in 2026: up to 12 weeks of family leave, up to 20 weeks of medical leave, combined maximum 26 weeks per year. The maximum weekly benefit increased to $1,230.39 (up from $1,170.64 in 2025). Leave is job-protected.

How do I register for Massachusetts unemployment insurance as an employer?

New Massachusetts employers register with the Department of Unemployment Assistance (DUA) through the UI Online portal. Registration is separate from your DOR withholding account and requires your FEIN and a description of your Massachusetts business activity.

The DUA registration portal is at uionline.detma.org. You'll need your Federal Employer Identification Number (FEIN), the date you first employed workers in Massachusetts, the nature of your business, and your payroll service information. Once registered, the DUA assigns your Massachusetts employer account number and your initial rate (2.42% for non-construction, 6.08% for construction).

DUA also operates a Combined Registration option: if you're setting up your Massachusetts tax accounts from scratch, you can register for both the DOR withholding account and the DUA UI account through MassTaxConnect, which routes the UI registration to the DUA automatically. This reduces the number of separate portals you need to manage.

What is the Massachusetts unemployment insurance tax rate in 2026?

Massachusetts UI rates run 0.94%–5.24% under Schedule E in 2026 for positive-rated employers. New non-construction employers start at 2.42% on the first $15,000 of each employee's wages.

Schedule E is in effect for calendar 2026, as established by Chapter 9 of the Acts of 2021. Your individual rate within the schedule tracks your experience: the ratio of UI benefits charged to your account against your taxable wages over the prior three fiscal years. New employers have no experience history and start at the assigned new-employer rate.

Massachusetts is FUTA credit-compliant: no outstanding federal loan balance as of 2026. You receive the standard 5.4% FUTA credit, leaving a net FUTA liability of 0.6% on the first $7,000 per employee per year ($42 per employee maximum at federal level).

How do Massachusetts employers file and pay unemployment insurance taxes?

Massachusetts employers file quarterly UI wage reports and pay UI taxes through the UI Online portal at uionline.detma.org. Reports are due within one month of each quarter-end. Late filings attract interest and penalties, and repeated late filing can affect your experience rate.

Each quarterly filing reports total wages paid and UI taxable wages (capped at $15,000 per employee) for the quarter. Payment accompanies the filing. UI Online accepts electronic checks and credit cards. Employers with no Massachusetts employees in a quarter must still file a zero-wage report if registered. Failure to do so counts against your filing record.

Interest on late payments runs at 12% per annum (1% per month). Employers who fail to file for multiple quarters are assigned a penalty rate. The DUA also has authority to pursue collections for unpaid UI contributions.

Experience Rate Notices arrive in January each year. You have 30 days to challenge your assigned rate if you believe the benefit charges are incorrect. This window matters: experience rates are locked in for the full calendar year once the 30-day challenge period closes.

What do out-of-state companies need to know before hiring in Massachusetts?

Massachusetts has no local income tax, one of the strictest independent contractor tests in the US, and a PFML program with a meaningful split between small and large employers. The flat state rate simplifies withholding; the ABC test and PFML are where compliance exposure sits.

Three things that catch first-time Massachusetts employers off guard. First, no local income tax: unlike New York City or Maryland's county piggyback, you withhold only the 5% state rate. This makes Massachusetts payroll cheaper to administer than many comparable northeast states. You don't need to track where employees live for a local rate; the state rate applies uniformly.

Second, the ABC test for worker classification (M.G.L. c. 149, § 148B) is one of the most restrictive in the country. All three prongs must be satisfied, and Prong B (that the worker performs services outside the usual course of your business) is the sticking point for technology, consulting, and professional services companies. Misclassification exposes employers to treble damages under the Wage Act, plus attorney fees and DUA contributions retroactively owed. If your Massachusetts freelancers could be considered employees under the ABC test, use the Contractor Classifier before proceeding.

Third, PFML complexity for growing teams: the jump from fewer than 25 to 25 or more covered individuals adds a 0.42% employer contribution on top of the employee-only 0.46% rate. Plan that cost step into your hiring model. The max weekly PFML benefit rose to $1,230.39 in 2026, significant enough that employees will use it; your payroll must be configured to handle PFML leave correctly.

Massachusetts minimum wage is $15.00/hour statewide (unchanged since January 2023). No scheduled increases are currently enacted. Final pay on termination is due immediately on the day of discharge. Massachusetts is one of the strictest in the US on this, with mandatory treble damages for late payment under M.G.L. c. 149, § 148. Accrued vacation is treated as wages and must be paid on separation.

Frequently asked questions about Massachusetts employer taxes

What is the Massachusetts state income tax rate for employers in 2026?

Massachusetts has a 5% flat state income tax rate in 2026, applying to most income types. A 4% surtax applies on income above $1,107,750, bringing the combined marginal rate to 9% for high earners. There are no progressive brackets for most income. The flat structure makes withholding straightforward for most employees.

What is the Massachusetts unemployment insurance tax rate in 2026?

Massachusetts UI rates run 0.94% to 5.24% for positive-rated employers under Schedule E in 2026. New employers (registered under 3 years) pay 2.42% for non-construction or 6.08% for construction. The taxable wage base is $15,000 per employee per year, making the maximum annual UI cost per new employee $363 at the 2.42% rate.

What is Massachusetts PFML and how much does it cost employers?

Massachusetts Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML) under M.G.L. c. 175M requires a total contribution of 0.88% of eligible wages for employers with 25 or more covered individuals. Employers with fewer than 25 employees pay nothing; employees at those firms cover the 0.46% contribution themselves. For large employers, the employer must pay at least 0.42% (the medical leave employer share).

Does Massachusetts have a local income tax?

No. Massachusetts does not permit municipalities to levy a local income tax. Unlike Maryland, New York City, or Philadelphia, there is no city or county payroll tax in Massachusetts. You withhold only state income tax at 5% (plus the surtax for very high earners). This makes Massachusetts payroll simpler than many comparable US states for employers.
