Massachusetts sets a $15.00/hr minimum wage (M.G.L. c. 151, § 1, effective 1 January 2023, no 2026 increase scheduled), weekly-only overtime at 1.5× after 40 hours (M.G.L. c. 151, § 1A), and a mandatory 30-minute unpaid meal break for any shift over six hours (M.G.L. c. 149, § 100). Sunday and holiday premium pay for retail workers was eliminated on 1 January 2023. The Massachusetts Wage Act (M.G.L. c. 149, §§ 148, 150) imposes strict-liability mandatory treble damages for any late or unpaid wage payment.

What is the minimum wage in Massachusetts in 2026?

The Massachusetts minimum wage is $15.00 per hour, effective 1 January 2023 under M.G.L. c. 151, § 1. No increase is scheduled for 2026.

Massachusetts set $15.00/hr as the end-state of a multi-year phase-in completed in January 2023. Unlike California, New York, or Washington, there's no announced path to $16.00 or beyond at the state level as of May 2026. If your payroll model was built on a lower figure, update it now.

When does overtime apply in Massachusetts?

Overtime in Massachusetts applies at 40 hours in a workweek, with a minimum rate of 1.5× the regular rate, under M.G.L. c. 151, § 1A. There is no daily overtime threshold in Massachusetts.

M.G.L. c. 151 § 1A

Massachusetts Overtime Rule

1.5× regular rate for all hours over 40 in a workweek. Weekly calculation only. No daily OT trigger.

Effective: long-standing MA law · Verified 30 May 2026

The 40-hour rule is weekly, not daily. An employee who works 10 hours on Monday and 6 hours on each remaining day (Monday 10 + Tue-Sat 6 = 40 total) earns no overtime. An employee who works 41 hours in any week earns 1.5× the regular rate for that one extra hour, regardless of how the hours were distributed across days.

Who is exempt from Massachusetts overtime law?

Massachusetts exempts certain categories from M.G.L. c. 151, § 1A that are not exempt under the federal Fair Labor Standards Act. This is the quirk that surprises most employers new to Massachusetts hiring.

What meal break is required in Massachusetts?

Employers must provide a 30-minute meal break for any shift exceeding six hours, under M.G.L. c. 149, § 100. The break is unpaid only if the employee is fully relieved of duties and free to leave the workplace.

The statute is short and unambiguous: no person shall be required to work more than six hours in a calendar day without at least a 30-minute meal interval. The trigger is any shift over six hours, regardless of whether it straddles a traditional lunch window.

Paid vs unpaid meal breaks

The break is unpaid if the employee: (a) is completely free of all duties during the break, and (b) is free to leave the workplace. If you require the employee to stay on-site, remain on-call, or handle any work task during the break, you must pay for the time. A sales associate covering the floor "just in case" during their supposed break is a paid break, full stop.

Rest breaks (not mandated at state level)

Massachusetts law does not require separate paid rest breaks or coffee breaks beyond the meal period. The federal Department of Labor, however, treats short rest periods (5 to 20 minutes) as paid working time when they occur. That federal rule applies in Massachusetts for employers covered by the FLSA, which is most employers with two or more employees engaged in interstate commerce.

Violation of § 100 carries a civil fine of $300 to $600 per infraction. That's the statutory exposure. The practical exposure on a Wage Act claim is the treble damages trigger described below.

Is Sunday or holiday premium pay required in Massachusetts?

No. Sunday and holiday premium pay for retail workers was eliminated on 1 January 2023 as the final step of the 2018 Grand Bargain phase-out. No premium-pay obligation exists for Sunday or holiday work under current Massachusetts law.

The 2018 Grand Bargain legislation traded a higher minimum wage for a phased elimination of mandatory retail premium pay. The final phase ran from 2020 to 2023, with the multiplier stepping down annually (1.3× in 2020, 1.2× in 2021, 1.1× in 2022) before reaching 1.0× on 1 January 2023. As of 2026, there is no legal requirement to pay retail workers any premium purely because of the day of the week.

What remains: Massachusetts Blue Laws still restrict which types of businesses must close or obtain permits to operate on certain Sundays and holidays. The work-restriction framework is separate from the pay-premium framework. Operating-permission rules and pay-premium rules were never the same statute.

Standard weekly overtime still applies. If an employee's Sunday shift pushes their weekly total above 40 hours, those excess hours are overtime at 1.5× as usual. The day of the week does not affect the overtime calculation, only the weekly total does.

What are the Massachusetts Wage Act penalties for employers?

Under M.G.L. c. 149, § 150, employers face mandatory treble damages (triple the unpaid amount) plus attorneys' fees and costs. Since the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court's 2022 ruling, this is strict liability: even one day of late payment triggers 3× damages regardless of employer intent.

The Massachusetts Wage Act, M.G.L. c. 149, §§ 148 and 150, is one of the most employee-protective wage statutes in the United States. Three features make it unusually powerful as a litigation tool:

Strict liability (no intent required). The Supreme Judicial Court ruled in April 2022 that an employer is liable for treble damages on any late wage payment, regardless of whether the delay was intentional, accidental, or already corrected before the lawsuit was filed. Fixing the mistake after the fact does not undo the violation.

Treble damages are liquidated damages, not discretionary. The statute says employees "shall be awarded" triple damages on prevailing. There is no judicial discretion to reduce the amount because the employer acted in good faith or the delay was brief. A $10,000 underpayment becomes a $30,000 judgment before fees.

Attorneys' fees shift to the employer. A winning employee recovers their legal costs from the employer. This fee-shifting provision makes Wage Act claims economically rational for employees to pursue even on relatively small amounts.

What else should you know before hiring in Massachusetts?

Massachusetts is a high-litigation state. The ABC test for worker classification, the strict Wage Act, and a well-resourced Attorney General's Office make compliance discipline non-negotiable from day one of employment.

The Massachusetts ABC test for contractor classification (M.G.L. c. 149, § 148B) is one of the strictest in the United States. Prong B requires the contractor to perform services "outside the usual course of the business of the employer." For most technology and professional services companies, that prong is nearly impossible to satisfy for workers doing core-business tasks. If you rely on contractors who look like employees, Massachusetts is where that exposure bites hardest.

Non-compete agreements are enforceable in Massachusetts but only when signed at the time of the offer (not post-hire), limited to 12 months, and reasonable in geographic and functional scope under the 2018 Non-Compete Agreement Act (M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L). Post-hire non-competes are unenforceable.

Massachusetts has no local income taxes, unlike Philadelphia, New York City, or certain Alabama municipalities. State-level compliance is the full scope of sub-federal tax obligation.

The Massachusetts Paid Family and Medical Leave program (M.G.L. c. 175M) requires employer contributions of 0.42% of covered wages for employers with 25 or more covered individuals. The programme covers up to 12 weeks of family leave and 20 weeks of medical leave per year, with a maximum weekly benefit of $1,230.39 in 2026.

Frequently asked questions

What is the overtime threshold in Massachusetts?

Massachusetts requires overtime pay at 1.5 times the regular rate for all hours worked over 40 in a workweek, under M.G.L. c. 151, § 1A. There is no daily overtime threshold. Hotel, restaurant, and hospital workers are exempt from the state overtime law but remain covered by the federal FLSA.

Does Massachusetts require a meal break?

Yes. Under M.G.L. c. 149, § 100, no employee may be required to work more than six hours without a 30-minute meal break. The break is unpaid if the employee is fully relieved of duties and free to leave the workplace. If the employer requires the employee to remain on-site or on-call, the break must be paid.

Is Sunday premium pay required in Massachusetts?

No. Sunday and holiday premium pay for retail workers was eliminated on 1 January 2023 as the final step of the 2018 Grand Bargain phase-out. Standard overtime (1.5× after 40 hours in a week) still applies if Sunday hours push the total over 40 for that workweek.

What are the penalties for Wage Act violations in Massachusetts?

Under M.G.L. c. 149, § 150, employees who prevail in a Wage Act claim are awarded mandatory treble (triple) damages as liquidated damages, plus attorneys' fees and litigation costs. Since the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court's 2022 ruling, this is a strict-liability standard: even one day of late payment triggers 3× damages, regardless of the employer's intent.

What is the minimum wage in Massachusetts in 2026?

The Massachusetts minimum wage is $15.00 per hour as of 1 January 2023, under M.G.L. c. 151, § 1. No increase is scheduled for 2026. The tipped minimum wage is $6.75 per hour, with tips required to bring total compensation to at least $15.00 per hour.
