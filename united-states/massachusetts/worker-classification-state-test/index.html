What is the Massachusetts ABC test?

The ABC test under M.G.L. c. 149, § 148B presumes every worker is an employee. You must prove all three prongs to classify someone as an independent contractor. There are no exemptions.

Massachusetts law starts with a presumption against contractor status. Unlike federal common-law tests, which weigh multiple factors and balance them, the ABC test is conjunctive: all three prongs must be satisfied. A single failure means the worker is an employee by statute, regardless of what your contract says.

M.G.L. c. 149, § 148B: the three prongs (verbatim)
Prong A
Control "the individual is free from control and direction in connection with the performance of the service, both under his contract for the performance of service and in fact"
Prong B
Scope "the service is performed outside the usual course of the business of the employer"
Prong C
Business "the individual is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession or business of the same nature as that involved in the service performed"

The presumption of employment is statutory. The burden of proof sits with the employer, not the worker. Your contracts don't change that burden. The arrangement must satisfy the test in fact, not just on paper.

Why does Prong B trip up most tech and professional services companies?

Prong B requires that the work be performed outside the usual course of the employer's business. A tech company engaging a software engineer for core product work fails this prong by default. There's no fix for it short of EOR.

Prong B is the practical barrier that makes Massachusetts one of the strictest classification states in the US. Think of it this way: if you run a software company and you bring in a contractor to write software, that work is squarely inside your usual course of business. The contractor fails Prong B and is legally an employee.

The same logic runs across professional services:

  • A marketing agency engaging a graphic designer for client campaigns. Same business, Prong B fails.
  • A law firm using a contract attorney for client matters. Same practice, Prong B fails.
  • A staffing agency placing workers in the same role the agency provides to clients. Same business, Prong B fails.

The safe Prong B case is a plumber hired by a restaurant to fix a pipe. Entirely outside the restaurant's usual course of business. That narrow exception rarely applies in knowledge-work sectors.

Prong B: the critical question

Is the contractor doing work that your business does for revenue? If yes, Prong B fails. The ABC test doesn't care about contract labels.

In Sebago v. Boston Cab Dispatch, Inc., 471 Mass. 321 (2015), the Supreme Judicial Court found taxicab drivers satisfied all three prongs because the radio association's usual course of business was dispatch, not driving. One of the few published cases where Prong B was cleared in a service-sector context. It required the business model to be genuinely distinct from the work performed.

What are the penalties for misclassification in Massachusetts?

Treble damages (triple the back wages) are mandatory under M.G.L. c. 149, § 150. Courts have no discretion to reduce them. Add attorney fees, civil fines up to $25,000, and criminal liability for wilful violations.

The financial exposure from a Massachusetts misclassification finding is severe and non-negotiable:

  • Treble damages (mandatory): Section 150 requires the court to award three times the unpaid wages and benefits. There is no judicial discretion. Unlike federal liquidated damages, Massachusetts treble damages are automatic once liability is established.
  • Attorney fees: The employer pays the worker's legal costs on top of the treble damages.
  • Civil fines: Up to $25,000 per violation.
  • Criminal liability (wilful): Up to $25,000 fine and one year in prison for a first offence. Corporate officers and agents carry personal liability. The corporate shield does not protect individuals who directed the misclassification.
  • Criminal liability (non-wilful): Up to $10,000 fine and six months' imprisonment for a first offence.

In Patel v. 7-Eleven, Inc., 489 Mass. 356 (2022), the Supreme Judicial Court confirmed that the ABC test applies to franchise relationships with no federal preemption under the FTC Franchise Rule. The reach of § 148B extends further than most employers anticipate.

Are there any exemptions to the Massachusetts ABC test?

No. Massachusetts § 148B has no industry-specific exemption pathways. Every three prongs must be satisfied for every worker in every industry.

California's AB5 (the other benchmark for strict ABC-test states) built in a list of profession-specific exemptions for doctors, lawyers, architects, and others. Massachusetts did not. Section 148B applies uniformly across all industries and all worker types with no carve-outs.

This is the structural reason why Massachusetts classification risk is qualitatively different from other US states. In California, a company can argue a licensed professional fits an exemption. In Massachusetts, that argument does not exist.

What this means for employers building teams in Massachusetts:

  • No safe-harbour certifications that convert contractor relationships to compliant ones.
  • No industry guild exceptions that protect your sector.
  • No grandfather clauses for pre-existing contractor arrangements.

The Massachusetts AG's Fair Labor Division also has independent enforcement authority. It doesn't need a worker complaint to open an investigation. AG-initiated audits of contractor-heavy industries, including tech, delivery, construction, and gig platforms, are ongoing.

What should you know before engaging workers in Massachusetts?

Massachusetts enforcement isn't passive. The Attorney General's Fair Labor Division has independent enforcement authority. It doesn't need a worker complaint to open an investigation. AG-initiated audits of contractor-heavy industries are active in the state, and the enforcement record shows the law works as designed. Personal liability for corporate officers who directed a misclassification arrangement means the risk doesn't stay inside the entity. Massachusetts earned sick time under M.G.L. c. 149, § 148C vests from day one: 1 hour per 30 hours worked, paid for employers of 11 or more. And final pay on termination is due immediately on the day of separation, not on the next payday.
