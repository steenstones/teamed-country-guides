Arkansas’s state minimum wage in 2026 is $11.00 per hour, set by Initiated Act 5 (2018) and codified at AR Code § 11-4-210. The rate has been held flat since 1 January 2021, with no CPI escalator and no scheduled raise. The state Act applies to employers with 4 or more employees; smaller employers default to the federal $7.25 floor under FLSA. Arkansas has no city or county minimum-wage overlays; the state rate runs everywhere.

The Initiated Act 5 origin

Voters approved Initiated Act 5, the Arkansas Minimum Wage Act ballot measure, in November 2018. It set a three-step minimum-wage schedule: $9.25 on 1 January 2019, $10.00 on 1 January 2020, and $11.00 on 1 January 2021. The third step is the rate currently in effect. The 2018 measure did not include an annual CPI indexation clause, and the Arkansas legislature has not enacted a separate escalator. This is the structural difference from Arizona’s Proposition 206 (2016), which both raised the state rate and wrote in annual CPI-U indexation each 1 January. An Arkansas employer can budget the same $11.00 in 2026 they paid in 2021; an Arizona employer cannot. The trade-off: Arkansas’s rate is materially lower because it has not moved with inflation for five years.

When does an Arkansas employer have to pay overtime?

An Arkansas employer pays overtime under the federal Fair Labor Standards Act, mirrored by the Arkansas Minimum Wage Act at AR Code § 11-4-211. The rule is 1.5 times the regular rate for hours worked over 40 in a workweek. Arkansas has no daily overtime trigger. Exempt classification follows the federal salary basis of $684 per week and the duties tests in 29 CFR Part 541.

Because Arkansas’s overtime statute simply mirrors FLSA without adding a state layer, the calculation is the standard FLSA workweek rule. Define a fixed 168-hour workweek (e.g. Sunday 00:00 to Saturday 23:59), total the non-exempt hours, and pay 1.5 times the regular rate on every hour over 40. The regular rate includes non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, and commission earned in the same week; it excludes discretionary bonuses, gifts, and reimbursement of expenses. The Arkansas Department of Labor and Licensing’s Labor Standards Division enforces the Arkansas Act through wage claims; FLSA wage claims can also run through the US Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division or as a private FLSA action.

Exempt vs non-exempt classification

To be exempt from FLSA overtime in Arkansas, an employee must meet both a salary basis test and a duties test:

  • Salary basis: at least $684 per week ($35,568 per year), paid as a fixed salary not reduced by quality or quantity of work. Arkansas defers to the federal floor; no state-level salary top-up applies.
  • Duties test: primary duty must be executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales as defined in 29 CFR Part 541. Job title alone never establishes exemption.

A salaried “manager” making $42,000 per year who spends 80 percent of the day operating equipment alongside hourly staff fails the duties test and is owed overtime on every hour over 40 in the workweek. The 2024 federal rule increase that would have raised the salary basis to $1,128 per week was vacated by the US District Court for the Eastern District of Texas in November 2024, so the operative floor in May 2026 is $684 per week. How does Arkansas’s tip credit work?

Arkansas allows a tip credit of up to $8.37 below the state minimum wage under AR Code § 11-4-212 and 11 CAR § 11-705. The tipped cash wage is $2.63 per hour ($11.00 minus $8.37). The employer must guarantee that combined cash wage plus tips equals or exceeds the $11.00 state minimum each pay period; any shortfall has to be made up by the employer. A tipped employee is one who customarily and regularly receives more than $20 per month in tips. Arkansas’s rule is more generous to employers than the seven no-tip-credit states (Alaska, California, Nevada, Oregon, Washington, Minnesota, Montana), and tighter than the federal $2.13 cash wage with $5.12 tip credit because Arkansas’s state floor is higher.

Three operational rules for restaurant, bar, and hospitality employers in Arkansas:

  • Make-up calculation runs each pay period. A server paid $2.63 per hour cash wage who books only $5.00 per hour in tips on a 40-hour week earned $7.63 per hour total. The employer must top up by $3.37 per hour for that week to reach the $11.00 state minimum. Track tip totals per shift and per pay period; do not net-out high-tip and low-tip weeks against each other.
  • Tip pools are permitted with limits. Arkansas employers can run a tip-pool arrangement that distributes pooled tips among customarily tipped employees (servers, bussers, bartenders). The 2018 federal amendment to the FLSA bars the employer or any manager or supervisor from sharing in any tip pool, regardless of whether the employer takes a tip credit. Arkansas defers to that federal rule.
  • Service charges are not tips. A mandatory service charge added to a bill by the employer is employer revenue, not employee property. If the employer chooses to pass some or all of a service charge to staff, that distribution counts as wages (subject to payroll tax) rather than tips and cannot count against the tip-credit ceiling.

The $8.37 Arkansas tip credit is one of the largest in the country in absolute dollars because the state minimum ($11.00) sits well above the federal floor ($7.25) but the tipped cash wage ($2.63) tracks just above the federal $2.13. Do Arkansas employers have to give meal or rest breaks to adults?

No. Arkansas law does not require employers to provide meal breaks or rest breaks to adult employees (age 18 and over). Federal FLSA does not mandate breaks either. If you do offer rest breaks of less than 20 minutes, federal law (29 CFR 785.18) counts them as paid hours worked. Meal periods of 30 minutes or more, where the employee is fully relieved of duty, can be unpaid.

The federal default rules govern what happens when you do choose to offer breaks:

  • Short rest breaks (5 to 20 minutes): 29 CFR 785.18 treats these as compensable working time. A 15-minute paid coffee break counts toward the 40-hour weekly overtime threshold.
  • Meal periods (30+ minutes): 29 CFR 785.19 allows these to be unpaid, provided the employee is fully relieved from duty for the purpose of eating. An employee required to stay at their desk and answer the phone during a 30-minute lunch is “engaged to wait” and the time is compensable, which would bring it back into the 40-hour weekly count.
  • Nursing mothers: the federal PUMP Act (Section 7(r) of FLSA, extended by the 2022 act) requires reasonable break time and a private non-bathroom space to express breast milk, for one year after the child’s birth. This rule applies in Arkansas and is non-negotiable for any FLSA-covered employer.

Most Arkansas employers offer a 30-minute unpaid lunch and one or two 10-15 minute paid breaks per 8-hour shift, by policy not statute. The structure has to be documented in the employee handbook and applied consistently; ad-hoc “take a break when you need one” phrasing creates wage-and-hour exposure when an employee later claims they worked through unpaid time. Minors under federal child-labor rules

Arkansas does not add a state-level meal-break rule for adult-aged workers, but federal child-labor rules (29 CFR Part 570) cap hours, restrict hazardous occupations, and require specific working-time limits for 14- and 15-year-olds. Schedule a 30-minute unpaid meal break for any minor working a 5+ hour shift as a defensive practice; document it on the timesheet. How do the Arkansas Wage Act rules on pay frequency and final pay work?

The Arkansas Wage Act (AR Code § 11-4-401 et seq.) sets two operational rules. Pay frequency must be at least semi-monthly under § 11-4-401. Final pay on involuntary separation is due within 7 days of discharge if the employee has demanded or requested payment (AR Code § 11-4-405(a)). Without a demand, final pay can wait for the next regular payday. A voluntary quit triggers final pay on the next regular payday. Violations expose the employer to double the wages due under § 11-4-405(b).

7d Arkansas Wage Act final-pay rule

The Arkansas Wage Act final-pay clock is demand-triggered. Involuntary separation: if the employee demands payment, pay all unpaid wages within 7 days of the discharge. Without a demand, pay on the next regular payday. Voluntary quit: next regular payday. Failure to pay timely exposes the employer to double the wages due under AR Code § 11-4-405(b). The two operational implications for inbound payroll:

  • Bi-weekly is compliant; weekly is over-compliant; monthly is non-compliant. Most multi-state employers default to bi-weekly (every 14 days), which satisfies the semi-monthly minimum under § 11-4-401 comfortably. Monthly payroll fails the at-least-semi-monthly rule for most employee types in Arkansas.
  • Discharge plus written demand triggers the 7-day clock. The Arkansas Wage Act differs from states with a flat next-day-or-near-next-day rule (Alaska 3 working days, Arizona 7 working days regardless of demand). In Arkansas, the 7-day clock starts when the employee demands payment in writing. Most employees do issue a demand within hours of termination, so configure payroll to run an out-of-cycle final-pay batch whenever an Arkansas separation event lands; do not wait to see whether a demand comes in.

Double damages under AR Code § 11-4-405(b) turn a small late-pay claim into a meaningful settlement. A single $3,000 unpaid final paycheck paid 14 days late, after the 7-day window closed on a demanded discharge, exposes the employer to $6,000 in damages plus the original $3,000, plus attorney fees if litigated. What are Arkansas’s child labour rules after the 2023 Youth Hiring Act?

The minimum age for employment in Arkansas is 14 years old under AR Code Title 11, Chapter 6. The Youth Hiring Act of 2023 eliminated the state work permit requirement for minors under 16, so employers no longer obtain a state certificate before hiring a 14- or 15-year-old. State hour caps still apply: 14-15 year-olds working when school is in session cannot start before 6am, end after 7pm, or exceed 8 hours per day, 6 days per week, or 48 hours per week. Federal 29 CFR Part 570 still applies on top, restricting hazardous occupations and time-of-day limits.

Two structural points for any Arkansas employer hiring minors in 2026:

  • The Youth Hiring Act removed the state permit, not the rules. Before 2023, an Arkansas employer hiring a 14- or 15-year-old had to obtain a state-issued work permit (Form A-3). The 2023 Act eliminated that paperwork. Hour caps, hazardous-occupation restrictions, and the 14-year minimum age still apply. The change is administrative: faster onboarding for legal minor hires, no change to the underlying labour-standards regime.
  • Federal 29 CFR Part 570 controls where it’s stricter. Federal child-labour rules sit on top of state. Where federal is stricter, federal applies. Specific examples: 14- and 15-year-olds cannot work in manufacturing, mining, or any occupation declared hazardous by the US Secretary of Labor (driving, operating power-driven machinery, roofing, demolition). Time-of-day limits under 29 CFR § 570.35 (7am-7pm during school year, extending to 9pm 1 June to Labor Day) bind in some windows even where the Arkansas cap would be more permissive. Run both checks before scheduling a minor.

For most knowledge-economy employers, this section is academic: tech, finance, professional services do not hire under 16. For retail, food service, agriculture, and seasonal hospitality, the post-2023 Arkansas regime is materially less paperwork-heavy than pre-2023, but the underlying duty-of-care obligations have not moved. How should multi-state employers handle Arkansas wage-and-hour law?

Apply the highest applicable standard per employee, by physical work location. Arkansas sits well above the federal floor on minimum wage ($11.00 vs $7.25), mirrors federal on overtime via AR Code § 11-4-211, and adds the Arkansas Wage Act’s pay-frequency and demand-triggered final-pay rules on top. There are no city or county overlays to track in Arkansas, which simplifies the wage-rate matrix compared to Arizona, California, or Washington.

Four operational rules for multi-state US employers with Arkansas headcount:

  1. Maintain a state matrix without city overlays for Arkansas. Document each state where you employ someone. Arkansas adds one row to the matrix; no Arkansas city or county overrides the state $11.00 rate. Re-check the matrix on 1 January each year, but Arkansas’s rate has not moved since 2021 and there is no scheduled raise.
  2. Tag each employee’s work location, not the company’s headquarters. The wage-and-hour rule that applies is the rule of the location where the employee physically performs the work. A Delaware-incorporated company with a remote employee living and working in Little Rock follows Arkansas wage-and-hour law. The cross-state rule rarely matters for salaried remote workers but always matters for hourly Arkansas headcount.
  3. Configure final-pay capability for the 7-day-on-demand clock. Most multi-state payroll systems are set up for the federal default of next-payday final pay. The Arkansas demand-triggered 7-day rule (AR Code § 11-4-405) needs a discrete flag on each Arkansas employee record to trigger an out-of-cycle pay run if a demand comes in after termination. Configure once, on the first Arkansas hire.
  4. Coverage threshold matters at small scale. The Arkansas Minimum Wage Act applies to employers with 4 or more employees; below that, the federal $7.25 floor governs. Most multi-state employers already have 4+ employees across the company and treat Arkansas as $11.00 from day one. Sole-proprietor-style employers with a tiny Arkansas footprint can run lawfully at federal $7.25, but the cliff edge at the fourth hire is operationally painful.

For most early-stage US employers, the practical move is a single national handbook that defaults to the strictest applicable state for any benefit, plus a state-specific addendum for jurisdictions with hard mandates (California, New York, Massachusetts, Washington, Oregon, Colorado, Illinois, and Arkansas for the Wage Act final-pay rule and the $11.00 minimum). Arkansas’s addendum is short: state the current Arkansas minimum wage with the 4-employee coverage threshold, state the $8.37 state tip credit ($2.63 tipped cash wage), state the FLSA-mirror overtime rule, state the 7-day-on-demand final-pay clock, and reference the Youth Hiring Act 2023 if you hire minors.
