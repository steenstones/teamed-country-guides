How does Arkansas state income tax and unemployment insurance work in 2026?

Arkansas has cut its top income tax rate four years running to a now-3.7 percent ceiling, paired with one of the lowest SUTA wage bases in the United States ($7,000) and a single withholding form (AR4EC) that uses the older exemption-count mechanics rather than the post-2020 federal style.

Arkansas state payroll in 2026: state income tax tops out at a 3.7 percent rate after four consecutive cuts under SB1 and HB1001 (signed 6 May 2026, retroactive to 1 January), down from 4.9 percent in 2023. State Unemployment Insurance (SUTA) costs 2.0 percent on the first $7,000 of wages for new employers and runs in the 0.1 percent to 5.0 percent band on experience, per Arkansas DWS. Every new hire completes Form AR4EC declaring marital status, exemptions, and any extra dollar withholding; if no form is filed the employer must withhold at single, zero exemptions (the highest rate). Employers withholding $1,000 or more per month file Form AR941M monthly by the 15th; smaller employers file Form AR941 quarterly; everyone reconciles annually on Form AR3MAR by 28 February. Arkansas has no city or county payroll tax, with one quirky exception: Texarkana, Arkansas workers who reside on the Texas side of the state line claim an Arkansas withholding exemption on Form AR-TX.

What is the Arkansas state income tax rate in 2026?

Arkansas charges a top rate of 3.7 percent on individual income in 2026, cut from 3.9 percent under SB1 / HB1001 (signed 6 May 2026, retroactive to 1 January). It is the fourth Arkansas income tax cut in four years: 4.9 → 4.7 → 4.4 → 3.9 → 3.7 percent. Lower-income filers under roughly $94,700 of net taxable income use a graduated five-band table starting at 0 percent.

Two tax tables, by income tier

Arkansas runs an unusual two-table withholding structure under AR Code Title 26, Chapter 51. Filers whose net taxable income is at or below the indexed annual threshold (around $94,700 for 2026) use the low-income graduated table: zero percent on roughly the first $5,600, then stepping through 2.0, 3.0, 3.4 percent bands to a top of 3.7 percent above approximately $26,400. Filers above the threshold use a simplified two-step table: 2.0 percent on the first $4,700, and 3.7 percent on everything above. The exact 2026 bracket dollar thresholds are indexed for inflation each year and finalised in the DFA AR1000F instruction booklet published late in the year. Source: Tax Foundation 2026 State Tax Rates and Arkansas DFA, accessed 21 May 2026.

How it compares regionally

Arkansas now sits below most of its south-east neighbours. Mississippi runs a 4.0 percent flat rate, Missouri 4.7 percent top, Louisiana 3.0 to 4.25 percent graduated. Tennessee and Texas levy no individual income tax at all, but Arkansas's four-year reduction trajectory has narrowed the gap meaningfully. What does an Arkansas employer pay in unemployment insurance (SUTA)?

New Arkansas employers pay 2.0 percent on the first $7,000 of each employee’s annual wages, capped at $140 per employee per year. After three full calendar years of benefit-risk experience, the rate moves into the experience-rated band of 0.1 percent to 5.0 percent, plus a 0.1 percent administrative assessment, per Arkansas DWS. The $7,000 wage base is one of the lowest in the United States.

Arkansas DWS calculates each employer’s experience rate annually from the reserve-ratio formula in AR Code Title 11, Chapter 10: cumulative contributions paid in, minus benefits paid out to former employees, divided by average taxable payroll. A high-turnover Arkansas employer trends toward the 5.0 percent ceiling; a stable employer with few separations settles near the 0.1 percent floor. FUTA credit interaction

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) is 6 percent on the first $7,000. An Arkansas employer that pays state SUTA in full and on time receives a FUTA credit of 5.4 percent, leaving an effective FUTA of 0.6 percent ($42 per employee per year). What is Form AR4EC and how does Arkansas withholding work?

Form AR4EC is the Employee’s Arkansas Withholding Exemption Certificate, the state equivalent of the federal W-4 but built on the older exemption-count mechanics: marital status, number of exemptions or dependents, and any flat dollar amount to withhold above the table rate. Every Arkansas new hire completes one. If the employee does not file an AR4EC, the employer must withhold at the highest rate (single status, zero exemptions) until the form arrives.

The mechanics differ from the post-2020 federal W-4. Arkansas did not adopt the federal redesign that removed allowances. AR4EC still asks for:

  • Marital status — single or married. The employer applies a different withholding table to each.
  • Total number of exemptions — the employee plus any dependents claimed for Arkansas purposes. Each exemption reduces the wage base that the table is applied to.
  • Additional dollar amount per pay period — a flat-dollar over-withholding line for employees who want to over-pay.
  • Texarkana exemption — Texas-resident workers based in Texarkana, AR claim full exemption from Arkansas withholding via the linked Form AR-TX.

The employer looks up the gross taxable wage on the Arkansas Withholding Tax Tables (issued each year by DFA) using the marital status and exemption count, and withholds the table figure plus any AR4EC additional dollar amount. There is no percentage-of-gross election like Arizona offers, and no calculator-style step grid like the federal 2020+ W-4.

The "highest rate" default

An employee with no AR4EC on file is treated as single with zero exemptions, which is the most aggressive withholding combination on the table. For a $60,000 Arkansas employee that’s roughly $2,200 a year, against $2,000 if the same employee filed an AR4EC claiming themselves as one exemption with single status. The gap is not enormous, but it accumulates across pay periods and the employee gets a refund instead of accurate take-home. When AR4EC needs refreshing

AR4EC does not require an annual refresh in the way Arizona’s A-4 does. The form stays in force until the employee’s exemption count changes (marriage, divorce, new dependent, dependent ageing out) or they elect a new additional-dollar amount. Best practice is to prompt employees to review the AR4EC at year-end alongside the federal W-4 review most payroll providers run in November or December, so any 2026-affecting changes land before the first January payroll. Source: Arkansas Form AR4EC, dws.arkansas.gov.

How does an Arkansas employer file withholding (AR941, AR941M, AR3MAR)?

Arkansas uses a simpler two-cadence system than the federal schedule. Employers withholding $1,000 or more per month file Form AR941M monthly by the 15th of the following month. Smaller employers file Form AR941 quarterly by the last day of the month after each quarter. Everyone reconciles annually on Form AR3MAR by 28 February, with W-2 transmittal (ARW-3) due 31 January. Filing runs through the Arkansas Taxpayer Access Point (ATAP) at atap.arkansas.gov.

The annual reconciliation lands a month later than the W-2 deadline, giving four weeks to chase up any quarter-to-W-2 discrepancies before AR3MAR locks.

Monthly threshold mechanics

Arkansas uses a per-month withholding total to set cadence. If you withhold $1,000 or more in any single month, you must file Form AR941M for that month and continue on the monthly cadence. Employers running multi-state payroll often cross the $1,000 Arkansas threshold once they have five or six full-time Arkansas employees. Electronic filing through ATAP

The Arkansas Taxpayer Access Point (ATAP) at atap.arkansas.gov is the canonical e-file portal for AR941, AR941M, AR3MAR, and ARW-3. Employers with more than 250 employees are required to file electronically. Below that threshold, e-filing is optional but practically universal for any employer running modern payroll software. ATAP also handles bulk file upload for W-2 and 1099 submissions and is the registration entry point for new employers. Source: Arkansas DFA Withholding Tax Branch and Due Dates page, accessed 21 May 2026.

Zero-filing rule

A monthly or quarterly filer with zero Arkansas withholding for the period still files a zero AR941M or AR941, otherwise ATAP flags a missing return and starts the delinquency clock. AR3MAR is required even in a zero year if the employer was active for any part of the year. Source: Arkansas DFA, accessed 21 May 2026.

Do Arkansas cities or counties charge a local payroll tax?

No. Arkansas has no city or county tax on payroll. There is no Arkansas equivalent of Birmingham’s occupational tax, Philadelphia’s wage tax, or NYC’s local income tax. The one true edge case is Texarkana: workers who reside in Texarkana, Texas and work in Texarkana, Arkansas claim an Arkansas withholding exemption on Form AR-TX. Withholding registration is state-only via the Arkansas Taxpayer Access Point.

Three things to keep straight on Arkansas local tax:

  1. No municipal occupational tax exists. Arkansas does not authorise cities or counties to levy a tax on payroll wages. The legislative pre-emption is long-standing and stable.
  2. The Texarkana carve-out is unique. Under AR Code § 26-51-301, wages earned by a Texarkana, Texas resident for work performed within the corporate limits of Texarkana, Arkansas are exempt from Arkansas income tax. The employer claims the exemption by collecting Form AR-TX from the employee and reporting on AR3MAR. There is no equivalent reciprocity for any other Arkansas border city.
  3. Local minimum wages do not exist either. Arkansas state law preempts city and county minimum-wage ordinances. The $11.00 per hour state floor applies everywhere in the state, no exceptions for Little Rock, Fayetteville, or any other municipality.

For Teamed clients the absence of local payroll tax is a genuine operational simplification. Multi-state employers running payroll in Pennsylvania (where dozens of municipalities run local Earned Income Tax) or Alabama (where Birmingham, Bessemer, and Gadsden all levy city occupational tax) feel the difference. Arkansas payroll is one state return, one state UI filing, one federal stack, with the AR-TX flag captured at onboarding for any Texarkana hire. Source: Arkansas DFA and AR Code § 26-51-301, accessed 21 May 2026.

What is the Arkansas minimum wage and overtime rule in 2026?

Arkansas’s state minimum wage in 2026 is $11.00 per hour, set under Initiated Act 5 (2018) and unchanged since 2021. There is no annual CPI escalator and no city above the state floor (state law preempts local minimum-wage ordinances). The tipped minimum is $2.63 per hour with the FLSA-style tip credit. Overtime follows the federal FLSA: 1.5 times regular rate for non-exempt hours over 40 in a workweek. Arkansas adds no state daily-overtime premium.

Overtime defers entirely to federal FLSA

Arkansas has no separate state overtime statute. Non-exempt employees earn the FLSA 1.5x premium for hours over 40 in a workweek; exempt employees are exempt under the federal test (executive, administrative, professional, computer, outside-sales). No daily-overtime rule, no double-time rule, no California-style alternative workweek schedules. Arkansas employers calculate overtime exactly as the federal rule requires. Source: Arkansas Minimum Wage Act, AR Code § 11-4-210, and 29 USC § 207 (FLSA), accessed 21 May 2026.
