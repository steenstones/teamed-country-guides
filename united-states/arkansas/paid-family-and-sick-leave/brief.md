## The story

Arkansas has **no state-mandated paid family leave** for private-sector employees and **no state-mandated paid sick leave**. There is no Arkansas equivalent of California's PFL, no Arkansas equivalent of New York's PFL, no Arkansas mini-FMLA. The only federal floor is the **federal Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA, 29 U.S.C. § 2601 et seq.)**, which provides **12 weeks of UNPAID, job-protected leave** for employers with 50+ employees within a 75-mile radius.

Two Arkansas-specific wrinkles set this state apart from the "no statute" sibling pattern:

1. **Act 850 of 2021 (A.C.A. § 23-86-501)** authorises licensed Arkansas insurance carriers to offer **voluntary paid family leave insurance products** to employers and employees. No mandate, no state administration, no payroll contribution required — but the regulatory framework exists, which several "no statute" states (Alabama included) do not have. An Arkansas employer that wants to offer paid family leave through an insurance vehicle has a sanctioned route.
2. **City of Little Rock 12-week paid parental leave policy (March 2026)** — the first Arkansas city with a formal municipal paid-parental-leave entitlement. Twelve weeks for full-time municipal employees with at least one year tenure, six weeks below one year, covering birth, adoption, foster, surrogacy, and pregnancy loss. Applies only to Little Rock municipal employees but signals direction-of-travel for Pulaski County private benchmarks.

For employers with under 50 employees, even federal FMLA doesn't apply. The leave package is entirely voluntary, designed by the employer.

This page is **honest about the absence of a mandatory state PFL/PSL law**. The buyer story:

1. Arkansas is attractive for cost-conscious employers because there is no state PFL payroll tax, no state PSL administrative burden, no SDI premium.
2. The trade-off is that competitive talent (especially Little Rock, Bentonville, and Fayetteville knowledge workers) increasingly expects parental leave, sick days, and bereavement built into the offer letter — they're comparing against jobs at companies with California, NY, or DC employees who get state benefits stacked on top.
3. Federal FMLA is the floor. If the employer crosses the 50-employee threshold (counting all US employees, not just Arkansas ones), the FMLA obligation kicks in.
4. The Arkansas Act 850 voluntary PFL insurance route is a path some mid-market employers should consider — it's a benefit the talent market increasingly notices.
5. Teamed brings benchmark data on what competitive Arkansas employers offer (typically 8-12 weeks paid parental leave for retention-critical roles, 5-10 paid sick days, 3-5 bereavement days).

## Factual uncertainty notes

- **FMLA 50-employee threshold counts all US employees, not just Arkansas ones.** This matters for multi-state employers. Drafted prose makes this explicit.
- **The 75-mile-radius rule** for FMLA-covered worksites means a fully remote employer might not be FMLA-covered for an Arkansas hire even at 50+ headcount if their employees are geographically dispersed. Drafted prose treats it as a parenthetical.
- **Arkansas Act 850 of 2021** — flagged as a Arkansas-specific differentiator vs sibling no-statute states (Alabama). Voluntary only; no take-up data published by AR Insurance Department. Drafted prose surfaces the framework but does not over-claim adoption rates.
- **Little Rock municipal policy** — applies only to City of Little Rock employees, not Pulaski County private employers. Drafted prose makes this explicit so a private employer doesn't misread it as a statewide rule.
- **No conflict with WebSearch** — every reputable source agrees Arkansas has no mandatory state PFL or PSL for private employers.
