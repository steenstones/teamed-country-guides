Does Arkansas require paid family or sick leave in 2026?

No. Arkansas has no mandatory state paid family leave law and no state paid sick leave law for private employers. Federal FMLA gives 12 weeks unpaid at 50+ employees. Arkansas Act 850 of 2021 sanctions voluntary private-insurer PFL products. Everything else is the policy you write.

Arkansas in 2026 has no mandatory state paid family leave law and no state paid sick leave law for private employers. The only mandatory leave floor is federal FMLA (29 U.S.C. § 2601), which provides 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period for employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. An employee qualifies after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked. Below the 50-employee threshold, federal FMLA does not apply, and Arkansas has no equivalent state floor. Arkansas Act 850 of 2021 (A.C.A. § 23-86-501) separately authorises licensed Arkansas insurance carriers to offer voluntary paid family leave insurance products on an opt-in basis; no employer or employee mandate, no state-administered claim portal. Everything else (paid parental, paid sick, paid bereavement, paid PTO) is voluntary, designed by the employer. Federal Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA, June 2023), USERRA for military duty, Ark. Code Ann. § 12-62-413 for private-sector state-active-duty leave, and Ark. Code Ann. § 16-31-106 jury duty job-protection (unpaid for private employers, retaliation a Class A misdemeanour) apply on top regardless of headcount.

Does Arkansas require paid family leave?

No, not for private employers. Arkansas has no mandatory state paid family leave law for the private sector. Nine US states plus DC do require it (California, Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Washington, and Washington DC); Arkansas is not one of them. There is no Arkansas payroll deduction for PFL, no state PFL claim portal for employees, and no state PFL programme to fund.

There is one Arkansas-specific wrinkle worth knowing. Act 850 of 2021, codified at Ark. Code Ann. § 23-86-501 et seq., authorises licensed Arkansas insurance carriers to offer voluntary paid family leave insurance products to Arkansas employers and employees. The framework sits in the insurance code rather than the labour code; it creates a regulated route for an employer that wants to offer paid family leave through an insurance vehicle without building a self-funded programme. The Arkansas Insurance Department supervises the carriers. Neither employer nor employee is required to enrol. This is unusual for a no-statute state and gives Arkansas-headquartered employers an off-the-shelf option that sibling states like Alabama and Tennessee do not have a sanctioned framework for.

A second carve-out applies only to state-government employees. Act 770 of 2023 grants eligible Arkansas state employees up to 12 weeks of paid maternity leave for qualifying events, administered by the Office of Personnel Management. This entitlement does not extend to private employers; it changes nothing for a private-sector Arkansas hire.

For a private Arkansas employer, hiring in Arkansas removes the state-level PFL withholding line that adds 0.5 to 1.0 percent of wages in California, New York, or Washington. There is no equivalent payroll deduction, no state PFL bond schedule, and no state agency claim portal to maintain. The cost line simply does not exist on an Arkansas payslip.

What this means for talent: an Arkansas employee comparing job offers against friends in California or New York will not have access to a state PFL programme to fall back on for the birth of a child or the care of a parent. The employer’s voluntary parental-leave policy carries the full weight of that retention conversation, and the gap between what your offer letter says and what a California or Bay Area employer offers is fully visible to the candidate. The City of Little Rock moved its municipal employees to 12 weeks of paid parental leave in March 2026 (6 weeks for under-one-year tenure), the first Arkansas city with a formal policy. That sets a 2026 reference point for Pulaski County knowledge-work compensation conversations.

Does Arkansas require paid sick leave?

No. Arkansas has no state paid sick leave law. Eighteen US states and dozens of cities require accrued paid sick time (California, Colorado, Connecticut, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Washington, plus DC); Arkansas is not one of them. The federal floor is also nothing for the private sector. Sick days at an Arkansas job are whatever the offer letter says they are.

There is also no city or county sick-time ordinance in Arkansas. The Arkansas Minimum Wage Act and the Arkansas Wage Payment Act regulate pay frequency, minimum wage ($11.00 per hour), and final-wage timing, but neither statute requires accrued paid sick time. The Arkansas General Assembly has consistently preempted local government from setting wage-and-leave floors above the state baseline.

This makes the offer-letter sick-day line load-bearing for retention. SHRM’s 2025 benefits survey shows the median Arkansas private-sector employer offers 5 paid sick days per year, accrued at roughly one hour per 30 hours worked. Competitive employers in knowledge-work sectors (tech in Bentonville and Fayetteville, professional services in Little Rock, healthcare administration in Springdale) typically go to 7 to 10 sick days, often consolidated into a single PTO bank with vacation rather than tracked separately.

One nuance: although there is no Arkansas state PSL, an Arkansas employee may still qualify for unpaid leave under federal FMLA if the absence is for a serious health condition (defined as inpatient care or continuing treatment by a healthcare provider, lasting more than three consecutive days) and the employer is FMLA-covered. A bad case of flu generally does not qualify; pneumonia hospitalisation typically does. Job protection during a serious-health-condition absence is the point, not the pay.

What does federal FMLA give Arkansas employees?

Federal FMLA gives qualifying Arkansas employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period, with group health coverage continued at the employer’s normal contribution. It applies only to employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. The employee qualifies after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked in the preceding 12 months.

The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 is the federal floor for unpaid, job-protected leave. It overrides nothing in Arkansas because Arkansas has no competing state floor for private employers. The 50-employee threshold counts every US employee, not just Arkansas ones, which catches multi-state employers off guard. Arkansas’s Act 850 voluntary insurance framework sits alongside FMLA; it adds an opt-in route to paid coverage without changing the underlying federal floor.

Five qualifying reasons trigger FMLA leave:

  • Birth and care of a newborn, within 12 months of birth
  • Placement of a child with the employee for adoption or for a placement under state child-placement law, within 12 months of placement
  • Care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition
  • The employee’s own serious health condition that makes them unable to perform the job
  • Qualifying military exigency arising from a family member’s active duty in the Armed Forces; up to 26 weeks for military caregiver leave

The 50-employee threshold counts your whole US workforce

An employer with 28 Arkansas employees and 25 Texas employees crosses the 50-employee FMLA threshold even though neither state alone gets there. The Department of Labor counts every employee on the US payroll for any 20 or more weeks of the current or preceding year. The 75-mile-radius rule then determines which specific worksites are FMLA-covered, which matters for fully remote employers whose employees are geographically dispersed across the south-central states.

What FMLA does not require

  • Pay during leave. FMLA leave is unpaid by statute. An employee may substitute accrued PTO; the employer may require it. There is no FMLA wage-replacement benefit. An Arkansas employer that has purchased a voluntary Act 850 PFL insurance policy may offer paid weeks alongside the federal floor; an Arkansas employer without such a policy offers only unpaid FMLA weeks unless the company self-funds a paid parental policy.
  • FMLA leave for employers under 50. A 25-employee Arkansas company has no FMLA obligation and Arkansas has no mini-FMLA. Below the threshold, parental leave and serious-illness leave are entirely voluntary.
  • FMLA leave for the first 12 months. A 3-month tenure Arkansas employee with a new baby has no FMLA right to job-protected leave; the employer may grant unpaid leave voluntarily and may terminate at any time, subject only to the federal anti-discrimination floor and the Arkansas at-will doctrine.

What protections apply for pregnancy and disability in Arkansas?

Federal law applies; Arkansas adds nothing specific beyond the at-will doctrine and the Civil Rights Act of 1993. The Pregnancy Discrimination Act (PDA, 1978) treats pregnancy like any other temporary disability for employer leave and benefits. The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA), effective 27 June 2023, requires reasonable accommodation for pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions, applying to employers with 15 or more employees. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) covers pregnancy-related disabilities such as gestational diabetes or severe preeclampsia.

The practical reading for an Arkansas employer at 15 to 50 employees: PWFA covers reasonable accommodation (modified duties, schedule changes, time off for medical appointments, lactation breaks), but FMLA does not yet cover the 12 weeks of job-protected leave. The accommodation conversation under PWFA may include voluntary unpaid leave as the accommodation, but the employer has no statutory obligation to hold the job open beyond the accommodation period.

At 50+ employees the package stacks: PWFA accommodation during the pregnancy, FMLA for the birth and bonding, ADA for any post-delivery serious complications. The three federal laws together resemble what a single state PFL programme covers in California or New York, except every line is unpaid unless the employer voluntarily layers pay on top, either through a self-funded parental-leave policy or through an Act 850 voluntary PFL insurance product.

What this means for offer letters

The most retention-critical voluntary line on an Arkansas offer letter is paid parental leave. The federal floor (12 weeks unpaid at FMLA-covered employers, accommodation under PWFA at 15+) leaves a wide gap that voluntary policy fills. Mid-market Arkansas employers commonly offer 6 to 8 weeks of paid maternity leave and 2 to 4 weeks of paid paternity or partner leave; top-quartile knowledge-work employers in Bentonville, Fayetteville, and Little Rock offer 12 to 16 weeks paid for the primary caregiver regardless of gender. The Walmart, Tyson, and J.B. Hunt benchmark for north-west Arkansas knowledge workers sits at the upper end of that range. What about military leave and jury duty in Arkansas?

Federal USERRA protects civilian jobs for service members. Ark. Code Ann. § 12-62-413 mirrors USERRA for Arkansas private-sector employees called by the Governor to state active duty. Jury duty is mandatory; Ark. Code Ann. § 16-31-106 protects an employee’s job, but private Arkansas employers are not required to pay regular wages during jury service. Retaliation against a juror is a Class A misdemeanour with fines up to $2,500.

USERRA and Arkansas military leave

The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA, 38 U.S.C. § 4301 et seq.) provides civilian reemployment rights for service members called to active duty, with a cumulative cap of five years of military absence. The employer pays nothing during the leave itself, but health-plan coverage must continue (employee may pay up to 102 percent of the full premium for COBRA-style continuation). On return, the employee is reinstated to the position they would have held had they not left, the so-called escalator principle.

Arkansas private-sector employees pick up matching protections under Ark. Code Ann. § 12-62-413 when the Governor calls members of the Arkansas National Guard or state militia to active state duty. The statute extends USERRA-equivalent leave, reinstatement, and benefits to state activations. Remedies for violations include reinstatement, back pay, and punitive damages. Public-sector employees fall under a more generous separate regime: Ark. Code Ann. § 21-4-212 grants Arkansas state employees 15 days per calendar year of paid military leave for annual training or other official duty, plus necessary travel time, without loss of pay and in addition to regular vacation. That public-employee entitlement does not extend to private employers; it is a state-personnel rule.

Jury duty in Arkansas

Ark. Code Ann. § 16-31-106 requires private employers to grant unpaid time off for jury service and prohibits the employer from requiring the employee to use annual, vacation, or sick leave for the absence. Ark. Code Ann. § 16-31-107 makes retaliation against an employee for jury service a Class A misdemeanour, with fines up to $2,500 and civil remedies including reinstatement and lost wages.

Unlike Alabama’s pay-continuation rule, Arkansas does not require private employers to continue regular pay during jury service. Public-sector employers (state and county) must grant paid leave. The court pays jurors a small per diem. Most Arkansas knowledge-work employers voluntarily continue full pay during jury duty as a retention signal, treating it as a low-cost benefit because jury service is infrequent and the alternative leaves the employee mildly penalised for civic participation.

Source: Arkansas Code, Sections 12-62-413, 21-4-212, 16-31-106, and 16-31-107; USERRA at 38 U.S.C. § 4301 et seq.; Justia primary text; accessed 21 May 2026.

What about employers with fewer than 50 employees in Arkansas?

Below the federal FMLA threshold, an Arkansas employer has no mandatory leave law to comply with at all, with three narrow exceptions: PWFA accommodation at 15+ employees, USERRA reemployment for service members (plus the Ark. § 12-62-413 state-activation mirror), and Ark. § 16-31-106 jury duty job protection. Everything else is voluntary, including paid parental leave, paid sick leave, paid bereavement, and unpaid time off for a child’s school visit.

The good news is the absence of compliance friction: no state PFL claim portal, no accrued-PSL ledger to maintain, no termination clock to manage during a protected leave at single-digit headcount. The bad news is the absence of employer cover: the small Arkansas employer is exposed to the full retention cost of a competitor offering a meaningful voluntary leave package.

The pattern we see at Teamed for clients hiring their first three to ten people in Arkansas is to write a short, clear voluntary leave policy that punches above the median: 8 weeks paid parental leave, 8 paid sick days per year, 3 paid bereavement days, and full pay during jury duty. The total cost is roughly 1.5 to 2.5 percent of payroll spread across the workforce, and it makes the difference between a Bentonville or Little Rock candidate accepting your offer and accepting an offer from a California-headquartered competitor with state PFL benefits stacked on top. For mid-market employers serious about a paid family leave benefit, the Act 850 voluntary insurance route deserves a quote-comparison alongside self-funding.

The headcount tipping point

An Arkansas employer hits the FMLA 50-employee threshold gradually as the US headcount grows, often without noticing. The trigger is 20 or more weeks of 50+ employees in either the current or preceding calendar year. Once tripped, the FMLA obligation continues for the rest of the current calendar year and the full following calendar year, even if headcount drops back below 50. The lookback rule catches employers who hire seasonally or scale through funding rounds; the FMLA obligation often outlasts the headcount that triggered it.
