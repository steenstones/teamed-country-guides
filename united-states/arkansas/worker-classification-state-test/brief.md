## Verified data (sources at bottom)

### Arkansas's test — Empower Independent Contractors Act of 2019

Arkansas codified the **IRS 20-factor common-law test** (Rev. Rul. 87-41) via the **Empower Independent Contractors Act of 2019** (Act 1055 of 2019, HB 1850), effective **24 July 2019**. The Act is codified at **Ark. Code §§ 11-1-201 to 11-1-204**.

The operative provision is **Ark. Code § 11-1-204** — "Determination of Employment Status" — which mandates that the 20-factor test be used by any employer or agency determining worker employment status in Arkansas.

### Statutory cross-references

The 2019 Act amended three other statutes to point to the 20-factor test:

- **Ark. Code § 11-10-210** (Employment definition for unemployment insurance) — cross-references the 20-factor test required by § 11-1-201 et seq.
- **Ark. Code § 11-9-102** (Workers' Compensation "Employee" definition) — cross-references the 20-factor test required by § 11-1-201 et seq.
- Wage and hour, equal pay, and state tax classifications all default to the same 20-factor test under Act 1055

### Distinctive Arkansas angle

**Single statutory test across all state purposes.** Most US states run conflicting tests: California uses ABC for wage/UI but common-law for tax; Massachusetts uses ABC for wage but common-law for UI; Alabama codified the 20-factor test for tax and UI but kept right-of-control for workers' comp. **Arkansas applies the same 20-factor test to all five state-law purposes** (UI, workers' comp, state income tax, wage and hour, equal pay). Get the test right once and you're aligned everywhere.

### The 20 factors (IRS Rev. Rul. 87-41, adopted by Arkansas)

Grouped by IRS into three categories:

**Behavioural control** (right to direct how work is done):
1. Instructions — does the firm tell worker when, where, how to work?
2. Training — does the firm train the worker in its methods?
3. Integration — are worker's services integrated into the firm's business?
4. Services rendered personally — must worker do the work personally?
5. Hiring, supervising, paying assistants — does the firm or worker?
6. Continuing relationship — recurring engagement or one-off?
7. Set hours of work — does firm set hours?
8. Full time required — must worker devote full time to firm?
9. Doing work on employer's premises — at firm's office or worker's?
10. Order or sequence set — does firm dictate work order?

**Financial control**:
11. Oral or written reports — required by firm?
12. Payment method — hourly/weekly (employee) vs by project (contractor)?
13. Expenses — who pays travel and business expenses?
14. Tools and materials — who furnishes them?
15. Significant investment — does worker have own facilities/equipment?
16. Profit or loss — can worker realise profit or suffer loss?

**Relationship of parties**:
17. Working for more than one firm at a time — exclusive or not?
18. Making service available to general public — does worker advertise?
19. Right to discharge — can firm fire at will?
20. Right to terminate — can worker quit without contractual breach?

### Misclassification exposure (Arkansas)

Stacked liability across at least five categories:

1. **Federal payroll tax (FICA, FUTA)** — unpaid employer 7.65% FICA + FUTA shortfall + IRC § 3509 reduced-rate penalty (unintentional) or full liability (intentional)
2. **Arkansas state income tax withholding** — top rate 3.9% (2024–2026), plus DFA penalty and interest
3. **Arkansas unemployment insurance (SUTA)** — unpaid contributions at new-employer rate of 2.0% on first $7,000 per worker per year (max ~$140 per year per worker)
4. **Federal FLSA back wages and overtime** — 2-year lookback (3 if wilful), plus liquidated damages equal to back wages
5. **Workers' compensation premium gap** — missed AWCC premium plus personal liability for any uncovered injury during period

### Arkansas-specific penalty layers

- **§ 11-10-519** — penalty for false statement or misrepresentation in UI matters; civil + criminal
- **§ 11-9-106** — workers' compensation misrepresentation penalty
- **SUTA dumping (DWS guidance)** — violations or attempted violations = **2% rate increase in the year of violation and the three succeeding years, plus a 10% penalty on total UI taxes due**

### Cost example for an employer

A misclassified $60,000-per-year Arkansas worker over 3 years, treated as 1099 then re-classified by audit:

- Federal employer FICA: $60,000 × 7.65% × 3 = **$13,770**
- Arkansas state income tax withholding (gap): ~3.9% × $60,000 × 3 = **~$7,020**, plus DFA penalty
- Arkansas SUTA gap: $140 × 3 = **$420** (very low — $7,000 wage base)
- Federal FLSA back wages + overtime (assume 8 hours weekly OT at 1.5x): ~**$30,000 back wages + $30,000 liquidated damages**
- Workers' comp premium gap: variable; uncovered injury can exceed six figures

**Conservative range: $20,000 to $80,000 per misclassified worker over 3 years**, before legal fees. Arkansas's state-level exposure is lower than higher-rate states (CA, NY) because both the income tax rate (3.9%) and the SUTA wage base ($7,000) are among the lowest in the US. Federal exposure dominates.

### Recent and pending changes

- **24 July 2019 — Act 1055** went live (the 20-factor codification).
- **27 February 2026 — federal DOL NPRM** (RIN 1235-AA46) to rescind 2024 "totality of circumstances" rule and restore 2021 "economic reality" test for FLSA. **Arkansas state-level test unaffected** — it sits in state statute (Act 1055), not federal regulation.
