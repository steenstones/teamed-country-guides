Which worker classification test does Arkansas use?

Arkansas applies the IRS 20-factor common-law test (Rev. Rul. 87-41), codified by the Empower Independent Contractors Act of 2019 at Ark. Code § 11-1-204. The same statutory test governs all state-law worker-classification questions: unemployment insurance, workers’ compensation, state income tax withholding, wage and hour, and equal pay. Arkansas has no state-specific ABC test.

Act 1055 of 2019 (HB 1850), signed 17 April 2019 and effective 24 July 2019, did three things at once. It enacted a new subchapter at Ark. Code §§ 11-1-201 to 11-1-204 codifying the 20-factor framework. It amended Ark. Code § 11-10-210 (the unemployment insurance employment definition) to cross-reference the new test. And it amended Ark. Code § 11-9-102 (the workers’ compensation employee definition) to do the same. The result is a single statutory test that the Arkansas Division of Workforce Services auditor, the Arkansas Workers’ Compensation Commission judge, and the Department of Finance and Administration examiner are all required to apply.

What are the 20 factors in Arkansas’s codified test?

The 20 factors come straight from IRS Rev. Rul. 87-41, incorporated by reference into Ark. Code § 11-1-204. The IRS now groups them into three categories: behavioural control (10 factors), financial control (6 factors), and relationship of the parties (4 factors). No single factor controls. The auditor weighs the full picture against the right-to-control benchmark.

Arkansas chose the 20-factor framing on purpose. The 20 factors are old, well-documented, and predictable. Every reasonable adviser already knows them. Codifying the IRS list rather than inventing a new state test removes interpretive drift between the Arkansas DWS auditor and the federal IRS examiner reviewing the same worker. That matters because federal payroll tax and Arkansas SUTA exposure typically rise or fall together on a misclassification.

How does Arkansas apply one test across UI, workers’ comp, tax, and wages?

By statute. Act 1055 of 2019 amended three other Arkansas codes at the same time it created the 20-factor framework: the UI code (§ 11-10-210), the workers’ comp code (§ 11-9-102), and the state tax and wage-and-hour provisions all now cross-reference the same Ark. Code § 11-1-204 test. That cross-referencing is what makes Arkansas distinctive. Most US states run conflicting tests on the same worker.

How Arkansas compares to neighbouring states

The uniformity matters because the alternative is hard. California’s ABC test under AB5 applies to wage and hour but not state income tax. Massachusetts uses ABC for wage and hour but common-law for UI. Alabama codified the 20-factor test for UI and tax in 2021 but kept right-of-control for workers’ comp. Each of those split frameworks means the same worker can be a contractor under one state test and an employee under another, with stacked exposure across both.

Three practical consequences of Arkansas’s uniformity

  • Single audit defence. The same documentation, the same factor analysis, the same rationale memo works whether the audit comes from DWS (unemployment), AWCC (workers’ comp), or DFA (income tax). One file covers all three.
  • No carve-out drift. A worker who passes the 20-factor test for tax purposes cannot be quietly re-classified for workers’ comp purposes on a different test, the way it happens in Alabama or Arizona. Arkansas closed that gap by statute.
  • Federal floor still applies. The federal FLSA economic-reality test runs separately for overtime exposure. Arkansas state law cannot pre-empt the federal floor. But the federal layer is the only divergence on a typical hire.

What does misclassifying an Arkansas worker actually cost?

Stacked liability across six categories. A misclassified $60,000-per-year Arkansas worker, audited and re-classified after 3 years on 1099, typically exposes the employer to roughly $20,000 to $80,000 per worker in back taxes, FLSA double damages, and workers’ comp gap, before legal fees. Arkansas’s state-level dollar exposure is among the lowest in the US because the state income tax rate (3.9%) and the SUTA wage base ($7,000) are both at the low end of the national range.

That is the civil-and-tax audit story. The criminal track is separate. Arkansas treats wilful misrepresentation in unemployment insurance matters as a misdemeanour under Ark. Code § 11-10-519, with civil repayment and 10 percent penalty as the standard remedy and criminal exposure layered in aggravated cases. Workers’ comp misrepresentation under Ark. Code § 11-9-106 carries its own civil and criminal penalties for false statements in connection with coverage or claims.

Why the Arkansas state-level numbers look smaller than other states

Two structural reasons. First, Arkansas’s top state income tax rate is 3.7 percent (cut from 3.9 percent retroactive to 1 January 2026 under SB1, the fourth consecutive Arkansas tax cut since 2022), one of the lowest rates in the US, so the state withholding exposure on a misclassification is materially lower than in a state like California or New York. Second, the SUTA wage base is $7,000, matching the federal FUTA floor and among the lowest in the country, so the unemployment-tax exposure caps out at the federal floor. The federal layer (FICA, FUTA, FLSA) dominates the Arkansas stack. State-level exposure typically adds 5 to 15 percent on top of the federal cost.

How does the 2026 federal rule change interact with Arkansas’s test?

On 27 February 2026, the federal Department of Labor issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking to rescind the 2024 ‘totality of the circumstances’ FLSA test and restore the 2021 economic reality test. The proposal affects federal FLSA wage and hour analysis only. Arkansas’s state-level 20-factor test is unaffected, because it sits in state statute (Act 1055 of 2019), not federal regulation.

The federal proposal matters for FLSA overtime exposure, which is one of the six stacked liability categories above. If the proposed rule lands, the threshold for finding a worker is an ‘employee’ for FLSA overtime purposes tightens slightly compared with the 2024 rule. The state-level Arkansas analysis under the 20-factor test runs on its own track, so a worker who passes the 20-factor test for Arkansas purposes can still be analysed under the new federal economic-reality test for FLSA, and reach a different conclusion in the edge cases.

Practical effect for an Arkansas hire

For typical full-time-equivalent roles, both tests reach the same answer: employee. The divergence sits in the edge cases, the highly-specialised consultant who works from home, uses their own tools, sets their own hours, and is paid by deliverable. That profile passes the 20-factor test and passes economic-reality. The role that fails one usually fails the other too. Where they diverge, the conservative approach is the answer with the broader coverage, which is usually ‘employee’.
