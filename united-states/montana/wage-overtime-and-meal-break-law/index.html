Key facts at a glance

Montana's minimum wage is $10.85/hour from 1st January 2026 (CPI-adjusted annually, up from $10.55 in 2025). No tip credit is allowed; every employee receives the full rate regardless of tips. Overtime is owed at 1.5x for hours over 40 in a workweek; there is no daily trigger. No adult meal-break statute in Montana; federal FLSA paid-break rules apply instead.

All rates are provisional. The annual CPI adjustment is announced by 30th September each year, effective 1st January. Verify the current rate against the Montana DLI 2026 Minimum Wage Poster before each payroll cycle.

What is Montana's minimum wage in 2026?

$10.85 per hour from 1st January 2026. Montana's rate rose from $10.55 in 2025 following the annual CPI adjustment, which is announced by 30th September and rounds to the nearest $0.05.

$10.85 / hour from Jan 2026
CPI-adjusted annually

Montana's minimum wage is set under the Montana Minimum Wage Law (MCA §39-3-404) and adjusts each year using the August-to-August Consumer Price Index. If CPI is negative in any period, the rate does not decrease; it holds at the prior year's level. Montana's higher state rate governs wherever the employer is covered by both state law and the federal FLSA; the federal $7.25/hour floor applies only where the state exemption applies.

Annual verification required

The Montana Department of Labor and Industry publishes the updated minimum wage poster each autumn. Your payroll must reflect the new rate by 1st January. Confirm each year at erd.dli.mt.gov before the December payroll cycle.

Small business exception

Montana permits a reduced rate of $4.00/hour for businesses that meet both conditions: annual gross sales of $110,000 or less, and no coverage under the federal FLSA. In practice, most employers of remote or distributed workers will be covered by the FLSA because of interstate commerce and revenue tests. If in doubt, assume the $10.85 state rate applies.

Tip credit prohibition

Montana is one of the few states that bans tip credits entirely. Every employee, including tipped restaurant and hospitality workers, must receive the full $10.85/hour minimum wage regardless of the tips they earn. No meal credit and no training wage exist under Montana law. Your payroll obligation is $10.85/hour before tips; tips are paid on top.

How does overtime work in Montana?

Overtime is owed at 1.5x the regular rate for all hours worked beyond 40 in a single workweek. Montana has no daily overtime trigger; the threshold is the 7-day workweek total only.

Montana follows the federal FLSA overtime structure. The workweek is any fixed, regularly recurring period of 168 consecutive hours (7 consecutive 24-hour periods). You define the workweek at the point of hire; it cannot be changed retroactively to avoid overtime liability. An employee who works 12 hours on Monday and nothing for the rest of the week owes no overtime under Montana or federal law.

Regular rate of pay

Overtime is calculated at 1.5x the "regular rate," not simply the posted hourly wage. The regular rate must include all remuneration for employment except statutory exclusions: shift differentials, non-discretionary production bonuses, and commission payments must be factored in before the multiplier applies. Discretionary bonuses, overtime premiums already paid, holiday gifts, and employer contributions to benefit plans are excluded from the regular rate calculation.

At Montana's $10.85/hour minimum, the minimum overtime rate is $16.275/hour. For salaried non-exempt employees, divide the weekly salary by hours actually worked to determine the regular rate, then pay 0.5x that rate for each overtime hour.

White-collar exemptions

Montana adopts the FLSA white-collar exemptions. An employee is exempt from overtime if they satisfy all three conditions:

  • Salary level: paid at least $684/week ($35,568/year) on a salary or fee basis (the federal threshold reinstated after the November 2024 court vacatur of the DOL's higher rules)
  • Salary basis: receives a predetermined, fixed salary not subject to reduction based on the quality or quantity of work performed
  • Duties test: primary duties qualify under the Executive, Administrative, Professional, Outside Sales, or Computer Employee exemption categories as defined by the FLSA
Salary threshold note

The DOL's 2024 rule that raised the overtime salary threshold to $43,888 (July 2024) and $58,656 (January 2025) was vacated by a federal district court in November 2024. The reinstated threshold is $684/week ($35,568/year). This is a fast-moving area; confirm the current federal level before relying on any salaried exemption.

Does Montana require meal breaks or rest periods?

No. Montana has no state statute requiring meal breaks or rest periods for adult employees. Break obligations come from federal FLSA rules on how breaks must be paid, and from whatever you include in your employment contracts.

Montana's Department of Labor and Industry confirms there is no adult meal-break or rest-period statute under Montana law. This is a common compliance assumption teams make when hiring remote workers in new states. Montana does not mirror California's mandatory 30-minute unpaid meal break after 5 hours, nor does it require any specific rest period. What you write into your policy is what you run.

Federal FLSA rules that do apply

The FLSA does not require meal or rest breaks either, but it governs how breaks must be paid when you provide them:

  • Short rest breaks (20 minutes or fewer): must be treated as compensable time and counted in the workweek total for overtime purposes.
  • Bona fide meal periods (30 minutes or more): need not be paid, provided the employee is completely relieved of all duties. If the employee must remain reachable, monitor systems, or respond to queries during the period, it becomes compensable time and counts toward the 40-hour overtime threshold.
Practical guidance

Even without a legal mandate, most Montana employers include a 30-minute unpaid meal break in their standard work day. Put the break policy in the offer letter. If the role requires on-call availability during lunch, that time is compensable; track it or remove the on-call requirement to preserve unpaid status. Ambiguity here creates wage-hour exposure in audits.

Minors under 16

Montana's child labour statutes set mandatory break protections for minor employees. Workers under 16 must receive a rest break after consecutive hours of work. Confirm the current minor-labour break schedule with Montana DLI before onboarding any worker under 16 into a role with extended shifts.

Final pay and wage protection rules in Montana

Involuntarily terminated employees must receive all final wages immediately on their last day. Employees who resign receive payment by the next regular payday. Montana Code Annotated §39-3-205 sets these deadlines; there is no grace period.

The Montana Wage Protection Act (MCA §39-3-201 et seq.) is the primary statute governing wage payment, timing, and enforcement in Montana. Under §39-3-205:

  • Involuntary termination: all earned wages must be paid immediately upon the employee's last day of employment. No waiting for the next scheduled payday.
  • Voluntary resignation: wages are due by the next regular payday following the employee's last day of work.
  • Disputed wages: the employer must pay the undisputed portion immediately while the dispute is resolved separately.

What counts as "wages"

Montana courts interpret "wages" broadly. Final pay includes all earned but unpaid wages at the agreed rate, any accrued vacation pay if your written policy treats accrued leave as wages on separation, and commissions that have been earned under the terms of your commission plan prior to the separation date. Policy language matters: if your employee handbook says "accrued PTO is forfeited on separation," that provision is enforceable. If it is silent, courts tend to award accrued leave.

00 Days to wait after involuntary termination

Montana is one of the strictest states on final-pay timing. Involuntary discharge triggers an immediate obligation. Missing that deadline exposes the employer to penalties and civil claims under the Wage Protection Act. Process final pay on the termination date, not the next payroll cycle.

Payroll frequency

Montana requires wages to be paid at least semi-monthly (twice per month) for most private employers. Whatever frequency you choose within that constraint, document it in the offer letter and apply it consistently. Unilateral changes to pay schedules without notice can be treated as a wage delay under the Wage Protection Act.

Wage reduction notice

Montana does not have a specific statutory notice period for prospective wage reductions in the same form as some other states, but general contract principles apply. Any reduction below the agreed rate requires clear advance written notice before the next pay cycle in which the reduced rate takes effect. Give the notice, document delivery, and retain a copy in the personnel file.
