Montana is one of a minority of US states with no earned sick time law. There is no accrual rate (like California's 1/30 or Arizona's 1/30 rules), no annual cap requirement, no poster mandate, and no paystub disclosure obligation arising from a sick leave statute. Private-sector employers are entirely free to design whatever sick leave programme fits their business.

State employees are a different matter. Permanent state employees accrue sick leave credits from their first day of service under MCA §§ 2-15-110 through 2-15-119. Full-time permanent employees earn sick leave at a rate set by those sections and are entitled to payment of accrued sick leave after 90 days of continuous service on separation. None of these provisions apply to private employers.

The freedom from a state mandate cuts both ways. You have maximum flexibility to design a competitive sick leave programme. But in a state where the WDEA makes your written policies legally binding, that flexibility comes with a drafting obligation most employers underestimate.

If you write a sick leave policy that says employees accrue eight hours per month, that accrued balance is an earned benefit. Policies that describe forfeiture on termination, or that create a use-it-or-lose-it cap, must be unambiguous or they become contested wages. Montana courts have treated accrued vacation as earned wages where policy language created a reasonable expectation of payment. The same reasoning can reach sick leave if your policy creates that expectation.

Does Montana have a state paid family and medical leave programme?

No. Montana has no state PFML programme. There is no payroll deduction, no state benefit fund, and no enacted legislation as of May 2026. Federal FMLA is the only statutory protection, and it starts at 50 employees.

Montana is not among the thirteen US states (plus Washington D.C.) that operate a state-funded paid family and medical leave programme. States including California, Colorado, Connecticut, Massachusetts, New York, Oregon, and Washington have all enacted employee-funded PFML schemes with payroll deductions and state benefit funds. Montana has not.

There is no Montana PFML bill that has passed as of May 2026. Remote employees hired in Montana by employers based in PFML states are not covered by their home-state's programme while working in Montana. They are governed by Montana law, which provides no equivalent protection.

This gap matters when you're hiring your first Montana remote worker and they previously lived in a PFML state. The expectation gap is real. A California hire moving to Montana loses California's state disability and PFL benefits on Montana hire. Your offer letter should address this explicitly, either by designing a voluntary paid parental and family leave policy or by being clear about what the programme does and does not include.

State employees have limited access to a parental leave benefit for birth or placement of a child, but this is a government-employer provision only. It does not apply to private companies.

The leave picture for Montana private-sector employers:

  1. Federal FMLA (29 U.S.C. § 2601), mandatory for employers with 50 or more employees within 75 miles; 12 weeks unpaid, job-protected
  2. Pregnancy leave (MCA § 49-2-310), reasonable unpaid leave for pregnancy; no paid entitlement mandated
  3. Voluntary employer PFML, entirely at your discretion; no statutory floor beyond the above

When does FMLA apply to Montana employers?

Federal FMLA applies when you have 50 or more employees within 75 miles of the worksite. Below that, there is no equivalent Montana state law. For distributed remote teams in Montana, this threshold catches many employers off guard.

The Family and Medical Leave Act (29 U.S.C. § 2601) applies in Montana exactly as in every other state. But Montana has no state analogue. There is no Small Employer Family Leave Act, no mini-FMLA applying at smaller headcounts, and no employer-size phase-in. If you're below 50 employees measured within 75 miles, your only leave obligation is what you write.

Employee eligibility requires:

  • At least 12 months of employment with the employer
  • At least 1,250 hours worked in the preceding 12-month period
  • The employer has 50 or more employees within 75 miles of the employee's worksite

Eligible employees receive 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per year for qualifying reasons: serious health condition of the employee or an immediate family member, birth or adoption of a child, or qualifying military exigency. FMLA leave for a serious health condition of a covered servicemember extends to 26 weeks.

Montana has no state extension of FMLA. A Montana employer with 45 employees owes their Montana workers zero weeks of statutory family or medical leave. That is a sharp contrast with states like California (CFRA at 5 employees), Oregon (OFLA at 10 employees), or Colorado (FAMLI at 10 employees).

How does the WDEA affect sick leave and vacation policy design?

Montana's Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act makes written employer policies legally binding. A sick leave or vacation policy that creates an accrual expectation can become a deferred-wages claim on termination. This is the single biggest leave-related risk most Montana employers don't see coming.

Montana is the only US state where at-will employment ends after the probationary period. Under the WDEA (MCA 39-2-901), terminations of post-probation employees require good cause and can result in up to four years of lost wages and fringe benefits. Crucially, fringe benefits are included in the damages calculation.

That matters for leave policy in two ways:

Accrued leave as earned wages. Montana courts have found that accrued vacation pay constitutes earned wages where the employer's written policy creates a reasonable expectation of payment. If your handbook says employees accrue 1.25 days per month, that accrued balance is wages, not a gratuitous benefit you can forfeit at will. Forfeiture clauses must be explicit, unambiguous, and consistently applied.

Sick leave that looks like vacation. Policies that blur the line between sick leave and PTO create forfeiture risk. A combined PTO bank described as "use or lose" with no clear forfeiture language can expose the employer on termination. Montana courts look at what the policy actually says, not what the employer intended. The rule of interpretation runs against the drafter.

Best practice for Montana employers:

  • State explicitly whether accrued sick leave is forfeited on termination and whether accrued vacation is paid out
  • Use separate sick and vacation buckets if your policy treats them differently on exit
  • Ensure offer letter, handbook, and employment agreement are consistent on every point relating to leave
  • Do not describe a forfeiture clause in the handbook but then pay out accrued leave in practice, the practice modifies the policy

What sick leave and family leave benefits should you offer in Montana?

The absence of a mandate is an opportunity, not a reason to do nothing. Montana's small-employer environment means your benefits package is a genuine differentiator. Here's what competitive Montana employers are designing.

Montana is a small-employer state. Approximately 90% of Montana businesses have fewer than 20 employees. Many compete for the same professional and technical talent as remote-first companies that offer California- or Oregon-level benefits. Because Montana imposes no sick leave floor, the gap between your policy and a competitor's policy is entirely visible to candidates.

What competitive employers in Montana are offering in 2026:

  • Sick leave: 5 to 10 days per year, either accrued (1 hour per 30 worked is common) or front-loaded annually. Accrual-based policies require clear carry-over and forfeiture language given WDEA.
  • Parental leave: 6 to 12 weeks paid parental leave for birth or placement, funded by the employer, not a state scheme. Common among companies hiring Montana remote workers from PFML states who expect parity.
  • PTO: Unlimited PTO policies are growing but carry WDEA risk if not drafted to exclude accrual. A true unlimited PTO policy with no accrual mechanism avoids the deferred-wages argument; a hybrid that tracks days and then calls it "unlimited" does not.
  • Short-term disability: Voluntary STD insurance fills the income-replacement gap that state PFML would otherwise cover. Premiums are employee-paid or employer-subsidised. Common denominations: 60% of weekly earnings for up to 12 weeks after a 7-day waiting period.

If you're hiring Montana remote workers from California, Oregon, or Colorado, where employees are accustomed to state PFML contributions and benefits, a voluntary STD and parental leave policy is the closest equivalent available. What does Montana law require for pregnancy and maternity leave?

Montana requires employers to provide reasonable unpaid leave for pregnancy under MCA § 49-2-310. No paid leave is mandated. This is an anti-discrimination requirement, not a paid benefit entitlement.

The Montana Human Rights Act at MCA § 49-2-310 requires employers to treat pregnancy as any other temporary disability for purposes of leave and return-to-work rights. You must offer the same leave terms you would extend to a similarly situated employee with a non-pregnancy medical condition. If your policy provides unpaid medical leave, you cannot deny it on grounds of pregnancy.

This is a floor, not a ceiling. The statute does not mandate paid leave, does not specify a duration, and does not require a policy more generous than your existing medical leave programme. In practice, the obligation is to:

  • Treat pregnancy-related absence as a medical leave under your existing policy
  • Permit a reasonable period of absence for childbirth and recovery
  • Restore the employee to the same or a comparable position on return

State employees with permanent full-time status may take up to 15 days of paid parental leave for birth or adoption (for the non-birthing parent or adoptive parent). This is not a private-sector entitlement.

If you are an FMLA-covered employer (50+ employees), pregnancy qualifies as a serious health condition under FMLA, and employees with 12 months service and 1,250 hours are entitled to 12 weeks unpaid leave. FMLA leave runs concurrently with any state or policy-based leave. Under FMLA, the employer designates the leave as FMLA when the qualifying condition arises; the employee cannot delay the designation.

What does Montana leave compliance require from employers?

Montana imposes no sick leave or PFML administrative requirements on private employers at state level. Your obligations are the federal layer plus the legal weight the WDEA places on whatever policy you write.

For Montana private employers, the compliance picture is:

FMLA administration (50+ employee employers only). Display the Department of Labor's FMLA poster. Provide FMLA notice within five business days of learning an employee may qualify. Designate leave as FMLA once the qualifying reason is established. Maintain FMLA records for three years. Teamed handles this automatically for covered employers.

Pregnancy non-discrimination (all employers). Treat pregnancy-related absence as medical leave under your existing policy. Restore employees to the same or comparable position. No additional posting requirement beyond general human rights notices.

WDEA-compliant leave drafting (all employers). Ensure offer letters and handbooks define accrual, carry-over, and forfeiture clearly. Inconsistent language between the offer letter and handbook has generated WDEA disputes in Montana courts. Both documents should be consistent and reviewed together.

No Montana sick leave poster or paystub disclosure requirement. Unlike Arizona or California, Montana does not require a state sick leave posting or per-paystub accrual disclosure. This is a genuine simplification for multi-state employers. Your payroll provider doesn't need to generate a Montana sick-leave line item on every paystub unless your own policy creates that obligation.

Minimum wage compliance. Any sick pay or other paid leave you provide must not fall below Montana's 2026 minimum wage of $10.85/hour. No tip credit is available; Montana prohibits tip credits entirely. The rate adjusts annually for CPI.

Private employers design their own sick leave policies. State employees accrue sick leave under MCA §§ 2-15-110 to 2-15-119, but those provisions do not bind private-sector employers.
Does Montana have a state paid family and medical leave programme?
No. Montana has no state PFML programme. There is no payroll deduction, no state benefit fund, and no enacted legislation as of May 2026. Federal FMLA (12 weeks unpaid, job-protected) applies only to employers with 50 or more employees within 75 miles of the worksite.
When does FMLA apply in Montana?
Federal FMLA applies to Montana employers with 50 or more employees within 75 miles of the worksite. Eligible employees must have 12 months of service and 1,250 hours worked in the preceding 12-month period. Below that threshold, no state or federal family leave protection applies.
Can Montana employers forfeit accrued vacation or sick leave on termination?
Only with a clear, unambiguous written forfeiture clause applied consistently. Montana courts have treated accrued vacation as earned wages where policy language created a reasonable expectation of payment. A WDEA-compliant forfeiture policy must be explicit, consistent with the offer letter, and applied without exception.
Is Montana's WDEA relevant to sick leave policy design?
Yes. Because the WDEA makes written employer policies legally binding, sick leave provisions in an offer letter or handbook carry real legal weight. A policy that creates an accrual expectation can become a deferred-wages claim on termination. Unambiguous drafting is more important in Montana than in most US states.
