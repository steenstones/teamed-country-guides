How does Montana’s worker classification state test work in 2026?

Montana runs an AB test — not the full ABC test used in California or Massachusetts. Prong B, the “outside usual course of business” prong, does not apply. To classify a worker as an independent contractor, you need to show freedom from direction and control, plus engagement in an independently established business. The Independent Contractor Central Unit issues Exemption Certificates. Construction adds a separate registration requirement. Get it wrong and you face penalties under § 39-3-209 on top of workers’ compensation back-premium exposure.

Montana does not run the full three-prong ABC test. It runs Prongs A and C only. Prong B — the rule that catches remote software contractors at California or Massachusetts ABC-test companies — does not apply here. That makes Montana noticeably more contractor-friendly than the strictest ABC-test states.

But there are two features that catch out-of-state employers. First, the Independent Contractor Central Unit: in Montana, independent contractors in most industries must obtain an Independent Contractor Exemption Certificate from the ICCU. Without one, the worker defaults to employee status for workers’ compensation purposes. Second, construction: every contractor performing construction work in Montana must register with the Department of Labor under the Construction Contractor Registration Act (§ 39-9-201).

Which worker classification test does Montana use?

Prong B — outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business — is not required in Montana for unemployment insurance purposes under MCA § 39-71-417.

For state income-tax withholding, the IRS 20-factor common-law right-of-control analysis applies. Workers’ compensation uses a separate control-test reading under the ICCU framework. Federal payroll tax still runs on the IRS common-law test.

Kieran is a Missoula-based product designer who consults for a software company on a fixed-scope UI project. He works from his own studio, uses his own tools, invoices at a project rate, and has three other active clients. Prongs A and C read contractor. He qualifies — provided he holds a valid ICCU certificate.

For most professional and knowledge-work roles in Montana, the IRS common-law test and the Montana AB test align. A contractor who passes Prong A (no direction or control) and Prong C (independently established business) will typically pass the IRS right-of-control analysis as well. The divergence from ABC-test states is in what you do not have to prove: you do not have to show the work falls outside your usual course of business.

The gap lies in the ICCU certificate regime. Even a worker who cleanly passes both prongs of the AB test defaults to employee status for workers’ compensation purposes if they do not hold a valid ICCU certificate. Compliance with the substantive test is necessary but not sufficient.

What are the two prongs of Montana’s AB test?

Two prongs. Both required. Prong A: the worker is free from the direction and control of the hiring entity, both in the contract and in fact. Prong C: the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business.

The Montana AB test sits in statute at MCA § 39-71-417, which sets out the conditions under which a person performing services is considered an independent contractor rather than an employee for workers’ compensation purposes. The Unemployment Insurance Division applies the same two-prong framework for state UI determinations under MCA § 39-51-203.

Prong A — free from direction and control

This prong asks whether the hiring entity controls not just the result of the work but the means and methods by which the work is performed. The key word in Montana’s statute is “direction and control” — both must be absent, not one or the other.

Montana courts and the DLI read Prong A broadly. An employer who does not exercise day-to-day supervision but reserves the contractual right to direct how the work is done will still fail Prong A. The existence of that right, even if never exercised, is control.

Kieran sets his own hours, works from his own studio, uses his own software licenses, delivers milestones rather than daily check-ins, and no one at the software company directs his methodology. Prong A passes.

Change the facts: Kieran is required to work within the company’s design system, attend daily standups on the company’s calendar, use company-issued Figma seats, and submit work for approval through the company’s internal review tool. Three of those four facts restore behavioural control to the hiring entity. Prong A fails, regardless of what the written contract says about contractor status.

Prong C — independently established business

Prong C asks whether the worker maintains an independently established trade or business that could exist without the particular hiring relationship. This is not satisfied by a business licence alone. The indicators are: the worker has their own tools and equipment, performs similar services for multiple clients, sets their own fees, markets their services to the public, and bears their own business expenses.

Kieran operates a registered sole proprietorship, has a portfolio website, has three active clients besides the software company, owns his own hardware, and sets his own project rates through a client engagement process he controls. Prong C passes.

A single-client “contractor” who has worked exclusively for one company for 18 months, uses that company’s tools, has no public-facing business presence, and has no current or recent relationships with other clients fails Prong C. The independent business exists on the invoice; not in practice.

What is the Montana Independent Contractor Exemption Certificate?

An Independent Contractor Exemption Certificate (ICEC) is a document issued by Montana’s Independent Contractor Central Unit (ICCU) that formally recognises a worker as an independent contractor for workers’ compensation purposes. Without an ICEC, a worker performing services in Montana is presumed to be an employee and must be covered under the hiring entity’s workers’ compensation policy.

The ICEC requirement sits at the intersection of the AB test and workers’ comp compliance. Passing both prongs of the substantive AB test establishes the contractor relationship on the facts. The ICEC is the administrative mechanism by which that status is recognised and recorded. Hiring an unregistered contractor without verifying their ICEC status creates workers’ comp exposure from day one.

How an ICEC works in practice

The ICEC is held by the contractor, not the hiring entity. Before engaging a Montana contractor, the hiring entity should verify that the contractor either holds a current ICEC or self-elects workers’ compensation insurance coverage for themselves. A contractor who carries their own workers’ comp policy can operate without an ICEC, but most sole proprietors and small-business contractors in Montana obtain the certificate rather than carrying their own policy.

The ICEC application is filed with the ICCU at Montana DLI. The ICCU reviews the application against the Prong A and Prong C criteria, confirms the absence of an employer-employee relationship, and issues a numbered certificate. The contractor’s ICEC number should be recorded in every engagement contract, master service agreement, and Statement of Work.

Construction contractors have a parallel requirement: in addition to the ICEC, they must register under the Construction Contractor Registration Act at MCA § 39-9-201. The two requirements are separate administrative processes.

What happens if a contractor works without an ICEC

If a Montana contractor is engaged without an ICEC and without self-elected coverage, and that worker is injured on the job, the hiring entity is exposed as the de facto employer for workers’ comp purposes. The Montana State Fund or an approved insurer will look back through the engagement, apply the Prong A and C analysis, and if either prong fails — or if neither was ever documented — the back-premium liability attaches to the hiring entity. That liability runs from the first day of engagement, not the day of injury.

What are the Montana construction contractor registration rules?

Any person or entity performing construction services for compensation in Montana must register as a Construction Contractor under MCA § 39-9-201 before performing any work. This is a separate requirement from the ICEC and applies to both employees and independent contractors engaged in construction.

The Construction Contractor Registration Act covers a broad definition of construction: new construction, renovation, repair, demolition, and alteration of structures. Residential and commercial work are both covered. Subcontractors must register independently — they cannot operate under a general contractor’s registration.

The double compliance check for construction engagements in Montana

A Montana construction engagement requires two confirmations before work starts. First, check that the subcontractor holds a current Construction Contractor Registration number under MCA § 39-9-201. Second, check that the subcontractor holds a valid ICEC — or carries their own workers’ compensation policy. Record both numbers in the sub-contract.

Jackson is an independent plumber in Great Falls who runs a sole proprietorship. He is registered under the Construction Contractor Registration Act, holds an ICEC, carries his own liability insurance, works for multiple GCs simultaneously, sets his own rates, and supplies his own tools. Both checks pass. A GC who books Jackson is protected on both the construction-registration front and the workers’ comp front.

Contrast a handyman who has no registration, no ICEC, no independent insurance, and works exclusively for one residential GC. The GC is exposed on both fronts: unregistered-subcontractor liability under MCA § 39-9-208 and workers’ comp back-premium liability under MCA § 39-71-417.

How does Montana’s workers’ compensation test work for independent contractors?

Montana workers’ compensation coverage is mandatory for most employers. The Montana State Fund and approved private insurers provide coverage. For independent contractors, the determining factor is whether the contractor holds a valid ICEC or has self-elected their own coverage. Without one of those two, the hiring entity carries the workers’ comp exposure.

The underlying substantive test is the AB test under MCA § 39-71-417. The ICEC is the administrative record of the contractor’s status. An employer whose contractor is injured and whose engagement file does not show a verified ICEC or self-elected coverage policy faces back-premium liability from the start of the engagement, plus potential civil penalties under MCA § 39-71-2907.

How does Montana’s AB test compare to other state worker classification tests?

Montana sits in the more contractor-friendly tier of US states. Its two-prong AB test is meaningfully less demanding than the full ABC tests used by California, Massachusetts, New Jersey, and other strict states. The absence of Prong B — which forces the hiring entity to show the work is outside its usual course of business — opens the contractor path for knowledge-work roles that would fail the Prong B screen in ABC-test states.

That said, Montana is not a pure common-law state like Texas or Florida. The AB test still requires a genuine independent business, and the ICCU certificate regime adds an administrative layer that pure common-law states do not have. Employers who treat Montana as “anything goes on 1099” — and skip the ICEC verification — create a workers’ comp exposure that a Texas or Florida contractor engagement would not carry.

What the ICEC regime costs a multi-state employer

Multi-state employers who maintain contractor workforces across the western US face a Montana-specific compliance layer that Idaho and Wyoming do not impose. A contractor who passes the substantive AB test but has not obtained their ICEC is, for Montana workers’ comp purposes, an employee of the hiring entity until the certificate is in hand. The practical instruction is simple: before any Montana contractor relationship goes live, request the contractor’s ICEC number — or confirm they carry a current self-elected workers’ comp policy — and record it in the contract file.

What a Montana audit typically looks like

Most Montana audits begin through one of three triggers: a former contractor files a state UI claim with the Unemployment Insurance Division, a workplace injury generates a workers’ comp claim that the ICCU reviews, or the Department of Labor selects an employer for a routine wage-and-hour audit under MCA § 39-3-202. Once any one agency opens a determination, the reclassification tends to flow across agencies through inter-agency data sharing. The DLI, the UI Division, and the Department of Revenue each have independent audit authority, and each can initiate assessments back to the start of the engagement.
