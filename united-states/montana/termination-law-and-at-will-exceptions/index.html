Montana termination law: the only US state that isn't at-will after probation

Last reviewed 31 May 2026 · Montana, United States guide

What makes Montana termination law different from every other US state?

Montana is the only US state where at-will employment does not apply after the probationary period. The Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act (WDEA, MCA 39-2-901 et seq.) requires good cause to dismiss a post-probation employee. The default probationary period is 12 months; employers may extend it in writing to a maximum of 18 months. Damages for wrongful discharge are capped at 4 years of lost wages and fringe benefits.

Montana is the exception

Every other US state treats employment as at-will: absent a contract or statutory protection, an employer can dismiss a worker for any reason or no reason with no liability. Montana tore up that assumption in 1987. The WDEA drew a line between the probationary period (at-will) and post-probation employment (good cause required). That line is the most under-explained fact in US employment compliance guides.

The statute doesn't require just cause (union-contract language). It requires good cause, defined at MCA 39-2-903(5) as reasonable job-related grounds for dismissal based on failure to satisfactorily perform job duties, disruption of the employer's operation, or other legitimate business reason. Subjective dislike, restructuring with no documented business rationale, or dismissal that looks pretextual won't survive a WDEA claim. The employer bears the burden of proof.

Two things the WDEA does that most employers miss: first, it preempts most common-law tort claims that would otherwise arise from wrongful discharge (MCA 39-2-913) — so it both creates a statutory cause of action and replaces the patchwork of common-law claims Montana courts previously recognised. Second, it caps damages at four years, which is a trade-off. Employers get predictable maximum exposure; employees get a clear statutory remedy without needing to plead tortious interference or fraudulent misrepresentation. Vettel-Becker v. Deaconess Medical Center (9th Cir.) confirmed the preemption scope.

How does the Montana probationary period work under the WDEA?

The WDEA default probationary period is 12 months from the date of hire. During probation, employment is at-will: you may dismiss for any reason or no reason, with notice. An employer may extend the period up to a maximum of 18 months, but only through a written policy communicated to the employee in an offer letter or employee handbook. If you set no written probationary period, good-cause protection may attach from the date of hire.

Probation period is set by employer written policy. No written policy = WDEA may apply from day one. Maximum extension: 18 months under MCA 39-2-910 (2021 amendment).

The 2021 Montana legislative session amended MCA 39-2-910 to clarify the maximum probationary period: employers may extend by up to six months beyond the 12-month default, giving a 18-month ceiling. Before 2021, some employer policies used longer probationary windows; those are no longer valid under the amended statute.

The written policy is the only lever you control at hire. Three options:

Option 1: default 12-month probation
Take the statutory default. Offer letter or handbook should acknowledge the 12-month probationary period; employment is at-will during that time. No additional drafting required beyond a clear statement.
Option 2: extended probation (up to 18 months)
Specify a period longer than 12 months (maximum 18 months) in the offer letter. Must be clearly communicated before the employee starts work. Useful for specialist roles where performance assessment takes longer.
Option 3: shorter or no probation
Employer may set a probationary period shorter than 12 months, or eliminate probation entirely. Eliminating probation means WDEA good-cause protection attaches from day one — generally inadvisable unless the role warrants it.

Montana courts treat employee handbooks as contracts when they contain specific language about termination standards, probationary periods, and performance expectations. A handbook that says "employees may be terminated for any reason at any time" then describes a performance improvement process can create confusion about whether at-will terms were modified. The handbook and the offer letter should be consistent. Teamed drafts both to be WDEA-coherent.

What counts as good cause for termination in Montana?

Under MCA 39-2-903(5), good cause means reasonable job-related grounds for dismissal based on: (1) failure to satisfactorily perform job duties, (2) disruption of the employer's operation, or (3) other legitimate business reason. The standard is objective: "reasonable" grounds judged by what a reasonable employer in similar circumstances would do. The employer bears the burden of proof.

Good cause is not the same as just cause (the union arbitration standard). Montana courts interpret it as a meaningful but not onerous requirement. What matters:

Performance failure
Documented failure to meet role requirements after the employee was made aware of the standard. Progressive discipline, written warnings, and a Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) all create good-cause evidence. Undocumented performance concerns are difficult to defend.
Operational disruption
Conduct that disrupts the employer's business operations — workplace conflicts, safety violations, policy breaches. Must be documented. Subjective descriptions of culture fit issues generally won't meet the standard without supporting evidence.
Legitimate business reason
Restructuring, role elimination, or reduction in force can constitute good cause, but the business rationale must be genuine, documented, and not pretextual. Courts look at whether the stated reason is the real reason.
What won't work
Dismissal for personal dislike, age, disability, or other protected characteristics (the WDEA doesn't displace discrimination claims — those still run under the Montana Human Rights Act). Dismissal with no documented reason. Pretextual restructuring targeting an individual.

The practical implication: every performance conversation with a post-probation Montana employee should be documented. A verbal "we're not happy with your work" has no evidentiary value in a WDEA hearing. The paper trail from the first warning to the final decision is what defends the employer. This is standard HR process in any US state; in Montana, it's legally consequential rather than just good practice.

Wrongful discharge claims must be filed within 1 year of termination (MCA 39-2-911). Claims proceed to arbitration under MCA 39-2-914 if the employer has an arbitration clause in its employment agreement; otherwise to district court. The employer's documentation of good cause is central to the defence at both forums.

What are the damages for wrongful discharge in Montana?

MCA 39-2-905 caps WDEA damages at lost wages and fringe benefits from the date of discharge up to 4 years, plus interest. Punitive damages are not available under the WDEA. The Act preempts most common-law tort claims (MCA 39-2-913), so the four-year cap is generally the ceiling for employer liability on wrongful discharge.

Lost wages
Wages the employee would have earned from date of discharge, capped at 4 years. Employee has a duty to mitigate by seeking comparable employment; earnings from substitute employment reduce the award.
Fringe benefits
Value of lost employer-provided benefits (health insurance, retirement contributions, etc.) from date of discharge, capped at the same 4-year window.
Interest
Statutory interest accrues on the lost wages and fringe benefit award. Pre-judgment interest can be significant in protracted cases.
Punitive damages
Not available under the WDEA. This is a significant departure from common-law wrongful discharge in other states. The four-year wage cap is the maximum.
Preemption (MCA 39-2-913)
The WDEA preempts most common-law tort claims arising from wrongful discharge. Employees cannot stack a WDEA claim with a separate negligent retention or tortious discharge claim. The statutory path is the exclusive remedy for the discharge itself.

Four years of lost wages is real exposure. For a Montana employee earning $80,000 per year, four years is $320,000 before interest and fringe benefits. For an employee earning $120,000, it's $480,000. This is not theoretical risk. Montana courts award WDEA damages where employers cannot document good cause. The documentation investment before termination is cheap by comparison.

The WDEA does not replace claims for discrimination or harassment under the Montana Human Rights Act (MCA Title 49). Those run in parallel and have their own remedies. The preemption under 39-2-913 applies to common-law wrongful discharge tort theories — not to statutory discrimination claims. An employer facing both a WDEA claim and a discrimination claim faces separate exposure under each regime.

Who is exempt from the WDEA in Montana?

The WDEA exempts: employees covered by a collective bargaining agreement that provides its own just-cause or good-cause termination protections; employees who have an express written employment contract for a specific term that sets out the conditions for termination; and certain short-term or temporary employees. The WDEA also doesn't apply during the probationary period.

Practical exemptions for most private employers:

During probation
At-will applies for the full probationary period (12 months default, up to 18 months written). No good cause required. Notice is still a professional standard but not a statutory requirement during probation.
CBA-covered employees
Union employees covered by a CBA with its own termination protections are exempt from the WDEA. The CBA arbitration procedure governs instead. Non-union employees at a unionised employer are not exempt.
Fixed-term contracts
An employee hired under an express written contract for a specific term with agreed termination conditions may fall outside the WDEA for breaches of that contract. The contract must be genuine and not merely a wrapper over indefinite at-will employment.
Independent contractors
The WDEA applies to employees, not contractors. But Montana's ICEC requirement and modified ABC test mean misclassification risk is elevated. A misclassified "contractor" carries WDEA exposure retroactively.

One nuance: the WDEA allows employers and employees to agree to arbitration as the exclusive forum for WDEA claims (MCA 39-2-914). A well-drafted mandatory arbitration clause doesn't exempt from the WDEA's substantive protections, but it channels disputes out of district court and into a faster, less public forum. Some employers use this to control the litigation environment; the trade-off is that the employee retains full WDEA rights in arbitration.

What must an employer do to protect against wrongful discharge claims in Montana?

Five steps before you hire in Montana: (1) put a clear written probationary period in every offer letter; (2) use a consistent handbook that matches the offer letter; (3) document every performance issue in writing from the first conversation; (4) follow a progressive discipline process before any post-probation termination; (5) obtain an Independent Contractor Exemption Certificate (ICEC) for every contractor role. Do all five and your WDEA exposure is manageable. Skip any of them and it grows.

Offer letter
State the probationary period explicitly: "Your employment is at-will during the first [12 / up to 18] months from your start date. Following the probationary period, termination will be for good cause as defined under the Montana Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act (MCA 39-2-901)." Both dates and the statute citation.
Employee handbook
Describe the probationary period, the performance standards, the progressive discipline process, and what good cause means in your context. Montana courts have held that handbook provisions are binding. Inconsistency between offer letter and handbook is a liability.
Performance documentation
Every performance conversation that could lead to termination should be in writing: email confirmation of verbal discussions, written warnings, PIP documents, manager notes. This is your evidence of good cause in any WDEA hearing.
Progressive discipline
A documented verbal warning, then written warning, then final written warning, then termination trail is strong evidence of good cause. It shows the employee was made aware of the problem and given an opportunity to correct it. Skipping steps is defensible in genuine gross misconduct situations; document why you skipped them.
ICEC for contractors
Every independent contractor in Montana needs an Independent Contractor Exemption Certificate from the Montana DLI, or the contractor must maintain their own workers' compensation coverage. Without it, misclassification risk multiplies WDEA exposure for workers who were in practice employees.

The written probationary period policy is the only structural protection available at hire. Once an employee passes the 12-month (or extended) probation without that written policy in place, you're operating without a runway. How does Montana compare to other US states on at-will employment?

Every other US state is at-will for private employment absent a specific exception (contract, whistleblower statute, discrimination, public policy). Montana's WDEA is the only state statute that establishes a general good-cause requirement after probation. It's not a narrow public policy exception or a union-specific rule. It's the baseline employment standard for post-probation private employees in Montana.

At-will exceptions in other US states exist but are narrow:

Federal discrimination law
Title VII, ADA, ADEA, and FMLA prohibit dismissal based on protected characteristics or protected activity. These apply in Montana and every state. They're narrow (specific protected classes) not general.
State public policy exceptions
Most states recognise a common-law public policy exception: you can't fire someone for refusing to break the law, for filing a workers' comp claim, for jury duty. These are specific exceptions, not a general good-cause standard.
Implied contract exceptions
Many states allow employees to argue that handbook language or employer conduct created an implied contract requiring cause for dismissal. Montana's WDEA replaces this common-law claim with a statutory standard.
Montana's WDEA
A general statutory good-cause standard for all private post-probation employees. Not limited to a protected class, a specific statutory right, or an implied contract argument. The baseline is: after probation, you need a documented reason.

The practical consequence for multi-state employers: your US termination template doesn't work in Montana. The at-will language in a standard US offer letter is accurate for 49 states; for Montana post-probation employees, that language misstates the law and may create the impression that the employer didn't understand its obligations. Montana-specific offer letters, performance documentation, and termination review are not optional — they're the standard.

What else do out-of-state companies need to know when hiring in Montana?

Montana has no state paid sick leave and no state paid family leave programme for private employers. The minimum wage is $10.85/hour from 1st January 2026 (CPI-adjusted annually, no tip credit). No county or local income tax. FMLA is federal only (employers with 50 or more employees within 75 miles). Workers' compensation is required. The WDEA is the most material compliance variable for most employers — but it doesn't stand alone.

Three other variables that affect your Montana compliance model:

Independent Contractor Exemption Certificate (ICEC). If you engage contractors in Montana, they must hold an ICEC from the Montana DLI or maintain their own workers' compensation insurance. Without an ICEC on file, a misclassification audit can reclassify the contractor as an employee retroactively — which means WDEA exposure applies to the entire relationship. Montana uses a modified ABC test for UI purposes (Prongs A and C only; Prong B is not required), making contractor status somewhat easier to defend than in full-ABC states. But the ICEC is non-negotiable. Source: Montana DLI, employment standards.

Montana Human Rights Act (MCA Title 49). Montana's anti-discrimination statute covers private employers with one or more employees for disability claims and one or more employees for most other protected characteristics (the federal threshold for Title VII is 15 employees). The WDEA preempts common-law wrongful discharge tort claims but it doesn't affect Human Rights Act claims. A dismissal that violates both statutes faces parallel proceedings.

Workers' compensation. Most Montana employers must carry workers' compensation. Cover through the Montana State Fund, an approved private insurer, or self-insure with DLI approval. Household domestic workers, casual employees, and workers employed within an Indian reservation by an enrolled tribal member are exempt; virtually all other employees are covered. Source: Montana DLI.
