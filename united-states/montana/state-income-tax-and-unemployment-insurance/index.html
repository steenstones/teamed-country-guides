What is Montana's state income tax rate in 2026?

Montana runs on two income tax brackets in 2026: 4.7% up to $47,500 for single filers, and 5.65% on income above that. The top rate dropped from 5.9% under HB 337, effective 1st January 2026.

HB 337, passed in the 2025 Montana Legislative Session, restructured the state's income tax brackets. For single filers and those filing married-filing-separately, the 4.7% rate applies to taxable income from $1 to $47,500; the 5.65% rate applies above that. For joint filers, the 4.7% bracket covers up to $95,000; for head-of-household filers, up to $71,250. There's no county or local income tax layered on top. Montana is a single-rate-at-state-level state.

What is Montana's unemployment insurance tax rate in 2026?

Montana UI rates run 0.00%–6.12% in 2026 on a taxable wage base of $47,300 per employee. New employers typically pay around 1.18%. Over 7,100 employers paid zero under HB 210's trust-fund reduction trigger.

Montana sets the UI taxable wage base annually at 80% of the prior calendar year's average annual wage, rounded to the nearest $100. For 2026 that figure is $47,300 — significantly higher than states like Maryland ($8,500) or Florida ($7,000), so Montana's UI exposure window is wider. For a $60,000 salary, you're paying UI contributions on 79% of the annual wage.

The rate schedule assigns employers to one of 12 tiers (Schedule I through XII) based on the ratio of Montana's UI Trust Fund balance to total covered wages as of 31st October each year. HB 210, enacted in the 2025 session, added an automatic rate-reduction trigger: when the fund exceeds 2.8% of total covered wages, all experience-rated employers receive a 0.20 percentage point reduction. That triggered for 2026, cutting rates for 32,873 employers and pushing over 7,100 to a zero rate. Montana reported $23 million in aggregate UI tax savings for businesses from these 2026 reforms. Source: Montana UI Contribution Rates; EY 2026 SUI Taxable Wage Bases.

How do employers register and remit Montana state income tax withholding?

Montana employers register via the Montana Taxpayer Access Point (TAP) and receive a Withholding Account Number within 10 to 14 days. Remittance frequency scales with withholding volume. The 2026 MW-4 form uses the federal standard deduction method.

The withholding cycle starts with the MW-4: every new hire submits one before their first paycheck. The 2026 version calculates withholding from the federal standard deduction by filing status — no separate Montana-specific allowances. Without a completed MW-4 on file, you withhold at the maximum applicable rate.

Montana's reciprocal agreement with North Dakota exempts Montana residents working in North Dakota from North Dakota withholding, and vice versa. For all other out-of-state employees earning Montana-sourced wages, apply standard Montana withholding rules. No other reciprocal state agreements exist.

Annual reconciliation uses Form MW-3, due 31st January each year. Employers filing 25 or more W-2 or 1099 forms must file electronically. For supplemental wages, non-cash compensation, or equity, consult Montana Publication 1 (Montana Guide to Wage Withholding, updated 2026). Source: Montana DOR, 2026 withholding updates.

What is the Montana Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act?

Montana is the only US state where at-will employment does not apply after the probationary period. The Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act (WDEA, MCA 39-2-901) requires employers to have 'good cause' to terminate an employee who's completed probation.

The WDEA, enacted in 1987 and codified at MCA Title 39, Chapter 2, Part 9, distinguishes Montana from every other US state. During the probationary period — which defaults to 12 months from hire and can be extended by the employer to a maximum of 18 months — employment is at-will: you can terminate for any reason or no reason on notice. After probation, 'good cause' is required.

'Good cause' under MCA 39-2-903 means reasonable job-related grounds for dismissal: failure to satisfactorily perform job duties, disruption of the employer's operation, or another legitimate business reason. The employer bears the burden of demonstrating good cause. Remedies for wrongful discharge include lost wages and fringe benefits for up to four years from the discharge date, plus interest. The WDEA preempts most common-law tort claims (MCA 39-2-913), capping recovery — but a four-year wage recovery cap is still a significant exposure for any termination that wasn't documented.

Three actions protect you. First: put the probationary period policy in writing — in the offer letter and the employee handbook. Montana courts treat handbook provisions as legally binding. Second: document performance issues contemporaneously. Verbal warnings, written PIPs, and performance review records all constitute good-cause evidence before you need it. Third: if your Montana worker is an independent contractor, require an Independent Contractor Exemption Certificate (ICEC) from the Montana DLI. Without one, misclassification risk carries WDEA exposure directly.

Does Montana have a local or county income tax?

No. Montana has no county or local income tax. You withhold at the state rate only, using a single Montana Withholding Account Number from TAP. Montana has a reciprocal agreement with North Dakota only.

Montana is one of a small set of US states with genuinely simple payroll tax structure: one withholding account, one set of tables, one remittance destination. No municipality, county, or school district income tax exists on top of state income tax. Contrast that with Maryland (24 local rates), Ohio (hundreds of local rates), or New York City (separate city income tax) — Montana's payroll withholding calculation produces a single combined state figure.

The North Dakota reciprocity agreement means Montana residents working in North Dakota don't need North Dakota state income tax withheld (and vice versa). For remote employees working in Montana but residing in another state, standard cross-border apportionment principles apply: Montana taxes wages earned in or attributable to Montana, while the employee's home state typically allows a credit for taxes paid to Montana.

What do out-of-state companies need to know before hiring in Montana?

Montana's minimum wage is $10.85/hour from 1st January 2026. The state has no mandatory paid sick leave, no state paid family leave programme, and no local income tax. The WDEA is the variable most first-time Montana employers underestimate.

Three things catch first-time Montana employers off guard. First, the WDEA. Most US employment guides treat at-will employment as universal; Montana is the exception. If you hire without a written probationary period policy, WDEA protection may attach from day one. The written policy — in the offer letter or handbook — is the lever employers control. Without it, you've shortened your own runway.

Second, the UI wage base. At $47,300, Montana's base is higher than many US states. For an employee earning $60,000 per year, you're paying UI contributions on 79% of their annual salary. New employer rate is approximately 1.18%, so the first-year UI cost is around $558 per $60,000 employee. Build it into your hiring cost model from day one, not at reconciliation. Source: Montana UI Division, employer resources.

Third, contractor classification. Montana requires an Independent Contractor Exemption Certificate (ICEC) from the Montana DLI for any contractor arrangement. Without an ICEC on file and a misclassification audit occurs, WDEA exposure multiplies the retroactive liability. Montana uses a modified ABC test (Prongs A and C only) for UI purposes — Prong B (work outside the usual business course) is not required, making it somewhat easier to defend independent contractor status than in full-ABC states, but the ICEC requirement is non-negotiable.

Montana has no state disability insurance tax, no SDI surcharge, and no paid family leave tax. Workers' compensation is required for most employers; obtain it through the Montana State Fund, an approved private insurer, or self-insure with DLI approval. The minimum wage adjusts annually by CPI, announced by 30th September and effective 1st January. Federal FMLA covers employers with 50 or more employees within 75 miles; Montana has no state FMLA extension for smaller employers.
