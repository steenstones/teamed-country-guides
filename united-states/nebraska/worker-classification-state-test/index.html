Nebraska is one of the more contractor-friendly US states for technology and professional-services work. There is no state ABC test. The IRS common-law test governs state income tax and workers' compensation. The Department of Labor's 10-factor right-to-control test governs unemployment insurance. The two analyses usually reach the same conclusion for a clean contractor arrangement.

The picture changes the moment construction or delivery services enter the frame. Nebraska's Employee Classification Act creates a Class III violation for misclassification in those sectors. A misclassified Nebraska construction worker triggers back UI contributions on the $9,000 wage base, back workers' comp premium, back state withholding at 4.55%, civil fines, and criminal misdemeanour exposure for wilful violations.

Most hiring guides treat Nebraska as a plain common-law state and stop there. The Employee Classification Act is the part they miss. It applies to every construction project and every delivery engagement in the state, not just the biggest ones.

This page covers the three Nebraska tests, the sectors where each applies, what misclassification actually costs, and how Teamed runs the classification determination for your Nebraska hires before the first contract is signed.

Which worker classification test does Nebraska use?

For state income tax withholding and workers' compensation, the federal IRS 20-factor common-law analysis applies. For unemployment insurance, the NDOL runs a 10-factor right-to-control test under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-604(5). Construction and delivery services fall under the Employee Classification Act with a separate presumption and penalty structure.

Elena is a software engineer in Omaha running her own LLC. She builds data-pipeline infrastructure for two fintech clients simultaneously, owns her laptop and dev environment, sets her own hours, and bills at a fixed project rate with no ongoing direction from either client. The IRS common-law analysis comes out contractor. The NDOL 10-factor reading follows. The Employee Classification Act does not apply because her work is not construction or delivery.

Change the scenario: Elena's brother Marcus runs a concrete-finishing crew in Lincoln and takes 1099 pay from a general contractor who provides all materials, gives daily site direction, and has no other active clients. Every factor fails under the Employee Classification Act. Marcus's crew are employees from their first day on site. The GC is exposed to back taxes, civil penalties, and criminal misdemeanour liability.

How does the IRS common-law test apply in Nebraska?

Nebraska follows the federal IRS 20-factor common-law test for state income tax withholding. The analysis groups into three categories: behavioural control, financial control, and type of relationship. No single factor is determinative.

Elena's engagement survives the common-law analysis because she controls when, where, and how she works; bills project-rate rather than hourly; owns her own tools and development environment; and runs multiple client relationships simultaneously. The Nebraska Department of Revenue reaches the same contractor conclusion as the IRS.

The three-category framework

Category Key factors Employee signal
Behavioural control Who directs how the work is done? Training, instructions, daily supervision Required hours; mandated methods; daily check-ins; work done on hirer's premises
Financial control Who controls the economic aspects? Investment in tools, multiple clients, profit or loss risk Hourly billing; hirer provides all tools; no other clients; no opportunity for profit or loss
Type of relationship Written contract terms; benefits; permanency; integral part of hirer's business Indefinite engagement; employee-type benefits; work that is core to the hirer's business

Nebraska has no override legislation that reweights the common-law factors for state tax purposes. If the IRS analysis reaches contractor, Nebraska state withholding follows. The state does not publish separate guidance that adds new factors or shifts the burden in the way that ABC-test states do.

The practical implication: a clean Nebraska tech-services engagement is genuinely simpler to document than the same engagement in California, Massachusetts, or even Illinois (for construction). The IRS three-category framework is well-trodden. A well-constructed contractor agreement that recites the right-of-control evidence, combined with operational facts that match the contract, survives Nebraska review without a separate state-specific analysis.

Where the common-law test produces genuine borderline cases:

  • Single-client consultants with indefinite tenure. Indefinite engagement at one company, working on the company's core product, with gradually increasing coordination: these are the cases that start as clean 1099 and drift into employee-shaped territory over 12 to 24 months.
  • On-site knowledge workers. A contractor who works 5 days a week in the hirer's Omaha office, uses the hirer's equipment, attends internal meetings, and is introduced as a team member has acquired several employee-shaped facts that the IRS analysis notices.
  • Nebraska Department of Revenue spot audits. Nebraska conducts payroll audits where withholding discrepancies surface. A large 1099 population with single-client facts is a common trigger.

How does Nebraska's unemployment insurance classification test work?

For unemployment insurance, the Nebraska Department of Labor applies a 10-factor right-to-control test under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-604(5). The test asks whether the hiring party has the right to control and direct the work: not just the result, but the means and manner of achieving it.

The NDOL UI test is a right-to-control analysis, not an ABC test. It does not presume employee status and does not require the hiring party to prove independence on all three prongs simultaneously. In practice, for clean professional-services arrangements, the NDOL reading aligns closely with the IRS common-law outcome.

The 10-factor right-to-control analysis (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-604(5))

Factor What it asks Contractor signal
1. Instructions Does the hirer control how the work is done, not just the outcome? Worker sets own methods; no daily direction from hirer
2. Training Does the hirer train the worker to do the job their way? Worker brings existing expertise; no hirer-provided training
3. Integration Is the worker's service integral to the business? Work is outside the hirer's core business activity
4. Personal services Must the work be done personally by this individual? Worker may subcontract or delegate without hirer approval
5. Hiring assistants Does the hirer control who assists the worker? Worker independently selects and pays their own assistants
6. Continuing relationship Is the relationship ongoing and indefinite? Project-based; defined end date; episodic rather than continuous
7. Set hours Does the hirer set the worker's hours? Worker determines when to work within agreed deliverable windows
8. Full-time work Is the worker required to devote full time to the hirer? Worker has multiple active clients; no exclusivity clause
9. Work location Does the hirer require the worker to work at a specific location? Worker chooses where they work; hirer gets deliverables, not attendance
10. Tools and materials Does the hirer supply tools, equipment, or materials? Worker uses their own equipment; hirer provides only project specs

Elena's engagement is straightforward on all 10 factors. She brings her own laptop, sets her own hours across two client relationships, works remotely, is not required to attend the office, and charges a fixed project fee rather than a time-and-materials rate with the hirer controlling the hours.

The NDOL weighs the factors holistically rather than applying a pass/fail on each one. A single weak factor does not automatically make a worker an employee for UI purposes. The Department looks at the totality of the arrangement. A worker who primarily sets their own schedule but uses the hirer's equipment on-site is a mixed picture; the NDOL reads the dominant pattern.

How the NDOL UI test differs from an ABC test

ABC-test states (California, Massachusetts, New Jersey) presume employee status unless the hiring party can prove all three prongs simultaneously. One failed prong makes the worker an employee for the relevant purpose regardless of how the other factors fall.

Nebraska's 10-factor right-to-control test is a balancing analysis. The Department weighs the totality. A contractor who fails one or two factors in a sea of contractor-favourable evidence is still more likely to pass Nebraska's UI test than California's ABC test. That is the structural difference, and it is why Nebraska is genuinely friendlier terrain for contractor relationships in professional services.

How does Nebraska's Employee Classification Act apply to construction?

The Nebraska Employee Classification Act (Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 48-2701 to 48-2716) applies to construction and delivery services. It treats misclassification as a Class III violation: civil penalties, back-tax liability, and criminal misdemeanour exposure for wilful violations. The Department of Labor enforces it.

Marcus's concrete crew is the example that makes the Act real. The GC provides all concrete, forms, and mixing equipment. A foreman gives daily instruction on pour sequencing, placement, and finish standards. Marcus's crew does not have liability insurance or other active clients. The Employee Classification Act reaches all of them as employees. The GC has been paying them as 1099 contractors for two years. The exposure is back UI contributions on $9,000 per worker per year, back workers' comp premiums for a concrete-work rate code, back state withholding at 4.55%, civil fines, and the NDOL can refer for criminal prosecution if it establishes the misclassification was wilful.

Sectors the Act covers

The Employee Classification Act covers two categories:

  • Construction services: any work on a building, structure, road, highway, bridge, or utility system. This includes general contractors, subcontractors, and specialty trades (electrical, plumbing, concrete, framing, roofing, landscaping, demolition).
  • Delivery services: any person engaged to deliver property of any kind as a business, including courier, freight, and last-mile delivery operations.

The Act does not cover technology workers, professional services, financial services, or most knowledge-work engagements. If Marcus were instead a Salesforce developer working remotely for a Lincoln fintech, the Employee Classification Act would not apply. The IRS common-law and NDOL 10-factor tests would govern.

What does "bona fide business" mean in practice?

For Marcus's crew to pass under the Act, the GC would need to show that the workers: run their own registered business entity (LLC or sole proprietorship with a business licence), carry their own general liability and workers' compensation insurance, have other active construction clients at the time of the engagement, and supply their own tools and equipment to the job. One-prong evidence is insufficient. The NDOL looks at the full operating picture.

Three patterns produce most construction misclassification exposure in Nebraska:

  • Sole-proprietor day-rate crews with no business licence, no insurance, no other active clients. They may file Schedule C but the operating reality is employee-shaped.
  • Single-client specialty subcontractors where the main GC provides materials, equipment, and site supervision. Prong one (direction) and prong two (other clients) both fail.
  • Delivery drivers treated as 1099 independent contractors but given fixed routes, company-branded vehicles, and mandatory scheduling windows. The Act covers them and the analysis mirrors construction.
Employee Classification Act, in one line
If you perform construction or delivery work in Nebraska, the default is employee. The contractor must prove genuine independence (licence, insurance, other clients, own tools) before the 1099 sticks.

What does Nebraska worker misclassification actually cost?

Outside construction: back state withholding at 4.55%, back UI on the $9,000 wage base, back workers' comp premium, and federal FICA/FUTA exposure. A misclassified $80,000-a-year Nebraska knowledge worker reclassified after two years runs $16,000 to $30,000 in back liability before penalties and interest.

Inside construction: add the Employee Classification Act civil fine, back withholding, back UI, back workers' comp on a physical-labour rate code, and criminal misdemeanour referral risk if the NDOL finds the misclassification was wilful.

How Nebraska misclassification audits typically start

The most common triggers are a former contractor filing a UI benefits claim after the engagement ends, a workers' compensation injury on a construction site where the worker claimed contractor status, or a Department of Revenue payroll audit that spots a large 1099 population with single-client facts.

Once the NDOL opens a determination, it shares findings with the Department of Revenue (withholding exposure) and the Workers' Compensation Court (premium exposure). The three-agency coordination is what makes the stacked liability real: a single classification decision becomes three separate recovery proceedings.

Section 530 of the Revenue Act of 1978 may still cap the federal FICA/FUTA piece if the employer had a reasonable basis for treating the worker as a contractor and consistently treated similarly situated workers the same way. Nebraska has no state equivalent of Section 530 for state-side withholding or UI exposure. The federal safe harbour, where it applies, only protects the federal payroll-tax line.
