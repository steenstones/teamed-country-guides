Nebraska is an at-will employment state under common law. You can terminate a Nebraska employee at any time without cause, so long as the reason doesn’t breach a statute, a contract, or Nebraska’s narrow public-policy exception established in Schriner v. Meginnis Ford Co., 421 N.W.2d 755 (Neb. 1988). Final wages are due on the next regular payday under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-1230. The Nebraska Fair Employment Practice Act (NEFEPA) prohibits discrimination but applies only to employers with 15 or more employees, matching the federal Title VII threshold. Nebraska is a deferral state: EEOC and Nebraska Equal Opportunity Commission (NEOC) charges both run on a 300-day clock. Nebraska has no state mini-WARN Act; federal WARN is the only advance-notice statute for mass layoffs.

Is Nebraska really an at-will employment state?

Yes. Nebraska at-will employment rests on common law. Either party can end the relationship at any time, for any reason not prohibited by law or contract.

Nebraska does not have a codified at-will statute. The doctrine is common law, and the public-policy exception is deliberately narrow. The Nebraska Supreme Court in Schriner v. Meginnis Ford Co., 421 N.W.2d 755 (Neb. 1988) established that a wrongful discharge tort exists where the discharge contravenes a clearly mandated public policy. The court did not create an open-ended exception map; it drew a tight line.

Daniel is a sales manager at a Lincoln distributor. Headcount reduction in Q3. You can end his role without notice and without cause. You cannot end it because he refused to falsify mileage records for expense claims. That refusal to commit an act in violation of a clear legal obligation falls squarely inside the Schriner exception.

At-will is the starting position and, in Nebraska, it stays close to that starting position. NEFEPA, the workers’ compensation statute, specific whistleblower protections, and your own handbook language decide where it actually ends.

What are the exceptions to at-will employment in Nebraska?

Nebraska recognises four categories of at-will exception. The public-policy exception is narrower than Iowa’s open case-law map and more tightly constrained than the statutory lists some states provide. There is no broad “anything an employer shouldn’t do” safety net: a plaintiff must tie the wrongful-discharge claim to a specific, clearly mandated public policy.

Why Nebraska’s narrow exception matters for multi-state employers

If your US footprint includes Iowa, Kansas, and Nebraska, you may be surprised that Nebraska’s public-policy exception is actually tighter than Iowa’s. Iowa’s case-law map is open: a plaintiff can argue a novel policy anchor from any Iowa statute. Nebraska’s Schriner doctrine insists on a clear, specifically mandated public policy. That makes Nebraska terminations generally lower risk on the wrongful-discharge tort, but the same rigour must still be applied to the NEFEPA analysis, the implied-contract review, and the final-pay calculation before the decision is made.

The handbook disclaimer that defeats implied-contract claims

Put the at-will disclaimer on the front page of the handbook. Repeat it on the signature page. Collect a fresh signed acknowledgement whenever the handbook is updated. Nebraska’s objective-expectation standard asks whether a reasonable employee would conclude from the handbook that dismissal required cause. “Progressive discipline applies whenever possible” without a reservation clause is a problem sentence. “Nothing in this handbook creates a contract of employment” is the sentence that kills the implied-contract theory.

Workers’ compensation retaliation

Nebraska statute Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-645 provides separate protection against discharge in retaliation for filing a workers’ compensation claim. This is a statutory cause of action distinct from the common-law Schriner exception, and it applies to all Nebraska employers regardless of size. A termination that follows a workers’ comp filing by less than 90 days requires contemporaneous documentation of an independent stated reason to withstand a retaliation challenge.

How does NEFEPA change your Nebraska termination exposure?

The Nebraska Fair Employment Practice Act covers employers with 15 or more employees, matching the federal Title VII threshold. That is a higher floor than the 4-employee threshold in Iowa and Kansas, which means some smaller Nebraska employers fall outside NEFEPA’s reach but must still manage the common-law public-policy exception.

Julia runs operations at a 12-person Omaha software firm. Federal Title VII doesn’t apply (below 15 employees). NEFEPA doesn’t apply either. If Julia is discharged for a reason that correlates with her disability or national origin, her claim would be a common-law wrongful-discharge tort anchored to the Schriner public-policy doctrine, not a NEFEPA or Title VII charge. The exposure is real, but the procedural terrain is different from a NEFEPA administrative claim.

Cross the 15-employee threshold and both NEFEPA and Title VII apply. The Nebraska Equal Opportunity Commission (NEOC) handles NEFEPA charges. Nebraska is a deferral state, so EEOC federal charges also run on the 300-day clock. Plaintiffs routinely dual-file with both bodies.

A Nebraska employer with 15 or more employees cannot terminate or otherwise disadvantage an employee because of marital status. What is the Nebraska final-pay rule under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-1230?

Under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-1230, wages are due on the next regular payday for the pay period in which the employee’s last day fell. Nebraska does not have a separate, accelerated final-pay deadline for involuntary terminations, unlike states that impose a same-day or 72-hour clock.

Nebraska final-pay statute: Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-1230

Next regular payday (all termination types)

Nebraska does not distinguish between voluntary resignation and involuntary termination for the payday deadline. Both situations trigger the same next-regular-payday rule. Complete your final-pay calculation before the last day, not after, so the payment can be issued on the existing payroll cycle without reconstruction under time pressure.

What counts as “wages” under Nebraska law

  • Base pay or hourly wages through the last day worked.
  • Commissions and bonuses already earned under the written plan, even if not yet calculated at separation. The calculation must be completed and paid by the next regular payday.
  • Accrued PTO or vacation, only if your written policy makes it payable at separation. Nebraska does not mandate PTO payout at termination. A clear written policy excluding payout is enforceable. If the handbook is silent, courts read the ambiguity against the employer.
  • Any overtime owed under the Fair Labor Standards Act through the last day worked. Nebraska follows federal FLSA for overtime; there is no additional state overtime statute.

No penalty multiplier, but the claim still runs

Unlike Iowa’s double-wages penalty under Iowa Code § 91A.8, Nebraska does not have a statutory liquidated-damages multiplier for unpaid final wages. The risk is a civil wage claim for the amount owed, plus potential costs. That is a more predictable exposure envelope than Iowa, but still requires a clean calculation and timely payment. An employee can file a wage claim with the Nebraska Department of Labor (NDOL) or pursue a civil action directly.

Nebraska Wage Payment and Collection Act

Nebraska’s wage payment statute (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-1228 et seq.) covers all employers operating in Nebraska. It requires wages to be paid at least semi-monthly or bi-weekly under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-1212. The NDOL Labour Standards unit enforces both the regular pay-frequency requirement and the final-pay deadline under § 48-1230. Wage complaints can be filed directly with NDOL or pursued through civil litigation without a prior administrative filing requirement.

Deductions from final pay

Nebraska permits deductions from wages where the employee has given written authorisation. Deductions for property damage, loan repayments, or unreturned equipment require prior written consent. Deductions that bring the employee below federal minimum wage ($7.25/hr, or Nebraska minimum of $15.00/hr effective 1st January 2026, whichever is higher) are not permitted. Attempting to recover unreturned property through deduction without prior written consent creates a wage claim.

How should you document a termination in Nebraska?

Five documents cover the Nebraska termination risk surface. A handbook with a clear at-will disclaimer. Contemporaneous performance records. A termination letter with a specific stated reason. A final-pay calculation dated against the next regular payday. And a protected-activity audit before the decision is made.

The handbook disclaimer (the most important single document)

Put the at-will disclaimer on the front page. Repeat it on the signature page. Collect a fresh signed acknowledgement at every meaningful handbook update. The implied-contract theory collapses when the handbook is clear, conspicuous, and signed. In Nebraska, the objective-expectation standard asks what a reasonable employee would conclude from the document (not what your lawyers intend). If the handbook describes a disciplinary sequence without explicitly reserving the right to skip steps, you have an implied-contract exposure to address before the termination decision, not after.

Contemporaneous performance documentation

The 300-day NEOC and EEOC charge windows are the timelines that matter most after separation. A performance file built retroactively is a pretext problem. Build it in real time: dated entries, specific facts, measurable gaps against stated standards. The deposition question “when did you write this note?” should always have one answer: before the protected activity became relevant to the decision.

Protected-activity audit before the decision

Before any Nebraska termination, your country specialist runs a protected-activity sweep. Workers’ compensation claims filed in the past 12 months (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-645). NEFEPA or EEOC complaints. FMLA requests. Military-service notifications (USERRA). Jury summonses. Any wage complaints filed with NDOL. If any protected activity sits within a timeline that suggests possible causation, the independent stated reason must be documented and dated before proceeding. Nebraska’s narrow Schriner exception does not eliminate risk. It concentrates it on the workers’ comp and NEFEPA statutes that do have statutory causes of action.

The final-pay calculation

Date it against the next regular payday under § 48-1230. Itemise base pay through the last day, accrued commissions under the written plan, and PTO payout per the written policy. Sign off internally before the employee’s last working day. Nebraska has no cure-window concept equivalent to Iowa’s 7-day written-demand window, but that makes speed more, not less, important: the obligation attaches at the regular payday and there is no notice-to-cure buffer.

What about mass layoffs in Nebraska?

Nebraska has no state mini-WARN Act. Federal WARN (29 U.S.C. § 2101) is the only advance-notice statute that applies to Nebraska layoffs.

Federal WARN requires 60 days’ written notice before a plant closing or mass layoff at a site with 100 or more full-time employees. Notice goes to affected employees, the Nebraska Department of Labor Rapid Response unit, and the chief elected local government official of the jurisdiction where the facility is located.

A qualifying mass layoff under federal WARN is a reduction at a single site, in any 30-day period, of either 50 to 499 employees who make up at least 33 percent of the active workforce, or 500 or more employees regardless of percentage. Rolling 90-day aggregation rules pull staged layoffs together into a single count, so a phased reduction programme that crosses the threshold within 90 days triggers the 60-day clock retroactively.

Nebraska Department of Labor Rapid Response

Once a federal WARN notice is filed, the Nebraska Department of Labour Rapid Response unit contacts both employer and affected employees to co-ordinate workforce-transition support: on-site briefings, resume workshops, unemployment-benefit pre-processing, and connections to the Nebraska Reemployment Assistance programme. Folding Rapid Response into your communication plan from day one reduces friction with NDOL during a sensitive operational period. Nebraska has no additional notice statute on top of federal WARN.

What WARN does not cover

Individual terminations and group separations below the 50-employee threshold are not WARN events in Nebraska. There is no Nebraska statute requiring employer notice for sub-threshold layoffs. The only Nebraska-specific obligation below WARN is the § 48-1230 payday rule on final wages, which applies regardless of how many employees are affected.
