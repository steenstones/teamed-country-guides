What paid family and sick leave does Nebraska require in 2026?

Nebraska has no state paid family leave. But it does have a new state sick leave law. 56 hours of paid sick leave per year at employers with 20 or more employees. 40 hours at 11 to 19 employees. Federal FMLA at 50-plus employees provides 12 weeks unpaid. No state premium deduction. No state paid family leave fund.

Which paid-leave laws apply to Nebraska employees in 2026?

Two frameworks apply: the Nebraska Healthy Families and Workplaces Act (Initiative 436, effective 1st October 2025) for paid sick leave, and federal FMLA for unpaid family and medical leave at 50-plus employees.

Nebraska has no state paid family leave insurance, no state pregnancy leave fund, and no state short-term disability programme. There is no payroll deduction for a state leave fund.

What changed on 1st October 2025

Initiative 436 was passed by Nebraska voters on 5th November 2024. It created the Nebraska Healthy Families and Workplaces Act and set an effective date of 1st October 2025. LB 415, passed by the Nebraska Legislature in spring 2025, made implementation amendments including the employer-size tiers. Employers whose existing PTO policies already met or exceeded the NHFWA floor were in compliance from day one. Employers whose policies fell short needed to restructure accrual and carryover.

The 80-hour hiring rule

NHFWA accrual starts after 80 hours of employment with the same employer. A new hire in week one has no accrued sick leave under the statute. From hour 81, the 1-per-30 accrual clock runs. This is worth noting in your offer letter if your voluntary PTO policy provides immediate access.

How does Nebraska's paid sick leave accrue in 2026?

Employees accrue 1 hour of paid sick leave for every 30 hours worked. The annual cap depends on employer size.

At 20 or more employees: the annual accrual cap is 56 hours. At 11 to 19 employees: 40 hours. Below 11 employees: the accrual right still applies but the leave is unpaid.

Accrual mechanics

  • Rate: 1 hour accrued per 30 hours worked. No different rate for salaried or exempt employees.
  • Start: accrual begins after 80 hours worked for the same employer. The 80-hour threshold resets for a new employer, not a new year.
  • Pay rate: the employee’s regular rate of pay at the time the leave is taken. Not the average rate. Not a flat minimum-wage substitute.
  • Carryover: unused accrued hours carry over to the next calendar year. The carryover is capped at the same size-based annual limit. Carryover is mandatory; you cannot "use it or lose it" at year-end.
  • Payout at separation: NHFWA does not require payout of accrued unused sick hours on termination. Verify against your own handbook; a policy that promises PTO payout may create an obligation even if the statute doesn’t.
  • Front-loading option: employers may front-load the full annual entitlement at the start of the year in lieu of tracking per-30-hour accrual. If you front-load 56 hours on 1st January, you satisfy the accrual requirement for that year.

Headcount counting rule

Employer size is measured as the number of employees you employed in Nebraska during the calendar year. If you had 11 or more employees at any point in the calendar year, you are a covered employer for that year. A mid-year growth from 10 to 11 employees makes you covered from the date the 11th employee starts.

What can Nebraska employees use paid sick leave for?

NHFWA covers the employee’s own illness or injury, preventive care, and caring for a family member. It also covers leave related to domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking.

A family member under NHFWA includes the employee’s child, spouse, domestic partner, parent, parent of a spouse or domestic partner, grandchild, grandparent, and sibling. The definition is broader than federal FMLA’s family member list.

Notice and documentation

You may require reasonable advance notice when the leave is foreseeable (a scheduled appointment, a planned medical procedure). When the leave is unforeseeable (an acute illness, an emergency), advance notice cannot be required. You may ask for documentation to verify the leave is for a qualifying reason, but only if the leave lasts more than three consecutive days. Requiring documentation for every single-day absence violates the statute. Refusing leave while waiting for documentation is also prohibited.

Retaliation prohibition

Taking NHFWA leave cannot be grounds for a negative performance evaluation, attendance-tracking disciplinary point, or termination. The statute explicitly prohibits counting NHFWA absences as unexcused under a no-fault attendance policy. If your attendance policy auto-triggers discipline at a set number of absences without distinguishing NHFWA from other absences, it is non-compliant on its face.

Does Nebraska have a state paid family leave programme in 2026?

No. Nebraska has no state paid family leave insurance fund, no payroll deduction for a state PFML programme, and no state-funded parental or bonding leave benefit.

Nebraska is among the majority of US states that have not enacted a PFML insurance programme. There is no premium to withhold, no state fund to remit to, and no state wage-replacement claim process for parental leave.

This distinguishes Nebraska from states like Colorado (FAMLI), California (SDI/PFL), Washington (PFML), and Massachusetts (PFML), all of which require payroll deductions and provide state-funded wage replacement during leave. A multi-state employer with workers in both Colorado and Nebraska carries FAMLI premium obligations for the Colorado headcount and none for Nebraska.

A Nebraska employee who needs bonding leave after the birth of a child has two options in the absence of a voluntary employer policy: use accrued NHFWA sick hours (limited to sick leave qualifying reasons under the act) or take unpaid FMLA leave if the employer has 50 or more employees and the employee is eligible.

How does federal FMLA apply to Nebraska employers in 2026?

Federal FMLA (29 U.S.C. § 2601) applies to Nebraska employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. Eligible employees get 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per year.

Nebraska has not enacted a state family leave law that expands on FMLA. The federal statute is the ceiling and the floor for family and medical leave protection.

FMLA eligibility

An employee is FMLA-eligible when they have worked for the employer for at least 12 months, worked at least 1,250 hours in the previous 12 months, and work at a location where the employer has 50 or more employees within 75 miles. Part-time employees who meet the 1,250-hour threshold are covered. Employees who miss that threshold are not, regardless of tenure.

Qualifying reasons under federal FMLA

  • Serious health condition of the employee
  • Care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition
  • Birth, adoption, or placement placement of a child (bonding leave within the first 12 months)
  • Qualifying military exigency related to a covered servicemember
  • Care for a covered servicemember (26 weeks per year under military caregiver FMLA)

NHFWA and FMLA do not run concurrently by default

NHFWA is a sick leave bank for short-duration qualifying absences. FMLA is a 12-week job-protection right for serious health conditions and family qualifying events. They measure different things and do not run concurrently as a structural matter, unlike the Colorado HFWA and FAMLI overlap. An employee who uses NHFWA hours for a two-day flu does not draw down their FMLA entitlement. An employee who takes FMLA leave for a scheduled surgery may elect or be required (under your handbook) to substitute NHFWA hours during the FMLA block if the absence qualifies under both statutes.

Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA)

The federal Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (effective 27th June 2023) requires employers with 15 or more employees to provide reasonable accommodations for pregnancy, childbirth, and related medical conditions. This is separate from FMLA and from NHFWA. A Nebraska employer at 15 to 49 employees is below the FMLA threshold but must still engage in the PWFA interactive accommodation process. Common accommodations include modified schedules, remote work during pregnancy, and temporary reassignment to light duties.

What did LB 415 change about Nebraska’s sick leave law?

LB 415 (signed into law during the 2025 Nebraska legislative session) amended Initiative 436’s original text before the law took effect. The key changes introduced the employer-size accrual tiers and clarified implementation details.

Without LB 415, the original Initiative 436 text would have applied a single 40-hour cap to all covered employers. LB 415 raised the cap to 56 hours for employers with 20 or more employees.

The legislature passed LB 415 in spring 2025 to address implementation concerns raised by the Nebraska Chamber of Commerce and the Nebraska Retail Federation. The amendments were signed before the 1st October 2025 effective date, so the two-tier structure was in place from day one of enforcement.

Key LB 415 provisions

  • Two-tier accrual cap: 56 hours/year at 20-plus employees; 40 hours/year at 11 to 19 employees.
  • Unpaid floor for small employers: employers under 11 employees must allow the time off but are not required to pay it.
  • Interaction with existing PTO policies: LB 415 confirmed that a general PTO policy satisfies NHFWA if it accrues at least as fast as 1-per-30 and allows use for NHFWA qualifying reasons. You do not need a separate sick leave bank if your PTO policy meets the floor.
  • NDOL enforcement authority: the Nebraska Department of Labor was designated as the enforcement body, with authority to investigate complaints and assess civil penalties for non-compliance.

Checking your existing PTO policy against NHFWA

Many employers discovered their voluntary PTO policy was close but not quite compliant. Common gaps were: the policy accrued more slowly than 1-per-30 for part-time employees; the policy had a waiting period longer than the NHFWA 80-hour window; the policy excluded fixed-term or seasonal employees who are covered under NHFWA; or the policy required minimum increments (for example, a half-day minimum) that exceeded what NHFWA allows (use in the smallest increment your payroll system tracks). Review your handbook against each of these before assuming compliance.
