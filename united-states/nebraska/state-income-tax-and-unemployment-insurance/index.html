NE income tax 2026

Nebraska moved from four income tax brackets to three on 1st January 2026 as part of Governor Pillen's LB 754 reform. Single filers pay 2.46% on the first $2,399 of taxable income, 3.51% from $2,400 to $17,999, and 4.55% above $18,000. Married-joint thresholds are exactly double.

The top rate was 5.84% as recently as 2024. Employers running Nebraska payroll today are withholding materially less than two years ago. In 2027 the top rate drops again to 3.99%.

Social Security income is fully exempt from Nebraska state tax from Tax Year 2025. Use the 2026 Circular EN withholding tables for wages paid on or after 1st January 2026. Employers with more than 24 employees must withhold at least 1.5% of taxable wages unless the employee provides written documentation to justify less.

What is Nebraska's unemployment insurance rate for employers?

New non-construction employers pay 1.25% UI on the first $9,000 of wages per worker. Experienced employers range from 0% to 5.4% across 20 rate categories.

Nebraska uses an array system with 20 rate categories, each covering 5% of total taxable payroll. The taxable wage base is $9,000 for most employers, rising to $24,000 for employers in the highest category (category 20). All mid-category rates are increasing for 2026 compared to 2025, though the floor (0%) and ceiling (5.4%) stay the same. Construction employers start at 5.4%.

Nebraska employers generally qualify for the full 5.4% FUTA credit, reducing your net federal UI rate to 0.6% on the first $7,000 per employee. At the standard $9,000 wage base, your Nebraska state UI cost for a new non-construction hire is at most $112.50 per year. That's a small cost line against the income-tax withholding picture.

What are Nebraska's minimum wage and paid sick time rules?

Nebraska's minimum wage hit $15.00/hr on 1st January 2026. Paid sick time (1 hour per 30 worked) is mandatory for employers with 11 or more employees from 1st October 2025.

The $15.00 minimum wage completes the phased increase started by Initiative 436 in 2022. From 1st January 2027, the rate adjusts annually for inflation. Tipped employees receive at least $7.50 cash, with tips bridging the gap to $15.00.

The Nebraska Healthy Families and Workplaces Act (Initiative 436, amended by LB 415) requires private employers with 11 or more staff to provide paid sick time. Employees earn 1 hour per 30 hours worked, beginning to accrue after 80 hours of employment. Employers with 20 or more workers must allow accrual and use of up to 56 hours per year; smaller covered employers cap at 40 hours. Unused hours carry over to the next year.

Nebraska has no state paid family leave (PFL) programme and no state disability insurance (SDI). Federal FMLA at 50 employees is the floor for job-protected family leave. Nebraska is simpler on the payroll compliance side than most coastal states: no SDI contributions, no PFL remittances, no complex rate notices.

How does at-will termination work in Nebraska?

Nebraska is a strict at-will state. No notice period is required from either party. Final wages are due by the next regular payday or within two weeks, whichever comes first.

At-will employment means you can end a role without cause and without advance notice, and the employee can leave on the same terms. The practical exceptions are standard across the US: wrongful discharge against public policy, implied contract from an unqualified employee handbook, and federal anti-discrimination protections under Title VII, ADEA, and the ADA.

Final pay must be delivered by the next scheduled payday or within 14 days of termination under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-1230. Nebraska does not require statutory severance pay or a redundancy formula. If your employment contract commits to separation pay, that's a contractual obligation, not a statutory floor.

No PFL or SDI means no state leave-replacement calculations on departure. The separation is clean. Your Teamed in-house legal specialist can draft compliant separation documentation that closes the risk loop and protects both parties.

How does Nebraska classify workers: employee or contractor?

Nebraska uses the common law control test for most classifications. For UI purposes, the ABC test applies. The Employee Classification Act targets construction and delivery services misclassification.

Under the ABC test used for UI, a worker is an employee unless all three conditions hold: A) they're free from your direction and control; B) the work falls outside your normal business activity; C) they're genuinely in business for themselves in that trade. Failing any single prong makes them your employee for UI purposes.

The Nebraska Employee Classification Act gives the Department of Labor authority to investigate and penalise misclassification in construction and delivery. These are the two sectors where labelling workers as 'subcontractors' to avoid UI and workers' compensation is most common. Penalties include back UI taxes, workers' comp premiums, and civil fines.

What should you know before hiring in Nebraska?

Nebraska is one of the few US states actively cutting its income tax mid-decade. The 4.55% top rate in 2026 becomes 3.99% in 2027 under LB 754, and the state fully exempted Social Security income from tax as of 2025. For talent attraction, that matters: take-home pay in Nebraska is rising faster than in most peer states.

There's no state payroll tax on the employer side beyond UI, no SDI, and no PFL programme. Your statutory footprint in Nebraska is smaller than in California or New York by a significant margin. The paid sick time law (effective October 2025) came from a ballot initiative, not a legislature bill, and was promptly amended (LB 415) to make small-employer treatment more practical.

Nebraska's labour market skews toward agriculture, financial services (Union Pacific, Berkshire Hathaway, and TD Ameritrade all base operations in Omaha), and a growing tech and insurance sector. Payroll frequency must be at minimum semi-monthly.
