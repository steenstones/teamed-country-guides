Is New Hampshire an at-will employment state?

Yes. New Hampshire follows the at-will doctrine: either party may end employment at any time, for any lawful reason, or for no reason. New Hampshire courts recognise a public-policy exception, and two statutes add further constraints: RSA 354-A on anti-discrimination and RSA 275-E on whistleblower protection.

At-will employment in New Hampshire means you can terminate without cause, without written documentation, and without advance notice, provided the decision doesn't cross one of the recognised exceptions. The New Hampshire Department of Labor and the Commission for Human Rights confirm this default rule for private-sector employment.

The doctrine runs in both directions. An employee may resign without giving notice, and the employer cannot condition the final paycheck on notice served or project handover completed. For involuntary dismissals, the final paycheck is due within 72 hours of termination, not the next scheduled payday.

New Hampshire does not require severance. There is no state-mandated notice period for individual terminations. The compliance obligations that do attach are time-sensitive: the 72-hour final pay window, and the RSA 354-A and RSA 275-E documentation review that should happen before any termination decision is executed.

The exceptions are where most New Hampshire termination disputes originate. Both are avoidable with sound documentation practices: a clear at-will disclaimer in every offer letter and handbook, and a written record confirming the business reason for each separation before any protected activity occurred or was reported.

What are the exceptions to at-will employment in New Hampshire?

New Hampshire recognises a public-policy exception from Cloutier v. A. & P. Tea Co. (1981), plus two statutory exceptions: RSA 354-A (anti-discrimination, 6+ employees) and RSA 275-E (Whistleblowers' Protection Act). Courts have also recognised limited implied-contract exceptions from handbook language.

1. The Cloutier public-policy exception

The New Hampshire Supreme Court in Cloutier v. A. & P. Tea Co., 121 N.H. 915 (1981) established New Hampshire's public-policy exception to at-will employment. A termination violates this exception when the employee was dismissed for:

  • Refusing to perform an act that violates the law or clear public policy, such as refusing to falsify records or commit fraud;
  • Exercising a legal right or obligation, such as filing a workers' compensation claim under RSA 281-A or asserting a wage entitlement;
  • Reporting a workplace violation to an appropriate authority, covered more specifically by RSA 275-E; or
  • Performing a public duty, such as jury service or military service under the federal USERRA.

Cloutier anchors the exception to a statutory or constitutional basis. A termination that feels unfair does not automatically trigger the doctrine. The employee must show the dismissal was causally linked to one of those protected activities. Temporal proximity alone does not establish the causal link, though it is a factor courts consider.

The practical implication: if your New Hampshire termination was made for documented performance or business reasons, and the employee had not recently filed a workers' comp claim, reported a violation, or engaged in a protected activity, the Cloutier exception is not in play. Build the written record before acting, not after.

2. RSA 354-A: NH Law Against Discrimination

RSA 354-A prohibits employers with 6 or more employees from terminating, or otherwise discriminating in the terms of employment, on the basis of age, sex, race, creed, colour, marital status, familial status, physical or mental disability, national origin, or sexual orientation. The New Hampshire Commission for Human Rights (NHCHR) enforces the statute. Federal Title VII, the ADA, and the ADEA operate in parallel; RSA 354-A's 6-employee threshold for most protections is lower than the federal 15-employee floor, extending coverage to small employers federal law does not reach.

Any termination decision should be reviewed against RSA 354-A before it is executed. If the employee belongs to a protected class, recently requested an accommodation, filed an internal complaint, or is being terminated contemporaneously with others who share a protected characteristic, document the independent business reason clearly. A contemporaneous written record is the first thing an NHCHR investigator will request.

3. RSA 275-E: Whistleblowers' Protection Act

RSA 275-E prohibits an employer from discharging, threatening, or otherwise retaliating against an employee who in good faith reports, or causes to be reported, a violation or suspected violation of law to a public body. The statute defines "public body" broadly to include a federal, state, or local body, and extends to employees who participate in an investigation or hearing by such a body.

Critically, RSA 275-E also protects internal reports: an employee who reports a suspected violation to a supervisor or internal authority has a protected activity for purposes of the statute, provided the report was made in good faith. This means an employee who emails their HR department about suspected wage theft and is then terminated within weeks has a viable RSA 275-E retaliation claim even if they never contacted an external body.

The 2-year statute of limitations under RSA 275-E runs from the date of the retaliatory act. Employees must first exhaust the administrative process through the NH Department of Labor before filing a civil claim. The practical defence is documentation: confirm no retaliatory motive exists in writing before acting, and preserve all records of the business reason for the decision.

When is the final paycheck due after termination in New Hampshire?

Under RSA 275:44, final wages are due within 72 hours of an involuntary dismissal. For voluntary resignation, wages are due on the next regular payday. The NH Department of Labor enforces wage payment and may assess penalties for non-compliance.

The 72-hour window on involuntary dismissal is the most common compliance gap for out-of-state employers managing New Hampshire employees. Most US states allow the final paycheck on the next regular payday for all separations. New Hampshire draws a clearer line: terminate someone, and their earned wages are due within three calendar days, not on your next payroll cycle.

What counts as wages for final pay purposes: all earned wages through the last day of work, including accrued overtime under the FLSA and any commission earned and payable under the employment agreement. New Hampshire law does not require payout of accrued vacation or PTO on termination unless a company policy or employment contract expressly states otherwise. Check your handbook language and offer letter before assuming PTO is non-payable at exit.

Employers cannot deduct unreturned equipment costs or other offsets from the final paycheck without a signed written authorisation or a court order. Conditioning payment on equipment return violates RSA 275:44. The obligation to pay wages earned is unconditional; equipment disputes are a separate civil matter.

Does New Hampshire require advance notice of termination?

No. New Hampshire imposes no statutory advance notice requirement for individual at-will terminations. Neither employers nor employees are legally obligated to give prior notice before ending the employment relationship.

New Hampshire's absence of a notice requirement applies in both directions. You can act on the day you decide to terminate. An employee can resign without two weeks' notice, and the employer cannot withhold the final paycheck because notice wasn't provided.

Three sources can override the no-notice default. First, any employment contract or collective bargaining agreement that specifies a notice period is contractually binding. Second, if your handbook or offer letter describes a notice process, those representations can create an implied-contract obligation courts have been willing to enforce. Third, the federal WARN Act requires 60 days' advance written notice for plant closings or mass layoffs of 50 or more employees at a single site, for covered employers with 100 or more workers. New Hampshire's WARN-equivalent statute, RSA 275-F, applies to employers with 100 or more employees and requires 60 days' notice for qualifying mass layoffs. Verify against the state threshold if you're reducing a large New Hampshire workforce.

For individual terminations, the timeline is immediate. What creates compliance pressure is not the notice but the final-pay timing (due within 72 hours for involuntary dismissals) and the RSA 354-A and RSA 275-E documentation review that should precede every separation.

How does RSA 354-A apply to small employers in New Hampshire?

RSA 354-A's anti-discrimination protections apply from 6 employees, nine years below the federal Title VII and ADA threshold of 15. This means New Hampshire businesses that federal law doesn't reach are still exposed to discrimination claims under state law.

The 6-employee threshold is one of the most important distinctions between New Hampshire employment law and federal employment law for smaller businesses. A company with 8 employees in New Hampshire faces RSA 354-A exposure for termination decisions even though Title VII, the ADA, and the ADEA would not apply federally. The NHCHR is the enforcement body, and it has a 180-day filing window from the date of the alleged discriminatory act for most claims.

For employers between 6 and 14 employees, RSA 354-A is the operative anti-discrimination framework. Decisions affecting those employees on the basis of sex, age, race, disability, national origin, marital or familial status, sexual orientation, or creed are actionable under state law regardless of whether federal thresholds are met. The compliance obligations are identical in substance to the federal framework: document the business reason, apply it consistently, and confirm no protected characteristic was a factor.

For employers with 15 or more employees, both RSA 354-A and federal law apply in parallel. An employee can file simultaneously with the NHCHR and the EEOC, and each agency may investigate independently. Dual exposure is the result: potentially duplicative proceedings if a discrimination claim arises. Consistent documentation practices protect against both.

RSA 354-A also covers reasonable accommodation obligations for employees with a disability, matching the reasonable-accommodation framework of the federal ADA. An employer's failure to engage in an interactive accommodation process before terminating a disabled employee is, by itself, a basis for an RSA 354-A claim even if the ultimate termination decision was made for non-discriminatory reasons.

How does RSA 275-E protect employees who report violations?

RSA 275-E (the NH Whistleblowers' Protection Act) prohibits retaliation against employees who, in good faith, report a violation or suspected violation of law to a public body or to an internal authority. The protection covers external and internal reports alike.

The breadth of RSA 275-E matters because it protects employees who report to their own HR department or supervisor, not only those who go to a government agency. An employee who raises a compliance concern internally and is then dismissed has a protected activity under the statute regardless of whether they ever escalated to a state or federal body.

Good faith is the operative standard. The employee does not need to prove the underlying violation actually occurred. They need to show the report was made genuinely, not fabricated as a shield against a pending termination for unrelated reasons. Courts have found the good-faith requirement is not met when an employee manufactures a complaint only after being notified of their impending dismissal.

Practical exposure pattern

The most common RSA 275-E risk pattern: an employee raises a concern about wages, working conditions, or regulatory compliance; management reacts poorly; the employee is dismissed shortly afterward. Even if the underlying termination reason was legitimate, the timing creates a retaliation inference that requires documented rebuttal. The employer must show the decision was made on grounds independent of the protected report, and that those grounds existed and were documented before the report was made.

Pre-termination checklist for RSA 275-E compliance: confirm the employee has not made a report of a legal violation in the preceding 90 days; verify the termination reason was documented before any reported violation; confirm no manager communicated the reported concern as a factor in any meeting or written communication leading to the decision; and confirm the decision was applied consistently to similarly situated employees who did not make such a report.

The statute of limitations under RSA 275-E is 2 years from the date of the retaliatory act. Exhausting the administrative process through the NH Department of Labor is a precondition to filing a civil claim. An employee who receives a right-to-sue letter after exhausting the DOL process can file in superior court for reinstatement, back pay, and other appropriate relief.

How do you terminate a New Hampshire employee without legal risk?

Document the business reason, process the final paycheck within 72 hours of involuntary dismissal, confirm the decision doesn't implicate the Cloutier public-policy categories, RSA 354-A protected classes, or an active RSA 275-E report, and verify no handbook language creates an implied-contract obligation the process hasn't followed. That's the New Hampshire compliance checklist.

New Hampshire's at-will doctrine gives employers real flexibility. Four compliance obligations are non-negotiable regardless: the 72-hour final pay window under RSA 275:44, the RSA 354-A pre-termination review, the RSA 275-E whistleblower check, and verification that any handbook procedures were followed if they exist.

Pre-termination checklist

Before you act: confirm the termination reason is not rooted in an RSA 354-A protected class; check whether the employee has recently filed a workers' comp claim, requested an accommodation, reported a legal violation to HR or a public body, or engaged in another protected activity; verify your handbook language doesn't create an implied-contract obligation that the termination process hasn't followed; confirm the business reason was documented before any protected activity occurred; and note whether any progressive-discipline procedure was triggered without being completed.

Day of termination

Issue a written notice confirming the termination date and the effective time. The final paycheck is due within 72 hours, not the next payday. Collect company equipment, revoke system access, and document what was communicated, when, and by whom. New Hampshire law doesn't prescribe a specific form for the termination notice, but a written record closes most factual disputes in later proceedings.

Do not condition the final paycheck on equipment return, exit interview completion, or signing a separation agreement. Final wages earned through the last day of work are due unconditionally within 72 hours under RSA 275:44. Equipment disputes are a separate civil matter and cannot delay the wage obligation.

Is severance pay required in New Hampshire?

No. New Hampshire does not require severance pay. It is voluntary unless required by an employment contract, company policy, or collective bargaining agreement. For employees aged 40 or over, any severance agreement waiving age-discrimination claims must comply with the federal OWBPA: a written waiver in plain language, a 21-day consideration period, and a 7-day revocation window after signing. Non-disparagement clauses cannot restrict an employee's right to file an NHCHR complaint or cooperate with a government investigation.

The state threshold mirrors the federal WARN Act threshold. Individual terminations are not covered; the statute is triggered only by qualifying mass separations that meet the defined thresholds on employee numbers and timeframes.
