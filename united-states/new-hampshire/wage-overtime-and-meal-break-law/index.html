New Hampshire's minimum wage is $7.25 per hour, the federal floor under the FLSA. RSA 279 ties the state rate to the federal minimum, and New Hampshire has not enacted a higher state rate. New Hampshire has no state overtime law: the FLSA's 40-hour weekly threshold governs, with overtime at 1.5× the regular rate and no daily OT trigger. RSA 275:30-a requires a 30-minute meal break for any shift exceeding five consecutive hours, unpaid provided the employee is fully relieved of duties. The break can be waived by agreement where the nature of the work permits the employee to eat while working. Final wages are governed by RSA 275:43 (regular paydays) and RSA 275:44 (72 hours on involuntary dismissal; next regular payday on voluntary resignation). New Hampshire is one of roughly 20 US states that have not enacted a minimum wage above the federal floor. RSA 279 makes this explicit: the state minimum is defined by reference to the federal minimum wage under the FLSA. If Congress raises the federal floor, New Hampshire's state rate rises automatically without a separate legislative act.

This matters for payroll configuration. An employer running multi-state payroll who sets a default $7.25 floor is correctly configured for New Hampshire, but should verify that any other state in the payroll run with a higher minimum is handled with the correct state-specific rate. A uniform $7.25 across all states is a common compliance gap for employers without a state-by-state wage matrix in their payroll system.

Tipped employees

New Hampshire allows a tip credit. Tipped employees may be paid a cash wage of $3.26 per hour, provided that tips received during the pay period are sufficient to bring total compensation to at least $7.25 per hour. If tips are insufficient in any workweek to cover the gap, the employer must make up the difference to reach the minimum. Tip pooling is permissible under the FLSA 2018 amendments provided no portion of the pool is retained by the employer or management employees.

Youth wage and opportunity wage

Federal law permits a youth minimum wage of $4.25 per hour for employees under 20 years of age during the first 90 consecutive calendar days of employment. New Hampshire does not extend a separate state sub-minimum rate beyond the federal opportunity wage provisions. After 90 days, or when the employee turns 20 (whichever is earlier), the full $7.25 minimum wage applies.

Exempt salary thresholds

New Hampshire does not impose a state-level salary-basis threshold for exempt executive, administrative, or professional employees. The federal FLSA threshold applies: $684 per week (the 2024 DOL threshold; verify against current DOL regulations before each payroll year). Highly compensated employees require a total annual compensation of at least $107,432 under federal rules, with no separate New Hampshire threshold. New Hampshire's compliance obligation for exempt classification tracks entirely to FLSA requirements.

Does New Hampshire have a state overtime law?

No. New Hampshire has not enacted a state overtime statute that exceeds FLSA requirements. The federal 40-hour weekly threshold governs: overtime must be paid at 1.5 times the regular rate for hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek. There is no daily overtime trigger, no extra obligation for shifts of 8 or 10 hours.

New Hampshire employers applying the FLSA's 40-hour workweek rule are fully compliant with state law. There is no supplemental New Hampshire statute requiring additional overtime pay, a shorter weekly threshold, or a daily overtime premium. This is a meaningful simplification for US multi-state employers: New Hampshire presents no overtime complexity beyond FLSA compliance.

How the 40-hour workweek works in practice

The FLSA workweek is any fixed, regularly recurring 168-hour period, seven consecutive 24-hour periods. It does not need to align with the calendar week. An employer may designate Tuesday to Monday as the workweek. What matters is consistency: a workweek definition must be established in advance and applied uniformly to each employee, and cannot be changed retroactively to avoid overtime obligations.

All hours worked in the workweek are counted toward the 40-hour threshold. This includes:

  • Pre-shift and post-shift work if suffered or permitted by the employer;
  • Mandatory training that directly benefits the employer (voluntary, non-job-related training does not count);
  • On-call time where the employee's freedom to use the time for personal activities is substantially restricted; and
  • Travel time during the workday between job sites (commute to and from home is excluded).

Overtime rate calculation

The regular rate is the base for the 1.5× calculation. The regular rate is not simply the hourly wage, it includes non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, commissions, and piece-rate earnings averaged over the workweek. Genuinely discretionary bonuses (announced at the employer's sole discretion without prior promise) are excluded from the regular rate. Employers who pay a non-discretionary monthly productivity bonus must recalculate the regular rate retroactively for the weeks in which overtime was worked.

A common New Hampshire compliance error: paying a flat weekly salary and treating employees as exempt without confirming they meet the duties test for the executive, administrative, or professional exemption. Paying a salary does not by itself create an exemption. The employee must also meet the FLSA duties test. A misclassified employee is entitled to recover up to three years of unpaid overtime (two years for non-wilful violations) under FLSA enforcement.

No daily overtime, what that means operationally

Employers can schedule 10- or 12-hour shifts in New Hampshire without triggering daily overtime. A 4x10 schedule, four 10-hour days, 40 hours total, involves no overtime obligation because the weekly total is 40 hours. A 3x12 plus one 4-hour shift, 40 hours total, similarly incurs no overtime. This gives New Hampshire employers operational scheduling flexibility that states with daily OT thresholds (California, for example) do not have.

Does New Hampshire require meal breaks or rest periods?

Yes. RSA 275:30-a requires employers to provide a 30-minute meal break for any employee working more than five consecutive hours. The break is unpaid provided the employee is completely relieved of all duties. An exception applies where the nature of the work permits the employee to eat while performing their duties, in that case, no formal break period is required.

When does the break obligation trigger?

The five-hour clock runs on consecutive hours of work, not hours within the workday. An employee who works 4 hours, takes a full 30-minute break (fully relieved), and then works another 4 hours has not triggered an additional break obligation under RSA 275:30-a, because neither consecutive segment exceeds five hours. The break resets the consecutive-hours counter. Employers should structure schedules to place the break no later than the fifth hour of a continuous shift.

Paid vs. unpaid: the compensability question

The 30-minute break is unpaid only when the employee is completely relieved of all duties. Complete relief means the employee is free to leave the premises, is not required to monitor any process, is not on call, and is not performing any task that benefits the employer. If any of these conditions are not met, the employee must stay on-site to monitor a machine, answer the phone if it rings, or remain available to address customer issues, the break period is not bona fide and must be counted as paid compensable time.

This is the most frequently litigated meal-break question in New Hampshire enforcement. An employer who designates a 30-minute meal break but routinely interrupts employees to answer questions or handle tasks has converted that time to compensable time. The NH Department of Labor takes the position that any substantial interruption of the break creates a compensability obligation for the full period if the interruption is frequent enough to mean the employee cannot truly be considered relieved.

The eating-while-working exception

RSA 275:30-a includes a carve-out for workplaces where the nature of the work permits the employee to eat a meal while performing their duties. Manufacturing lines where an employee can eat at their station, certain retail and service environments, and roles requiring continuous physical presence may qualify. The exception requires that the employee is in agreement and is not denied the ability to eat. It does not apply simply because the employer designates the role as one where eating on-shift is expected. The NH Department of Labor applies this exception narrowly: the work itself must genuinely permit simultaneous eating, not merely tolerate it.

Rest periods (short breaks)

New Hampshire does not have a state statute requiring short rest periods or coffee breaks for adult employees. Under federal FLSA interpretation, short breaks of 20 minutes or fewer are compensable working time if the employer provides them. Breaks of 30 minutes or more are non-compensable if the employee is fully relieved. New Hampshire does not supplement the FLSA rest-break rules with a separate state mandate for adult workers.

Minor employees

RSA 276-A governs youth employment in New Hampshire and imposes additional restrictions on shift lengths and break requirements for employees under 18. These rules interact with RSA 275:30-a. Employers with minor workers should consult the NH Department of Labor's youth employment guidelines separately from the adult meal-break framework.

Compliance note

Schedule and timekeeping records should clearly show when meal break periods began, how long they ran, and whether the employee was fully relieved. If a dispute arises, the burden of proof that the break was bona fide rests with the employer. Electronic timekeeping that records break start/stop and captures any mid-break system activity is the most defensible record.

When must a final paycheck be issued in New Hampshire?

Under RSA 275:43 and RSA 275:44, final wages are due within 72 hours of an involuntary dismissal. For voluntary resignations, wages are due on the next regular payday. The obligation to pay is unconditional, the employer cannot withhold the final cheque pending equipment return, exit interview completion, or signing of a separation agreement.

The 72-hour final-pay window on involuntary dismissal is one of the tightest in New England. Most US states allow final pay on the next regular payday for all separation types. New Hampshire draws a clear distinction: the employer's payroll cycle is irrelevant when a termination is employer-initiated. The obligation is triggered on the effective date of dismissal and runs for 72 calendar hours regardless of whether a payroll run is scheduled in that window.

What must be included in the final paycheck: all earned wages through the last day of work, including any overtime accrued in the final workweek at the correct 1.5× rate. Accrued unused vacation or PTO is not required by New Hampshire law unless the employer's written policy or employment contract expressly provides for payout. Employers should audit their handbook language before assuming PTO is non-payable at termination, a policy that states "employees accrue and may use PTO" without expressly disclaiming payout creates an ambiguity the NH Department of Labor has treated in the employee's favour in enforcement actions.

Deductions and offsets

An employer may not deduct unreturned equipment costs, loan repayments, training costs, or other offsets from the final paycheck without a written authorisation signed by the employee in advance, or a court order. A deduction from the final paycheck without either is a wage theft violation under RSA 275. Equipment disputes, loan balances, and bond agreements must be pursued separately through civil means; they do not give the employer the right to hold or reduce earned wages.

Enforcement and penalties

The NH Department of Labor enforces final-pay obligations. Employers who fail to pay on the statutory schedule may be assessed penalties in addition to the underlying wages owed. The 72-hour window is hard: there is no grace period, no extension for payroll cycle timing, and no exception for employees dismissed after a bank closure. Employers without an off-cycle payroll capability should establish a process before they need it.

Frequently asked questions about New Hampshire wage and overtime law

What is the minimum wage in New Hampshire in 2026?

New Hampshire's minimum wage is $7.25 per hour in 2026. RSA 279 ties the state minimum wage to the federal FLSA floor, so New Hampshire has not enacted a higher state rate. Tipped employees may receive a cash wage of $3.26 per hour provided tips bring total hourly compensation to at least $7.25. New Hampshire is one of approximately 20 states that use the federal floor as their state minimum.

Does New Hampshire have daily overtime?

No. New Hampshire has no daily overtime threshold. The FLSA's 40-hour weekly workweek standard applies: overtime at 1.5 times the regular rate is required for all hours worked beyond 40 in a defined workweek. There is no state law requiring premium pay for shifts exceeding 8 or 10 hours. Employers can schedule extended daily shifts without triggering a daily OT obligation, provided weekly hours remain at or below 40.

Does New Hampshire require a meal break?

Yes. RSA 275:30-a requires a 30-minute meal break for any employee who works more than five consecutive hours. The break is unpaid if the employee is completely relieved of duties during the full period. An exception applies where the nature of the work permits the employee to eat while performing their duties and the employee agrees. New Hampshire does not mandate short rest breaks for adult employees beyond the RSA 275:30-a meal-break requirement.

Is the meal break paid or unpaid in New Hampshire?

The RSA 275:30-a break is unpaid provided the employee is fully relieved of all duties. If the employee is required to remain on call, monitor equipment, or respond to customers during the break, the period is compensable working time under FLSA principles and must be paid at the applicable rate. Employers who routinely interrupt meal breaks through operational necessity have converted those periods to paid time.

When must a final paycheck be issued after termination in New Hampshire?

Under RSA 275:44, all earned wages must be paid within 72 hours of an involuntary dismissal. For voluntary resignation, wages are due on the next regular payday under RSA 275:43. The employer cannot withhold the final paycheck pending equipment return, exit interview, or execution of a separation agreement. Equipment and loan disputes are separate civil matters and do not affect the wage payment obligation.

Does New Hampshire have a tipped minimum wage?

Yes. New Hampshire allows a tipped minimum wage of $3.26 per hour for employees who customarily and regularly receive tips. The employer must ensure that tips received are sufficient to bring total hourly compensation to at least $7.25 per hour. If tips fall short in any workweek, the employer must make up the difference. Tip pooling is permissible under FLSA 2018 rules, provided no portion of pooled tips is retained by the employer or management-level employees.
