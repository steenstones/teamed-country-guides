New Hampshire is one of the simplest leave compliance environments in the United States for private employers. The state mandates nothing on paid family leave, nothing on paid sick time, and nothing on PTO accrual. The federal FMLA floor kicks in at 50 employees. Everything in between is the policy you write.

What makes New Hampshire distinctive is the Granite State Paid Family Leave plan (GSPFL): the first opt-in, not mandatory, state-run paid family leave programme in the country, launched 1 January 2023. Private employers can choose to participate. Those who do give their employees 6 weeks of paid leave at 60% wage replacement, administered through MetLife. Those who don't stay on the federal-floor-only model.

This page covers the GSPFL opt-in structure, the federal FMLA floor, the narrow pregnancy and military carve-outs that always apply, and the voluntary benchmarks that decide whether a Manchester or Portsmouth hire chooses your offer letter over a competitor that opted into the GSPFL or offers a richer voluntary package.

Does New Hampshire require paid family leave?

No mandatory state paid family leave law exists for private employers in New Hampshire. The state runs the Granite State Paid Family Leave plan (GSPFL) as a voluntary opt-in programme, not a mandate.

The GSPFL launched 1 January 2023 as the first opt-in (not compulsory) state paid family leave plan in the country. Private employers choose whether to participate. Employees at non-participating employers can buy individual policies directly from MetLife, the state-contracted carrier.

Brooke manages a 25-person tech team at a Manchester software firm. When the GSPFL launched, her company evaluated the opt-in cost and decided to participate. Now her employees have access to 6 weeks of paid family leave at 60% of their average weekly wage, administered by MetLife with no additional HR overhead for the employer. Competitors who didn't opt in are fielding questions from candidates about why they're missing what is, effectively, a state-backed benefit.

Granite State Paid Family Leave Plan — key facts

Launched: 1 January 2023. Carrier: MetLife (state-contracted). Duration: up to 6 weeks. Replacement rate: 60% of average weekly wage. Employer participation: voluntary. State employee auto-enrolment: yes. Verify current programme status with the NH Insurance Department before quoting to employees.

How the GSPFL opt-in works for private employers

A private employer who joins the GSPFL pays a premium to MetLife through the state programme. In return, their employees gain access to 6 weeks of paid leave at 60% wage replacement for qualifying family reasons (bonding with a new child, caring for a seriously ill family member, or a military exigency qualifying event). The employer does not administer the claims; MetLife does.

The qualifying reasons under the GSPFL align with federal FMLA family leave triggers, not the employee's own serious health condition. An employee who needs leave for their own illness falls back on the employer's voluntary short-term disability plan or unpaid FMLA leave, not the GSPFL.

Employees at non-participating employers

If your company does not opt into the GSPFL, your New Hampshire employees can still purchase individual policies directly through MetLife under the same state-negotiated rates. The employer has no obligation to facilitate this; the employee buys the policy personally. The benefit structure and 60% replacement rate are the same as the employer-sponsored version.

The absence of a mandate is also the source of New Hampshire's payroll simplicity: no employer PFL withholding line on a New Hampshire payslip, no state claim portal for mandatory benefits, no payroll rate notice tied to a PFL fund. The GSPFL is a premium-based insurance product, not a payroll tax.

Does New Hampshire require paid sick leave?

No. New Hampshire has no state-mandated earned sick time law for private employers. Paid sick leave is a voluntary policy choice.

There is no accrual floor, no minimum number of sick days, no carry-over requirement, and no anti-retaliation rule tied to a state sick leave statute. What the offer letter says is what the employee has.

It's a single combined bank, no separate sick category. Under New Hampshire law, that design is entirely compliant. No handbook addendum required. No sick-day carve-out mandated. How does the Granite State Paid Family Leave plan work for employers?

The GSPFL is a premium-based insurance programme contracted to MetLife by the state of New Hampshire. Participating employers pay a per-employee premium; MetLife administers all claims. The programme launched 1 January 2023 as the first opt-in state PFML in the United States.

It is the only state-run paid family leave mechanism in New Hampshire. Verify current participation rates, premium schedules, and plan terms with the NH Insurance Department (nh.gov/insurance) before quoting specific costs to candidates or employees.

First-in-class: what the opt-in model means

Most state PFML programmes impose a payroll deduction on all covered employees and require all covered employers to participate. New Hampshire's GSPFL inverts this. There is no mandatory payroll deduction, no state fund, and no universal employer obligation. The state negotiated a group insurance rate with MetLife and made it available to employers (and individuals) who want it.

For employers building New Hampshire teams, this creates a genuine competitive differentiator: opting into the GSPFL costs a known premium per employee, runs through MetLife, and removes the claims-management burden from your HR team. Non-participating employers can still offer voluntary employer-paid short-term disability that achieves similar wage-replacement outcomes, but without the state-negotiated structure.

What does federal FMLA give New Hampshire employees?

Federal FMLA gives qualifying New Hampshire employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period. Group health coverage continues at the employer's normal premium share.

It applies only to employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius, after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked.

The 50-employee count is national, not just New Hampshire. An employer with 30 Manchester employees and 25 employees in a Vermont office crosses the line even if neither state location alone reaches the threshold.

The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 is the federal floor for unpaid, job-protected leave. New Hampshire has no state FMLA analogue that lowers the 50-employee threshold. Below 50 employees, the FMLA obligation does not apply and New Hampshire imposes no substitute state mini-FMLA.

Five qualifying reasons trigger FMLA leave:

  • Birth and care of a newborn, within 12 months of birth
  • Placement of a child with the employee for adoption or state-supervised care, within 12 months of placement
  • Care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition
  • The employee's own serious health condition that makes them unable to perform the job
  • Qualifying military exigency arising from a family member's active duty; up to 26 weeks for military caregiver leave

FMLA and the GSPFL: how they interact

If an employer opts into the GSPFL and the employee triggers a qualifying family leave event, FMLA leave (where applicable) and the GSPFL benefit can run concurrently. The GSPFL converts the first 6 weeks of FMLA's 12-week unpaid entitlement into 60%-paid leave. The remaining 6 weeks of FMLA remain unpaid unless the employer's voluntary policy provides additional payment.

For employers below 50 employees, FMLA does not apply. The GSPFL still operates independently if the employer is a participant, but there is no federal job-protection entitlement wrapping around the paid benefit. An employee taking GSPFL leave at a sub-50-employee firm has contractual job rights (whatever the offer letter says) but not statutory FMLA job protection.

What protections apply for pregnancy and disability in New Hampshire?

Federal law applies across New Hampshire. The state adds one layer: New Hampshire RSA 354-A prohibits discrimination based on sex, including pregnancy and childbirth, at employers with 6 or more employees, a lower threshold than federal law.

The federal Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA) requires reasonable accommodation for pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions at any employer with 15 or more employees, effective 27 June 2023.

What about military leave and jury duty in New Hampshire?

Federal USERRA protects civilian jobs for service members. New Hampshire mirrors federal protection for National Guard members called to state active duty, with pay protection for the first 15 days per calendar year under RSA 110-B:65.

Jury duty protection exists in New Hampshire: employers cannot discharge or threaten an employee for jury service (RSA 500-A:14). Pay continuation during jury duty is not required by state law.

What about employers with fewer than 50 employees in New Hampshire?

Below 50 employees, New Hampshire imposes no mandatory paid leave of any kind for private employers, with two narrow exceptions: PWFA accommodation at 15 employees, and the RSA 354-A pregnancy discrimination prohibition at 6 employees.

Everything else is voluntary. No state mini-FMLA, no state PFL mandate, no state PSL accrual rule. The GSPFL is available as a voluntary opt-in regardless of headcount.

For a 20-person Concord startup, this means the compliance map is simple: PWFA at 15 employees, RSA 354-A at 6, federal minimum wage, USERRA, jury duty anti-discharge, and whatever the offer letter says on leave. The GSPFL is a premium decision, not a legal requirement.

The 50-employee headcount tipping point

The FMLA 50-employee threshold counts the employer's entire US payroll, not just New Hampshire headcount. A company with 30 Concord employees and 25 Massachusetts employees is FMLA-covered, even though neither state office alone reaches the threshold. The 75-mile radius applies to each individual worksite, not to the company as a whole.

Once an employer crosses 50 employees for 20 or more workweeks in the current or preceding calendar year, the FMLA obligation continues for the rest of the current year and all of the following year, even if headcount subsequently falls below 50. The lookback rule catches employers who scale through funding rounds or seasonal hiring.
