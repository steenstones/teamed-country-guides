Does New Hampshire have a state income tax on wages?

No. New Hampshire does not tax wages, salaries, or self-employment income. It never has.

The state's last income-related tax, the Interest and Dividends Tax at 3%, was repealed effective 1st January 2025. House Bill 2, passed during the 2023 legislative session and signed by Governor Sununu, accelerated the repeal from the originally planned 2026 date. The NH Department of Revenue Administration confirmed the repeal is now in effect.

As of today, there is no New Hampshire income tax of any kind. That means no withholding account to register with the NH DRA, no state withholding forms to collect from employees, and no state payroll tax calculated from paychecks.

Your payroll setup in New Hampshire runs on federal obligations alone: federal income tax (using the employee's W-4), Social Security at 6.2% on the first $184,500, and Medicare at 1.45% on all wages.

Multi-state note: Employees who split time between New Hampshire and a state with income tax (Massachusetts is the common case for NH-based workers) require withholding on wages earned in the other state. Your EOR handles the allocation calculation and filing in both states.

No state withholding means no state quarterly reconciliation, no state W-2 addendum, and no year-end state filing. The payroll compliance burden in New Hampshire is among the lowest of any US state.

What is the New Hampshire unemployment insurance rate in 2026?

New employer rate: 2.70% on the first $14,000 per employee. Maximum UI cost per new-employer employee: $378 per year.

New Hampshire Employment Security (NHES) administers the state's unemployment insurance programme. Experience-rated employers pay between 0.10% and 7.00% depending on claims history. The taxable wage base is $14,000 per employee per year, one of the lower bases in the US state set.

What is the worker classification test in New Hampshire?

New Hampshire applies two separate tests. Pass both or the worker is an employee.

Most states use a single classification test. New Hampshire uses two, and passing one does not satisfy the other. That dual exposure is the compliance risk most national payroll and EOR guides miss entirely when covering the state.

Workers' compensation (RSA 281-A:2, VI(b)): All workers are presumed employees. To rebut the presumption, an employer must demonstrate that all 12 statutory criteria apply. Since 1st January 2011, a signed written agreement affirming all 12 criteria constitutes prima facie evidence under RSA 281-A:2, VI(c). The agreement alone won't protect you if the day-to-day working relationship contradicts it.

Unemployment insurance (RSA 282-A:9, III, ABC Test): Services are presumed to be "in employment" unless the employer demonstrates all three parts:

  • Part A: The worker is free from direction and control in performing services.
  • Part B: Services are performed outside the usual course of the employer's business, or off all the employer's premises.
  • Part C: The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business.

An employer might satisfy the UI ABC test but fail the 12-criteria workers' comp test because one criterion is unmet. A misclassification finding on either test carries back-taxes, penalties, and potential workers' comp liability exposure. McLane Middleton notes that New Hampshire's bar remains higher than the federal standard regardless of federal rule changes.

How do you terminate an employee in New Hampshire?

At-will. No notice period required. Final paycheck due within 72 hours if you terminate.

New Hampshire follows at-will employment, meaning either party may end the relationship at any time, for any lawful reason, without advance notice. There is no state statutory notice period for at-will dismissals.

The practical obligation is the final paycheck. RSA 275:44 requires payment within 72 hours when the employer terminates. If the employee resigns, the final paycheck is due on the next regular payday. That 72-hour window is tighter than many states, so your payroll provider needs to process the final run immediately on termination notice.

Federal protections apply in full: Title VII, the ADA, the ADEA, and the FMLA all govern termination decisions. New Hampshire's anti-discrimination statute (RSA 354-A) covers employers of six or more and mirrors federal protections. Workers' compensation retaliation is a separate risk: RSA 281-A prohibits terminating an employee for filing a workers' comp claim.

No mandatory severance under New Hampshire law. If severance is offered, it becomes a contractual obligation and must be paid as agreed.

What are the workers' compensation and wage rules in New Hampshire?

Workers' comp is mandatory from employee one. Minimum wage is $7.25 per hour, tied to the federal rate.

RSA 281-A makes workers' compensation insurance mandatory for all employers with at least one employee. Coverage must come through a private carrier. New Hampshire has no state fund. Report workplace injuries to your carrier within 5 days of notification from the employee. Non-compliance is costly: a $2,500 one-time penalty plus $100 per employee per day, and deliberate non-compliance is a class B felony.

On wages: New Hampshire's minimum wage is set by RSA 279:21 at the federal floor, currently $7.25 per hour, unchanged since 2009. There is no scheduled increase. Tipped employees may be paid $3.27 per hour cash wage with a $3.98 tip credit, provided total compensation reaches $7.25.

What should you know before hiring in New Hampshire?

New Hampshire is the only US state with no income tax of any kind as of 2025. That makes payroll setup genuinely simpler: no state withholding account, no state tax forms, no quarterly state withholding filings. The compliance surface is narrower than almost any other state.

Where complexity lives is on two fronts. First, worker classification: the dual test (RSA 281-A:2 for workers' comp, RSA 282-A:9 for UI) catches contractors who pass one test but fail the other. Second, multi-state workers: the Boston metro draws many employees who live in New Hampshire but earn wages in Massachusetts. That split creates a Massachusetts withholding obligation on Massachusetts wages even when the employer account is NH-based. Teamed manages both jurisdictions from a single invoice on one platform.

What is the New Hampshire unemployment insurance rate for new employers in 2026?

What is the worker classification test in New Hampshire?

New Hampshire uses two tests. Workers' compensation uses the 12-criteria test under RSA 281-A:2 (all 12 must be met). Unemployment insurance uses the ABC test under RSA 282-A:9 (all three parts must be met). Passing one test does not satisfy the other.

What is the New Hampshire final paycheck law?

Under RSA 275:44, if an employer terminates an employee, the final paycheck is due within 72 hours. If the employee resigns, payment is due on the next regular payday.

What is the minimum wage in New Hampshire in 2026?

New Hampshire's minimum wage equals the federal minimum wage: $7.25 per hour. RSA 279:21 ties the state rate to the federal floor. It has not increased since 2009 and no increase is currently scheduled.
