How does New Hampshire’s worker classification test work in 2026?

Two tests on every hire. A 7-criteria test under RSA 281-A:2 for workers’ comp. An ABC test under RSA 282-A:9 for unemployment insurance. Pass one, fail the other. The bills stack independently.

Most employers ask “which test does New Hampshire use?” The real answer is: two tests, on two different statutory tracks, with separate consequences if either fails.

For workers’ compensation, RSA 281-A:2 imposes a 7-criteria test (all seven must be met to rebut the employee presumption). For unemployment insurance, RSA 282-A:9 runs a classic ABC test (all three parts must be satisfied). A worker who passes the 7-criteria test can still be an employee for UI purposes.

There is no safe-harbour equivalent in New Hampshire comparable to the federal Section 530 relief. The workers’ comp penalty for non-compliance is $2,500 plus $100 per employee per day under RSA 281-A. Purposeful non-compliance is a class B felony.

This page walks both tests, the written-agreement prima facie defence, and the stacked liability a misclassified $60,000-a-year New Hampshire contractor produces at year three of a state audit.

New Hampshire runs two classification tests. Which one applies to you?

Both. Every single worker you engage in New Hampshire is subject to the 7-criteria test under RSA 281-A:2 for workers’ comp coverage, and the ABC test under RSA 282-A:9 for unemployment insurance. The tests run in parallel, not in sequence.

Meredith is a software developer based in Manchester, New Hampshire. A 40-person SaaS company engages her on a 1099 contract at $5,000 a month. She works from home, sets her own hours, uses her own laptop, and has a federal EIN. The company runs her through the 7-criteria test, she passes all seven, and the engagement starts. Six months later the New Hampshire Employment Security (NHES) opens an unemployment insurance audit. The audit uses the ABC test. Part B asks whether her service (software development) is outside the company’s usual course of business. It isn’t. Part B fails. Meredith is an employee for UI purposes. The company owes back UI contributions, penalty, and interest. The workers’ comp test result is irrelevant.

New Hampshire’s two state-law tests are stricter than most US states. The 7-criteria workers’ comp test requires unanimous satisfaction of all criteria. The ABC UI test is widely regarded as one of the most difficult US standards to meet for Part B. Plan classification on day zero with both tests applied to the same role. Passing one is not a proxy for the other.

What are the 7 criteria in New Hampshire’s workers’ comp test?

All 7 criteria under RSA 281-A:2, VI(b)(1)(A–G) must be satisfied. A worker who fails any single criterion is an employee for workers’ comp purposes. The presumption of employment is strong and one-directional.

The 7-criteria test replaced an earlier 12-part test when New Hampshire amended RSA 281-A:2 in 2012 (effective 6 August 2012). The current statute asks seven yes-or-no questions. Every answer must be yes for the worker to be an independent contractor for workers’ compensation.

CriterionWhat the statute asksPractical signal of IC status
(A) Tax ID Does the worker have a federal EIN or SSN, or have they agreed in writing to carry employer obligations under state law? Worker has obtained a business EIN and uses it for invoicing
(B) Control over means and manner Is the result, rather than the method, the primary element bargained for? Does the worker control how the work gets done? Company specifies deliverable; worker chooses tools, approach, and process without instruction
(C) Control over timing Does the worker control when the work is performed? Does the employer dictate working hours? Worker sets own schedule; company may set a deadline but not daily hours. Scheduling agreements for completion milestones are permissible.
(D) Hiring and paying assistants Does the worker hire, pay, and supervise their own assistants? Worker subcontracts parts of the engagement at their own cost, without employer approval of the subcontractor
(E) Independent business Does the worker hold out as being in business for themselves, or are they registered as a business entity? Do they have continuing or recurring business liabilities? Worker operates as an LLC or sole proprietor with a business bank account and business insurance; serves multiple clients
(F) Work completion responsibility Is the worker contractually responsible for satisfactory completion? Can they be held liable for failure to complete? Engagement agreement includes a completion obligation and a cure period for non-performance; worker, not the company, bears the risk of re-work
(G) Non-exclusive Is the worker free to work for other employers during the engagement? No exclusivity clause; worker actively serves multiple clients in the same period. De facto exclusivity (billing only one client) creates risk even without a contractual exclusivity requirement

Criterion (G) is the one most workers flunk in practice. A developer who is technically free to work elsewhere but in practice invoices only the one client (because the scope fills their week) starts to look exclusive to an auditor reviewing time logs and bank statements. State investigators look at the facts, not just the contract.

How is the ABC test for unemployment different from the 7-criteria workers’ comp test?

The ABC test under RSA 282-A:9 is three parts, not seven. But Part B (the “usual course of business” criterion) is a categorical blocker for most knowledge-worker engagements. Services that are central to what the company does fail Part B automatically, regardless of how much control the worker has over their own process.

02 Two Tests, One Hire

New Hampshire runs two separate classification tests on every worker. RSA 281-A:2’s 7-criteria test covers workers’ compensation. RSA 282-A:9’s ABC test covers unemployment insurance. A 1099 that satisfies one can fail the other. Separate exposure, separate enforcement, separate bills.

7-criteria · workers’ comp (RSA 281-A:2) ABC test · UI (RSA 282-A:9) IRS 20-factor · federal payroll tax Run all tracks on day zero
PartWhat RSA 282-A:9 requiresWhere it typically fails
Part A: Free from control The individual is free from direction and control over performance of the services, both under the contract and in fact. Daily stand-ups, Jira board assignments, mandatory tool use, sprint planning attendance — any of these signals control “in fact” even when the contract says otherwise
Part B: Outside usual course of business The service is either (i) outside the employer’s usual course of business, or (ii) performed outside all of the employer’s places of business. Any service central to what the company sells or delivers fails part (i). Remote-from-home work can satisfy part (ii), but only if the employer has no place of business where that function occurs — a narrow reading by NH Employment Security
Part C: Established independent business The individual is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business of the same nature as the service performed. Workers who provide the same service to multiple clients and operate as a genuine business satisfy Part C. Workers who are doing this role for the first time, or as a side arrangement, often fail it.

New Hampshire Employment Security applies this test strictly. The department has consistently held that knowledge workers performing services core to the hiring company’s product (software developers at a software firm, writers at a publishing house, accountants at an accountancy) fail Part B regardless of remote work, their own tooling, or their free hours. The presumption of employment attaches at the moment the service is within the company’s usual business, and no other factor can offset that.

James is a UX designer based in Nashua. A Manchester-based product design agency engages him on a six-month 1099 contract. He uses his own software licences, sets his own hours, invoices by the project, and works with two other agencies simultaneously. He passes all seven criteria on the workers’ comp test. On the ABC test, Part B fails: UX design is the agency’s core service. He is an employee for UI. The NHES audit arrives at month seven and recovers back contributions at 2.70% on a $14,000 wage base plus interest. The workers’ comp result is irrelevant to that outcome.

The practical implication: Part B is a categorical test, not a balancing test

Balancing tests (like the IRS 20-factor or the federal economic-reality test) allow a strong performance on some factors to offset weakness elsewhere. Part B of New Hampshire’s ABC test does not work that way. The question is binary: is this service inside the employer’s usual course of business? If yes, Part B fails. Full stop. No amount of worker independence on parts A or C rescues a Part B failure.

For employers hiring functional specialists who work on the company’s core deliverable (which covers most knowledge-worker 1099 arrangements), the safe answer in New Hampshire is W-2.

Does a written agreement provide a safe harbour for the workers’ comp test?

Partly. A written agreement signed by both parties at the time of engagement, affirming all 7 criteria are met, constitutes prima facie evidence of independent contractor status for workers’ comp under RSA 281-A:2, VI(c). It shifts the burden of proof at audit. It is not a conclusive safe harbour.

The prima facie evidence provision has been in force since 1 January 2011. A written agreement that:

  1. Describes the services to be performed, and
  2. Affirms that all 7 criteria in RSA 281-A:2, VI(b)(1)(A–G) are met

…is treated as prima facie evidence by the New Hampshire Department of Labor. In practice, it shifts the burden to the auditor to demonstrate that the criteria were not actually met. It does not bind the auditor. The facts on the ground (how the work was actually done, how often the worker communicated with the company, whether they actually served other clients) can rebut prima facie evidence.

What does misclassifying a New Hampshire worker actually cost?

Stacked liability across at least four enforcement tracks. A misclassified $60,000-per-year worker, audited at year three, exposes the employer to $60,000 to $100,000 in back taxes, back wages, FLSA double damages, and workers’ comp gap, before legal fees.

The workers’ comp consequence is the most open-ended. If Meredith had a workplace injury during the engagement (a repetitive strain claim, a slip and fall during an on-site visit, anything that triggers a compensation claim) and the company had no workers’ comp policy covering her, the company pays the medical bill directly, bears the wage-replacement obligation, and loses the statutory immunity that would have capped the claim at the workers’ comp schedule. A single uncovered injury can put six figures on the table before the UI audit ever opens.

The workers’ comp penalty track runs separately

Under RSA 281-A, an employer operating without required workers’ comp coverage faces a $2,500 one-time penalty plus $100 per employee per day of non-compliance. Purposeful non-compliance is a class B felony. The penalty clock starts from the first day a covered worker was engaged without a policy in place, not from the date the auditor finds it.

New Hampshire’s workers’ comp market is private-carrier only. There is no state fund. An employer who discovers a gap in coverage after the engagement has started cannot retroactively fill it. The gap period is exposed.

How does the 2026 federal rule interact with New Hampshire’s state tests?

The 2026 federal proposed rulemaking (restoring the 2021 economic-reality test for FLSA classification) sits on a separate track. New Hampshire’s state-law tests under RSA 281-A:2 and RSA 282-A:9 are unaffected by federal rule changes.

The federal Department of Labor proposed in February 2026 to restore the 2021 “economic reality” standard for determining whether a worker is an employee under the Fair Labor Standards Act. This matters for FLSA overtime analysis and the stacked-liability calculation above. It does not amend New Hampshire statutes.

In practice, New Hampshire’s ABC test for UI is already stricter than any version of the federal economic-reality test for the Part B question. A worker who fails NH’s Part B (because their work is inside the employer’s usual course of business) would likely also be an employee under any version of the federal test. The federal rule change narrows the edge cases at the margin. It does not open up classification options that NH already closes.
