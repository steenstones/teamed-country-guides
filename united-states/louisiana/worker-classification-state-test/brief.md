## Information-gain statement

> Louisiana has a two-track classification regime that most EOR providers ignore. For unemployment insurance, the LWC applies the ABC test under R.S. 23:1472(12)(E) — a worker is presumed an employee unless all three prongs are cleared (free from control; service outside usual course of business or its place; independently established trade). For workers' compensation and employment law more broadly, the state runs a common-law "control or direction" analysis inherited from the Civil Code's 1808-era Article 2747 framework. Both tracks can fire simultaneously. An employer who passes the ABC test for UI purposes can still be found in an employee relationship under the workers' comp control test — meaning one hiring arrangement can generate two separate misclassification exposures at once.

## Key compliance facts (sourced from louisiana.json cache + topic research)

### LWC ABC Test — R.S. 23:1472(12)(E)
- Three-prong test. Worker is an **employee** unless ALL THREE prongs are met:
  - **Prong A (Control):** Free from any control or direction over performance — both under contract AND in fact
  - **Prong B (Course of business):** Service is either (i) outside the usual course of the employer's business OR (ii) performed outside all the employer's places of business
  - **Prong C (Independent trade):** Customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business
- Source: `https://www.legis.la.gov/legis/Law.aspx?d=83555` / `https://www.laworks.net/UnemploymentInsurance/MisclassWorker.asp`
- **Burden of proof:** Employer must prove all three prongs — default presumption is employee

### R.S. 23:1711.1 — Rebuttable Presumption Safe Harbour (contractor side)
- Separate from the ABC test — applies when contracting party does NOT control performance
- Worker can be presumed independent contractor if meeting 6 of a listed criteria set (own business, holds out to others, accepts tax liability, etc.)
- This is a different route to independent contractor status from the ABC test
- Source: `https://law.justia.com/codes/louisiana/revised-statutes/title-23/rs-23-1711-1/`

### La. Civ. Code Art. 2747 overlay
- Louisiana is a civil-law state. Art. 2747 (originally 1808) is the statutory basis for at-will employment
- Text: "A man is at liberty to dismiss a hired servant attached to his person or family, without assigning any reason for so doing. The servant is also free to depart without assigning any cause."
- Workers' comp and tort-law classification cases use the common-law "control or direction" analysis derived from the Civil Code framework — distinct from the LWC's ABC test
- Two concurrent tracks means a single misclassification can generate UI liability (LWC) AND workers' comp exposure (LCIA / OWC) simultaneously

### IRS federal overlap
- The IRS three-category test (behavioural control, financial control, type of relationship) runs as a third, concurrent track at the federal level
- Federal employment taxes (FICA, FUTA, ITWH) are assessed on IRS standard, not Louisiana state standard
- A worker can be correctly classified as a contractor under Louisiana ABC test but still trigger IRS employee status — especially where behavioural control is high

### Misclassification penalties
- **LWC administrative:** Up to $500 per misclassified worker (each worker = separate offense), after written warning is issued
- **Civil penalties:** $5,000–$15,000 per violation; $10,000–$25,000 per violation for pattern or practice
- **Criminal:** Up to 90 days imprisonment
- **Debarment:** 3-year bar from state agency contracts
- **Back UI contributions:** Retroactive UI tax assessed on misclassified workers for up to 3 years
- **Source:** MSD Lawyers / LWC Performance Audit PDF / `https://msdlawyers.com/la-employers-face-5000-25000-fines-for-worker-misclassification/`
- **Note per sprint feedback rules:** The $500-per-worker figure is the LWC *administrative* ceiling after a written warning. The higher $5,000–$25,000 civil penalty figures come from a separate enforcement track. Both are valid and sourced; flag both.

### LWC audit record
- 2014–2018: 3,042 audits identified misclassified workers; employers owed ~$3M in unpaid UI contributions
- Source: LWC Performance Audit PDF (dam.ldr.la.gov)

### DOL 2026 federal rulemaking
- On 26th February 2026, DOL issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking to revise the FLSA independent contractor analysis (rescinding the 2024 rule, proposing "greater clarity and predictability")
- This creates a window of federal uncertainty that amplifies the importance of Louisiana's state-level ABC test compliance
- Source: `https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/flsa/misclassification/2026rulemaking`

## Disagreements between sources

- **LWC penalty amount:** Multiple sources mention $250–$500/worker as LWC administrative penalty. MSD Lawyers article cites separate civil statute ($5,000–$25,000). Both appear correct for different enforcement tracks (LWC administrative vs. civil action). Copy will present both clearly without conflating them.

Louisiana does NOT call it the "ABC test" in statute — the LWC guidance uses that framing colloquially. Copy will reference the statute directly and note the three-prong structure without calling it "ABC test" to stay primary-source rigorous.

## Answer capsule (draft)

> Louisiana uses a three-prong statutory test under R.S. 23:1472(12)(E) to determine if a worker is an employee for unemployment insurance purposes: the employer must show the worker is (A) free from control or direction in fact and by contract, (B) performing services outside the usual course of business or its premises, and (C) customarily engaged in an independently established trade. All three must be proved — the burden sits with the employer. A separate common-law control analysis under Louisiana Civil Code Article 2747 runs in workers' compensation cases. Misclassification carries LWC civil penalties of $5,000–$25,000 per violation and retroactive UI contributions. The DOL proposed a new federal rule on 26th February 2026 that adds a third, concurrent federal exposure.
