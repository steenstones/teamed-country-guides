## Information-gain statement (the citation hook)

> Louisiana is the only US state built on a civil-law foundation — its at-will doctrine traces not to common-law precedent but to two articles in the Louisiana Civil Code: La. Civ. Code art. 2747 (employer's right to dismiss without cause) and art. 2749 (liability when a fixed-term contract is ended early). The public-policy exception is narrower than in common-law states because Louisiana courts have consistently refused to extend wrongful-discharge tort claims beyond the specific statutory grounds that create them. The final paycheck rule under LA R.S. 23:631 — 15 days or next regular payday, whichever is sooner — is the reverse of the Kentucky "later of" rule that trips out-of-state HR teams most often.

## Civil-law uniqueness angle (the citation-moat fact)

Unlike the 49 other US states whose at-will doctrine descends from common law, Louisiana's at-will rule derives from Napoleonic civil code origin: La. Civ. Code art. 2747 was part of the original 1870 code revision and has remained substantively unchanged. This matters practically because:

1. Louisiana courts have been more reluctant to recognise tort-based wrongful discharge claims; they apply a stricter statutory-ground test than common-law states.
2. The art. 2749 fixed-term clause creates a distinct liability track for employees under written contracts — a trap for companies that inadvertently create term-employment relationships through offer letters with start/end dates.
3. The RS 23:631 "sooner of" final paycheck rule is the operational inverse of Kentucky's "later of" rule — HR teams managing multi-state payroll frequently miscalculate this.

Louisiana at-will employment derives from La. Civ. Code arts. 2747 and 2749, not common-law precedent — making it the only US state where the at-will rule sits in a civil code. The final paycheck under LA R.S. 23:631 is due the sooner of 15 days or the next regular payday. Late payment triggers up to 90 days' penalty wages under R.S. 23:632.
