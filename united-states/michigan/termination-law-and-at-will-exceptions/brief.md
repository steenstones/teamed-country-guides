## Information-gain statement

> Michigan is at-will employment with three judicially-created exceptions that most EOR guides don't explain separately: the *Suchodolski* public-policy exception (412 Mich 692), the *Toussaint* implied-contract exception (408 Mich 579, 1980), and the statutory Whistleblowers' Protection Act (Act 469 of 1980, MCL 15.361 et seq.). The *Toussaint* doctrine is the hidden compliance landmine: any Michigan employer handbook that lists reasons for termination, describes a performance improvement process, or uses phrases like "we treat employees fairly" may create an enforceable implied just-cause contract — stripping at-will status without the employer realising it. This page names the three exceptions, cites the cases, and explains how EOR insulation through Suna Solutions protects against all three.

## Answer Capsule (40–80 words)

> Michigan is an at-will employment state, but three exceptions override that default. The *Suchodolski* public-policy exception (412 Mich 692) bars termination for exercising a statutory right. The *Toussaint* implied-contract exception (408 Mich 579, 1980) means any handbook promising "fair treatment" may create an enforceable just-cause contract. The Whistleblowers' Protection Act (MCL 15.361 et seq.) prohibits firing employees who report illegal activity to public authorities. Final wages are due by the next regular payday under MCL 408.475.

## Source disagreement check

- MCL 408.475 described in cache as "next regular payday"; some sources say "immediately / as soon as determined with due diligence." Primary text of MCL 408.475 is authoritative: statute says wages must be paid "as soon as the amount can be determined with due diligence" and on the next regular payday. These are consistent — "next regular payday" is the outer limit; immediate payment when calculable is the standard. No material conflict.
