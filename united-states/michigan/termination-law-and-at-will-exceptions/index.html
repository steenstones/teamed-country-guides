Michigan is an at-will employment state, but three exceptions override that default. The Suchodolski public-policy exception (412 Mich 692) bars termination for exercising a statutory right. The Toussaint implied-contract exception (408 Mich 579, 1980) means any handbook promising fair treatment may create an enforceable just-cause contract. The Whistleblowers’ Protection Act (MCL 15.361 et seq.) prohibits firing employees who report illegal activity to public authorities. Final wages are due by the next regular payday under MCL 408.475.

Is Michigan an at-will employment state?

Yes. Michigan is an at-will employment state. Either party can end the employment relationship at any time, for any lawful reason, or for no reason at all.

The at-will default sits in Michigan common law, not a single codified statute. Courts have upheld it consistently for over a century. What matters for employers hiring in Michigan is the word 'lawful': the at-will rule only protects terminations that don't run into one of three recognised exceptions.

Those three exceptions are: the public-policy exception (Suchodolski), the implied-contract exception (Toussaint), and the statutory anti-discrimination and anti-retaliation protections (Whistleblowers’ Protection Act, Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act, Persons with Disabilities Civil Rights Act).

Michigan does not recognise the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing as a limit on at-will employment. That removes one category of wrongful-termination exposure that California employers carry, and it's one reason why Michigan is considered a cleaner hiring environment for US-state EOR clients.

Know the three exceptions before you write the offer letter.

What is the Toussaint exception in Michigan employment law?

The Toussaint exception creates an implied just-cause contract when your handbook, offer letter, or verbal statements promise fair treatment or list reasons for termination. It's the most preventable compliance risk in Michigan.

In Toussaint v. Blue Cross & Blue Shield of Michigan, 408 Mich 579 (1980), the Michigan Supreme Court held that employer statements in personnel manuals, if specific enough, create enforceable promises to terminate only for cause. You don't need a signed contract. The policy language does the job.

The standard was refined in Rowe v. Montgomery Ward, 437 Mich 627 (1991), which required 'special circumstances' for purely oral promises. But handbook-based implied contracts remain highly viable in 2026. The phrases that create exposure are exactly the ones well-meaning HR teams write: 'we treat employees fairly', 'termination will follow a performance improvement process', or a list of dismissal grounds that implies those are the only grounds.

What is the public policy exception to at-will employment in Michigan?

The public-policy exception, established in Suchodolski v. Michigan Consolidated Gas Co., 412 Mich 692, bars termination when the reason violates a clear mandate of public policy.

Michigan courts recognise three categories under Suchodolski:

CategoryWhat it coversCommon examples
1. Refusing to commit an illegal act Discharge for refusing to break the law on the employer's behalf Falsifying records, disposing of hazardous waste illegally
2. Exercising a statutory right Termination for using a right the law establishes Filing a workers’ compensation claim, taking FMLA leave
3. Reporting a violation of law Discharge for whistleblowing to a public body Reporting MIOSHA violations, fraud to a regulator

Workers’ compensation retaliation is one of the most common Suchodolski-based claims. Filing a claim is a clearly established statutory right; terminating within a short window after a claim is filed draws scrutiny. Document the legitimate business reason before any adverse action, and document it before, not after a demand letter arrives.

For employers hiring Michigan workers through EOR: the EOR structure doesn't create a safe harbour from wrongful-termination liability. The underlying termination decision has to be lawful. What does the Michigan Whistleblowers’ Protection Act protect?

Michigan's Whistleblowers’ Protection Act (Act 469 of 1980, MCL 15.361 et seq.) prohibits firing or retaliating against an employee who reports, or is about to report, a suspected violation of law to a public body.

The WPA is broader than federal whistleblower protections in one specific way: it covers employees who report a suspected violation, not just a confirmed one. An employee who reports to a government agency in good faith, believing a law has been broken, is protected even if no violation is ultimately found.

Critical compliance points for Michigan employers:

WPA elementDetailStatute
Filing window 90 days from alleged violation MCL 15.363(1)
Scope of ‘public body’ Government agencies, courts, law enforcement, regulators. Not internal HR. MCL 15.361(d)
Remedies on success Reinstatement, back pay, full fringe benefits, litigation costs, attorney fees MCL 15.364
Employer burden Must show the same action would have been taken absent the protected activity Case law

The WPA interacts with the Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act (MCL 37.2202) and the Persons with Disabilities Civil Rights Act (MCL 37.1202). All three can support independent claims arising from the same termination.

One termination can generate three independent claims. Review before acting.

What other Michigan statutes protect employees from wrongful termination?

Three statutes sit alongside the common-law exceptions: the Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act, the Persons with Disabilities Civil Rights Act, and the federal anti-discrimination layer.

The Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act (ELCRA, MCL 37.2202) prohibits termination based on race, colour, religion, sex, national origin, age, height, weight, marital status, sexual orientation, and gender identity. Michigan is one of the few states with statutory protection for height and weight: relevant where roles have physical requirements.

The Persons with Disabilities Civil Rights Act (PDCRA, MCL 37.1202) covers disability-based discrimination and applies to employers with one or more employees, not 15 as under the federal ADA.

Federal layer: Title VII, the ADEA, and the ADA apply on top of Michigan statutes. Where federal and state protections differ, an employee can claim under whichever standard is more favourable.

Any termination of a Michigan employee should be documented with a clear, non-discriminatory, non-retaliatory reason before notice is given. The documentation is your first defence, not something you reconstruct after a demand letter arrives.

ELCRA covers the employer hiring their first Michigan worker.

What are the WARN Act requirements for Michigan employers planning layoffs?

Michigan follows the federal WARN Act only. Covered employers, those with 100 or more full-time employees, must give 60 days advance written notice before a qualifying mass layoff or plant closing. Michigan has no separate state WARN law.

The federal WARN Act (29 CFR Part 639) triggers when a covered employer plans:

Event typeThresholdNotice required
Plant closing 50 or more workers lose employment within 30 days 60 days to affected employees, Michigan LEO Workforce Development, and local government
Mass layoff 500+ workers, or 50–499 workers representing 33%+ of workforce 60 days (same recipients)
Employer size threshold 100+ full-time employees (or equivalent in combined part-time hours) Applies WARN Act coverage

Michigan does not have a state-level WARN statute. For employers with 50 to 99 employees: federal WARN doesn't apply, Michigan WARN doesn't exist, so no statutory notice obligation exists below 100 employees, though you may still have contractual notice obligations.

When must Michigan employers pay final wages after termination?

Michigan requires final wages to be paid as soon as the amount can be determined with due diligence, and no later than the next regular payday. That's the outer limit, not the target.

MCL 408.475 governs final pay for both voluntary and involuntary separations. The same deadline applies whether the employee resigned or was discharged: next regular payday, on the schedule in effect during the final pay period.

Two points employers frequently miss:

You can't delay final pay pending return of company property. Withholding wages to recover a laptop is an MCL 408.475 violation. Equipment recovery is a separate civil matter.

Earned but unused PTO depends on your written policy. Michigan has no statute requiring PTO payout on separation, unlike California. If your policy says PTO is forfeited on termination, it's forfeited, but the policy must be written, clearly communicated, and applied consistently.

What should you know before terminating a Michigan employee through EOR?

Michigan's combination of at-will employment and the Toussaint implied-contract doctrine makes handbook review one of the most important pre-hire steps, more important than in most US states. Standard handbook templates drafted for California or New York often contain the exact language that creates a Michigan implied just-cause contract.

The Suchodolski, Toussaint, and WPA exceptions all generate independent litigation routes. A single termination that touches all three, for example dismissing an employee who raised a safety complaint (WPA), after a documented performance process (Toussaint), where the employee has a protected characteristic (ELCRA), can produce three simultaneous claims with three separate remedies.

Workers’ compensation retaliation remains one of the most common Suchodolski-based claims in Michigan. Terminating within a short window after a workers’ comp filing draws scrutiny. The legitimate business reason needs to be documented before the adverse action, not after.
