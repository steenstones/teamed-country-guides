What is Michigan's state income tax rate for employers in 2026?

Michigan taxes all individual income at a flat 4.25% in 2026. There are no brackets. The personal exemption is $5,900 per allowance claimed on Form MI-W4, up from $5,800 in 2025.

Michigan's flat-rate structure makes withholding calculation simpler than progressive-bracket states. You multiply taxable wages by 4.25% after subtracting the value of exemptions claimed on Form MI-W4. A single employee with no dependents claims one exemption ($5,900), reducing their annual taxable income by that amount before the flat rate applies.

The 2026 personal exemption of $5,900 was confirmed by Michigan Treasury in April 2026. Employees should update their MI-W4 if circumstances changed; a new dependent or a marriage changes the exemption count and the withholding calculation. Without a completed MI-W4 on file, Michigan law requires withholding at the single/zero exemptions rate, which over-withholds for most employees.

Michigan's flat rate has held at 4.25% since 2016, with a temporary reduction to 4.05% for 2023 under the state's revenue trigger mechanism, before restoration to 4.25% for 2024 and forward. There is no separate state standard deduction.

Employers file withholding returns and remit through Michigan's combined SUW (Sales, Use and Withholding) system. Annual reconciliation uses Form 5081, due 28th February. Source: Michigan Department of Treasury, 2026 Withholding Guide (446 Rev. 02-26).

Are tip income and overtime pay exempt from Michigan income tax in 2026?

Yes, temporarily. Michigan exempts tip income and overtime pay from the 4.25% personal income tax for tax years 2026, 2027, and 2028 under the Tipped and Hourly Workers Act, effective 1st January 2026.

Michigan's Tipped and Hourly Workers Act created a temporary income tax exemption for two wage categories. Tip income (gratuities received from customers) and overtime pay (hours above 40 per week at 1.5× rate) are excluded from Michigan taxable income for the three-year window. The exemptions expire after the 2028 calendar year unless the legislature extends them.

Two practical payroll changes follow. First, tipped employees need less Michigan state tax withheld against their tip amounts; you separate tip income from regular wages in your payroll system and reduce Michigan withholding on those amounts. Second, overtime hours must be flagged separately so the overtime premium is excluded from Michigan taxable wages. The base regular-rate earnings for overtime hours remain taxable; only the 0.5× uplift is exempt.

Build the 2028 sunset into any long-term cost modelling for tipped or overtime-heavy Michigan workforces. Source: Honigman LLP, Michigan Tipped and Hourly Workers Act compliance alert.

How does Michigan employer withholding work?

Michigan employers withhold state income tax at a flat 4.25% on each paycheck using the employee's MI-W4. Remittance frequency depends on your total withholding liability: annual, quarterly, monthly, or accelerated (EFT required at $40,000+ prior-year threshold).

The withholding process starts with Form MI-W4. Every new Michigan employee submits this before their first paycheck, capturing filing status, exemptions claimed, and any additional withholding requested. Employees who don't submit an MI-W4 default to the single/zero exemptions rate, producing over-withholding and W-2 friction at year-end.

Around 24 Michigan cities in total impose local income taxes; confirm each employee's city of residence at onboarding. What is Michigan's unemployment insurance tax rate in 2026?

Michigan UI tax rates run 0.06%–12.2% in 2026, up from a 10.3% ceiling in 2025. New employers pay 2.7%. The taxable wage base is $9,000 per employee per year.

The rate increase at the ceiling (12.2% vs 10.3% in 2025) reflects Michigan UIA's response to pandemic-era trust fund drawdowns. Most experienced employers don't sit at the ceiling; your individual rate tracks actual benefit charges against your account across the prior three fiscal years.

Michigan's experience rating has three components: the Chargeable Benefits Component (CBC), the Account Building Component (ABC), and the Nonchargeable Benefits Component (NBC). The UIA computes your rate and sends Form UIA 1771 each January. You have 30 days to challenge it.

How do I register for Michigan unemployment insurance as an employer?

New Michigan employers register through MiUI, the UIA's new employer portal launched December 2025 to replace the legacy MiWAM system. You'll need your Federal Employer Identification Number (FEIN) to start.

Michigan completed a major UI portal migration in December 2025. MiUI is now the employer-facing system for tax functions: registration, quarterly wage reporting, and payments. MiWAM still exists for some claimant-side functions but is no longer the primary employer tax portal.

The Q1 2026 transition caused disruption. The UIA granted a one-time penalty waiver for employers who missed the standard April 25, 2026 quarterly deadline, extending it to 31st May 2026. If you're setting up Michigan payroll now, file through MiUI from the start.

Does Michigan require paid sick leave in 2026?

Yes. Michigan's Earned Sick Time Act (ESTA), Public Act 2 of 2025, took effect 21st February 2025 and requires all private employers to provide paid sick time. Large employers (11+ employees) provide up to 72 hours; small employers up to 40 hours.

The ESTA replaced the narrower Paid Medical Leave Act (PMLA) and extends paid sick leave obligations to all employers, including those with fewer than 50 employees. Accrual runs at one hour per 30 hours worked, starting from the employee's date of hire. New employees may be required to wait up to 120 calendar days before using accrued hours, though the accrual clock starts on day one.

Employers can frontload the full annual amount at the start of the benefit year instead of tracking accrual. Carryover caps are 72 hours for large employers and 40 hours for small employers.

The ESTA covers a broad range of qualifying reasons: personal illness, medical appointments, care for qualifying family members, and domestic violence leave. It does not create a paid family leave entitlement. Michigan has no state paid family and medical leave (PFML) programme. Federal FMLA (unpaid, 12 weeks) applies only to employers with 50 or more employees.

New businesses get three years from first hire, or until they reach 11+ employees (whichever comes first), before full large-employer ESTA compliance kicks in. Sources: Paychex, Michigan ESTA compliance guide; Burr & Forman, ESTA amendments effective date.

What should you know before running Michigan payroll in 2026?

Michigan's flat income tax is simpler than most US states, but 2026 added two operational wrinkles. The temporary tip and overtime exemptions require payroll system changes before you can withhold correctly. And the MiUI platform transition means any UI account setup or Q1 2026 filing done through the old MiWAM portal needs to be verified in the new system.

Detroit is the most common city-tax complication. If any Michigan employees live in Detroit, you need a separate Detroit withholding account and must apply Detroit's tables (2.4% residents, 1.2% non-residents). Grand Rapids, Lansing, Flint, and roughly 20 other cities also levy local income taxes; confirm each employee's city of residence at onboarding, not after the first paycheck.

Michigan is an at-will state. Final paychecks are due on the next regular payday after separation (MCL 408.475); there's no shorter window triggered by the method of termination. Michigan has no state PFML, which makes it a lower statutory leave-cost state compared to New York, California, or Washington.

What is the MiUI portal in Michigan?

MiUI is Michigan's new unemployment insurance employer portal, launched December 2025 to replace the legacy MiWAM system. Employers use MiUI to register for UI, file quarterly UIA 1028 wage reports, and pay UI contributions. Quarterly reports are due on the 25th of the month after each quarter-end.

Does Michigan require paid family leave in 2026?

No. Michigan does not operate a state paid family and medical leave (PFML) programme. Michigan employers must comply with the Earned Sick Time Act (ESTA, effective 21 February 2025), which requires paid sick time for all employees: 72 hours per year for employers with 11 or more employees, 40 hours for smaller employers. Federal FMLA (unpaid, 12 weeks) applies to employers with 50 or more employees.
