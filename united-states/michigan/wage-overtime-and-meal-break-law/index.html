How does Michigan wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

Michigan's minimum wage hits $13.73 in 2026. Overtime is federal only. No adult meal-break mandate. And from this year, overtime pay itself is temporarily exempt from state income tax.

Michigan's minimum wage is $13.73 per hour from 1 January 2026, under the Improved Workforce Opportunity Wage Act (Public Act 337 of 2018, as amended). The tipped cash wage is $5.49; tips plus cash must equal $13.73 or the employer makes up the gap. Michigan has no state overtime law; the FLSA 40-hour weekly threshold and 1.5x multiplier govern. There is no statutory meal-break for adult employees; a 30-minute break is required only for minors working more than 5 consecutive hours. Final wages are due on the next regular payday under MCL 408.475. Note: Michigan temporarily exempts overtime pay from its 4.25% state income tax for 2026–2028.

What is the minimum wage in Michigan in 2026?

Michigan's minimum wage is $13.73 per hour from 1 January 2026. It's one of the higher floors in the Midwest.

The rate comes from the Improved Workforce Opportunity Wage Act, which Michigan's legislature amended significantly in 2024 through HB 4001 and HB 4002. The original 2018 ballot initiative (the "Improved Workforce Opportunity Wage Act") set a trajectory toward $12.00; the 2024 amendments replaced that schedule. The current 2026 figure of $13.73 was confirmed by the Michigan Department of Labor and Economic Opportunity in December 2025.

The 2024 legislative rewrite: what changed

In 2023, Michigan voters passed Proposal 22-2, which restored the original ballot-initiative wage schedule and would have eliminated the tipped wage credit entirely by 2028. The Michigan legislature pre-empted the full ballot outcome in February 2024 through HB 4001 and HB 4002. The 2024 amendments preserved the tip credit (tipped workers still receive a sub-minimum cash wage of $5.49), replaced the original ballot schedule with a new trajectory, and set 2026 at $13.73. The tipped credit path forward differs materially from the original ballot measure. Any payroll system seeded from pre-2024 guidance should be re-verified.

Marcus is a software engineer in Grand Rapids. His offer letter anchors to $85,000 per year, well above any minimum-wage question. The $13.73 floor still matters for Marcus's employer: if any deduction, rounding error, or missed hours results in effective compensation below $13.73 per hour in a workweek, the employer faces back-wage liability. The floor is a floor, not just a headline for low-wage workers.

Verify the 2026 rate against the Michigan LEO press release (michigan.gov/leo, December 2025) before finalising any offer below $20 per hour for a Michigan employee. Future-year rates are not yet confirmed beyond 2026; the post-2024-amendment schedule is subject to further legislative adjustment.

Michigan follows federal overtime law. No daily trigger, no double-time.

Michigan has no state overtime statute. The FLSA 40-hour weekly threshold and 1.5x regular rate govern every non-exempt Michigan employee. No daily overtime, no seventh-day premium.

There is no Michigan equivalent to California's 8-hour daily trigger or Washington's seventh-day rule. You pay overtime on every hour over 40 worked in a single 168-hour workweek. The regular rate calculation includes non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, and commissions earned during the same workweek; it excludes discretionary bonuses, gifts, and expense reimbursements.

The Michigan-specific wrinkle: overtime pay is temporarily exempt from state income tax

2026–2028 overtime tax exemption: Michigan has temporarily exempted overtime pay from its 4.25% state income tax for tax years 2026, 2027, and 2028. Employers must track overtime pay separately in payroll systems and reduce Michigan withholding on those hours accordingly. The exemption expires on 31 December 2028. This is a state withholding obligation only; federal income tax still applies to overtime pay at normal rates.

Priya is an operations manager in Ann Arbor, salaried at $72,000 per year. She regularly works 50-hour weeks during product launches. Her employer must confirm two things: first, that her primary duties clear the executive or administrative exemption under the FLSA duties test (job title and salary alone do not make her exempt); second, if she is non-exempt, that every hour over 40 in a workweek is paid at 1.5 times her regular rate. If she is exempt, the company does not need to pay overtime but still must not reduce her salary below $684 per week in any week where she works.

Exempt classification in Michigan

To be exempt from overtime in Michigan, an employee must pass both a salary basis test and a duties test. The operative salary threshold is $684 per week ($35,568 per year). The Biden administration's 2024 DOL rule that would have raised this to $1,128 per week was vacated nationwide by a federal court in Texas in November 2024. The $684-per-week floor is what applies in Michigan as of May 2026. Misclassification is the most common overtime-exposure source in Michigan. The state's wage-and-hour enforcement record is above the US average; a misclassified employee has four years to pursue a wilful FLSA claim.

Michigan is also among the states with active plaintiff-side wage litigation. A Detroit-area software developer paid as exempt but whose primary duty is executing defined tasks without genuine discretion fails the FLSA duties test. Back overtime runs from the date of misclassification. Include the duties-test review in your onboarding flow before the offer letter issues, not after.

Does Michigan require meal breaks for employees?

No, for adult employees. Michigan has no statutory meal-break or rest-break requirement for workers aged 18 and over. The minor-employee rule requires a 30-minute break after 5 consecutive hours for workers under 18.

MESA Minor rule

Michigan minor meal-break rule

The Michigan Youth Employment Standards Act requires a 30-minute break for any employee under 18 who works more than 5 consecutive hours. No equivalent provision covers adult workers aged 18 and over. The break applies at the shift level, not the daily level.

Federal FLSA rules fill the gap for adults. When you choose to offer breaks:

  • Rest breaks under 20 minutes count as paid working time and go into the 40-hour weekly overtime calculation.
  • Meal periods of 30 minutes or more, where the employee is fully relieved of all duties, can be unpaid and excluded from hours worked.
  • Working through a meal period converts it to paid time. An employee who answers email or takes client calls during a 30-minute unpaid lunch has not received a bona fide meal period.

Jordan works a warehouse shift in Lansing, starting at 6:00 am and ending at 2:30 pm. The employer designates a 30-minute unpaid lunch at 11:00 am. If Jordan answers two work messages on her phone during that window, the 30 minutes are on the clock and go toward overtime. Document your break policy in writing and train supervisors on the paid-vs-unpaid distinction. Ad-hoc break practices are the easiest source of retroactive wage claims.

The PUMP Act, a 2022 federal amendment to the FLSA, requires all employers to provide reasonable break time and a private, non-bathroom space for nursing employees to express breast milk for up to one year after a child's birth. This applies in Michigan regardless of employer size. Document the accommodation.

Most Michigan employers offer a 30-minute unpaid lunch and two 10-to-15-minute paid breaks per eight-hour shift as standard practice, not statute. That practice is fine; what matters is that it is written, consistently applied, and not modified mid-shift without a policy update.

When must an employer pay a final paycheck in Michigan?

MCL 408.475 requires final wages on the next regular payday following separation. Michigan makes no distinction between a resignation and a termination for final-pay timing.

The Michigan Payment of Wages and Fringe Benefits Act, MCL 408.471 through 408.490, is the governing statute. The deadline is the next scheduled regular payday after separation. Unlike California, which requires immediate payment on the day of an involuntary termination, Michigan draws no discharge-versus-resignation distinction. Both trigger the same next-regular-payday rule.

Two points for payroll teams running Michigan:

  • No same-day rule. If you terminate an employee on a Monday and your payroll cycle closes on Friday with a payment on the following Thursday, that is compliant. You are not required to issue an out-of-cycle payment on the day of termination.
  • Deductions from final pay require written consent. Michigan courts have consistently held that deducting for unreturned laptops, outstanding advances, or uniforms without prior written authorisation violates MCL 408.477. Get the written deduction authorisation before the employee's first day, not at offboarding.

Michigan's next-regular-payday rule is administratively simpler than immediate-pay states, but its deduction restrictions carry real bite. Running an unauthorised deduction through a final paycheck is the same violation as withholding the wages entirely under Michigan law. Document every authorised deduction in the offer letter and onboarding packet.

What is the Michigan Payment of Wages and Fringe Benefits Act?

MCL 408.471–408.490 governs pay frequency, final-pay timing, deduction limits, and fringe-benefit obligations for every Michigan private employer with at least one employee.

The Michigan Payment of Wages and Fringe Benefits Act is the primary wage-payment statute. Beyond final pay, its key operating requirements:

  • Pay frequency. Wages must be paid at least semi-monthly (twice per month) under MCL 408.472. Bi-weekly cycles satisfy this. Monthly-only pay is not permitted.
  • Written itemised pay statement. Each pay period, employees must receive a statement showing total hours worked, pay rate, gross wages, and all deductions. Electronic payslips meet this requirement.
  • Deductions. Employers may only deduct amounts authorised by law (tax, court-ordered withholding) or specifically authorised in writing by the employee. An authorisation obtained post-hire does not validate a retroactive deduction.
  • Fringe benefits. The Act covers accrued vacation, commissions, and other earned fringe benefits. Accrued vacation you have promised is a wage under Michigan law; a policy that forfeits earned vacation on termination is unenforceable unless it meets specific notice requirements.

Michigan's Wage and Hour Division investigates wage complaints. For unpaid-wage claims, the Division can order back wages plus an equal penalty amount; for wilful violations the Division and courts can also award attorney fees. The combination of a double-damages penalty and mandatory attorney-fee exposure is why Michigan sits among the higher-litigation states in the Midwest. A named in-house payroll specialist monitoring every Michigan payslip for deduction accuracy and fringe-benefit treatment is the operational minimum for a clean compliance posture.

Accrued vacation treatment is the fringe-benefit trap most out-of-state employers hit first. If your employee handbook says "accrued vacation is paid out on termination," Michigan courts treat that as a wage promise. A written "use-it-or-lose-it" policy that meets the Act's notice requirements can limit liability; a policy that silently forfeits earned vacation on the last payslip cannot.
