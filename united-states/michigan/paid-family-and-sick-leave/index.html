How does Michigan paid family and sick leave work in 2026?

72 hours of paid sick leave for larger employers. A 2025 law that survived a constitutional challenge and then got amended the same day it took effect. And no state PFML at all, unlike California, New York, or Washington.

Michigan has no state Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML) programme. There are no employer or employee PFML payroll taxes and no state wage-replacement insurance for parental or family-care leave. What Michigan does have, since 21st February 2025, is the Earned Sick Time Act (ESTA), Public Act 2 of 2025, which replaced the narrower Paid Medical Leave Act and extended paid sick leave to all private employers. Under HB 4002 (signed the same day), employers with 11 or more employees provide up to 72 hours of paid sick leave per year; employers with 10 or fewer employees provide up to 40 hours. Accrual is 1 hour per 30 hours worked. New employees may wait up to 120 days before using accrued hours. Federal FMLA (unpaid, 12 weeks) applies to Michigan employers with 50 or more employees. What is Michigan's Earned Sick Time Act (ESTA) and who does it cover?

One platform handles the entire stack: payroll, contracts, taxes, and compliance, no third-party tools to stitch together.Michigan's Earned Sick Time Act, Public Act 2 of 2025, took effect 21st February 2025 and applies to all private employers in Michigan, regardless of size. It replaced the narrower Paid Medical Leave Act, which only covered employers with 50 or more employees.

The ESTA's origin is notable. It started as a 2018 ballot initiative, was immediately amended by the lame-duck legislature to create the less protective Paid Medical Leave Act, and then was reinstated in its original form by the Michigan Supreme Court in Mothering Justice v. Attorney General (decided 18th July 2022). The ruling gave the legislature 90 days to adopt or amend the original initiative. The legislature adopted it with significant amendments via HB 4002, signed the same day the law took effect on 21st February 2025.

What did HB 4002 change about the Michigan Earned Sick Time Act?

HB 4002, signed 21st February 2025, was the legislature's amendment to the reinstated ESTA initiative. It kept the universal employer coverage and the 72-hour cap for larger employers, but created a two-tier structure and extended the small-employer compliance deadline.

Understanding the amendment matters for compliance audit purposes. The original reinstated initiative would have required all private employers, regardless of size, to provide 72 hours of paid sick leave annually. HB 4002 changed three things:

  1. Two-tier accrual caps. Employers with 10 or fewer employees are capped at 40 hours paid per year, not 72. The 1-hour-per-30-hours accrual rate applies to both tiers; only the annual cap differs.
  2. Extended small-employer deadline. Small employers were given until 1st October 2025 to comply fully, giving a roughly seven-month transition window from the 21st February 2025 effective date.
  3. New business transition period. New businesses get three years from their first hire, or until they reach 11 or more employees (whichever comes first), before the full large-employer ESTA obligations apply. In practice this means a start-up can operate under the small-employer (40-hour) rules for up to three years even if it crosses the 11-employee threshold.

What HB 4002 did not change: the universal private-employer coverage, the 1-hour-per-30-hours accrual rate, the 120-day waiting-period option, the frontloading option, the qualifying reasons for leave, the anti-retaliation protections, and the notice and documentation rules.

One important nuance: the 90-hour annual cap that was present in the original 2018 initiative text is not in the enacted law. The ESTA as enacted via HB 4002 has 72-hour and 40-hour caps, not 90 hours. If you see references to a 90-hour threshold in older commentary predating the HB 4002 amendment, those reflect a version of the law that was never implemented. Sources: Michigan LEO, ESTA FAQ; Paychex, Michigan ESTA compliance guide.

How does Michigan ESTA accrual and carryover work?

Employees accrue 1 hour of paid sick time for every 30 hours worked, starting from their hire date. Carryover caps match the annual caps: 72 hours for larger employers, 40 hours for smaller employers.

The accrual calculation is straightforward. A full-time employee working 40 hours per week accrues approximately 1.33 hours per week and reaches the 72-hour cap in roughly 54 weeks. Part-time and variable-hours employees accrue proportionally.

Employers have two implementation options:

  • Accrual method. Track hours worked, apply the 1:30 ratio, credit earned hours weekly or per pay period, enforce the annual cap and carryover cap.
  • Frontloading method. Grant the full annual cap (72 or 40 hours) at the start of each benefit year. No weekly tracking required. The waiting period still applies for new employees (they can be required to wait up to 120 days before use, even if frontloaded).

Frontloading avoids the administrative overhead of per-hour tracking and is the more common approach for Michigan employers running EOR arrangements. There is no ESTA requirement to pay out unused accrued hours on separation (unlike California), unless you have combined ESTA with a general PTO policy that includes a payout-on-termination term.

Carryover caps mirror the annual caps. Accrued hours above the cap at year-end are forfeited. An employer may choose to allow carryover above the cap, but nothing in ESTA requires it. Sources: Michigan LEO, ESTA FAQ.

What qualifies for Michigan ESTA paid sick leave?

Michigan ESTA covers six broad qualifying reasons: the employee's own health, care for a family member, preventive medical care, domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking affecting the employee or a family member. Employers cannot require disclosure of the specific reason.

The ESTA qualifying reasons are deliberately broad to prevent employers from interrogating employees about the nature of their absence. The six categories are:

  1. Personal illness or injury. The employee's own physical or mental illness, injury, health condition, or need for medical diagnosis, care, or treatment.
  2. Family member care. Care for a qualifying family member with an illness, injury, health condition, medical diagnosis, or need for care or treatment.
  3. Preventive care. The employee's or family member's preventive care medical appointment.
  4. Domestic violence (employee). Leave related to domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking experienced by the employee, including medical attention, legal proceedings, safety planning, or relocation.
  5. Domestic violence (family member). Same as above for a qualifying family member who experiences domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking.
  6. Public health closure. Closure of the employer's business or the employee's child's school or childcare facility by order of a public health authority due to a public health emergency.

Qualifying family members under ESTA include: the employee's child (biological, adopted, placement, or step-child), spouse, domestic partner, parent (biological, adoptive, placement, or step-parent), grandparent, grandchild, or sibling. Employees may also use ESTA leave to care for a person for whom the employee is responsible for providing care.

Documentation rules. For absences longer than three consecutive days, an employer may request reasonable documentation (such as a signed statement from a healthcare provider) confirming the leave is for a qualifying reason. The employer cannot require documentation specifying the diagnosis or the details of the domestic violence situation. Employers must pay for any out-of-pocket documentation cost. Sources: Michigan LEO, ESTA: Workers' Rights; Michigan Earned Sick Time Act, MCL 408.961 et seq.

Does Michigan have a state Paid Family and Medical Leave programme in 2026?

No. Michigan has no state-funded Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML) programme in 2026. There is no employer payroll contribution and no state wage-replacement benefit for parental leave, bonding leave, or family care leave beyond the ESTA paid sick leave entitlement.

This is the line that surprises most multi-state employers moving from California, New York, Washington, Colorado, or Massachusetts into Michigan. Those states run statutory insurance programmes (SDI/PFL, NYPFL/DBL, WA PFML, FAMLI, PFML) that pay partial wage replacement during qualifying leaves, funded by payroll contributions. Michigan runs none of those.

In Michigan, an employee taking bonding leave after the birth of a child, or extended leave to care for a seriously ill parent, receives:

  • Up to 72 hours of ESTA paid sick time (if they have accrued it) for any qualifying ESTA reason.
  • Federal FMLA unpaid leave of up to 12 weeks (if the employer has 50 or more employees and the employee is eligible).
  • Whatever voluntary employer-paid parental or family-care policy the employer has chosen to offer.

There is no Michigan state benefit you can point the employee to. No state wage-replacement payment will arrive. An employee taking 12 weeks of FMLA leave to bond with a new child will receive ESTA pay for the first 72 hours (9 days at 8 hours per day) and then nothing, unless the employer has a voluntary paid parental leave policy.

This makes voluntary employer-provided paid parental leave a meaningful differentiator in the Michigan talent market. The bench of competitors who offer paid parental leave in Michigan includes every employer who has designed a policy; there is no state floor driving the market from below.

Does federal FMLA apply to Michigan employers?

Yes. The federal Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) applies to Michigan employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. Eligible employees get up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period. Michigan has no separate state FMLA equivalent.

Michigan has no state equivalent of FMLA. The federal Act is the only job-protected leave entitlement for events like bonding with a new child or caring for a seriously ill family member, and it kicks in only at the 50-employee threshold.

For Michigan employers below 50 employees, ESTA provides paid sick time for qualifying reasons (including care for a family member's illness). There is no job-protected unpaid family leave for employers in the sub-50 band beyond any contractual commitments in an employment agreement. This is a material gap for small-to-medium Michigan employers that do not run a voluntary paid family-care policy.

ESTA leave and FMLA run concurrently for employers that have both obligations. An employee using ESTA hours for a reason that also qualifies for FMLA (such as a personal serious health condition) is consuming both entitlements at the same time, not in sequence. This is the standard US approach: designate qualifying absences simultaneously against both buckets. Source: 29 U.S.C. §§ 2601 et seq.; 29 CFR Part 825.

What notice and documentation can Michigan employers require under ESTA?

Employers can require advance notice of up to 7 days for foreseeable leave. For unforeseeable leave, employers can require notice as soon as practicable. For absences over three consecutive days, employers can request documentation.

Michigan ESTA strikes a deliberate balance between employer scheduling needs and employee privacy. The main rules:

  • Foreseeable leave (scheduled appointments, planned procedures): up to 7 days advance notice is allowed. Employers can require the notice in writing and specify a standard form.
  • Unforeseeable leave (sudden illness, emergency): notice must be given as soon as practicable. An employee who calls in sick on the day of the absence has satisfied the notice requirement if they contact the employer consistent with the employer's usual absence reporting procedures.
  • Reason disclosure: employees are never required to disclose the specific nature of a health condition or the details of a domestic violence situation. The qualifying reason category (own illness, family care, domestic violence) is sufficient.
  • Documentation for extended absences: for absences of more than three consecutive work days, a signed statement from a healthcare provider or other authorised documentation source confirming the leave qualifies under ESTA is allowed. The employer cannot demand a specific diagnosis. If the documentation requirement imposes an out-of-pocket cost on the employee, the employer must cover that cost.

Anti-retaliation. Employers cannot discipline, discharge, demote, or otherwise retaliate against an employee for using ESTA leave, requesting ESTA leave, or filing an ESTA complaint. Requesting leave is protected regardless of whether the leave is ultimately approved. The burden shifts to the employer to show any adverse action was taken for a reason unrelated to the ESTA leave.

Posting requirement. Michigan employers must display the ESTA notice poster issued by the Michigan Department of Labor and Economic Opportunity in a conspicuous workplace location. A digital version must be provided to remote employees. Failure to post is a violation regardless of whether any employee was harmed. Source: Michigan LEO, ESTA employer obligations.

What pregnancy and parental accommodation does Michigan require?

Michigan's Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act (ELCRA) prohibits employment discrimination based on sex, which courts and the Michigan Civil Rights Commission interpret to include pregnancy and related conditions. The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (federal, effective June 2023) adds an explicit reasonable-accommodation obligation for all employers with 15 or more employees.

Two layers of protection apply:

1. Michigan Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act. ELCRA covers all Michigan employers with one or more employees. It prohibits discrimination based on sex (including pregnancy and pregnancy-related conditions) in hiring, terms of employment, discharge, and reasonable accommodation. The Michigan Civil Rights Commission and Michigan courts treat pregnancy as a condition requiring reasonable accommodation under the same interactive-process framework applied to disability accommodation. Common accommodations: more frequent breaks, modified duty, temporary reassignment, time off for prenatal appointments.

2. Federal Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA). The PWFA, which took effect June 2023, applies to employers with 15 or more employees. It requires reasonable accommodation for known limitations related to pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions. It applies even when the limitation is not a disability under the ADA. The employer must engage in an interactive process on request and may deny only on an undue-hardship showing.

Lactation. The federal PUMP Act (Providing Urgent Maternal Protections for Nursing Mothers Act, 29 U.S.C. § 218d) requires reasonable break time and a private space (not a bathroom) for nursing employees to express milk for one year after childbirth. Applies to all employers covered by the FLSA. Michigan does not add a separate state lactation law, so the PUMP Act is the operative standard.

How does Michigan ESTA stack with FMLA and employer PTO?

ESTA and FMLA run concurrently when the qualifying reason overlaps. ESTA paid hours can be used during FMLA leave to receive pay while the FMLA clock runs. Employer PTO and ESTA are tracked separately; combining them has payout-on-separation consequences.

Michigan is simpler than Illinois or California on leave stacking because there is no second state programme (no PFML insurance, no city ordinance like Chicago). The stack for a Michigan employee is:

  1. ESTA paid sick time. Up to 72 hours (or 40 hours for small employers) for any qualifying ESTA reason. No disclosure of specific reason required. This is the paid floor.
  2. Federal FMLA (if applicable). Up to 12 weeks job-protected unpaid leave for qualifying FMLA events, at 50+ employer threshold. Runs concurrently with ESTA when the reason qualifies for both.
  3. Voluntary employer PTO. Any hours the employer offers above the ESTA floor. The employer can designate voluntary PTO to run during FMLA as well.

Concurrent running. When an employee uses ESTA hours for a reason that also qualifies under FMLA, the employer should designate the absence against both buckets simultaneously. This is standard US FMLA administration. The employee receives paid time from ESTA (or PTO) during the same week that counts against their 12-week FMLA entitlement. The employee is not entitled to 72 hours of ESTA first, then 12 weeks of FMLA on top.

PTO and ESTA tracking. If you maintain a combined PTO policy (one bucket for sick, personal, and vacation), that combined PTO is subject to Michigan Wage Payment and Collection Act payout-on-separation rules for any accrued portion. ESTA hours tracked separately do not require payout on separation under ESTA itself. Keeping them on separate ledgers avoids inadvertently creating a larger payout obligation.

Michigan paid family and sick leave: common questions

Does Michigan have a state paid family leave programme?

No. Michigan does not have a state Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML) programme. There is no employer or employee payroll contribution for state PFML, and no state wage-replacement benefit for parental or family-care leave. Michigan workers rely on the Earned Sick Time Act (ESTA) for paid sick leave, and on federal FMLA (unpaid, 12 weeks) for longer family or medical leave events at employers with 50 or more employees.

How many paid sick days does Michigan require in 2026?

Michigan ESTA requires up to 72 hours per year (9 days at an 8-hour day) for employers with 11 or more employees, and up to 40 hours per year (5 days) for employers with 10 or fewer employees. Accrual runs at 1 hour per 30 hours worked. Employers may frontload the full amount at the start of the benefit year as an alternative to tracking accrual.

What is the Michigan ESTA employer size threshold?

The ESTA two-tier threshold is 11 employees. Employers with 11 or more employees provide up to 72 hours of paid sick leave per year. Employers with 10 or fewer employees provide up to 40 hours. Count all employees at the start of each benefit year to determine which tier applies.

Can Michigan employers require a waiting period before using sick leave?

Yes. Michigan ESTA allows employers to require new employees to wait up to 120 calendar days before using accrued sick time. Accrual starts from day one; only the right to use the time is delayed. After 120 days, employees can access any hours they have accrued.

Does Michigan ESTA require payout of unused sick leave on termination?

No. ESTA does not require payout of unused accrued sick leave on employment separation. However, if you have combined ESTA with a general PTO policy (a single bucket for sick, personal, and vacation time), the Michigan Wage Payment and Collection Act may require payout of the full combined balance on separation. Tracking ESTA hours separately from voluntary PTO avoids this risk.

Does Michigan ESTA apply to part-time and temporary workers?

Yes. ESTA applies to all employees, including part-time, seasonal, and temporary workers. Accrual is proportional (1 hour per 30 hours worked), so a part-time employee working 15 hours per week accrues at half the rate of a full-time employee. There is no minimum hours-per-week threshold to be covered by ESTA.
