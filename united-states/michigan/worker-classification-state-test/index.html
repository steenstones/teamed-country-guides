What test does Michigan use to classify workers as employees?

Michigan uses three separate tests depending on context. There's no single universal classification rule in this state.

For Unemployment Insurance (UI) purposes, Michigan adopted the IRS 20-factor common-law control test effective 1st January 2013, codified at MCL 421.42 (Michigan Employment Security Act). The test derives directly from IRS Revenue Ruling 87-41. Any worker an employer is required to withhold federal income tax from is prima facie an employee under the Michigan Employment Security Act.

For income-tax withholding, the relevant framework is the IRS three-category control test: Behavioural Control (instructions, training), Financial Control (investment, profit/loss opportunity, methods of payment), and Type of Relationship (contracts, benefits, permanency, integral services).

For the construction sector, a third framework applies: the Michigan Construction Industry Worker Classification Act (2017), which sets specific criteria for construction contractors and subcontractors.

Michigan has no ABC test. Senate Bills 6 and 7 (reintroduced 2026) would create one, but neither has been enacted as of 30th May 2026.

What are the 20 factors Michigan uses for unemployment insurance?

Michigan UIA applies IRS Revenue Ruling 87-41's 20-factor test. No single factor is decisive.

The 20 factors group into three broad questions: who controls the work method, who bears the financial risk, and what kind of relationship exists. Here's the full list Michigan UIA applies under MCL 421.42:

MCL 421.42: The 20-factor checklist
  1. Instructions: must comply with instructions about when, where, how
  2. Training: required to follow set methods (indicates employment)
  3. Integration: work is integral to the core business
  4. Services rendered personally: must perform personally
  5. Hiring/supervising/paying assistants: done by the hiring party
  6. Continuing relationship: ongoing vs project basis
  7. Set hours of work: hours set by employer
  8. Full-time required: works substantially full time
  9. Doing work on premises: works at employer's location
  10. Order or sequence set: required to follow set sequence
  11. Oral or written reports: required to report progress
  12. Payment by hour, week, or month: periodic payment
  13. Payment of expenses: employer pays business/travel costs
  14. Furnishing tools/materials: employer supplies equipment
  15. Significant investment: worker invests in own facilities (IC indicator)
  16. Realising profit or loss: worker bears own financial risk (IC indicator)
  17. Working for multiple firms simultaneously: multiple unrelated clients (IC indicator)
  18. Making services available to general public: markets independently (IC indicator)
  19. Right to discharge: employer can fire without contractual consequences
  20. Right to terminate: worker can quit without financial liability
Factors 15–18 favour independent contractor status. All others favour employment.

No single factor controls the outcome. A worker strong on factors 15–18 (independent investment, profit/loss risk, multiple clients, public marketing) may still be classified as an employee if factors 1–14 point the other way. The Michigan UIA weighs the totality of the relationship.

What are the penalties for misclassifying workers in Michigan?

Current penalties include back taxes, workers' comp liability, and civil fines. Proposed 2026 legislation would add felony charges for repeat offenders.

Michigan's misclassification consequences run across four enforcement channels today. The proposed Senate Bills 6 and 7 would add a fifth (felony-level criminal exposure) if enacted.

How does the Michigan construction worker classification work?

Construction workers face a separate Michigan statute from 2017. The IRS 20-factor UI test doesn't apply as the primary framework here.

The Michigan Construction Industry Worker Classification Act (effective 2017) creates a distinct classification regime for the construction sector. If you're engaging workers on Michigan construction projects, the IRS 20-factor test used for UI is not your primary reference. This sector-specific statute is.

Under the CIWCA, a construction worker is more likely to be considered a bona fide independent contractor if they: hold a valid Michigan construction contractor licence; operate as a separate business entity; provide their own tools and equipment; control the method and manner of their own work; work under a written contract that specifies deliverables; are free to work for multiple clients simultaneously; and bear their own business risk.

Michigan construction workers face the highest misclassification audit rates in the state. The $1,000/day workers' comp penalty is the dominant financial exposure for construction employers. A misclassified worker injured on a Michigan job site creates direct liability from day one.

"In Michigan construction, the licence question isn't a formality. It's the first gate. A worker without a valid contractor licence is presumed an employee under the CIWCA regardless of what the contract says."

Frequently asked questions: Michigan worker classification

Does Michigan use the ABC test for worker classification?

No. Michigan does not have an ABC test as of 30th May 2026. The state uses the IRS 20-factor common-law control test (MCL 421.42) for Unemployment Insurance and the IRS three-category control test for income-tax withholding. Senate Bills 6 and 7, reintroduced in 2026, would introduce a stricter test but have not been enacted.

What is the Michigan 20-factor test for worker classification?

Michigan adopted the IRS 20-factor test from Revenue Ruling 87-41 effective 1st January 2013 under MCL 421.42. The factors cover instructions, training, integration, personal service requirement, control over assistants, continuity of relationship, set hours, full-time requirement, work location, sequence of work, reporting, payment method, expenses, tools, worker investment, profit/loss risk, multi-firm work, public availability, right to discharge, and right to terminate.

What are the penalties for worker misclassification in Michigan?

Current law: back UI contributions plus interest, workers' comp liability including a $1,000-per-day fine for lack of coverage, and civil fines up to $1,000 under the Improved Workforce Opportunity Wage Act. Proposed Senate Bills 6 and 7 (not yet enacted) would add a $10,000 fine and two-year felony for repeat intentional misclassification.

Does the Michigan construction worker classification work differently?

Yes. The Michigan Construction Industry Worker Classification Act (2017) creates a separate classification regime for construction workers. The IRS 20-factor UI test is not the primary framework for construction. Construction workers face the highest misclassification audit rates in Michigan.
