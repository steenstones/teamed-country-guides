What is the New Jersey state income tax rate for employers in 2026?

New Jersey state income tax uses seven graduated brackets in 2026, from 1.4% on the first $20,000 to 10.75% above $1,000,000. Employers withhold using NJ-W4 elections.

New Jersey has one of the more complex individual income tax structures in the United States. The graduated rate schedule means the effective rate for most mid-range earners sits in the 5.525% to 6.37% range, but the 10.75% top bracket (applied to NJ taxable income above $1 million) is the highest in the mid-Atlantic region.

What is the full New Jersey employer payroll tax stack in 2026?

New Jersey employers pay UI, TDI, and WF/SWF on the $44,800 wage base. Employees contribute UI, TDI, FLI, and WF/SWF, with TDI and FLI using the higher $171,100 wage base.

Unemployment Insurance (UI). New Jersey uses Tax Table C for 2025-2026. New employers pay a flat 2.8% combined rate for the first three calendar years; this includes 2.6825% UI plus 0.1175% for the Workforce Development and Supplemental Workforce Funds. After three years, experience-rated employers are assigned rates between 0.5% and 3.6% for positive reserve ratios, or 5.1% to 5.8% for deficit reserve ratios. The taxable wage base increased to $44,800 in 2026 (from $43,300 in 2025). The maximum weekly UI benefit rose to $905 per week (from $875).

Temporary Disability Insurance (TDI). Both employers and employees contribute. Employer TDI rates are experience-rated from 0.10% to 0.75% on the $44,800 wage base. Employees contribute 0.19% on wages up to $171,100, with a maximum annual withholding of $325.09. The maximum weekly TDI benefit is $1,119 in 2026, calculated at 85% of the employee's average weekly wage. TDI provides income replacement for non-work injuries, illness, or pregnancy; it's distinct from workers' compensation.

Family Leave Insurance (FLI). Funded entirely by employee payroll deductions. The 2026 employee rate is 0.23% on wages up to $171,100, with a maximum annual contribution of $393.53. Employees who take qualifying family leave receive 85% of their average weekly wage, up to $1,119 per week, for up to 12 weeks. This benefit pays during NJFLA leave (see Family Leave section below). Employers contribute nothing to FLI directly.

What family leave does New Jersey require employers to provide in 2026?

The New Jersey Family Leave Act (NJFLA) provides 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 24-month period. From 17th July 2026, it covers employers with 15 or more employees (reduced from 30).

Change effective 17th July 2026: The NJFLA now covers employers with 15 or more employees (previously 30). Employee eligibility drops from 12 months/1,000 hours to 3 months/250 hours worked. If your New Jersey headcount crosses 15, you're covered from that date.

NJFLA leave is unpaid, but employees combine it with FLI cash benefits, paid at 85% of average weekly wages up to $1,119/week. The result is near-full income replacement for most employees during qualifying family leave. Qualifying reasons include bonding with a new child, caring for a seriously ill family member, or responding to a public health emergency.

A3451 (signed January 2026, effective July 17 2026) added a separate job-protection obligation for employees taking TDI or FLI leave. Covered employers must reinstate employees to the same or equivalent position (same seniority, status, benefits, and pay) upon return from TDI or FLI leave. This is distinct from FMLA (federal, 50 employees, 12 months eligibility) and applies in addition to it.

New Jersey also requires all employers to provide earned sick leave: one hour per 30 hours worked, up to 40 hours per benefit year, under the New Jersey Earned Sick Leave Law (N.J.S.A. 34:11D-1 et seq.). Applies from the first employee. Employees can carry over up to 40 unused hours.

How does New Jersey classify workers as employees or independent contractors?

New Jersey uses the ABC test under N.J.S.A. 43:21-19(i)(6). All three prongs must be satisfied to classify a worker as an independent contractor. The burden of proof is on the employer.

The ABC test has three prongs:

  • Prong A. The worker is free from control and direction over the performance of services, both by contract and in fact.
  • Prong B. The work performed is either outside the usual course of the business for which the work is performed, or is performed outside all the places of business of the enterprise.
  • Prong C. The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business.

Prong B is the most common failure point. If your contractor's work is the same type of work your business does (a software company hiring a software engineer as a contractor, for example), Prong B is not satisfied regardless of how the contract is written. The test applies across the New Jersey Unemployment Compensation Law, the Wage and Hour Law, and the Wage Payment Law.

On 5th May 2026, the New Jersey Department of Labor and Workforce Development adopted final regulations clarifying ABC test application. The new rules become operative on 1st October 2026 and provide specific guidance on how the NJDOL will interpret and enforce the test. Misclassification exposure in New Jersey includes back UI, TDI, FLI contributions plus interest and penalties. The same six-year statute of limitations applies as for wage complaints.

What does New Jersey's WARN Act require for mass layoffs?

New Jersey's WARN Act requires 90 days notice plus one week of severance per year of service for qualifying mass layoffs. It covers employers with 100 or more employees anywhere in the US.

New Jersey's WARN Act, officially the Millville Dallas Airmotive Plant Job Loss Notification Act (N.J.S.A. 34:21-1 et seq.), is significantly stricter than the federal WARN Act. The key 2023 amendments moved NJ WARN from 60 to 90 days notice and added mandatory severance pay, eliminating the employer's ability to waive the severance obligation even with adequate notice.

The employer threshold is the other key difference. NJ WARN applies to employers with 100 or more employees anywhere in the United States, not just at the New Jersey location, as long as the employer has operated in New Jersey for more than three years. What should out-of-state companies know before hiring in New Jersey?

New Jersey's payroll layer is one of the most complex in the northeast. The two-tier wage base, July 2026 NJFLA expansion, and October 2026 ABC test rules mean three compliance deadlines in one calendar year.

Three things that catch out-of-state employers in New Jersey. First, the two-tier wage base ($44,800 for UI/TDI employer/WF; $171,100 for employee TDI/FLI) generates calculation errors in payroll systems that only handle a single state wage base. This produces both under-withholding and over-employer-contribution simultaneously. Second, the NJFLA coverage expansion effective 17th July 2026 means any employer reaching 15 employees in New Jersey is now subject to 12 weeks of job-protected leave obligations, including the new TDI/FLI job reinstatement right under A3451. Third, the NJDOL's final ABC test regulations (operative 1st October 2026) tighten enforcement of Prong B specifically for service businesses. If contractors perform work that is the same type as your core business, you have a reclassification risk.

New Jersey minimum wage is $15.92/hour from 1st January 2026, above the federal floor. At-will employment applies, with no statutory notice requirement for standard terminations. Final pay is due by the next regular payday.

Frequently asked questions about New Jersey employer taxes

What is the New Jersey UI tax rate for new employers in 2026?

New Jersey UI tax rate for new employers in 2026 is 2.8% combined (2.6825% UI plus 0.1175% WF/SWF) on the first $44,800 of each employee's wages. Experience-rated employers pay 0.5% to 3.6% under Tax Table C for positive reserve ratios.

What is New Jersey's state income tax top rate in 2026?

New Jersey's top state income tax rate is 10.75% in 2026, applying to taxable income above $1,000,000. The state uses seven graduated brackets from 1.4% on the first $20,000 up to 10.75% above $1 million.

Does New Jersey have Family Leave Insurance for employees?

Yes. New Jersey Family Leave Insurance (FLI) is funded entirely by employee payroll deductions at 0.23% on wages up to $171,100 in 2026 (maximum $393.53/year). Employees receive 85% of average weekly wages up to $1,119/week for up to 12 weeks of qualifying family leave.

What is the New Jersey WARN Act notice period?

New Jersey's WARN Act (Millville Dallas Airmotive Plant Job Loss Notification Act) requires 90 days advance notice for qualifying mass layoffs, up from 60 days before the April 2023 amendments. Employers with 100 or more employees anywhere in the US must also pay one week of severance per year of service.

What is the New Jersey ABC test for independent contractors?

New Jersey uses the ABC test under N.J.S.A. 43:21-19(i)(6) to determine worker classification. Employers must establish all three prongs: the worker is free from control; the work is outside the usual course of the business; and the worker is engaged in an independently established trade.
