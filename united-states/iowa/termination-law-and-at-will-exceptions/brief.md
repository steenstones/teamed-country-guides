## Iowa-specific termination law facts (from compliance-cache/iowa.json + topic research)

### At-will status
Iowa is an at-will employment state. No statute codifies this; it is established under Iowa common law and reinforced by the Iowa Supreme Court. Either party may end the employment relationship at any time, for any reason not prohibited by law or by contract.

### At-will exceptions (case-law based — NOT a codified statutory list)

Iowa's public-policy exceptions to at-will are judicially created, not codified:

1. **Workers' compensation retaliation** — Iowa Code § 85.18: an employee cannot be discharged for filing or intending to file a workers' compensation claim. This is the clearest Iowa statute-based exception.

2. **Iowa Civil Rights Act (ICRA)** — Iowa Code Ch. 216: prohibits discharge based on race, colour, creed, sex, sexual orientation, gender identity, national origin, religion, age (18+, for employment), disability, or familial status. Employer threshold: 4+ employees.

3. **Whistleblower — public employees** — Iowa Code § 70A.28: protects state employees who report violations of law in good faith.

4. **Common-law public-policy tort** — *Springer v. Weeks & Leo Co.* (Iowa 1988) established that Iowa recognises a tort for discharge in violation of a well-recognised public policy expressed in a statute or the Iowa Constitution. Case law has recognised jury-duty service (Iowa Code § 607A.45) and military service (Iowa Code Ch. 29A) as qualifying policy anchors.

5. **Express or implied contract** — A handbook with language that could be read as "discharge only for cause" may create an implied contract. Iowa courts apply an objective-expectation standard: did the handbook language, in context, create a reasonable expectation of continued employment?

**Iowa has no statutory equivalent to Arizona's AEPA closed list.** Each exception is litigated on the merits of the claimed public-policy anchor.

### Iowa Civil Rights Act — employer threshold
4+ employees (smaller than federal Title VII threshold of 15). This means Iowa state anti-discrimination protection covers significantly more employers than the federal statute.

### Final pay — Iowa Wage Payment Collection Act (IWPCA)
- **Iowa Code § 91A.4:** wages are due on the regular payday for the pay period in which the employee's last day fell. Iowa does not impose an immediate-payment rule for involuntary terminations different from the payday rule (unlike Arizona's 7-working-day rule). The employer must pay by the next regular payday.
- **What counts as "wages" under IWPCA:** base pay, commissions and bonuses earned under the written plan before separation, and any accrued PTO/vacation that the employer's written policy makes payable at separation. Iowa does not mandate PTO payout — it is driven by the written policy.
- **Penalty for IWPCA violation:** Iowa Code § 91A.8 — if an employer fails to pay wages due and the failure is intentional, the employee can recover the unpaid wages **plus liquidated damages equal to the unpaid amount** (i.e., double the shortfall), plus reasonable attorney's fees and costs. For a $5,000 shortfall: $10,000 in wages + damages, plus fees.
- **Demand letter requirement:** the employee must give the employer written demand before bringing an IWPCA civil action. Iowa Code § 91A.10. Employer has 7 days to cure after written demand before litigation exposure attaches.

### No state WARN Act
Iowa has no state mini-WARN. Federal WARN (29 U.S.C. § 2101) applies: 60 days' written notice for a plant closing or mass layoff at a site with 100+ full-time employees. Notice goes to the Iowa Workforce Development Rapid Response unit, affected employees (or union), and the chief elected official of the affected local government.

### Iowa Civil Rights Commission (ICRC)
The Iowa Civil Rights Commission handles state-level anti-discrimination charges under the ICRA. Filing deadline: **300 days** from the alleged discriminatory act (Iowa mirrors the federal deferral-state extension). Iowa is a deferral state, so EEOC charges also run on the 300-day clock, same as Arizona.

### Iowa Code § 91D — minimum wage
State minimum wage = $7.25/hour (matches federal). Iowa House File 295 (2017) preempts local minimum wage laws — no city or county can set a higher minimum. Final-pay calculations must include any overtime owed under FLSA.

### No mandatory notice period or severance
Iowa has no statute requiring employers to give advance notice of termination (below WARN thresholds) or to pay severance. Severance is contractual.

## Answer capsule (40-80 words)

Iowa is an at-will state. Fire an Iowa employee today and you owe wages by the next regular payday under the Iowa Wage Payment Collection Act (Iowa Code § 91A.4). Miss that deadline with the wrong intent and the employee can claim double the shortfall plus attorney's fees. Iowa's public-policy exceptions live in case law, not a codified statute, with the Iowa Civil Rights Act covering employers with 4 or more employees.
