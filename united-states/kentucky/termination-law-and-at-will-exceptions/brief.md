## Information-gain statement

Kentucky is at-will with a narrower-than-average set of statutory exceptions. The critical differentiators no competitor explains precisely: (1) the final paycheck deadline under **KRS 337.055** is *the later of* next regular payday *or* 14 days — not whichever is sooner, meaning a resigned employee whose next payday falls in 20 days waits 20 days; (2) the implied-contract exception turns on whether the employer handbook *omits a clear at-will disclaimer* (Nork v. Fetter Printing Co., 738 S.W.2d 824, 1987); (3) the KCRA applies at **8 employees** for most protected classes but jumps to **15** for disability and pregnancy — a threshold gap that catches small US remote employers off-guard; (4) the statute of limitations for KCRA claims shortened from 5 years to **3 years** effective 15 July 2024 (HB 320).

## Tom tagline-close anchor (Kentucky-specific)

Key facts to wire into Tom's note:
- KRS 337.055 final paycheck: "the later of" framing — 14 days OR next regular payday, whichever is later
- KCRA fires at 8 employees for most protected classes — not the familiar 15 threshold most HR teams assume from Title VII
- Statute of limitations on discrimination claims shortened to 3 years (from 5) as of 15 July 2024 — the employer's exposure window changed last year
- Handbook disclaimer = your implied-contract defence — without one, a handbook policy can flip an at-will relationship
