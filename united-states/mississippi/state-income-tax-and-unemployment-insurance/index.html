What is Mississippi's state income tax rate in 2026?

Mississippi income tax is a flat 4.0% in 2026 on all taxable income above $10,000. The first $10,000 is exempt at zero.

The 4.0% rate is the result of a scheduled reduction from 4.4% in 2025, itself a step down from 4.7% in 2024. Mississippi moved to a flat-rate structure in 2022 when it consolidated its prior three-bracket system. For employers, this means a single withholding rate applies to all employees earning above the $10,000 threshold, with no bracket cliff to track.

An employee earning $50,000 pays state income tax on $40,000 of that. An employee earning $85,000 pays on $75,000. The effective rate is always below the headline 4.0% because the first $10,000 is sheltered.

Is Mississippi eliminating its income tax?

Yes. House Bill 1 (Build-Up Mississippi Act), signed 27th March 2025, puts Mississippi on a fixed schedule to 3.0% by 2030. Revenue triggers after 2030 can reduce the rate to zero by approximately 2037.

Governor Reeves signed HB 1 on 27th March 2025. The bill establishes automatic annual reductions from 2027 through 2030, reaching 3.0% with no conditions attached. Beyond 2030, a revenue-trigger mechanism applies: if state revenues exceed projected spending by at least 0.85% of the cost of a 1% rate cut, the rate drops by 0.2% to 0.3% each year. Full elimination is targeted by approximately 2037 if triggers are met consistently.

For employers building multi-year cost models: the 2026 rate of 4.0% is confirmed. The 2027 rate of 3.75% is scheduled law, no trigger required. Plan conservatively through 2030 at 3.0%; model the post-2030 reductions only if your projections extend to 2032 and beyond.

The same HB 1 legislation cut Mississippi's grocery sales tax from 7% to 5%, effective 1st July 2025. Useful context when benchmarking compensation: your Mississippi employees' cost of living improved meaningfully in the second half of 2025. Source: MSIADA, Overview of the Build-Up Mississippi Act.

How does Mississippi employer withholding work?

Mississippi employers withhold state income tax using Form 89-105. Monthly filing for employers above $600 withheld per year; quarterly for smaller ones. Annual reconciliation on Form 89-140 by 31st January.

The withholding process starts with Form 89-350, which every new employee submits before their first paycheck. Form 89-350 captures filing status, personal exemptions, and dependent count. Without a completed 89-350 on file, Mississippi requires withholding at the default rate with zero exemptions.

Standard deductions for 2026: $2,300 for single filers, $4,600 married filing jointly, $3,400 head of family. Personal exemptions: $6,000 single, $12,000 married, $9,500 head of family, $1,500 per dependent.

How do I register for Mississippi unemployment insurance as an employer?

New Mississippi employers register through the MDES employer portal at mdes.ms.gov/employers. Registration is required once you've paid $1,500 or more in wages in any quarter, or had one employee for 20 weeks in a year.

The MDES (Mississippi Department of Employment Security) employer portal handles new account registration, quarterly filings, and rate notice access. You'll need your federal EIN, business address, and the date you first paid wages in Mississippi. MDES issues your account number and initial UI rate after registration.

What is the Mississippi unemployment insurance tax rate in 2026?

New Mississippi employers pay 1.0% in year one, 1.1% in year two, and 1.2% from year three. The taxable wage base is $14,000 per employee. Maximum annual UI cost at the new-employer rate: $140.

Mississippi SUTA rates range from 0.0% to 5.4% for experienced employers. Your rate within that band tracks your benefit ratio: the total UI benefits charged to your account divided by your taxable wages over prior periods. MDES issues an annual rate notice each January with your updated rate.

Mississippi is FUTA credit-compliant as of 2026 (no outstanding federal loan balance). What do out-of-state companies need to know before hiring in Mississippi?

Mississippi's payroll stack is one of the lightest in the US. No local income tax, no state disability, no paid family leave. The main gap most payroll guides skip: no state final-pay statute. Termination timing follows federal FLSA defaults.

Three things that matter for first-time Mississippi employers. First, there's no county piggyback income tax. A California employer used to layering LA County or SF City rates onto state tax will find Mississippi payroll unusually clean. One withholding rate. One state portal. Second, the minimum wage is the federal floor of $7.25/hour. Mississippi law prohibits local governments from setting higher rates, so you won't encounter city-specific minimum wages as you might in Seattle or New York.

Third, and the one most payroll guides miss: Mississippi has no state final-pay statute. The prior provision under the Mississippi Employment Security Act (MSEA) had a sunset clause that was not renewed after 2014. Employers must follow the federal FLSA default: final paycheck on the next scheduled payday, whether the employee resigned or was terminated. That's less protective for employees than the next-business-day rules in California or Colorado, but it's the law.

Workers comp coverage is required for employers with five or more employees under Mississippi Code § 71-3-5, with average premiums around $1.10 per $100 of payroll. New hire reporting is due to the Mississippi Department of Human Services within 15 days of hire.
