## Editorial position

Mississippi is the most employer-friendly at-will state in the country when measured by the narrowness of its public-policy exception. The McArn rule (1993) covers one scenario only: firing an employee for refusing to commit an illegal act at the employer's direction. No general public-policy tort. No implied-covenant-of-good-faith tort. No private-sector whistleblower statute.

The editorial lens is: state-law shield is genuine but narrow; the federal claim layer is where the real risk sits; and the 180-day EEOC window is shorter in Mississippi than in most states (no state-agency worksharing agreement to extend it to 300 days).

---

## Key facts sourced from compliance cache

- **At-will baseline:** Strict. Presumed in all employment absent a written contract.
- **Public-policy exception:** McArn v. Allied Bruce-Terminix Co., 626 So. 2d 603 (Miss. 1993). Refusal to commit an illegal act only. Narrowest in US.
- **Implied-contract exception:** Recognised where handbook language is specific, communicated, and accepted. Clear at-will disclaimer defeats claim.
- **Private-sector whistleblower protection:** None. Miss. Code Ann. § 25-9-171 covers state employees only.
- **Final pay statute:** None. FLSA next-regular-payday default applies on both voluntary and involuntary separations.
- **State WARN Act:** None. Federal WARN only (100+ FTE employer threshold, 60 days' notice).
- **Severance requirement:** None statutory. Contractual only.
- **EEOC charge window:** 180 days (not 300 — no state worksharing agreement).
- **Federal appeals court:** Fifth Circuit (among most employer-friendly on summary judgment).
- **Pay frequency:** Miss. Code § 71-1-35 — 50+ employees must pay at least twice monthly.
