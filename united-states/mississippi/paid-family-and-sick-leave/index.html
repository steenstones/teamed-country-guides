Does Mississippi require paid family or sick leave in 2026?

No. Mississippi has no state paid family leave law and no paid sick leave mandate for private employers. Federal FMLA gives 12 weeks unpaid at 50+ employees. Below that threshold, the leave package is entirely yours to design.

Mississippi is one of the clearest states to answer on leave law: there is none that applies to private employers. No state paid family leave programme, no state paid sick leave mandate, no state disability insurance payroll deduction. What exists is the federal floor (FMLA at 50 employees), and everything else is the voluntary package you write into the offer letter.

The State of Mississippi runs a Paid Parental Leave Pilot for its own government employees (launched 2023). It does not affect private employers. It does, however, set a floor expectation in the minds of candidates who've worked in state government and now consider private-sector roles.

A competitive voluntary leave package in Mississippi runs 1.5 to 2.5 percent of payroll. That is the cost of not losing a hire to a remote-first employer who offers state-funded benefits through a California or New York payroll.

Does Mississippi require paid family leave?

No. Mississippi has no state paid family leave law. No contributions, no claim portal, no benefit schedule.

The state is not among the nine states plus Washington DC that run statutory paid family leave programmes. There is no Mississippi PFL deduction on the payslip, no employer-side PFL premium, no leave benefit for a Mississippi private-sector employee who needs time off for a new child or a seriously ill family member beyond what federal FMLA provides unpaid.

Dana is an operations manager at a 28-person Ridgeland software firm. She is benchmarking her parental-leave offer against a competitor with California employees who collect the California PFL benefit at up to 70 percent of wages for eight weeks. Dana's offer letter has to do the entire job itself because Mississippi provides nothing.

Her decision: 10 weeks paid for the primary caregiver, 4 weeks for the secondary, day-one eligibility. Cost across the workforce is approximately 1.8 percent of payroll. The hire she would have lost otherwise cost more than a year of that line.

The Mississippi Paid Parental Leave Pilot (state employees only)

In 2023, the State of Mississippi introduced a Paid Parental Leave Pilot for state government employees. The pilot offers eligible state employees up to 6 weeks of paid parental leave following the birth, adoption, or state-supervised placement of a child. It is administered through the Mississippi State Personnel Board and applies only to permanent full-time state employees.

This programme has no legal effect on private employers. It does not create a state PFL mandate, does not establish a contribution schedule, and does not create any entitlement for employees of private companies. It matters commercially, not legally: Mississippi state government employees now have an employer-paid parental leave benefit, and private employers who compete for the same talent pool are measured against it.

Does Mississippi require paid sick leave?

No. Mississippi has no state paid sick leave law for private employers.

Paid sick days at a Mississippi private-sector job are whatever the offer letter says they are. The 18 states and dozens of cities that mandate accrued paid sick time do not include Mississippi, and Mississippi law prohibits local governments from enacting their own minimum-leave requirements that exceed state law.

State government employees accrue sick leave under the Mississippi State Personnel Board rules (typically one day per month), but that has no bearing on private employers. A private company in Jackson, Gulfport, or Oxford can offer zero, two, or fifteen paid sick days and remain fully compliant with Mississippi law.

Marcus is a developer at a 22-person Hattiesburg startup. He has been there 9 months. He gets sick and burns through his three sick days. His offer letter capped him at three. FMLA does not apply (company is under 50 employees). There is no state PSL floor. Whether he gets paid for day four is a manager's call. That uncertainty is what loses him to the remote-first competitor six months later.

What does federal FMLA give Mississippi employees?

Federal FMLA gives qualifying Mississippi employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period. Group health coverage continues at the employer's normal contribution rate.

It applies only to employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. The employee qualifies after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked. FMLA is federal law; Mississippi has no state FMLA analogue that lowers the threshold.

The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 is the entire federal floor for unpaid, job-protected leave in Mississippi. The 50-employee threshold counts all US employees, not just Mississippi ones. A company with 32 Mississippi employees and 20 Tennessee employees is FMLA-covered for both states. The multi-state headcount rule catches employers off guard more often than the 75-mile radius clause.

50+ US employees · 75-mile radius 12 months tenure · 1,250 hours 12 weeks unpaid · job protected Group health continued

Five qualifying reasons trigger FMLA leave:

  • Birth and care of a newborn, within 12 months of birth
  • Placement of a child for adoption or state-supervised care, within 12 months of placement
  • Care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition
  • The employee's own serious health condition that prevents them performing the job
  • Qualifying military exigency; up to 26 weeks for military caregiver leave

What FMLA does not cover

  • Pay during leave. FMLA is unpaid by statute. There is no federal or Mississippi wage-replacement benefit.
  • Employers under 50. A 35-employee Mississippi company has no FMLA obligation. No state mini-FMLA fills the gap.
  • New hires. An employee with less than 12 months' tenure has no FMLA right even if the employer has 50+ employees.
  • State disability insurance. Mississippi has no SDI programme. There is no weekly benefit that partially replaces wages during a medical leave.

What protections apply for pregnancy and disability in Mississippi?

Federal law applies. Mississippi adds nothing on top.

The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA), effective 27th June 2023, requires reasonable accommodation for pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions at any employer with 15 or more employees. This is the most important pregnancy-related protection for employers below the 50-employee FMLA threshold.

Between 15 and 49 employees, PWFA covers reasonable accommodation. FMLA does not yet apply, so there is no statutory 12-week job-protected hold for the birth itself. An employer with 22 employees in Hattiesburg must accommodate a pregnant employee's schedule changes and medical appointments but is not obligated to hold the job for 12 weeks of unpaid leave beyond what PWFA's accommodation framework requires.

The gap this creates

Between 15 and 49 employees, the gap between PWFA accommodation (which Mississippi employers must provide) and FMLA job-protected leave (which they don't yet owe) is the most common leave-policy design question at this headcount band. Most Mississippi employers in this range offer 6 to 8 weeks of paid parental leave voluntarily to close it. The State of Mississippi's own 2023 Pilot set the bar at 6 weeks for state employees; private employers competing for the same talent need to meet or exceed that.

What about military leave and jury duty in Mississippi?

Federal USERRA protects civilian jobs for service members. Mississippi mirrors it for state active duty under Mississippi Code Annotated section 33-1-19.

Mississippi requires employers to give employees time off for jury service and prohibits any adverse action for serving. Pay continuation during jury duty is not required by the state statute for private employers, but most multi-state employers continue full pay and offset the court per diem.

What about employers with fewer than 50 employees in Mississippi?

Below 50 employees, Mississippi imposes no mandatory leave law at all for private employers, with three narrow federal exceptions: PWFA accommodation at 15+, USERRA reemployment, and jury duty non-retaliation.

Everything else (paid parental leave, paid sick leave, paid bereavement, paid time off, short-term disability) is voluntary. The offer letter is the only document that matters.

That is the freedom and the risk. A 28-person company in Ridgeland pays nothing in state PFL premiums, runs no leave ledger, and has no statutory protected-leave clock to manage. The cost is entirely borne when the employee who doesn't get paid parental leave accepts a job somewhere else.

The 50-employee FMLA trigger in Mississippi

An employer in Mississippi hits the FMLA 50-employee threshold based on total US headcount, not just Mississippi headcount. The trigger is 20 or more weeks of 50+ employees in either the current or preceding calendar year.

Once triggered, the FMLA obligation continues for the rest of the current calendar year and the full following year, even if headcount drops below 50. For a Mississippi employer scaling through a funding round, the lookback means FMLA obligations outlast the headcount that tripped them.

Dana's 28-person Ridgeland firm is below 50 employees. If the company closes a Series A and hires 25 people over the next 6 months, it crosses the FMLA threshold in the same quarter. FMLA notice posters, rights-and-responsibilities notices, and the eligibility-determination process become mandatory obligations that quarter.

Designing the voluntary package: Mississippi benchmarks for 2026

The pattern Teamed sees for clients hiring their first three to fifteen Mississippi employees: 8 weeks paid parental leave, 7 paid sick days, 3 paid bereavement days, full pay during jury duty. Cost is approximately 1.5 to 2.5 percent of payroll. It is the difference between retaining a hire through family formation and losing them to a remote-first employer with California or New York employees on state-funded leave.

Private employers who want to recruit from that talent pool need to match or exceed it. Eight weeks paid, offered by a private employer from day one, beats the state's own pilot programme.
