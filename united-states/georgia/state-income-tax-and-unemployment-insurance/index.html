How does Georgia state income tax and unemployment insurance work in 2026?

Georgia is a flat-tax state on a downhill schedule. HB 463 (signed 11 May 2026 by Governor Brian Kemp, retroactive to 1 January) cuts the rate from 5.19 percent to 4.99 percent and sets a new 0.125 percentage-point annual reduction toward a 3.99 percent floor, paired with a $9,500 SUTA wage base, a single withholding form (G-4) that retained the older allowance mechanics, and zero city or county payroll tax.

Georgia state payroll in 2026: the flat state income tax rate is 4.99 percent under HB 463 (signed 11 May 2026 by Governor Brian Kemp, retroactive to 1 January), down from 5.19 percent in 2025. Future cuts run at 0.125 percentage points per year on a published schedule toward a 3.99 percent long-term floor, conditional on annual revenue triggers. State Unemployment Insurance (SUTA) costs 2.7 percent on the first $9,500 of wages for new employers, with experience-rated employers banded between 0.04 percent and 8.10 percent, per Georgia Department of Labor. Every new hire completes Form G-4 declaring marital status and number of allowances; Georgia did not adopt the post-2020 federal W-4 redesign. Employers file Form G-7M monthly, G-7Q quarterly, or a G-7 semi-weekly schedule depending on prior-year withholding totals, and reconcile annually on Form G-1003 by 31 January. Georgia has no city, county, or municipal payroll tax, anywhere in the state.

What is the Georgia state income tax rate in 2026?

Georgia charges a 4.99 percent flat rate on individual income in 2026, cut from 5.19 percent under HB 463 (signed 11 May 2026 by Governor Brian Kemp, retroactive to 1 January). This is the third Georgia rate cut in three years and arrives three years ahead of the original HB 1437 schedule. Future cuts run at 0.125 percentage points per year toward a 3.99 percent floor, subject to revenue triggers.

The schedule, not the rate, is the planning hook

Georgia stands out among US states in that its income tax rate is reliably moving downward on a published legislative schedule. HB 1437 (2022) set the original 0.10 percentage-point annual reduction targeting a 4.99 percent floor by 2029. The 2025 Assembly accelerated that schedule under HB 111, and the 2026 Assembly accelerated it again under HB 463, reaching the 4.99 percent floor three years early and setting a new 0.125 percentage-point annual reduction targeting a long-term 3.99 percent floor by approximately 2034. Any forward-looking offer-letter take-home model that uses a static rate is wrong; bake the schedule in. Source: OCGA 48-7-20 as amended by HB 463, accessed 26 May 2026.

The revenue-trigger contingency

HB 463 makes each year’s annual cut contingent on state revenue benchmarks. In any year the trigger does not fire, the rate stays flat. This is not a problem in practice for a payroll model (you stress-test both the cut-fires and the cut-skips scenarios), but it is a reason not to embed the 2027 rate as 4.865 percent in your gross-to-net calculator until DOR confirms it in late December 2026 via the annual Important Tax Updates page. Source: HB 463 fiscal note, legis.ga.gov, accessed 26 May 2026.

How it compares regionally

Georgia now sits below most of its south-east neighbours on the personal income side. Mississippi runs a 4.0 percent flat rate, Tennessee and Florida levy no individual income tax, North Carolina runs a 4.25 percent flat rate, South Carolina tops at 6.4 percent on a graduated scale, Alabama tops at 5 percent. Georgia’s pull is the schedule: rate certainty plus a downward trajectory the rest of the region cannot match year-on-year. Source: Tax Foundation 2026 State Tax Rates, accessed 26 May 2026.

What does a Georgia employer pay in unemployment insurance (SUTA)?

New Georgia employers pay 2.7 percent on the first $9,500 of each employee’s annual wages, capped at $256.50 per employee per year. After roughly two to three years of benefit-risk experience, the rate moves into the experience-rated band of 0.04 percent to 8.10 percent, capped between $3.80 and $769.50 per employee per year, per Georgia Department of Labor. The $9,500 wage base has been unchanged since 2024 under OCGA 34-8-49.

Georgia DOL calculates each employer’s experience rate annually from the reserve-ratio formula in OCGA 34-8-155 and 34-8-156: cumulative contributions paid in, minus benefits paid out to former employees, divided by average taxable payroll. A high-turnover Georgia employer trends toward the 8.10 percent ceiling; a stable employer with few separations settles near the 0.04 percent floor. The annual rate notice (DOL-626) is published via the Employer Portal at gdol.ga.gov on or around 23 December for the following calendar year. Authority: Georgia Code Title 34, Chapter 8, Article 7 (Tax Contributions).

The early-year cash-flow point

Because the $9,500 wage base is comparatively low (less than half of California’s $7,000 federal FUTA floor mirror is the closest US analogue, and most northern states sit at $20,000-$50,000), a full-time Georgia employee earning $55,000 hits the SUTA cap in early March. After that, SUTA is zero on every paycheque for the remainder of the year. The annual UI bill is concentrated in Q1, not spread evenly. FUTA credit interaction

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) is 6 percent on the first $7,000 of each employee’s wages. A Georgia employer that pays state SUTA in full and on time receives a FUTA credit of 5.4 percent, leaving an effective FUTA of 0.6 percent ($42 per employee per year). Late or partial Georgia SUTA payment erodes the federal credit and pushes the effective FUTA rate up. Teamed’s payroll books both lines together so the credit never gets lost. Source: IRS Topic 759, accessed 26 May 2026.

Registration threshold

You become a liable Georgia employer once you (a) pay $1,500 or more in wages in any calendar quarter, OR (b) employ at least one worker for any portion of a day in 20 different weeks within a calendar year. The Employer Status Report (Form DOL-1A) registers a new account on the Employer Portal. Most modern hires trip the $1,500 quarterly threshold in the first pay period. Source: OCGA 34-8-33, accessed 26 May 2026.

What is Form G-4 and how does Georgia withholding work?

Form G-4 is the Employee’s Withholding Allowance Certificate, the Georgia equivalent of the federal W-4 but built on the older allowance mechanics: filing status, total allowances, dependents, and any additional flat-dollar withholding. Every Georgia new hire completes one. If the employee does not file a G-4, the employer must withhold at zero allowances, single status until the form arrives, which is Georgia’s highest withholding combination.

The mechanics differ from the post-2020 federal W-4. Georgia did not adopt the federal redesign that removed allowances. G-4 still asks for:

  • Marital and filing status — single, married filing jointly, married filing separately, or head of household. The employer applies a different withholding table to each.
  • Total allowances — the employee plus spouse (if claimed) plus dependents plus additional allowances for age 65+ or blind status. Each allowance reduces the wage base that the table is applied to.
  • Additional dollar amount per pay period — a flat-dollar over-withholding line for employees who want a bigger refund or who run multiple jobs.
  • Exempt status — the employee certifies under penalty of perjury that they had no Georgia liability last year and expect none this year. Exempt status expires every 15 February and the employee must refile.

The employer looks up the gross taxable wage on the 2026 Georgia Employer’s Withholding Tax Guide (published by DOR in December 2025) using the marital status and allowance count, applies the 4.99 percent flat rate to the reduced base, and withholds the table figure plus any G-4 additional dollar amount.

The "more than 14 allowances or exempt" review trigger

One Georgia mechanic catches out-of-state employers: any G-4 claiming more than 14 allowances or claiming exempt status must be mailed to the Georgia DOR Withholding Tax Unit within 10 days of receipt, and the employer continues to withhold at the prior rate (or at zero allowances if there is no prior G-4) until DOR confirms or rejects the claim. The review trigger is a anti-abuse mechanism for the allowance system; getting it wrong means under-withholding on a high-allowance employee, with the employer left holding the penalty. Teamed’s onboarding flow flags the 14-allowance and exempt elections at the point of digital signing, so the review filing is automatic. Source: Georgia DOR G-4 instructions, dor.georgia.gov/g-4-employee-withholding, accessed 26 May 2026.

When G-4 needs refreshing

G-4 does not require an annual refresh in the way some states demand. The form stays in force until the employee’s circumstances change (marriage, divorce, new dependent, dependent ageing out, additional job, second-earner status, retirement). The one annual exception is the exempt election, which automatically expires every 15 February; an employee claiming exempt must file a fresh G-4 by that date or the employer reverts to zero allowances, single status. Best practice is to prompt all Georgia employees to review G-4 at year-end alongside the federal W-4 review most payroll providers run in November or December. Source: Georgia Form G-4 (Rev. 08/15/24).

How does a Georgia employer file withholding (G-7, G-7M, G-1003)?

Georgia uses a three-cadence system set by the employer’s prior-year withholding total. Employers with more than $50,000 annual Georgia withholding file semi-weekly via the G-7 Schedule. Mid-size employers ($200 to $50,000) file Form G-7M monthly. Small employers (under $200) file Form G-7Q quarterly. Everyone reconciles annually on Form G-1003 by 31 January with W-2 and 1099-NEC copies attached. Filing runs through the Georgia Tax Center at gtc.dor.ga.gov.

01 Georgia Withholding Cadence

Four state forms run the lifecycle of Georgia withholding. The cadence is set by your prior-year (lookback) withholding total, not by your current-year payroll size, so a fast-growing employer can stay on a quarterly cadence for a full calendar year before stepping up to monthly or semi-weekly the following January. The annual G-1003 reconciliation is due 31 January alongside the federal W-2 deadline — one less date to track.

The lookback threshold mechanic

Georgia sets your filing cadence each January based on your total Georgia withholding during a lookback period (the 12 months ending 30 June of the prior year). The cadence stays fixed for the full calendar year regardless of intra-year payroll changes. Employers running multi-state payroll often cross the $50,000 annual threshold once they have 12 to 15 full-time Georgia employees in the high-wage tech or finance sectors. Teamed’s payroll engine flags the lookback-period crossover at the December close so the new cadence is live by the first January payroll, not a missed-monthly penalty on the Q1 close.

Electronic filing through Georgia Tax Center

The Georgia Tax Center (GTC) at gtc.dor.ga.gov is the canonical e-file portal for G-7, G-7M, G-7Q, and G-1003. Any employer paying via electronic funds transfer must also file the matching return electronically. Practically that captures every employer running modern payroll software. GTC handles bulk W-2 and 1099 submissions and is the registration entry point for new employers. The matching UI portal at gdol.ga.gov runs the DOL-4N quarterly UI tax and wage report. Source: Georgia DOR Withholding Tax page and 2026 Employer’s Withholding Tax Guide, accessed 26 May 2026.

Late penalty

A late G-7, G-7M, or G-7Q triggers a penalty of $25 plus 5 percent of the total withholding tax per month late, capped at $25 plus 25 percent. A zero-withholding period still requires a zero return on cadence; missing one starts the delinquency clock even though no tax is due. Source: OCGA 48-7-126, accessed 26 May 2026.

Do Georgia cities or counties charge a local payroll tax?

No. Georgia has no city, county, or municipal tax on payroll, anywhere in the state. There is no Georgia equivalent of Birmingham’s occupational tax, Philadelphia’s wage tax, or the dozens of Pennsylvania local Earned Income Tax jurisdictions. Withholding registration is state-only via Georgia Tax Center; UI registration is state-only via the Georgia Department of Labor Employer Portal.

Three things to keep straight on Georgia local tax:

  1. No municipal payroll tax exists. Georgia does not authorise cities or counties to levy a payroll-wage tax. The legislative position is stable and has not been litigated in recent sessions.
  2. Local minimum wages do not exist either. Georgia state law preempts city and county minimum-wage ordinances under OCGA 34-4-3.1. The federal FLSA $7.25 per hour floor applies everywhere, no exceptions for Atlanta, Savannah, or any other municipality.
  3. Local sales tax is the only sub-state layer. Counties run a local-option sales tax (LOST and SPLOST) on top of the 4 percent state rate, but that is a consumer-side levy and does not touch payroll. No multi-state payroll provider needs to register for it.

For Teamed clients the absence of local payroll tax is a genuine operational simplification. Multi-state employers running payroll in Pennsylvania (dozens of municipalities run local EIT), Ohio (RITA and CCA jurisdictions), Alabama (Birmingham, Bessemer, Gadsden city occupational tax), or New York (city income tax for NYC residents) feel the difference. Georgia payroll is one state return, one state UI filing, one federal stack. Source: Georgia DOR and OCGA 34-4-3.1, accessed 26 May 2026.

What is the Georgia minimum wage and overtime rule in 2026?

Georgia’s state minimum wage in 2026 is $5.15 per hour under OCGA 34-4-3, but the federal Fair Labor Standards Act $7.25 per hour floor preempts the state rate for any employer covered by FLSA. In practice every modern employer pays at least the federal $7.25 per hour. Overtime follows FLSA: 1.5 times regular rate for non-exempt hours over 40 in a workweek. Georgia adds no state daily-overtime premium and no state-specific exemption test.

The Georgia $5.15 per hour figure is a statutory anomaly with essentially no real-world application. Source: US Department of Labor and OCGA 34-4-3, accessed 26 May 2026.

Overtime defers entirely to federal FLSA

Georgia has no separate state overtime statute. Non-exempt employees earn the FLSA 1.5x premium for hours over 40 in a workweek; exempt employees are exempt under the federal test (executive, administrative, professional, computer, outside-sales). No daily-overtime rule, no double-time rule, no California-style alternative workweek schedules. Georgia employers calculate overtime exactly as the federal rule requires. Source: 29 USC § 207 (FLSA), accessed 26 May 2026.
