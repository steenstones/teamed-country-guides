## Answer-first thesis
Delaware is statutorily at-will but the Delaware Supreme Court is unusual for recognising **all three** common-law exceptions to varying degrees. The lead angle, mirroring the Delaware state-tax sibling page, is the **corporate-incorporation vs employment distinction**: more than 1.8 million entities are incorporated in Delaware under Title 8, but Delaware termination law applies only to actual Delaware-working employees under Title 19. The doctrinal triangle is *DuPont v. Pressman* (1996) — narrow public-policy exception plus a four-category implied-covenant-of-good-faith claim; *Lord v. Souder* (Del. Super. 2000) — public-policy refinement and four-category wrongful-discharge catalogue; *Schuster v. Derocili* (2001) — covenant extends to refusal-of-sexual-advances facts; *Mann v. Cargill Poultry* (1990) — handbook implied-contract exception. The cash mechanic under **19 Del. C. § 1103** is moderate: next regular payday or 3 business days after last day worked, whichever is later, for BOTH voluntary and involuntary terminations (uniform rule, no next-business-day acceleration like Connecticut or same-day rule like California). Late pay triggers liquidated damages at **10% of unpaid wages per day** (Sundays/holidays excluded) up to the unpaid amount. **No state mini-WARN** — federal WARN floor only. DDEA (Delaware Discrimination in Employment Act, **19 Del. C. § 711**) covers employers with **4+ employees** (broader than Title VII's 15) with a **300-day filing window** dual-filed with EEOC.

## PROMPT FACT CORRECTIONS (verify-prompt-facts compliance)
The orchestrator prompt contained four factual issues. All have been verified against primary sources. Flagged `factual_uncertainty:prompt_correction` in HIGH_CONCERN.md.

1. **§ 1109 "wages due and payable within 7 days of payday → criminal misdemeanour for late pay"** — **INCORRECT**. § 1109 is the "Benefits and wage supplements" provision (30-day rule for employer benefits payments). It is not a 7-day rule and not the criminal-wage-theft hook. The criminal wage-theft statute is **19 Del. C. § 1102A (substantive wage-theft definition, 2024 SB 35 amendments)** cross-referenced into **11 Del. C. § 841D (Class E felony for wage theft >$1,500, Class B misdemeanour below)**. The page treats § 1102A / § 841D as the criminal hook and frames § 1109 correctly as a benefits-supplement statute.
   - Source: https://law.justia.com/codes/delaware/title-19/chapter-11/section-1109/ (§ 1109 text)
   - Source: https://law.justia.com/codes/delaware/title-11/chapter-5/subchapter-iii/section-841d/ (§ 841D wage theft criminal classification)

2. **§ 1103 "next regular payday for both voluntary and involuntary"** — VERIFIED CORRECT. Delaware's § 1103 is uniform across termination types: "next date the wages would be paid through the last day worked under the employer's regular pay cycle as if the employment had not stopped" OR **3 business days after the last day worked, whichever is later**. The "3 business days" element is sometimes missed in summary treatments.
   - Source: https://law.justia.com/codes/delaware/title-19/chapter-11/section-1103/

3. **Schuster v. Derocili "2002"** — **INCORRECT**. Decided in **2001** (775 A.2d 1029). Page uses the correct year throughout.
   - Source: https://caselaw.findlaw.com/de-supreme-court/1121045.html

4. **Implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing — "narrow circumstances"** — VERIFIED CORRECT. The Delaware Supreme Court in *DuPont v. Pressman* recognised **four narrow categories** of implied-covenant breach in employment contracts: (i) where the termination violated public policy; (ii) where the employer misrepresented an important fact and the employee detrimentally relied; (iii) where the employer used superior bargaining power to deprive an employee of clearly identifiable compensation related to past service; (iv) where the employer falsified or manipulated employment records to create fictitious grounds for termination. Delaware is genuinely unusual among US states for recognising the covenant in employment at all, but the practical scope is narrow.
   - Source: https://law.justia.com/cases/delaware/supreme-court/1996/679-a-2d-436-1.html (*DuPont v. Pressman*, 679 A.2d 436)
