Delaware turned on paid family and medical leave benefits on 1 January 2026 after a year of payroll contributions that began 1 January 2025. The Healthy Delaware Families Act (HDFA) under 19 Del. C. Chapter 37 gives eligible employees up to 12 weeks of paid parental leave per application year, plus up to 6 weeks every 24 months each for own medical leave, family caregiver leave, and military qualifying exigency, capped at 12 weeks combined in any application year. Wage replacement is 80 percent of the employee's average weekly wage, with a $900 maximum weekly benefit and a $100 minimum, fixed in statute for 2026 and 2027 and CPI-W indexed thereafter. The programme is funded by a 0.8 percent payroll contribution on wages up to the federal Social Security wage base ($184,500 for 2026), with the employer prohibited from deducting more than 0.4 percent (half the rate) from the employee. Eligibility is 60 percent of working hours physically in Delaware, 12 months of employment, and 1,250 hours in the prior 12 months with the same employer. The programme uses an unusual employer-size tier: employers with fewer than 10 Delaware employees are exempt; 10 to 24 Delaware employees owe parental leave only at 0.32 percent; 25 or more Delaware employees owe the full 0.8 percent programme. Delaware does not have a state-mandated paid sick leave statute; routine short illness remains employer-discretionary, with federal FMLA as the unpaid floor at 50-plus employees.

Which paid-leave laws apply to Delaware employees in 2026?

Two: Delaware Paid Leave under the Healthy Delaware Families Act, and federal FMLA at 50-plus employees. There is no Delaware state paid sick leave statute. HDFA is the new layer; federal FMLA is the unpaid floor. A multi-state employer who arrives in Delaware expecting a California-style sick-leave bank on top of HDFA will find a gap that has to be filled by voluntary employer policy.

Delaware became the eleventh US state to enact paid family and medical leave when Governor Carney signed Senate Bill 1 of the 152nd General Assembly on 10 May 2022, codified as 19 Del. C. Chapter 37 and branded the Healthy Delaware Families Act. The Act was designed with a deliberately slow ramp: payroll contributions began on 1 January 2025 so the state insurance fund could build a year of reserves, and benefit claims became available on 1 January 2026. The 2026 calendar year is the first year an eligible Delaware employee can actually file an HDFA claim, which is why most pre-2026 employer guidance still treats the programme as a payroll-cost-only event rather than a live benefit. That changes this year.

What Delaware did not do is bolt a separate state paid sick leave statute on top. Unlike California, Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Washington, and a growing list of other states, Delaware has no statewide mandatory paid sick leave law for private employers. There are also no Delaware municipal paid sick leave ordinances. Routine short illness, the half-day for a child's flu, the doctor's appointment that does not rise to a serious health condition, is not covered by HDFA (HDFA covers serious health conditions and bonding) and is not covered by any other state mandate. Employers fill that gap voluntarily with PTO, a sick-leave bank, or unlimited-time-off policies. The federal FMLA floor of 12 weeks unpaid, job-protected leave at 50-plus-employee employers is unchanged and continues to run alongside HDFA where both apply.

How much is the Delaware Paid Leave contribution in 2026 and who pays it?

0.8 percent of taxable wages for the full programme, with the employer prohibited from deducting more than 0.4 percent (half) from the employee. The default published split is 50/50 (0.4 percent each), but the employer can elect to absorb more, up to the full 0.8 percent. Wages are capped at the federal Social Security wage base, so the maximum annual contribution per employee for 2026 is roughly $1,476 ($184,500 × 0.8 percent), of which the employer cannot push more than $738 onto the employee. The contribution rate is fixed in statute for 2025 and 2026; the Department of Labor sets future-year rates by regulation.

Delaware Paid Leave is funded through a single combined 0.8 percent payroll contribution that is internally split across three benefit components. Employers report and remit one quarterly contribution to the DE DOL Paid Leave portal; the Department allocates the receipts across the parental, medical, and family-caregiver insurance pools.

The internal allocation of the 0.8 percent (for size-tier purposes)

  • Parental leave component: 0.32 percent — the portion owed by every covered Delaware employer with 10 or more employees
  • Medical leave component: 0.40 percent — owed only by employers with 25 or more Delaware employees
  • Family caregiver leave component: 0.08 percent — owed only by employers with 25 or more Delaware employees
  • Total: 0.80 percent for full-programme employers (25+); 0.32 percent for parental-only employers (10-24); 0 percent for sub-10 employers

The 50 percent employee-deduction ceiling

19 Del. C. § 3705 prohibits an employer from deducting more than half of the contribution from the employee. For the full 0.8 percent rate, the employee can pay at most 0.4 percent and the employer must pay at least 0.4 percent. The default published split is 50/50, but two patterns appear in practice. Larger employers in competitive talent markets often absorb the full 0.8 percent as an employer-paid contribution to keep the payslip line clean and signal a benefits posture; smaller employers and those with thin margins typically run the default 50/50. There is no compliance benefit either way; the employer choice is a compensation-design decision, not a regulatory one.

What does Delaware Paid Leave actually cover and for how many weeks?

Up to 12 weeks of parental leave per application year and up to 6 weeks every 24 months each for own medical leave, family caregiver leave, and military qualifying exigency, capped at 12 weeks combined in any single application year. Wage replacement is 80 percent of the employee's average weekly wage, with a $900 weekly maximum and a $100 weekly minimum, fixed in statute for 2026 and 2027 and CPI-W indexed each January thereafter. The benefit is paid by the state insurance fund directly to the employee; the employer's payroll obligation during the leave is benefit continuation only.

The four HDFA benefit components are designed to be drawn from separate clocks, with a hard ceiling that no employee can claim more than 12 weeks of HDFA benefits in any single application year regardless of how many qualifying reasons apply. Parental leave runs on an annual reset; the medical, family-caregiver, and military exigency components each run on a rolling 24-month look-back.

The four qualifying reasons

  • Parental leave: bonding with a new child by birth, adoption, or placement for fostering, claimable within 12 months of the qualifying event. Up to 12 weeks per application year. This is the longest individual entitlement and the most commonly used.
  • Own serious health condition (medical leave): for the employee's own serious health condition as defined by the regulations (broadly mirrors FMLA). Up to 6 weeks every 24 months.
  • Family caregiver leave: to care for a family member (spouse, parent, child, or domestic partner) with a serious health condition. Up to 6 weeks every 24 months.
  • Military qualifying exigency: arising from a family member's call to active military duty or active-duty status. Up to 6 weeks every 24 months.

Wage replacement at 80 percent, capped at $900

The HDFA wage-replacement formula is flat-rate rather than progressive: every covered employee receives 80 percent of their average weekly wage, computed from the highest of the four most recent completed calendar quarters before the application date. The maximum weekly benefit is $900 for 2026 and 2027, fixed in statute, then indexed to the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-W) and rounded to the nearest even $5 starting 1 January 2028. The minimum weekly benefit is $100 (or the employee's full wage if it is below $100). For a Delaware employee earning $1,500 a week ($78,000 annualised), the benefit is 80 percent of $1,500 = $1,200 a week, which is then trimmed to the $900 cap. For an employee earning $1,100 a week ($57,200 annualised), the benefit is $880 a week, just under the cap. The cap binds at an annualised salary of roughly $58,500; every Delaware employee earning above that hits the cap.

Eligibility: 60 percent in Delaware, 12 months, 1,250 hours

To be a covered individual under HDFA, an employee must satisfy three tests against the same employer in the 12 months immediately preceding the benefit application:

  • Primary worksite test: at least 60 percent of working hours physically in Delaware. A remote worker who occasionally crosses state lines for client meetings still qualifies as long as the 60 percent floor is met. A worker who lives in Delaware but commutes to Philadelphia or Maryland for the majority of their hours does not qualify.
  • Tenure test: at least 12 months of employment with the same employer (consecutive or not, but with the same employer).
  • Hours test: at least 1,250 hours worked for that employer in the 12 months immediately preceding the application. Mirrors the federal FMLA hours test.

The 1,250-hour floor means a typical 24-hour-a-week part-time employee qualifies after roughly 12 months on payroll; a part-timer below 24 hours a week may not. Seasonal workers and short-tenure hires do not qualify for HDFA benefits in their first year.

Job protection and benefit continuation during HDFA leave

19 Del. C. § 3708 embeds job-protection within HDFA itself: the employee's job, or an equivalent position, must be available at the end of the leave, with the same pay, benefits, and other terms of employment. This is distinct from federal FMLA's job-protection right and applies to every HDFA-covered employer (10 or more Delaware employees), not just the 50-plus federal-FMLA threshold. Employer-provided health insurance continues during the leave on the same terms as if the employee were actively working; the employee remains responsible for their share of the premium, and the employer for the employer share. Where federal FMLA also applies (50-plus employees), the two job-protection clocks run concurrently.

The state insurance fund pays the wage-replacement benefit directly to the employee, typically by electronic transfer, within a target window after each weekly certification. The employer's payroll obligation during the leave is limited to benefit continuation; the wage-replacement cash flow runs through the Department of Labor, not the employer's payroll bank account.

Does Delaware require employer paid sick leave?

No. Delaware has no state-mandated paid sick leave statute and no municipal sick leave ordinance. Routine short illness, a child's ear infection, a non-serious doctor's appointment, a flu day, sits outside HDFA (which covers serious health conditions and bonding) and outside federal FMLA (which is unpaid and applies only at 50-plus employees). Employers fill the gap voluntarily with PTO, a sick-leave bank, or unlimited time off. For multi-state employers arriving from California or Connecticut, this is the most commonly missed structural difference.

Eighteen US jurisdictions had statewide mandatory paid sick leave laws on the books as of mid-2026 (California, Colorado, Connecticut, the District of Columbia, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Washington, plus a handful with imminent effective dates). Delaware is not one of them. The Delaware General Assembly considered paid sick leave bills in successive sessions during the 2010s and 2020s; none were enacted into law. Senate Bill 1 of the 152nd General Assembly, the Healthy Delaware Families Act, was the legislature's chosen vehicle, and it deliberately addressed serious-health-condition and bonding absences rather than routine short illness.

What HDFA covers (and does not cover)

  • Covered by HDFA: serious health condition (employee's own), serious health condition of a family member, parental bonding, military qualifying exigency. All require medical certification appropriate to the qualifying reason and meet a "serious" threshold drawn from FMLA precedent.
  • Not covered by HDFA: a single doctor's appointment that does not relate to a serious health condition; one or two days of common illness (flu, cold, gastrointestinal upset); a child's routine paediatric visit that is not a chronic-condition follow-up; a mental health day; bereavement (HDFA does not include bereavement as a qualifying reason). For these absences there is no state-mandated paid benefit.

What Delaware employers typically do instead

The market norm for Delaware employers above 25 employees is a separate PTO bank of 10 to 15 days a year, used for a combination of vacation, personal time, and routine illness, on top of HDFA. Smaller Delaware employers tend to run a leaner PTO bank or, for highly skilled professional roles, an "unlimited" PTO policy. Public-sector Delaware employers and a growing minority of private-sector employers maintain a dedicated sick-leave bank separate from vacation PTO, on the model of pre-PA-24-8 Connecticut. Sector matters: a Delaware bank or insurance employer competing for talent against Philadelphia and New York City employers, both of which have municipal paid sick leave ordinances, often offers a dedicated sick bank as a recruitment-parity measure even though Delaware itself does not require one.

Federal FMLA as the unpaid backdrop

At Delaware employers with 50 or more employees within 75 miles, federal FMLA applies and provides 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period for the employee's own serious health condition, a family member's serious health condition, parental bonding, or military qualifying exigency. FMLA is the floor underneath HDFA at the 50-plus threshold; below 50 employees there is no federal sick-leave entitlement at all and HDFA is the entire compliance picture for serious-condition absences (plus the 10-employee HDFA threshold itself).

How do Delaware Paid Leave employer-size tiers work in 2026?

Three tiers by Delaware-employee headcount: fewer than 10 employees exempt; 10 to 24 employees owe parental leave only at 0.32 percent; 25 or more employees owe the full programme at 0.8 percent. Seasonal and part-time employees working under 1,250 hours a year do not count toward the headcount threshold. The tiering is a Delaware-specific design choice; no other US state paid-leave programme uses a graduated employer-size carve-out on this scale.

Most peer state paid family and medical leave programmes use universal employer coverage: every employer with at least one covered employee participates and contributes, regardless of size. California SDI / PFL, Connecticut PFMLA, Massachusetts PFML, New Jersey TDB / FLI, New York PFL, Oregon PLO, Rhode Island TDI / TCI, Washington PFML, and DC PFL all use universal coverage. Delaware broke from that pattern when SB 1 was drafted, deliberately tiering coverage to reduce the start-up burden on the state's substantial population of small employers.

How to count Delaware employees for the tier test

The headcount test runs on the average number of Delaware-primary-worksite employees over the prior 12 months, where Delaware-primary means at least 60 percent of working hours physically in Delaware. Seasonal employees and part-time employees working fewer than 1,250 hours in the 12-month measurement period are excluded from the count. A 30-employee Delaware company with 8 full-time permanent staff and 22 short-season hires might end up below the 10-employee threshold and exempt; a 12-employee Delaware company with all full-time permanent staff sits squarely in the 10-24 parental-only tier. The headcount is measured by the employer, not by an industry classification or a state-issued certificate.

Multi-state employer tier counting

For a multi-state employer, only the Delaware-primary-worksite employees count toward the tier. A 5,000-person multinational with 6 Delaware-primary employees is exempt from HDFA contributions and from the parental-only tier; a 200-person company with 27 Delaware-primary employees sits in the full-programme tier and owes 0.8 percent on all 27. This is materially different from the California SDI / PFL and Connecticut PFMLA universal-coverage pattern, where the first Delaware-primary employee at a multi-state employer would automatically trigger participation.

Tier transitions

An employer that crosses a tier threshold mid-year (a 9-employee Delaware operation that hires a 10th, or a 24-employee operation that hires a 25th) becomes covered at the new tier on the first day of the next calendar quarter, not retroactively. Once an employer has been covered under a tier, the Department of Labor uses the prior 12-month average to determine continuing coverage; an employer that drops back below a threshold for a sustained period (typically four consecutive quarters) can apply for de-coverage at the lower tier.

Can a Delaware employer opt out of state Paid Leave with a private or grandfathered plan?

Yes, but the initial opt-out window ran 1 September to 1 December 2024 and the grandfathered-plan window runs only through 31 December 2029. Approximately 120 Delaware employer plans were approved for grandfathering during the initial window; new entrants now go onto the state plan by default. Private plans that meet or exceed every state-plan benefit can still be approved on a continuing basis, but employee contributions under any approved private plan cannot exceed what the state plan would charge.

Three pathways exist under 19 Del. C. Ch. 37 for an employer to avoid participating in the state insurance fund: (a) a fully insured private plan approved by the DE DOL; (b) a self-insured private plan approved by the DE DOL; or (c) a grandfathered plan, which is an existing employer paid-leave benefit that pre-dated the HDFA effective date and was approved during the initial grandfathering window.

The grandfathering window (closed)

Employers had from 1 September 2024 to 1 December 2024 to file a grandfathering application for an existing paid-leave benefit (private insurance contract, self-insured plan, or employee-handbook policy) that pre-dated HDFA. Approximately 120 Delaware employer plans were approved during the window. A grandfathered plan can be operated in lieu of the state plan for a five-year window ending 31 December 2029, after which the employer must either migrate to the state plan, file a new private-plan application, or apply for an extension under any future rules promulgated by the DE DOL. Grandfathered plans cannot be amended to add new eligibility conditions or reduce benefits without losing the grandfathered status.

Continuing private-plan approval

Outside the initial grandfathering window, an employer may still apply for private-plan approval on a continuing basis. The DE DOL reviews each application against three statutory requirements:

  • Benefit parity: the private plan must meet or exceed every state-plan benefit on every dimension: wage-replacement percentage, weekly benefit cap, qualifying reasons, leave duration, eligibility tests, and job protection.
  • Employee contribution ceiling: the employee's contribution under the private plan cannot exceed what the employee would pay under the state plan (0.4 percent of wages up to the federal SS wage base, max $738 a year for 2026).
  • No additional eligibility conditions: the private plan cannot add eligibility hurdles beyond the state plan's 60 percent in Delaware / 12 months / 1,250 hours test. This is a hard fail in many existing employer plans designed before HDFA, which often layer on a probationary period or a benefit-tier-by-job-level rule.

The opt-out economics in 2026

For most Delaware employers the state plan wins on total cost of administration, particularly in the programme's early years when claims experience is being established. The state plan is a fixed-rate 0.8 percent contribution with no underwriting volatility, the contribution remittance flows through one DE DOL portal, and the state insurance fund handles claim intake and adjudication. A private plan requires an insurance carrier relationship (Aflac, MetLife, The Hartford, Mutual of Omaha, Principal, Sun Life, and Unum all wrote Delaware PFML-compliant products as of mid-2026), additional claim-management workflow with the carrier, and the annual DE DOL filing demonstrating continuing parity. For a Delaware employer with a young workforce and low expected claims, the underwritten rate can come in below 0.8 percent; for an older or higher-risk workforce, the state plan's fixed rate is the better economics.

The most common pattern for a multi-state employer arriving in Delaware in 2026 is to participate in the state plan from day one, observe a year of claims experience, and revisit the private-plan question in 2027 once there is real Delaware-specific data to underwrite against.
