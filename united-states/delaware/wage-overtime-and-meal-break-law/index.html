How does Delaware wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

Delaware runs a $15.00 state minimum wage from 1 January 2025 (unchanged in 2026) under 19 Del. C. § 902, follows federal-FLSA overtime only with no daily trigger, and mandates a 30-minute meal break for any shift of 7.5 consecutive hours or more under 19 Del. C. § 707. Pay frequency under § 1102 is at least once a month, with bi-weekly the operational default. Final pay on separation is owed on the next regular payday or three business days after the last day worked, whichever is later, with a 10 percent-per-business-day liquidated-damages penalty under § 1103 for late payment without reasonable grounds. The $15 floor and the 7.5-hour meal-break trigger are the two rules a federally-configured payroll engine will miss on the first Delaware payroll.

Delaware wage and hour law in 2026 sits as a high-minimum, light-touch jurisdiction with two distinctive rules. The state minimum wage is $15.00 per hour, in effect since 1 January 2025 under Senate Bill 15 (2021) and unchanged in 2026, set by 19 Del. C. § 902. Tipped employees sit at $2.23 per hour cash plus a tip credit of up to $12.77 per hour to reach the $15.00 floor, an unusually wide tip credit by US-state standards (the cash floor has been frozen since 2007). Overtime is federal FLSA only: 1.5x the regular rate over 40 hours in a workweek, with no Delaware state overtime statute and no daily trigger. The distinctive rule is the meal break: 19 Del. C. § 707 requires 30 consecutive minutes for any shift of 7.5 consecutive hours or more, taken after the first 2 hours and before the last 2 hours of the shift. Delaware is one of only about eight US states that mandate adult meal breaks at all. Pay frequency under 19 Del. C. § 1102 sits at the lighter end: at least once a month, with bi-weekly the operational default for most employers. Final pay on separation under 19 Del. C. § 1103 is owed on the next regular payday or three business days after the last day worked, whichever is later, with 10 percent of the unpaid wages per business day as liquidated damages if the employer fails without reasonable grounds.

What is Delaware’s minimum wage in 2026?

Delaware’s state minimum wage in 2026 is $15.00 per hour, in effect since 1 January 2025 and unchanged for 2026, set by 19 Del. C. § 902(a) under Senate Bill 15 (2021). Tipped employees sit at $2.23 per hour cash with a tip credit of up to $12.77 per hour bringing the effective minimum to $15.00 (tip credit set by reference to the federal tip credit percentage as of 15 June 2006 under § 902(b)). The $15.00 floor is the result of a four-year phase-in from $9.25 (2021) through $10.50, $11.75, $13.25 to $15.00 on 1 January 2025. Delaware sits in the top tier of US state minimum wages alongside Washington, California, Connecticut and Massachusetts; no local city ordinance overrides it.

Three rules that follow from the wage stack:

  • No annual indexation. Delaware’s minimum wage does not adjust automatically with inflation or the federal Employment Cost Index. The 2021 SB 15 schedule terminated at $15.00 on 1 January 2025 and no further increases are scheduled. A future rise would require a new bill through the General Assembly. Payroll engines configured to expect an annual January bump (the pattern in Connecticut, New York, Washington, and California) won’t see one in Delaware until the legislature acts.
  • The tip credit is wide and frozen. $12.77 of tip credit per hour is one of the largest in the country in absolute dollars, because the $2.23 cash floor has been frozen since 2007 while the state minimum climbed from $7.15 to $15.00. Tipped employees who don’t reach $15.00 effective hourly when tips are added must be topped up by the employer to the $15.00 floor for that period. Payroll engines need a per-pay-period tip-credit reconciliation, not a pay-the-cash-rate-and-trust-the-tips default.
  • Youth and training-wage carve-out. Workers under 18 and new hires in their first 90 days may be paid at $8.75 per hour under § 902(c). The exception is narrow (it expires after 90 days for new hires, and at age 18 for youth workers) and is rarely used by professional-services or corporate employers, but it sits in the statute and shows up in food-service, retail, and hospitality payrolls.

The SB 15 phase-in is finished

Delaware was a $9.25 minimum wage state until Senate Bill 15 passed in July 2021, putting the floor on a four-step climb: $10.50 on 1 January 2022, $11.75 on 1 January 2023, $13.25 on 1 January 2024, and $15.00 on 1 January 2025. The 1 January 2025 step took Delaware from a middle-of-the-pack state minimum to the top tier. The 2026 rate is the same as the 2025 rate because the schedule terminated and no indexation kicked in afterwards. The CPI between 1 January 2025 and 1 January 2026 ran 2.4 percent; the real value of the $15.00 floor has eroded by that amount over the year. Source: 19 Del. Does Delaware have a state overtime law?

No. Delaware does not have a state overtime statute. Overtime in Delaware is governed exclusively by the federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA): 1.5x the regular rate for any hours worked over 40 in a workweek under 29 U.S.C. § 207(a)(1). There is no daily overtime trigger, no consecutive-shift trigger, and no 7th-day premium. Delaware sits in the operationally lightest tier of US states on overtime, similar to Alabama and Florida and lighter than Colorado (daily-12) or California (daily-8). Overtime applies to all non-exempt employees regardless of pay basis (hourly, salary, piece-rate, commission), with the regular rate calculated from all remuneration in the workweek divided by hours worked.

The simplicity is the upside and the trap. Delaware overtime tracking federal FLSA exactly means a multi-state payroll engine running FLSA-only configuration won’t under-pay Delaware overtime on the calculation side. The trap is the meal-break rule (7.5-hour trigger under § 707, stricter than the federal default), the pay-statement requirement under § 1108, the tip-credit reconciliation under § 902(b), and the 10-percent-per-day liquidated-damages exposure under § 1103 if final pay slips past the next regular payday. A US-wide payroll team that assumes Delaware is just “FLSA with a $15 minimum” gets the overtime right and the scheduling, pay-statement, and final-pay mechanics wrong.

Exempt salary threshold

Delaware applies the federal FLSA exempt-salary threshold without a state-level uplift. The current floor sits at $684 per week ($35,568 per year) for executive, administrative, and professional exemptions under 29 CFR Part 541. The US Department of Labor’s 2024 proposed increases were partially struck down by federal court in late 2024, and the threshold reverted to the 2019 figure. Delaware has no state-specific exempt floor, so the federal number is the operative one. An employee paid $36,000 per year on a manager title is exempt in Delaware on the salary basis alone, provided the duties test under Part 541 is met. Misclassification still creates wage exposure: an “exempt” employee who fails the duties test is owed overtime on every workweek over 40 hours for the 2-year FLSA claim look-back (3 years if willful). Source: 29 CFR Part 541; Delaware Department of Labor Office of Labor Law Enforcement; 29 U.S.C. § 207. Accessed 26 May 2026.

The regular-rate calculation matters

Delaware courts follow federal FLSA case law on the “regular rate” calculation: all non-discretionary remuneration earned in the workweek (cash wages, non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, commission, on-call pay) divided by total hours worked. A non-discretionary attendance bonus that pushes a $20-per-hour employee’s effective rate to $21 in a given week means the overtime rate for that week is $31.50, not $30. Payroll engines that calculate overtime on base rate only without recapture of bonus and commission allocations under-pay by 5 to 10 percent on weeks with bonus payouts. The fix is automated regular-rate recalculation at week close, not at base-rate setup.

How do Delaware meal breaks work under 19 Del. C. § 707?

19 Del. C. § 707 requires a 30 consecutive-minute meal break for any employee who works 7.5 consecutive hours or more. The break must be given after the first 2 hours and before the last 2 hours of the shift, and the employee must be fully relieved of duty for the period to be unpaid. There is no separate rest-break requirement under Delaware law. The 7.5-hour trigger is stricter than the federal default (FLSA mandates no meal break at all), and Delaware sits in a small group of about eight US states that legislate adult meal breaks. Violations carry a civil penalty of $1,000 to $5,000 per breach, and the statute also creates an anti-retaliation cause of action for any employee discharged or disciplined for complaining.

7.5 Consecutive-hour trigger

Delaware’s meal-break trigger sits at 7.5 consecutive hours under 19 Del. A 30-minute consecutive break has to be scheduled after hour 2 and before the last 2 hours of the shift. National payroll engines configured to the federal floor don’t auto-schedule the break or auto-deduct the time; that’s a Delaware-specific configuration step.

What “fully relieved of duty” means in practice

The 30-minute meal break only stays unpaid if the employee is completely off the clock and free from all work obligations. A receptionist who covers the phone during a meal break is on duty and the time reverts to paid working hours. A retail associate asked to handle one customer transaction during the 30 minutes is on duty and the time reverts to paid. The reversion is automatic: payroll auto-deductions for the unpaid slot get reversed at pay-period close, and the time counts toward weekly hours worked for overtime purposes. A pattern of recorded meal breaks shorter than 30 consecutive minutes or with documented duty performed during the break is a Delaware DOL Office of Labor Law Enforcement flag and a wage-claim risk under 19 Del. C. § 1113.

The exemptions are narrow and specific

19 Del. C. § 707(b) names two statutory exemptions: certified school-board professionals working directly with children, and collective bargaining agreements or written employer-employee agreements that displace the rule. The administrative exemptions under 19 DE Admin Code § 1327 add three more operational pathways: nature-of-work exemptions (continuous-process operations where the worker cannot be relieved, common in healthcare and 24-hour manufacturing), small-shift exemptions (fewer than five employees on a shift at a single location), and special-circumstance exemptions granted on application to the Department of Labor. The exemption that catches employers most often is the “written employer-employee agreement” carve-out. Unlike many states, Delaware permits an individual written agreement to displace the meal-break rule, which is a meaningful operational option. The agreement must be specific, signed, dated, and on file. A general at-will employment agreement that doesn’t name the § 707 right does not qualify.

The penalty side

A § 707 violation carries a civil penalty of $1,000 to $5,000 per breach under § 707(c), assessed by the Delaware Department of Labor on a per-employee, per-incident basis. A single rolled-back meal break for one employee is a separate violation from a rolled-back break for a second employee on the same shift. The anti-retaliation provision adds a separate cause of action: any employee discharged, demoted, or disciplined for complaining about a § 707 breach has a direct statutory claim, and Delaware courts have read the protection broadly. The penalty is administrative, not criminal; there is no felony exposure on meal-break violations equivalent to Connecticut’s wage-payment criminal track under § 31-71g. The exposure is dollars per breach, multiplied across affected employees, with no statutory cap. Source: 19 Del. C. § 707; 19 DE Admin Code § 1327 (Rules Relating to Exemptions from Meal Break Requirement); Delaware Department of Labor Office of Labor Law Enforcement. Accessed 26 May 2026.

How often must Delaware employers pay wages?

Under 19 Del. C. § 1102, Delaware employers must pay wages at least once a month, on a regular pay day designated in advance and posted at the place of employment. The statute permits more frequent cadences (weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly) at the employer’s option, but never less frequent than monthly. Bi-weekly is the operational default for most Delaware employers because it aligns with federal Form 941 deposit cadence and Delaware Division of Revenue Form W-1 monthly withholding deposits. Wages may be paid by cash, by check payable on demand without deduction or fee, or by direct deposit with the employee’s written consent into a financial institution of the employee’s choosing. If an employee is not present on payday, payment is made by mail on request, by the next regular workday they are present, or by credit to the designated bank account.

Delaware sits at the lighter end of US state pay-frequency regulation. Most US states allow semi-monthly or monthly as a default; Delaware permits monthly as the statutory floor without a state-application process, which makes it easier than Connecticut (weekly-or-biweekly default with semi-monthly requiring CT DOL approval). The trade-off is that the lighter touch is matched by stricter final-pay damages under § 1103 and a pay-statement requirement under § 1108 that most US states don’t have at the statutory level.

Five practical rules for any Delaware payroll:

  • Bi-weekly is the operational default. Most Delaware employers run bi-weekly (every 14 days, 26 pay periods per year) rather than monthly, because it aligns cleanly with the federal Form 941 deposit cadence and the Delaware Division of Revenue Form W-1 monthly withholding cycle. Bi-weekly is fully compliant with § 1102 without any DOL filing or notice.
  • Semi-monthly and monthly are also fine. An employer may legitimately run a semi-monthly cycle (1-15 / 16-end-of-month) or a monthly cycle without any DOL application. The statutory minimum is monthly; anything more frequent is at the employer’s discretion. This is a meaningful operational simplification for executive payrolls, professional services partnerships, and any business where bi-weekly creates payroll-cycle complexity.
  • Direct deposit needs written employee consent. Direct deposit is permitted under § 1102 only with the employee’s written consent and only into an account at a financial institution of the employee’s choosing. An employer cannot mandate direct deposit, cannot force the employee onto a specific bank, and cannot use direct-deposit consent as a condition of hire. The default is cash or check payable on demand without deduction or fee.
  • The pay day must be posted. § 1102 requires the regular pay day to be designated in advance and posted conspicuously at the place of employment. A multi-state employer with national payroll can satisfy this through the Delaware office’s employee notice board, the Delaware section of an electronic handbook, or the Delaware-specific addendum to the offer letter, but the posting requirement is operative.
  • Final pay under § 1103 is separately regulated. The regular pay-frequency rule under § 1102 doesn’t govern final pay at separation. Final pay is on the later of the next regular payday or three business days after the last day worked, with 10-percent-per-business-day liquidated damages for late payment without reasonable grounds. See the next section.

For multi-state employers, the operational pattern that works is: run Delaware headcount on bi-weekly from day one (the cleanest fit with federal payroll cycles), post the pay day on the Delaware-office notice or in the Delaware addendum to the handbook, collect written direct-deposit consent on the day-one onboarding form, and never assume the national pay-frequency cadence transfers without the § 1102 posting check. Source: 19 Del. C. § 1102; Delaware Department of Labor Office of Labor Law Enforcement, Wage Payment guidance. Accessed 26 May 2026.

What pay statement does Delaware require under 19 Del. C. § 1108?

19 Del. C. § 1108 requires any Delaware employer with more than three employees to give each employee, at the time of payment, a written or electronic statement showing earnings, hours, deductions, and allowances for the pay period. The statement may be provided on the check, on a separate slip, or electronically in a form the employee can retain; an employee who receives an electronic statement may request a paper version on request. The same statute requires written notice at the time of hire of the rate of pay and the day, hour, and place of payment, and requires the employer to retain all wage and hour records for at least three years. Most US states don’t have a pay-statement requirement at the statutory level, so this is one of the Delaware-specific scheduling rules that catches multi-state payrolls running a federal-only configuration.

The § 1108 statement is one of the cleanest documentation requirements in Delaware payroll. The rule list is short, the format is flexible, and the threshold (more than 3 employees) excludes very small employers. The operational lift sits in three places:

  • Day-one written hire notice. Every Delaware new hire needs a written document at the time of hire that states the rate of pay, the day of payment, the hour of payment, and the place of payment. Most US offer letters cover pay rate, but the day-hour-place specification is rarer. A multi-state offer letter template that doesn’t name the Delaware payday cadence (e.g. “every other Friday by 5pm ET, by direct deposit to your designated account, or by physical paycheque at the Delaware office”) is non-compliant for Delaware hires.
  • Statement on every paycheque. The statement requirement is per-payday, not per-month or per-year. Every bi-weekly run produces a statement; every off-cycle run (bonus, commission, retro adjustment, final pay) also produces a statement. Payroll engines that consolidate statements at month-end are non-compliant for Delaware.
  • Electronic-paper opt-out. An employee who receives an electronic statement and requests a paper version must be given the paper version on request. The fix is a default of electronic delivery with a one-click paper-on-request flow in the employee self-service portal.

Penalty exposure under § 1108 is the same wage-claim track as the rest of Chapter 11: a complaint to the Office of Labor Law Enforcement, civil-penalty assessment by the DOL, and liability for the employee’s actual losses plus reasonable attorney’s fees on a successful claim. There is no per-statement statutory damages number equivalent to California Labor Code § 226(e) wage-statement penalties, which limits the upside exposure compared to California but still puts the documentation requirement at a high enough standard that automated payroll is the only realistic compliance path. Source: 19 Del. C. § 1108; Delaware Department of Labor Office of Labor Law Enforcement; 29 CFR Part 516 (federal payroll records). Accessed 26 May 2026.

When is final pay due in Delaware?

Final pay in Delaware is due on the later of the next regular payday or three business days after the last day worked, under 19 Del. C. § 1103. The rule applies to all separations: voluntary quit, involuntary discharge, layoff, or suspension. Late payment without reasonable grounds for dispute triggers liquidated damages of 10 percent of the unpaid wages for each business day (excluding Sundays and legal holidays) the failure continues. Payment is made through the usual pay channel or by mail to the address the employee provides. Accrued vacation is owed at separation only if the employer’s written policy or contract treats vacation as earned wages.

The § 1103 structure is operationally distinctive in two ways. First, the deadline is the later of two dates, not the earlier. For an employee discharged on the Wednesday of a bi-weekly cycle that pays the following Friday, the next regular payday (Friday, two days later) falls inside the three-business-day window, and final pay is due Friday. For an employee discharged on a Monday two days after the previous Friday payday, the next regular payday is 12 days out and the three-business-day rule operates instead, with final pay due Thursday. Most US states use one or the other; Delaware uses the later of both, which simplifies the operational rule to “pay by the next regular payday unless that’s more than 3 business days away, in which case pay sooner.”

Second, the 10-percent-per-business-day liquidated-damages rule is sharp. A $4,000 final-pay obligation that goes 10 business days late without reasonable grounds for dispute accrues $4,000 in liquidated damages on top of the underlying wage owed, doubling the liability. The damages clock starts the business day after the § 1103 deadline and runs continuously (excluding Sundays and legal holidays) until payment. The “reasonable grounds for dispute” safe harbour is narrow in case law: a genuine, documented disagreement over the amount owed (e.g. disputed commission or contested vacation accrual) can excuse the delay, but administrative failure or payroll-system error cannot.

Three operational rules for Delaware final pay

  • Build a 3-business-day off-cycle process. The standard “final pay on next regular payday” default works in Delaware only when the next payday is within 3 business days of the last day worked. For any longer gap, an off-cycle payroll run within 3 business days is required. A multi-state payroll engine that defaults to next-regular-payday for all states will breach Delaware’s § 1103 on roughly half of all separations (the half that fall in the first week of a bi-weekly cycle).
  • Document accrued-vacation policy explicitly. Delaware doesn’t require employers to treat vacation as earned wages, but if the employer’s handbook, offer letter, or employment contract says vacation is earned, then it is, and it’s owed at separation alongside earned wages, with the same 10-percent-per-day exposure on late payment. The fix is a Delaware-specific handbook clause that names the policy choice (vacation is a benefit not earned wages, or vacation is earned wages payable at separation) and is consistent with the practice across all Delaware separations.
  • Calendar the deadline at the offboarding meeting. The 3-business-day clock starts at the last day worked, not at the next pay-period close. An employee discharged at the end of a Friday shift is owed final pay by close of business the following Wednesday at the latest (assuming no holidays). A multi-state HR function that handles Delaware offboarding through a 14-day payroll-cycle cadence will miss the deadline unless an off-cycle trigger fires at separation.

The 10-percent-per-day rule is one of the stronger wage-payment damages provisions in any US state. Most US states use either a multi-day grace period followed by waiting-time penalties (California) or a flat civil penalty (Texas). Delaware compounds the exposure daily until cure, which creates a sharp incentive to handle Delaware separations through a documented off-cycle process rather than relying on the regular payroll cadence. Source: 19 Del. C. § 1103; 19 Del. C. § 1113 (enforcement); Delaware Department of Labor Office of Labor Law Enforcement. Accessed 26 May 2026.

How should multi-state employers handle Delaware wage-and-hour law?

Treat Delaware as a state where the wage calculation tracks federal FLSA but the scheduling, pay-statement, and final-pay mechanics don’t. Run Delaware headcount on bi-weekly pay frequency by default under 19 Del. C. § 1102, configure shift schedules to auto-trigger a 30-minute meal break for any shift of 7.5 consecutive hours under § 707, produce an itemised pay statement on every payday under § 1108, and build a 3-business-day off-cycle final-pay process for all separations under § 1103. The wage rate ($15.00/hr in 2026) and overtime calculation (federal-FLSA weekly-40) won’t catch a national payroll out; the meal-break trigger, the pay-statement requirement, the day-one written hire notice, and the 10-percent-per-day final-pay damages will.

Five operational rules for multi-state US employers with Delaware headcount:

  1. Default Delaware headcount to bi-weekly pay. Bi-weekly (every 14 days, 26 pay periods per year) is fully compliant under 19 Del. C. § 1102 and aligns with federal Form 941 deposit cadence and the Delaware Division of Revenue Form W-1 monthly withholding cycle. Monthly is permitted but rarely operationally clean for a multi-state payroll. Semi-monthly is permitted and often used for executive or partnership payrolls.
  2. Auto-schedule meal breaks for shifts of 7.5 consecutive hours or more. Configure the time-and-attendance system to flag any Delaware employee scheduled for 7.5 hours or more and auto-insert a 30-minute meal-break block in the middle of the shift (after the first 2 hours, before the last 2). If the time-clock records less than 30 consecutive minutes for the break, auto-reverse the unpaid deduction and flag the entry for audit. Document the meal-break offer in the schedule, not just the deduction in the wage record. Penalty exposure under § 707(c) is $1,000 to $5,000 per breach, per affected employee.
  3. Produce the § 1108 pay statement on every payday. Every bi-weekly run produces an itemised statement showing earnings, hours, deductions, and allowances. Every off-cycle run (bonus, commission, retro adjustment, final pay) also produces a statement. The statement may be electronic and retainable; an employee who requests a paper version must be given one. Employers with 3 or fewer Delaware employees are exempt, but most multi-state employers cross that threshold quickly.
  4. Build the 3-business-day final-pay process for all separations. 19 Del. C. § 1103 gives the employer the later of the next regular payday or 3 business days after the last day worked to deliver all earned wages. For separations where the next payday is more than 3 business days away (roughly half of separations on a bi-weekly cadence), an off-cycle payroll run is required. The standard “final pay on next regular payday” default fails on Delaware separations that fall in the first week of a bi-weekly cycle.
  5. Add a Delaware-specific addendum to the national handbook. Document the bi-weekly pay default (or semi-monthly/monthly with rationale), the 7.5-hour meal-break trigger and 30-minute break process, the federal-FLSA overtime calculation (weekly-40 only), the day-one written hire notice naming rate of pay and day, hour, and place of payment, the per-payday pay-statement requirement, the 3-business-day final-pay rule with 10-percent-per-day liquidated damages, and the vacation-as-earned-wages policy choice.

Delaware’s wage-and-hour surface area is smaller than California’s and lighter on overtime than Colorado’s, but the meal-break rule, the pay-statement requirement, and the sharp final-pay damages make it operationally distinct from a federal-only state like Alabama or Florida. Borderline classifications get flagged to your country specialist for a 15-minute decision call before the offer goes out. The day-one written hire notice required under § 1108 names pay rate, the bi-weekly pay day, hour of payment, and place of payment (electronic deposit by default, with the Delaware-office address as the fallback for paper paycheques). Direct-deposit consent is collected on the same onboarding form.
  • Time and pay: the platform records workweek, daily hours, shift start and end times, scheduled meal breaks, and actual meal-break durations. Overtime calculates federal-FLSA weekly-40 only, with regular-rate recapture for non-discretionary bonuses, commissions, and shift differentials so the overtime premium reflects all earnings in the workweek. Meal-break entries shorter than 30 consecutive minutes auto-reverse the unpaid deduction and flag for audit.
  • Meal-break automation: any Delaware shift scheduled for 7.5 consecutive hours or more auto-inserts a 30-minute meal-break block after the first 2 hours and before the last 2 hours of the shift. If the employee’s time-clock shows continuous duty across the scheduled break, the platform reverses the auto-deduction, pays the time, rolls the hours into the weekly-40 overtime calculation, and surfaces the entry on the wage statement so the audit trail is clean against the $1,000-to-$5,000 § 707(c) penalty.
  • Itemised pay statement on every payday: every bi-weekly run produces the § 1108 statement showing earnings, hours worked, deductions, and allowances. The statement is electronic by default and retainable; any employee who requests a paper version receives one through the employee self-service portal. Off-cycle runs (bonus, commission, retro adjustment, final pay) also produce statements.
  • 3-business-day final-pay process: any separation triggers a final-pay calculation against the § 1103 deadline. If the next regular payday falls within 3 business days of the last day worked, final pay rides the regular cycle; if not, an off-cycle payroll run delivers earned wages, accrued vacation (if your policy treats vacation as earned wages), and any other owed compensation within the 3-business-day window. The platform enforces the clock by separation date and surfaces the deadline at offboarding.
  • Tipped-employee compliance: for hotel, restaurant, and bartending roles paid at the $2.23 tipped cash floor, the platform reconciles tip credit on a per-pay-period basis. If tips plus cash wage do not bring the effective rate to $15.00, the platform tops up the cash wage to close the gap and surfaces the entry as a payroll exception so the audit trail is clean.
  • Vacation-policy clarity: the onboarding handbook clause names whether your policy treats accrued vacation as earned wages payable at separation or as a benefit forfeited on departure. The choice is documented per employee at hire so the § 1103 final-pay calculation has an unambiguous answer at offboarding.
