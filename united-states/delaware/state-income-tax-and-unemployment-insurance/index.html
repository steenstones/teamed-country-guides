How does Delaware state income tax and unemployment insurance work in 2026?

Delaware runs a seven-bracket progressive income tax topping out at 6.6 percent above $60,000 of taxable income, pairs it with one of the lowest new-employer UI rates in the United States at 1.0 percent on a phased-in $14,500 wage base, and uses a state-specific DE-W4 form because Delaware kept the allowance-count withholding model after the federal redesign removed it.

Delaware payroll in 2026: state income tax runs a seven-band statutory ladder from 0 percent to 6.6 percent under Del. Code Title 30 § 1102, with the top rate triggering at just $60,000 of taxable income (same threshold for every filing status). Unemployment Insurance costs 1.0 percent on the first $14,500 of wages for new employers under Schedule B in 2026, with an experience-rated band of 0.3 percent to 5.4 percent and a separate 0.2 percent Operations and Technology Assessment billed quarterly. Every new hire completes Form DE-W4, distinct from the federal W-4 because Delaware kept the allowance-count model. Withholding deposits scale on a lookback period: Form W-1Q quarterly if annual withholding stayed at or below $6,020, Form W-1 monthly through $33,460, and Form W-1A eighth-monthly above that. The critical buyer trap: incorporating in Delaware (Title 8) creates no Delaware payroll obligation by itself; the trigger is paying a Delaware-resident employee or a non-resident performing personal services inside Delaware.

Does incorporating in Delaware create a Delaware payroll obligation?

No. Incorporating in Delaware under Title 8 of the Delaware Code creates a Delaware franchise-tax liability (an annual fee on the entity) and potentially a corporate income tax filing at 8.7 percent on apportioned federal taxable income, but it creates zero Delaware withholding obligation by itself. Delaware payroll is triggered by Title 30 Chapter 11, which kicks in only when you pay a Delaware-resident employee or a non-resident performing personal services within Delaware. The two statutes are independent.

This is the single most common point of confusion for multi-state founders. Roughly two-thirds of Fortune 500 companies are incorporated in Delaware. Almost none of them run payroll there. Their employees sit in California, New York, Texas, Washington, and a hundred other states, and payroll obligations follow the employee's residency and place of personal-services performance, not the parent's state of incorporation.

The two common buyer scenarios

Scenario A: Delaware C-Corp, all employees in California. The company files Delaware franchise tax annually and a Delaware corporate income tax return reporting apportioned activity. It pays California state withholding, California UI, California SDI, California ETT on every paycheque. Delaware withholding obligation: zero. Delaware UI obligation: zero. The Delaware Division of Revenue never sees a W-1Q from this employer.

Scenario B: California LLC, one remote employee living in Wilmington, Delaware. The company registers with the Delaware Division of Revenue under Title 30 § 1151 and with the Delaware Department of Labor under Title 19 Chapter 33. It files Form W-1Q or W-1 depending on the lookback period and Form UC-8 plus UC-8A quarterly. No Delaware franchise tax (no Delaware incorporation), no Delaware corporate income tax (no Delaware corporate activity), but full Delaware payroll obligations from day one.

The Wilmington wage tax layer

If the Delaware-resident employee in Scenario B happens to work inside Wilmington city limits, a third layer attaches: a 1.25 percent City of Wilmington Earned Income Tax on every wage dollar earned within city limits, regardless of the employee's residency. Wilmington is the only Delaware municipality with a payroll-level wage tax. Employer registers separately with the City of Wilmington Department of Finance. Source: Wilmington City Code Chapter 38, accessed 26 May 2026.

What is the Delaware state income tax rate in 2026?

Delaware charges a seven-bracket progressive income tax running from 0 percent on the first $2,000 of taxable income up to a top rate of 6.6 percent on income above $60,000. The full ladder is 0, 2.2, 3.9, 4.8, 5.2, 5.55, and 6.6 percent. Same brackets apply to every filing status, set by Del. Code Title 30 § 1102. The rate ladder has been unchanged since 2014 and the thresholds are not inflation-indexed.

The no-doubling rule for joint filers

Delaware does not double the bracket thresholds for joint filers the way most states do. A married-filing-jointly Delaware household with $120,000 of combined taxable income pays the top 6.6 percent on the portion above $60,000, identical treatment to a single filer with $120,000. For high-earning dual-income Delaware households this is a meaningful negative against most other states' tax codes. Source: Del. Code Title 30 § 1102, accessed 26 May 2026.

The standard deduction and personal credit

Delaware allows a standard deduction of $3,250 single or $6,500 married-filing-jointly, plus a personal credit of $110 per exemption under Title 30 § 1110. The credit applies as a flat reduction against tax liability, not as an above-the-line deduction from gross. So a single Delaware employee earning $60,000 with one personal exemption pays roughly $2,635 in state income tax for 2026 ($2,745 statutory tax less the $110 personal credit). The same employee on $120,000 pays about $6,475.

How Delaware compares regionally

Delaware's 6.6 percent top rate sits in the middle of the Mid-Atlantic pack. New Jersey tops out at 10.75 percent, Maryland at 5.75 percent (plus county piggyback rates of 2.25 to 3.20 percent), Pennsylvania has a flat 3.07 percent, and Virginia has a 5.75 percent top. The Delaware structure is unusual for one reason: the top bracket triggers at just $60,000, lower than almost any other progressive-rate state, which means a senior individual contributor on a $150,000 base pays the same marginal rate as a $1 million executive. Source: Tax Foundation 2026 state-rate tables, accessed 26 May 2026.

What this means for offer letters

A Delaware employee earning $100,000 pays roughly $5,150 in state income tax for 2026 at the 6.6 percent top bracket; the same offer in Pennsylvania pays about $3,070 (flat 3.07 percent), in New Jersey about $5,920 (mid-bracket), and in Texas $0 (no state income tax). Bake the full Delaware ladder into your offer-letter take-home calculator before the first Delaware hire. The 6.6 percent top kicks in at $60,000 of taxable income, well within entry-level professional comp.

What does a Delaware employer pay in unemployment insurance (UI / SUTA)?

New Delaware employers pay 1.0 percent on the first $14,500 of each employee’s annual wages under Schedule B in 2026, capped at $145 per employee per year. After two to three full calendar years of contribution-and-benefit experience, the rate moves into the experience-rated band of 0.3 percent to 5.4 percent. Delinquent employers pay 6.3 percent. A separate 0.2 percent Operations and Technology Assessment is now billed quarterly as its own line, starting in 2026.

The HB 433 wage-base phase-in

Delaware passed House Bill 433 in 2024 to put the UI taxable wage base on a permanent statutory schedule. The phase-in is $12,500 in 2025, $14,500 in 2026, and $16,500 from 2027 onwards. Before the phase-in, the wage base sat at $10,500 and got adjusted ad hoc through the legislative cycle. HB 433 also reduced the new-employer rate to 1.0 percent for the 2025 to 2026 transition and shifts the rate-calculation methodology from Benefit Wage to Benefit Ratio for 2027 onwards. Build the higher 2027 wage base into multi-year payroll budgets now. Source: Delaware General Assembly HB 433 (2024); EY Tax News 2026, accessed 26 May 2026.

The April rate-notice cadence is unusual

Most US states issue UI rate notices in December for the following calendar year. Delaware issues them in early April for the calendar year already in progress. The 2026 rate notices went out on 6 April 2026 with a compliance deadline of 29 May 2026, meaning Q1 was already filed at the prior year's rate and Q2 is the first quarter at the new rate. Annual budgets built on the December rate-notice assumption need a Delaware adjustment line. Source: Delaware Department of Labor employer-services page, accessed 26 May 2026.

FUTA credit interaction

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) is 6.0 percent on the first $7,000. A Delaware employer that pays state UI in full and on time receives a FUTA credit of 5.4 percent, leaving an effective FUTA of 0.6 percent ($42 per employee per year). Delaware has not been subject to FUTA credit reduction in the recent five-year window. Verify the current-year status via IRS Schedule A (Form 940) each January. Source: IRS Topic 759 and Form 940 Schedule A, accessed 26 May 2026.

The early-year cash-flow point

Because the $14,500 wage base is moderate, a full-time Delaware employee earning $80,000 hits the UI cap in late February. After that, UI is zero on every paycheque for the remainder of the year. The annual UI bill is concentrated in Q1 and early Q2, not spread evenly. What is Form DE-W4 and how does Delaware withholding work?

Form DE-W4 is the Delaware Employee’s Withholding Allowance Certificate, the state equivalent of the federal Form W-4 but built on the allowance-count model Delaware kept after the federal redesign in 2020 removed allowances. Every new Delaware hire should complete a DE-W4 alongside the federal W-4. If the employee does not file a DE-W4, the employer defaults to single with zero allowances, the most aggressive rate on the Delaware withholding tables.

Three Delaware-specific withholding forms exist, and they get confused often:

  • Form DE-W4 (or SD/W4A) — the standard state withholding certificate for resident employees. Captures filing status, allowance count, and any additional-dollar over-withholding. Use this for almost every Delaware hire.
  • Form W-4NR — for non-residents performing personal services in Delaware. Allowance mechanics differ from the resident form because non-residents are taxed only on Delaware-source income.
  • Form W-4DE — a separate, narrow exemption form used only by military spouses claiming exemption from Delaware withholding under the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act. The name confusingly mirrors DE-W4 but the use case is different. Do not collect this from non-military hires.

Why employees should always file a DE-W4

The 2020 federal W-4 removed allowances and switched to a dollar-amount-of-deductions model. Delaware kept the allowance-count model on the DE-W4, including a $110 personal credit per exemption under Title 30 § 1110. An employer that accepts only the federal W-4 has to make assumptions about the Delaware allowance count, and Delaware Division of Revenue guidance defaults to single with zero allowances when no state-specific form is on file. For a $100,000 Delaware employee that’s roughly $580 a year over-withheld against a properly completed DE-W4 claiming one personal credit. The gap accumulates and the employee gets a refund instead of accurate take-home.

The allowance-count math

DE-W4 worksheet drives the allowance count. One allowance for the employee, one for the spouse, one per dependent, plus additional allowances for itemised deductions and adjustments to income that lower the Delaware tax base. The employer looks up the wage on the Delaware Withholding Tables (revenue.delaware.gov) using filing status and allowance count, then withholds the table figure plus any DE-W4 additional dollar amount. How does a Delaware employer file payroll tax returns (W-1Q, W-1, W-1A)?

Delaware uses a three-tier deposit cadence driven by a lookback period. Every employer files Form W-1Q quarterly if annual withholding stays at or below $6,020, Form W-1 monthly through $33,460, and Form W-1A eighth-monthly above $33,460.01. UI runs on a parallel quarterly cadence: Form UC-8 plus UC-8A by the last day of the month after each quarter. The lookback period is 1 July through 30 June immediately preceding the calendar year. New employers default to monthly until the first lookback period completes.

The lookback-period mechanic

The lookback period runs 1 July through 30 June immediately preceding the calendar year for which cadence is being set. An employer that grows past $33,460 in annual withholding during 2025 moves up to eighth-monthly cadence for 2026, then files Form W-1A within three banking days of each eighth-monthly period end. Electronic filing through OneStop Delaware

OneStop Delaware (onestop.delaware.gov) is the canonical registration portal for the Combined Registration Application that handles both Division of Revenue and Department of Labor enrolment in a single submission. Once registered, employers file W-1Q, W-1, W-1A, and W-3 electronically through the Division of Revenue portal, and UC-8 plus UC-8A through the Department of Labor portal. EFT deposit is mandatory for any employer required to deposit federal employment taxes electronically under IRC § 6302, one year after the federal requirement attaches. Source: Delaware Division of Revenue, accessed 26 May 2026.

Zero-filing rule

An employer with no Delaware wages for a period still files a zero return, otherwise the Division of Revenue and the Department of Labor each flag a missing filing and start the delinquency clock. The W-1Q is required even in a zero quarter if the employer is still registered. Source: Delaware Division of Revenue, accessed 26 May 2026.

Does Delaware’s ABC test affect payroll tax obligations?

Yes. Delaware uses a three-prong ABC test under Del. Code Title 19 § 3302(10)(K) to define employment for UI coverage purposes. A worker misclassified as an independent contractor under that test is retroactively an employee for Delaware UI, meaning the employer owes back contributions, interest, and statutory penalties for the full misclassification period. Workers’ compensation classification follows a separate Borello-style multi-factor analysis under Title 19 § 2301.

The Delaware ABC test asks three questions about the worker, and all three must be satisfied for the worker to be classified as a contractor:

  1. Prong A — control: the individual is free from control and direction over the work performed, both under the contract and in fact.
  2. Prong B — usual course or location: the service is performed either outside the usual course of business or outside all places of business of the enterprise. Delaware’s Prong B is broader than California’s strict outside-the-usual-course-only version because the Delaware statute allows the location alternative.
  3. Prong C — independent business: the individual is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business.

The hiring entity bears the burden of proving all three prongs. Failure on any one prong reclassifies the worker as an employee for UI. The Delaware Department of Labor will assess back UI contributions on every dollar the misclassified worker earned during the contractor period plus interest. Unlike California, Delaware does not maintain a statutory list of profession-by-profession ABC carve-outs; the test applies uniformly. Source: Del. Code Title 19 § 3302(10), accessed 26 May 2026.

How Delaware differs from California on ABC

Delaware’s Prong B is meaningfully broader than California’s. California requires that the work be outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business, period. Delaware allows either outside the usual course OR outside all places of business of the enterprise. So a remote contractor performing services from their own location, never at the company’s offices, satisfies Delaware Prong B on the location alternative even if the work is in the company’s core business. That alternative does not exist under California Labor Code § 2775. For Delaware-resident remote contractors, the location prong is often easier to satisfy than it would be for the same engagement in California. Federal IRS overlay

Even when a worker satisfies Delaware’s ABC test as a contractor for state UI purposes, the IRS applies its own twenty-factor common-law control test for federal employment-tax purposes. Federal misclassification can attach federal income tax withholding, FICA, and FUTA liabilities independently of the Delaware state analysis.
