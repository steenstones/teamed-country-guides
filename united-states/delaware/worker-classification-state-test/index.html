How does Delaware’s worker classification test work in 2026?

Delaware runs a three-prong ABC test for unemployment insurance under Del. Code Title 19 § 3302(10)(K). Workers’ compensation runs a separate right-of-control analysis under Title 19 Chapter 23. State income tax withholding follows the federal IRS common-law factors via the Division of Revenue. Construction sits under a fourth statute, the Delaware Workplace Fraud Act (Title 19 Chapter 35), with civil and criminal penalties and a public Workplace Fraud Violators List. Incorporating in Delaware does not create a Delaware payroll obligation by itself; paying a Delaware-resident worker does.

Delaware’s worker classification framework in 2026 is a three-test split across three agencies, plus a construction-only fourth statute. Unemployment insurance applies a three-prong ABC test under Del. Code Title 19 § 3302(10)(K), administered by the Delaware Department of Labor, Division of Unemployment Insurance. Workers’ compensation applies a separate multi-factor right-of-control test under Del. Code Title 19 Chapter 23, administered by the Office of Workers’ Compensation. State income tax withholding follows the federal IRS common-law / 20-factor test for W-2 versus 1099 issuance, administered by the Delaware Division of Revenue. Construction work sits under a fourth statute, the Delaware Workplace Fraud Act (Del. Code Title 19 Chapter 35, §§ 3501-3511), with civil and criminal penalties, stop-work orders, and a public Workplace Fraud Violators List. Delaware’s Prong B is disjunctive (EITHER outside the usual course OR outside all places of business), structurally closer to Connecticut and New Jersey than to California’s strict conjunctive Prong B under Cal. Lab Code § 2775. There is no California-style profession carve-out list. And incorporating in Delaware (Title 8) does not, by itself, create any Delaware employment-tax obligation under Title 30 Chapter 11 or Title 19 Chapter 33.

Which worker classification test does Delaware use?

Three tests, three agencies, plus a fourth statute for construction. The ABC test under Del. Code Title 19 § 3302(10)(K) applies to unemployment insurance. A separate multi-factor right-of-control test under Del. Code Title 19 Chapter 23 applies to workers’ compensation. The federal IRS common-law / 20-factor test applies to state income tax withholding through the Division of Revenue. The Delaware Workplace Fraud Act (Title 19 Chapter 35) overlays construction work only.

Delaware sits in the ABC family for unemployment insurance, but it’s a structurally simpler ABC state than California or Massachusetts. The Delaware version of Prong B is disjunctive: the work is contractor work if it’s performed EITHER outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business OR outside all of the enterprise’s places of business. That EITHER/OR reading is the structural difference from the strict California version, where Prong B is conjunctive and demands the work be outside the usual course of business, full stop. Delaware’s reading sits next to Connecticut and New Jersey on the disjunctive ABC family tree.

What are the three prongs of Delaware’s ABC test?

All three prongs must be met for contractor status under Del. Code Title 19 § 3302(10)(K). Prong A: the worker is free from control and direction in performing the service, both under contract and in fact. Prong B: the service is performed either outside the usual course of the employer’s business OR outside all the places of business of the enterprise. Prong C: the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade of the same nature as the service performed.

ProngStatutory text (paraphrased from § 3302(10)(K))What it actually tests
A The worker has been and will continue to be free from control and direction in connection with the performance of the service, both under the contract for performance of services and in fact. Who controls how the work is done. Both the written contract AND the day-to-day reality have to read ‘independent’.
B The service is performed either outside the usual course of the business for which the service is performed or is performed outside of all the places of business of the enterprise for which the service is performed. The disjunctive prong. Delaware allows EITHER condition to satisfy B, unlike California which demands ‘outside the usual course’ on its own.
C The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business of the same nature as the service performed. Real, ongoing, public-facing business of their own. Other clients, marketing, business licence, separate insurance.

Why Delaware’s disjunctive Prong B matters

Delaware’s Prong B is the structural difference between this state and California. The California version (Cal. Lab Code § 2775(b)(1)(B)) requires the work to be outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business, full stop. The Delaware version allows the same contractor relationship to satisfy Prong B by being performed either outside the usual course or outside all the places of business of the enterprise. A software developer hired by a software company in California fails Prong B every time. The same engagement in Delaware may satisfy Prong B if the developer works from a separate office, their own home studio, or a coworking space that is not one of the employer’s places of business. Delaware DOL reads the ‘places of business’ clause narrowly, focusing on whether the employer maintains any physical premises where the contractor regularly performs the service.

The corollary is that fully-remote contractor engagements have a structurally easier path through Delaware’s Prong B than they do through California’s. The narrow path still requires Prongs A and C to hold: freedom from control and direction in fact (not just on paper), and a customarily independent trade with other clients, marketing, and its own business presence. Failing A or C still reclassifies the worker as a Delaware UI employee regardless of how clearly Prong B is satisfied. And the burden of proof sits on the hiring entity for all three prongs. Source: Del. Code Title 19 § 3302(10)(K); Delaware DOL Independent Contractor Determination guidance, accessed 26 May 2026.

How does Delaware’s ABC test compare to California, Massachusetts, and Connecticut?

Delaware sits in the disjunctive ABC family alongside Connecticut and New Jersey, structurally easier than California or Massachusetts. The Delaware framework also has no profession-by-profession carve-out list (no equivalent of California Labor Code § 2776-2787), so the same ABC analysis applies uniformly across industries. Most other US states (Arkansas, Arizona, Alabama, New York, Texas, Florida, Colorado) apply the IRS common-law test for UI and never trigger ABC analysis at all.

The US worker classification map is structurally three groups for state unemployment insurance. The strict ABC group (California, Massachusetts) presumes employment and demands proof of all three prongs with a conjunctive Prong B. The disjunctive ABC group (Connecticut, New Jersey, Delaware) demands all three prongs but allows EITHER reading on Prong B. The common-law group (most of the US, including Arkansas, Arizona, Alabama, Colorado, New York, Texas, Florida, Illinois) applies the IRS multi-factor analysis and reaches probabilistic answers. Delaware sits in the second group, with the additional simplification that no profession is exempted by statute.

The simplicity argument for Delaware

Delaware’s framework is genuinely simpler to administer than California’s. One ABC test for UI applies across industries with no profession-specific carve-outs. The disjunctive Prong B reading gives a real path through for fully-remote engagements where the hiring entity has no Delaware premises. The right-of-control test for workers’ comp under Title 19 Chapter 23 reads like the IRS common-law factors that most employers already know. And income tax withholding follows the federal IRS test directly through the Division of Revenue, so the W-2 versus 1099 call lines up with how the employer files at the federal level.

The complexity that does exist sits in one place: construction. The Delaware Workplace Fraud Act overlays the ABC test for construction services with its own statutory presumption of employment, civil and criminal penalties, stop-work orders, and a three-year listing on the public Workplace Fraud Violators List for violators. Outside construction, the framework is one of the cleaner state-level worker-classification regimes in the United States. Source: Del. Code Title 19 §§ 3302(10)(K), 3501-3511; Cal. Lab Code §§ 2775-2787; Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 149 § 148B, accessed 26 May 2026.

What is the Delaware Workplace Fraud Act and when does it apply?

The Delaware Workplace Fraud Act (Del. Code Title 19 Chapter 35, §§ 3501-3511) overlays the standard ABC test for construction work only. It presumes an employer-employee relationship for any individual performing construction services, sets civil penalties up to $5,000 per misclassified worker on a first violation and $20,000 per misclassified worker on a knowing violation, allows stop-work orders, and lists violators publicly on the Workplace Fraud Violators List for three years. The Act does not apply outside construction.

The Workplace Fraud Act is the single largest classification trap for any construction-adjacent employer in Delaware. It was enacted to address widespread 1099-mislabelling in the construction industry, where the misclassification pattern reliably evades both UI contributions and workers’ comp premium. The Act’s defining moves are three: a statutory presumption of employment for construction services, a heavily punitive penalty regime with criminal exposure layered on top, and a public-list shaming mechanism that materially affects the employer’s ability to bid on future construction work.

When the Act bites and when it doesn’t

The Workplace Fraud Act applies only to construction services as defined at § 3501. Software contractors, professional services, design work, marketing, and creative engagements fall outside the Act and remain under the standard § 3302(10)(K) ABC test for UI plus the Title 19 Chapter 23 right-of-control test for workers’ comp. A digital agency in Wilmington hiring a freelance copywriter does not trigger Workplace Fraud Act analysis. A general contractor in New Castle County engaging carpenters on 1099 absolutely does.

The Delaware Contractor Registration Act, layered on top of the Workplace Fraud Act, requires any business performing construction services or maintenance in Delaware to register with the Delaware DOL Office of Contractor Registration before work begins. Failure to register carries its own penalty schedule running from $1,000 to $85,000 per violation. The combined regime means construction misclassification in Delaware reliably costs more, faster, than misclassification in any other industry. Source: Del. Code Title 19 Chapter 35, §§ 3501-3511; Delaware DOL Workplace Fraud Act guidance; Delaware Contractor Registration Act, accessed 26 May 2026.

Does incorporating in Delaware create a Delaware payroll obligation?

No. Incorporating in Delaware under Title 8 (General Corporation Law) does not, by itself, create any Delaware employment-tax obligation. The triggers for Delaware payroll obligations under Title 30 Chapter 11 (withholding) and Title 19 Chapter 33 (UI) are paying a Delaware-resident employee or paying a non-resident performing personal services inside Delaware. The state of incorporation is independent of the state of employment.

This is the single most misunderstood point for clients with Delaware-incorporated parents and engineers sitting in California, New York, Texas, or anywhere else. Delaware is the corporate-incorporation capital of the United States by a wide margin: more than 1.8 million business entities are registered under Title 8, including a majority of US public companies and most venture-backed startups. The Delaware resident workforce is roughly 480,000. The gap is not an accident. Title 8 was designed to be a sophisticated corporate-law framework that companies adopt regardless of where their employees physically work.

The Delaware franchise tax (a separate annual fee under Title 8 § 503) and the Delaware corporate income tax (Title 30 § 1903, currently 8.7 percent on apportioned federal taxable income) follow the incorporation. Employment taxes do not. A San Francisco engineer working remotely for a Delaware-incorporated parent runs on California payroll: California PIT withholding, California UI, California SDI, California paid family leave, California workers’ comp under California rates. The same engineer triggers zero Delaware Title 30 withholding and zero Delaware Title 19 UI contributions for the parent, because the engineer is neither a Delaware resident nor performing personal services in Delaware.

How does the 2024 federal US DOL contractor rule interact with Delaware’s ABC test?

The US DOL final rule on independent contractor classification under FLSA, effective 11 March 2024, applies an economic-reality test for federal wage-and-hour purposes. It does not override Delaware’s state ABC test for UI. Federal FLSA reads economic-reality factors; Delaware UI still demands proof of all three ABC prongs. A worker can satisfy one and fail the other.

The 2024 federal rule replaced the prior employer-friendlier 2021 rule and returned the FLSA classification analysis to the multi-factor economic-reality test the courts had applied for decades. Six factors are weighed, none controlling: opportunity for profit or loss, investments by the worker and the employer, degree of permanence of the work relationship, nature and degree of control, whether the work performed is integral to the employer’s business, and skill and initiative. The test reaches a probabilistic answer balanced across the six factors.

For a Delaware engagement, the federal economic-reality test runs in parallel to the state ABC test. A contractor who satisfies the economic-reality test for federal FLSA purposes (no overtime entitlement, no minimum-wage protection under federal law) can still fail Delaware’s ABC test for state UI purposes if Prong B does not hold. The reverse pattern (passes Delaware ABC, fails federal economic-reality) is rarer because the Delaware analysis demands all three prongs and the federal analysis weighs factors, but it is structurally possible. The two tests apply to two different statutes (FLSA wage-and-hour versus Delaware unemployment compensation) and are enforced by two different agencies (US DOL Wage and Hour Division versus Delaware DOL Division of Unemployment Insurance). One test passing does not insulate against the other.

The audit pattern most multi-state employers walk into

A pattern that recurs in 2026 audits: an employer relies on the 2024 economic-reality test to defend a federal FLSA position for a remote Delaware contractor, and assumes the same analysis covers state UI. The Delaware DOL opens a UI audit after the former contractor files for unemployment benefits and cannot collect; the audit applies the ABC test, and reclassifies on Prong B if the contractor regularly worked at a Delaware location of the employer. The economic-reality analysis does not appear in the Delaware DOL audit file. The Delaware answer is governed entirely by § 3302(10)(K), not by federal regulations. Treat the two tests as separate exposures and run both at the contract stage. Source: US DOL final rule on independent contractor classification under FLSA, effective 11 March 2024; Del. Code Title 19 § 3302(10)(K), accessed 26 May 2026.
