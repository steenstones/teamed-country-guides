## The story

Alaska is the state-with-a-recent-statute frame. Two big things changed in November 2024 when Alaska voters passed **Ballot Measure 1**:

1. **A new mandatory paid sick leave entitlement** took effect **1 July 2025**. Alaska now has a real state PSL law, codified in **Alaska Statutes Title 23, Chapter 10, Article 4A**. Most online content is stale (pre-ballot) and still says Alaska has no PSL — that is wrong as of mid-2025.
2. A staircased increase to the state **minimum wage**: $13/hr in 2025, $14/hr in 2026, $15/hr in 2027 (then CPI-indexed).

The sick-leave entitlement specifics:
- Employers with **fewer than 15 employees**: minimum **40 hours/year** of paid sick leave accrual.
- Employers with **15 or more employees**: minimum **56 hours/year** of paid sick leave accrual.
- Accrual rate: **1 hour earned per 30 hours worked**.
- Carry-over allowed up to the annual cap (the entitlement does not reset every year — unused time carries over, capped at the annual entitlement).
- Usable for: own illness, family member illness, and **safe leave** (domestic violence, sexual assault, stalking — for the employee or covered family member).
- Available to use after **90 days of employment**.

What **didn't change**:
- **Paid family leave**: still **NONE** at the state level. Alaska remains one of the 41 states without state PFL. The only floor is federal **FMLA** (29 U.S.C. § 2601), unpaid, job-protected, 12 weeks, 50+ employees within 75-mile radius, 12-month tenure + 1,250 hours rule.
- **Federal protections that still apply on top**: PWFA (Pregnant Workers Fairness Act, 15+ employees), USERRA (military leave), Alaska's state jury-duty protections.

## Page promise

A US employer in Alaska is required by state law (since **1 July 2025**) to provide accrued paid sick leave to all employees, with the cap depending on headcount (40 hours/year under 15 employees; 56 hours/year at 15+). Paid family leave remains unmandated at the state level — federal FMLA is the only floor and applies only at 50+ employees. PWFA, USERRA, and Alaska jury-duty rules stack on top.

## Risks / uncertainties

- The **annual carry-over cap** is set to the annual entitlement (40 or 56 hours). Some early DOL/state-level guidance documents had slightly different framing (some described it as no annual reset, some as annual cap = annual entitlement). The page reflects the latter (cleaner and the safer position for employers).
- The **15-employee threshold** for the higher accrual cap counts Alaska employees, but the question of whether to count multi-state US headcount has not been definitively settled in early implementing regulations. The page conservatively reads this as "Alaska employees on the Alaska payroll" but flags this as worth checking with counsel for multi-state employers approaching the threshold.
