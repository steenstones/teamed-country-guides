What leave must Alaska employers provide in 2026?

Paid sick leave is now mandatory in Alaska under Ballot Measure 1 (effective 1 July 2025): 40 hours/year under 15 employees, 56 hours/year at 15+. No state paid family leave; federal FMLA at 50+ employees. Most online content is still pre-ballot.

Alaska in 2026 does require paid sick leave for the first time, under Ballot Measure 1 (certified November 2024, in force from 1 July 2025) and codified at Alaska Statutes Title 23, Chapter 10, Article 4A. Employees accrue paid sick leave at 1 hour per 30 hours worked, capped at 40 hours per year for employers with fewer than 15 employees and 56 hours per year for employers with 15 or more. Time is available to use after 90 days of employment, carries over up to the annual cap, and covers the employee’s own illness, a family member’s illness, and safe leave (domestic violence, sexual assault, stalking). Alaska has no state paid family leave law; the only family-leave floor is federal FMLA (29 U.S.C. § 2601), which provides 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave at employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. PWFA, USERRA, and Alaska’s jury duty job-protection rules stack on top.

Does Alaska require paid sick leave?

Yes, since 1 July 2025. Alaska voters passed Ballot Measure 1 in November 2024, and the paid-sick-leave provisions took effect halfway through 2025. The entitlement is codified at Alaska Statutes Title 23, Chapter 10, Article 4A. Most pre-2025 vendor content still says Alaska has no state PSL; that is wrong as of 1 July 2025.

The accrual mechanic is simple. Every Alaska employee earns 1 hour of paid sick leave for every 30 hours worked. The annual cap depends on employer size: 40 hours per year at employers with fewer than 15 employees, and 56 hours per year at employers with 15 or more. Accrual starts on the first day of employment, but the employee cannot use the time until they have completed 90 days of employment. Unused hours carry over to the next year, capped at the annual entitlement (so a 14-employee Alaska business can never owe more than 40 hours of accrued sick leave per employee at any one time).

The qualifying reasons are broader than a typical sick-day policy. Sick leave under Ballot Measure 1 covers the employee’s own physical or mental illness, the care of a covered family member with an illness, and what the statute calls safe leave: absences related to domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking, whether the victim is the employee or a covered family member. Safe leave covers court appearances, relocation, counselling, and obtaining services from a victim-services organisation.

What this means for an Alaska handbook in 2026

If your Alaska employee handbook was last updated before mid-2025, it almost certainly needs a rewrite. Three things must appear: (1) the accrual rate of 1 hour per 30 hours worked, (2) the correct annual cap for your headcount band, and (3) the broader scope of sick leave (own illness, family illness, and safe leave). A handbook that still describes paid sick leave as “discretionary” or “at manager approval” is no longer compliant.

For employers with established PTO policies that already exceed the statutory floor, the rule is generally that the existing PTO bank can satisfy the entitlement as long as it can be used for the same qualifying reasons (including safe leave) and the accrual rate is no slower than 1 per 30. Most Alaska handbooks need updating to cover safe leave specifically, even if their generous PTO totals already exceed 56 hours.

Does Alaska require paid family leave?

No. Alaska has no state paid family leave law. Nine US states plus DC do require it (California, Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Washington, and Washington DC); Alaska is not one of them. There is no Alaska state PFL insurance tax, no state PFL programme to fund, and no state PFL claim process for employees. Ballot Measure 1 created a paid sick leave entitlement only; it did not create a paid family leave entitlement.

For an Alaska employer, hiring in Alaska removes the state-level PFL withholding line that adds 0.5 to 1.0 percent of wages in California, New York, or Washington. There is no equivalent payroll deduction for PFL, no state PFL bond schedule, and no state agency PFL claim portal to maintain. The cost line simply does not exist on an Alaska payslip.

What this means for talent: an Alaska employee comparing job offers against friends in California or New York will not have access to a state PFL programme to fall back on for the birth of a child or the care of a parent. The employer’s voluntary parental-leave policy carries the full weight of that retention conversation, especially for Anchorage knowledge workers who compare against Seattle and Bay Area roles where state PFL is layered onto company benefits.

What does federal FMLA give Alaska employees?

Federal FMLA gives qualifying Alaska employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period, with group health coverage continued at the employer’s normal contribution. It applies only to employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. The employee qualifies after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked in the preceding 12 months.

The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 is the federal floor for unpaid, job-protected family leave. It sits on top of Alaska’s new paid sick leave entitlement: FMLA covers parental bonding and serious health conditions, the Alaska statute covers shorter sick-day absences and safe leave. The 50-employee threshold counts every US employee, not just Alaska ones, which catches multi-state employers off guard.

Five qualifying reasons trigger FMLA leave:

  • Birth and care of a newborn, within 12 months of birth
  • Placement of a child with the employee for adoption or for a placement under state child-placement law, within 12 months of placement
  • Care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition
  • The employee’s own serious health condition that makes them unable to perform the job
  • Qualifying military exigency arising from a family member’s active duty in the Armed Forces; up to 26 weeks for military caregiver leave

The 50-employee threshold counts your whole US workforce

An employer with 12 Alaska employees and 45 Washington employees crosses the 50-employee FMLA threshold even though Alaska alone does not get there. The Department of Labor counts every employee on the US payroll for any 20 or more weeks of the current or preceding year. The 75-mile-radius rule then determines which specific worksites are FMLA-covered, which matters for fully remote employers whose employees are geographically dispersed across the lower-48 states and Alaska.

What FMLA does not require

  • Pay during leave. FMLA leave is unpaid by statute. An employee may substitute accrued PTO or accrued Ballot Measure 1 sick leave; the employer may require substitution of accrued paid time. There is no FMLA wage-replacement benefit.
  • FMLA leave for employers under 50. A 25-employee Alaska company has no FMLA obligation and Alaska has no mini-FMLA. Below the threshold, parental leave and serious-illness leave longer than the sick-leave accrual are entirely voluntary.
  • FMLA leave for the first 12 months. A 3-month tenure Alaska employee with a new baby has no FMLA right to job-protected leave; the employer may grant unpaid leave voluntarily.

Source: US Department of Labor, FMLA Final Rule (29 CFR Part 825), accessed 21 May 2026.

What protections apply for pregnancy and disability in Alaska?

Federal law applies; Alaska adds nothing specific. The Pregnancy Discrimination Act (PDA, 1978) treats pregnancy like any other temporary disability for employer leave and benefits. The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA), effective 27 June 2023, requires reasonable accommodation for pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions, applying to employers with 15 or more employees. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) covers pregnancy-related disabilities such as gestational diabetes or severe preeclampsia.

The practical reading for an Alaska employer at 15 to 50 employees: PWFA covers reasonable accommodation (modified duties, schedule changes, time off for medical appointments, lactation breaks), but FMLA does not yet cover the 12 weeks of job-protected leave. The accommodation conversation under PWFA may include voluntary unpaid leave as the accommodation, but the employer has no statutory obligation to hold the job open beyond the accommodation period.

At 50+ employees the package stacks: PWFA accommodation during the pregnancy, FMLA for the birth and bonding, ADA for any post-delivery serious complications, plus the 56-hour Ballot Measure 1 sick leave bank that the employee can use during routine prenatal appointments and recovery. The three federal laws together resemble what a single state PFL programme covers in California or New York, except every line is unpaid (the Ballot Measure 1 sick leave is paid but capped at 56 hours).

What this means for offer letters

The most retention-critical voluntary line on an Alaska offer letter is paid parental leave. The federal floor (12 weeks unpaid at FMLA-covered employers, accommodation under PWFA at 15+) plus 56 hours of paid sick leave still leaves a wide gap that voluntary policy fills. Mid-market Alaska employers commonly offer 6 to 8 weeks of paid maternity leave and 2 to 4 weeks of paid paternity or partner leave; top-quartile knowledge-work employers in Anchorage offer 12 to 16 weeks paid for the primary caregiver regardless of gender. What about military leave and jury duty in Alaska?

Federal USERRA protects civilian jobs for service members; Alaska Statutes 26.05.075 mirrors USERRA for Alaska National Guard members called to state active duty. Jury service is mandatory and Alaska Statutes 09.20.037 protects an employee’s job during service. Alaska, unlike Alabama, does not require employers to continue pay during jury duty; it only protects the job. Neither USERRA nor jury duty is a state PFL or PSL programme; these are narrow, separate carve-outs.

USERRA and Alaska military leave

The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA, 38 U.S.C. § 4301 et seq.) provides civilian reemployment rights for service members called to active duty, with a cumulative cap of five years of military absence. The employer pays nothing during the leave itself, but health-plan coverage must continue (the employee may pay up to 102 percent of the full premium for COBRA-style continuation). On return, the employee is reinstated to the position they would have held had they not left, the so-called escalator principle.

Alaska Statutes 26.05.075 adds state-active-duty protection (Alaska National Guard or Alaska Naval Militia members called by the Governor): same job-protection logic, no statutory pay during state-active-duty absence, no length cap for state active duty specifically. State and federal protections stack; the employee gets whichever framework is more favourable for the specific call-up.

Jury duty in Alaska

Alaska Statutes 09.20.037 prohibits an employer from discharging, threatening, intimidating, or coercing an employee because of jury service or a jury summons. Unlike Alabama’s pay-continuation rule, Alaska does not require the employer to pay regular wages during jury service. The employee may use accrued PTO or accrued Ballot Measure 1 sick leave (some employers permit this; the statute does not require it), and the court pays a small per-diem.

Most Alaska employers in knowledge-work sectors voluntarily continue full pay during jury duty as a retention signal, treating it as a low-cost benefit because jury service is infrequent. Source: Alaska Statutes 26.05.075 and 09.20.037, accessed 21 May 2026.

What about employers with fewer than 15 employees in Alaska?

Below 15 employees, an Alaska employer must still provide 40 hours/year of paid sick leave under Ballot Measure 1 and remains exempt from PWFA accommodation and federal FMLA. USERRA reemployment and Alaska’s jury-duty job protection apply regardless of size. Everything else is voluntary, including paid parental leave, paid bereavement, and additional paid sick days beyond the 40-hour floor.

The compliance burden at single-digit Alaska headcount is lighter than at California or New York equivalents, but it is no longer zero. The new Ballot Measure 1 accrual ledger has to be maintained: every Alaska employee earns 1 hour per 30 worked, available after 90 days, capped at 40 per year, carrying over to the next year up to the cap. The absence does count against any FMLA tracking if you later cross the 50-employee threshold.

The 15-employee threshold tipping point

An Alaska employer hits the 15-employee threshold under Ballot Measure 1 when the Alaska headcount reaches 15 (the multi-state question is unsettled in early implementing guidance; we read this conservatively as Alaska employees on the Alaska payroll). At the 15-employee point, the annual sick-leave cap rises from 40 to 56 hours per year per employee. The employer’s accrual ledger needs to switch over for new hires on the day the threshold trips, and existing employees’ caps move up at the start of the next benefit year (or on the trip date, depending on the policy).

The 50-employee threshold tipping point

An Alaska employer crosses the FMLA 50-employee threshold gradually as the total US headcount grows, often without noticing. The trigger is 20 or more weeks of 50+ US employees in either the current or preceding calendar year. Once tripped, the FMLA obligation continues for the rest of the current calendar year and the full following calendar year, even if headcount drops back below 50. The lookback rule catches employers who hire seasonally or scale through funding rounds; the FMLA obligation often outlasts the headcount that triggered it.
