How does Alaska state income tax and unemployment insurance work in 2026?

Alaska has no state income tax, so federal-only on wages. Unemployment insurance is the surprise: employers pay 1.99 percent (new) or 1.00 to 5.40 percent (experience-rated) on the first $54,200, and Alaska is the only state where employees pay UI too, at 0.50 percent of the same wage base.

Alaska state payroll in 2026 is two layers, not three. State income tax: none. Alaska repealed the personal income tax in 1980 and never reinstated it, so no state W-4, no state withholding return, no state reconciliation. State Unemployment Insurance (SUI): unique. Employers pay 1.99 percent on the first $54,200 of wages as new employers (capped at $1,078.58 per employee per year), then 1.00 to 5.40 percent once experience-rated. Alaska is the only state where employees pay UI too, at 0.50 percent on the same wage base, deducted from each paycheque. Quarterly filing runs on Form TQ01C, due 30 April, 31 July, 31 October, 31 January, filed through the MyAlaska portal. Statute: Alaska Employment Security Act, AS 23.20.

Does Alaska have a state income tax?

No. Alaska has no personal income tax. It was repealed in 1980 when Prudhoe Bay oil revenue replaced the state budget’s reliance on income tax, and it has stayed zero ever since. Federal income tax still applies on a Form W-4 in the normal way; there is no state W-4 equivalent.

What this means operationally:

  • No state withholding on payroll. Federal income tax withholding (FIT) runs as usual on the federal W-4, but nothing state-side gets deducted.
  • No state W-4. Alaska does not publish a state withholding exemption certificate. The federal W-4 is the only income-tax onboarding form.
  • No state quarterly return. The A-1 / A-6 / state reconciliation cadence that exists in income-tax states does not apply. The only Alaska-specific quarterly cadence is the unemployment-insurance return (Form TQ01C, covered below).
  • No state annual reconciliation. Federal Form W-2 still reports federal income tax withheld in Box 2; Box 17 (state income tax withheld) stays blank for Alaska wages.

One quirk worth knowing for take-home maths: Alaska residents also receive the annual Permanent Fund Dividend (PFD), a per-resident share of state oil revenue. The PFD is federally taxable on the resident’s personal Form 1040 but it is not employer-administered and does not flow through payroll. Quote Alaska offers on gross-to-net assuming federal-only withholding and the candidate keeps materially more of each paycheque than a same-salary offer in California or New York. Source: Alaska Department of Revenue, accessed 21 May 2026.

What this means for offer letters

A $90,000 base in Alaska net-pays meaningfully better than the same $90,000 in California (state tax 9.3 percent at that bracket) or New York City (state plus city around 10 percent combined). When recruiting from a high-tax state, the headline number stays the same; the take-home story improves.

What does an Alaska employer pay in unemployment insurance (SUI)?

New Alaska employers pay 1.99 percent on the first $54,200 of each employee’s annual wages, capped at $1,078.58 per employee per year. After experience-rating, the employer rate lands in the 1.00 percent to 5.40 percent band. Alaska’s $54,200 wage base for 2026 is one of the highest in the country, so the maximum-dollar cost per employee is meaningful even at the bottom of the rate band.

Alaska sets the rate class using the formula in AS 23.20.285, broadly an average quarterly decline quotient that measures how stable the employer’s headcount has been over the look-back window. Stable, low-turnover employers settle near the 1.00 percent floor. High-turnover or layoff-heavy employers drift to the 5.40 percent ceiling. The annual rate notice is mailed by the Alaska Department of Labor & Workforce Development each winter; the rate then applies to wages paid in the following calendar year. Authority: AS 23.20, Alaska Employment Security Act.

STEP and TVEP add-ons

Two small state-training programmes ride on top of the headline UI rate: the State Training and Employment Program (STEP) and the Technical and Vocational Education Program (TVEP). Both are small fractional-percentage assessments collected with the UI contribution. They are billed alongside the UI rate, not stacked into the 1.00 to 5.40 percent figure. Teamed’s payroll line-items each assessment separately so the invoice always reconciles to the rate notice.

FUTA credit interaction

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) is 6.0 percent on the first $7,000. An Alaska employer that pays state UI in full and on time receives a FUTA credit of 5.4 percent, leaving an effective FUTA of 0.6 percent ($42 per employee per year). Late or partial state UI payment erodes the credit. Teamed’s payroll books both lines together so the credit never gets lost. Source: IRS Topic 759, accessed 21 May 2026.

Why do Alaska employees pay unemployment insurance too?

Alaska is the only US state where employees pay a portion of unemployment insurance. In 2026 the employee contribution is 0.50 percent of wages up to the same $54,200 wage base, deducted from each paycheque and remitted by the employer alongside the employer contribution. Maximum employee deduction per year: $271.00.

The 0.5 percent shows up on the payslip as a separate line and surprises both sides:

  • Employers need a payroll system that knows to split UI two ways for Alaska wages but not for any other state. A multi-state employer using a US-wide payroll provider has to make sure the Alaska rule is configured per-employee, not globally.
  • Employees often query the deduction the first time they see it. It is genuinely an Alaska-only line item, and it cannot be opted out of. Statute requires it.

The split exists because Alaska’s Employment Security Act funds the UI Trust Fund from both sides of the employment relationship. The intent is to keep the employer rate lower than it would otherwise be while still keeping the Trust Fund solvent through Alaska’s seasonal employment swings (fishing, tourism, oil). Mechanically:

  1. Each pay cycle, payroll calculates 0.5 percent of gross Alaska wages (capped at $54,200 cumulative for the year).
  2. The amount is withheld from the employee’s net pay and shown on the payslip as an Alaska UI deduction.
  3. The employer pools the employee withholdings with its own UI contribution and remits both on Form TQ01C quarterly.
  4. The employee’s annual Form W-2 shows the cumulative employee UI deduction in Box 14.

For an employee earning $100,000 in Alaska, the cumulative employee UI cap of $271.00 hits before the end of the calendar year (because earnings cross $54,200 mid-year), so paycheques later in the year show $0 on the UI line. The employer side stops at the cap too. Source: Alaska Department of Labor & Workforce Development, Employment Security Tax FAQ, accessed 21 May 2026.

How does an Alaska employer file Form TQ01C?

Form TQ01C is Alaska’s quarterly contribution and wage report, due by the last day of the month after each quarter: 30 April, 31 July, 31 October, 31 January. Employers with 50 or more employees must file electronically through the MyAlaska portal. Below that, paper TQ01C is still accepted, but online filing is faster.

The TQ01C captures both the employer’s UI contribution and the employee’s 0.5 percent withholding pooled together, plus the wage detail for each employee in the quarter. Filing late triggers interest at the rate set in AS 23.20 and erodes the federal FUTA credit downstream.

What goes on the form

TQ01C captures three things per employee for the quarter: gross wages, the employer UI contribution at the assigned rate, and the employee 0.5 percent withholding. The continuation sheet (TQ01C Continuation) handles employers with more than the per-page row count. Total contribution due is paid alongside the filing. Source: Alaska Department of Labor & Workforce Development, ES Tax Forms, accessed 21 May 2026.

Online filing through MyAlaska

The MyAlaska portal at my.alaska.gov hosts the Employment Security Tax module. Once an employer is registered (Form TREG, see below), the assigned Alaska Employer ESC account number plus a MyAlaska login is enough to file TQ01C online and pay the contribution. Paper filings still accepted; online is required for 50+ employees. Teamed’s payroll engine files TQ01C electronically every quarter so the cadence never slips.

Zero-wage quarters

If an Alaska employer has no Alaska wages in a quarter (for example, the only Alaska employee was on unpaid leave the whole quarter), file a zero TQ01C anyway. A missing return triggers a delinquency notice and starts the clock on penalty assessment.

How does an Alaska employer register for UI tax?

File Form TREG (Alaska Employer Registration) with the Alaska Department of Labor & Workforce Development as soon as you have wages payable to an Alaska employee. Registration assigns an Alaska Employer ESC account number, which is what every TQ01C, payment, and rate notice cross-references for the rest of the employment relationship.

Three things to have ready before you start TREG:

  1. Federal Employer Identification Number (FEIN). Alaska’s system cross-references the IRS record.
  2. First date of Alaska wages. Registration is required from the first day Alaska wages are paid; backdating is allowed if you file shortly after.
  3. Business structure and contact details. Legal entity name, mailing address, payroll contact, and bank details for ACH payment of contributions.

The standard sequence for a US employer hiring its first Alaska worker:

  • Step 1. Form TREG submitted to the Alaska Department of Labor & Workforce Development (paper or via MyAlaska).
  • Step 2. Alaska assigns an Employer ESC account number, typically within two to three weeks.
  • Step 3. Employer signs up on MyAlaska for the Employment Security Tax module, links the account number.
  • Step 4. First TQ01C filed at the next quarter end, paying both employer and employee UI contributions for the period.
  • Step 5. Annual rate notice issued by the Alaska Department of Labor for the following calendar year. For the first two years before experience-rating, the rate is the new-employer 1.99 percent.
