Out-of-state employers and HR teams trying to decide whether an Alaska remote worker is a 1099 contractor or a W-2 employee. ## Verified data (sources at bottom)

### Alaska's test for worker classification — three different tests for three purposes

Alaska runs three parallel classification tests on the same worker, each tied to a different statute and a different agency:

1. **Unemployment insurance (and state UI tax):** Strict statutory **ABC test** under **AS 23.20.525(a)(8)(A)(B)(C)**. Administered by Alaska Department of Labor and Workforce Development, Employment Security Division (UI tax). All three conditions must be met or the worker is presumed an employee.
2. **Workers' compensation premium and coverage:** **Right-of-control test** under **AS 23.30** (Alaska Workers' Compensation Act), separate definition of "employee" at AS 23.30.395. Administered by Alaska Division of Workers' Compensation. Civil penalty up to **three times the unpaid premium** for misclassification.
3. **Federal payroll tax (FICA, FUTA, federal income tax withholding):** Federal **IRS 20-factor common-law test** (Rev. Rul. 87-41), administered by the IRS.

Alaska has **no state personal income tax**, so state income tax withholding is not a fourth track — this simplifies Alaska compared with Alabama or California, but the ABC test for UI is significantly stricter than the 20-factor test, so the headline answer is "Alaska is harder, not easier."

**Alaska SUTA taxable wage base 2026: $54,200** (per Alaska Department of Labor primary source; see prior sprint page master-0010 for confirmation).

### The Alaska ABC test — verbatim statutory language (AS 23.20.525(a)(8))

"Service performed by an individual whether or not the common law relationship of master and servant exists, unless and until it is shown to the satisfaction of the Department that:

(A) the individual has been and will continue to be free from control and direction in connection with the performance of such service, both under his contract for the performance of service and in fact;

(B) the service is performed either outside the usual course of the business for which the service is performed or is performed outside of all the places of business of the enterprise for which the service is performed; and

(C) the individual is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business of the same nature as that involved in the service performed."

All three conditions must be met. Failure of any one means the worker is an employee for Alaska UI purposes.

### Alaska Department of Labor official guidance on each prong

**Prong A — lack of direction and control:**
- Control is exercised when workers are totally dependent upon the employer economically
- An independent contractor has freedom of action: chooses own hours, days, materials; hires own assistants; sets own fees
- In contract labour, the entity contracting is concerned only with the finished product; in an employer-employee relationship, concern is with the way the work is done

**Prong B — outside the usual course of business OR off-premises:**
- The product or service the employer organized to deliver constitutes the usual course of business
- The premises where work is done is the employer's place of business (a construction site is the usual place for contractors; the point of sale for salespeople)

**Prong C — independently established trade or business:**
- A sign of an independent business is not the possession of a business licence, but showing the business of the worker could exist independent of the particular employer
- An independent contractor customarily does work for others, advertises, provides own tools, submits proposals or bids, sets own fees, and is liable for the performance of the work
- **Critical:** "Written contracts with employees that state they are independent contractors responsible for their own taxes are null and void. Such contracts are not sufficient to alter the real status between the employer and the worker." — Alaska Department of Labor, "Do you have contract labor?" Rev. 12/17.

### The $50 / 24-day safe harbour (AS 23.20.526(a)(3))

Even where work is outside the usual course of business, the worker is still covered for UI purposes if:
- Cash remuneration for the service is **$50 or more** in a calendar quarter, AND
- The service is performed by a worker who is **regularly employed** to do this work (defined as working some portion of the day on each of 24 days within a calendar quarter, OR during the preceding quarter)

In plain English: even one-off small payments above the $50 threshold can pull a contractor into employment territory if the same person was a regular worker for the firm in the prior quarter.

### Workers' compensation — the second test

Alaska Workers' Compensation Act (AS 23.30) carries its own definition of "employee" at AS 23.30.395 and applies a **right-of-control test** that is similar to the federal IRS common-law test, NOT the ABC test. So a worker who passes ABC for UI can still be deemed an employee for workers' comp purposes.

**Misclassification penalty under AS 23.30.080 / .085:**
- Civil penalty of **up to three times the workers' compensation insurance premium** that the employer would have paid had the worker been properly classified
- Premium calculated on full payroll the employer would have reported, including in-kind payments
- Stop-work orders issued for uninsured employers
- Personal liability for any uncovered injury during the look-back period
- Loss of statutory tort-suit immunity

### Misclassification penalties — Alaska total stacked exposure

A misclassified Alaska worker exposes the employer to stacked liability across at least four categories:

1. **Federal payroll tax** (IRS): unpaid employer FICA (6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare = 7.65%, on first $176,100 for SS in 2026), unpaid employee FICA (recovered from employer if can't be recovered from worker), FUTA (0.6% on first $7,000 after credit), penalties under IRC §3509 if unintentional, full liability if intentional
2. **Alaska state unemployment insurance (SUTA)**: unpaid contributions at the new-employer rate (varies, see notes) on the Alaska taxable wage base of **$54,200 in 2026**, plus penalty and interest. Loss of the FUTA 5.4% credit can take federal FUTA exposure from 0.6% to a full 6.0%.
3. **Federal FLSA back wages and overtime**: 2-year lookback, 3 years if wilful; plus **liquidated damages equal to back wages** (double damages by default)
4. **Workers' compensation civil penalty plus uncovered injury liability**: up to **3x the unpaid premium** under AS 23.30; personal liability for any uncovered injury during the look-back period — open-ended

Note: Alaska has **no state personal income tax**, so there is no state tax withholding component to add — one of the rare classification simplifications. But the UI ABC test is significantly stricter than the IRS 20-factor test, so the net exposure profile is **harder** than in common-law states like Alabama.

### Cost example for an employer

A misclassified Alaska employee earning $60,000 per year over 3 years, treated as 1099 then re-classified by audit:

- Unpaid employer FICA: $60,000 x 7.65% x 3 = **$13,770**
- Unpaid Alaska SUTA: variable by rate, on $54,200 wage base (2026); estimate $1,000 to $1,500 per worker per year at new-employer rate range, so **$3,000 to $4,500 over 3 years**, plus penalty and interest
- Federal FLSA back wages and overtime (assuming 8 hours weekly OT at 1.5x): variable, could exceed **$30,000 over 3 years** if non-exempt
- FLSA liquidated damages: equal to back wages, **another $30,000**
- Workers' comp civil penalty: up to **3x the unpaid premium**; for a typical office role at, say, $1,800 annual premium, that's $5,400 in penalty alone over 1 year; clerical risk class is low, construction much higher
- Workers' comp uncovered injury: open-ended; a single head injury can exceed six figures

Conservative back-of-envelope total exposure: **$70,000 to $130,000 per misclassified worker over 3 years**, before legal fees and uncovered injury liability.

### Recent changes that move the math (2025-2026)

- **27 February 2026 — federal DOL Notice of Proposed Rulemaking** to rescind the 2024 "totality of circumstances" rule and restore the 2021 "economic reality" test for FLSA classification. This is a federal proposal, not yet final; Alaska state law (the ABC test for UI) is unaffected, but the federal FLSA floor may shift mid-2026.
- No 2025-2026 Alaska legislation changes the ABC test or the workers' comp test. SB158 (2023) tightened Workers' Compensation Act misclassification penalties; no new Alaska bill in the current session changes either standard.
