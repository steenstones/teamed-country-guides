Alaska’s worker classification in 2026 runs on three parallel tests. Unemployment insurance uses a strict statutory ABC test under AS 23.20.525(a)(8): the worker must be free from control, perform services outside the firm’s usual course of business or off its premises, AND be customarily engaged in an independently established trade. All three prongs, no exceptions. Workers’ compensation (AS 23.30) uses a separate right-of-control test with a civil penalty of up to three times the unpaid premium for misclassification. Federal payroll tax still runs on the IRS 20-factor common-law test. Alaska has no state personal income tax, so there’s no fourth track. But the ABC standard makes Alaska stricter than Alabama or any common-law state, and an employer who carries the Alabama answer north gets reclassified. Total stacked exposure on a misclassified $60,000 Alaska worker over 3 years: roughly $70,000 to $130,000 before legal fees and uncovered injury liability.

Which worker classification test does Alaska use?

Three tests, three statutes, three agencies. Alaska is one of 27 ABC-test states for unemployment insurance, applying the strict three-prong standard at AS 23.20.525(a)(8)(A)(B)(C). Workers’ compensation runs on a separate right-of-control test at AS 23.30. Federal payroll tax (FICA, FUTA, federal withholding) still uses the IRS 20-factor common-law test. Alaska has no state personal income tax, so there is no fourth test.

An out-of-state employer who hires an Alaska remote worker, applies the IRS 20-factor test, decides ‘contractor’, and issues a 1099 has answered the wrong question. The IRS test governs federal payroll tax. The Alaska Department of Labor and Workforce Development applies a different and stricter test to the same worker for state unemployment insurance purposes, and the Alaska Division of Workers’ Compensation applies a third test for premium and coverage. A worker who passes the IRS common-law test as a contractor can still be deemed an employee for Alaska UI under the ABC standard, with separate back-tax liability.

What are the three prongs of Alaska’s ABC test?

All three prongs must be met or the worker is presumed an employee for Alaska unemployment insurance. The statute reads: a worker is excluded from coverage only where the Department is satisfied that (A) the individual is free from control and direction in connection with the service, both under contract and in fact; (B) the service is performed outside the firm’s usual course of business OR outside all of the firm’s places of business; AND (C) the individual is customarily engaged in an independently established trade of the same nature as the service performed.

Prong A — lack of direction and control

Control is exercised when workers are totally dependent upon the employer economically. An independent contractor has freedom of action: chooses own hours, days, materials, hires own assistants, acquires own customers, sets own fees. In contract labour, the entity contracting is concerned only with the finished product; in an employer-employee relationship, the concern is with the way the work is done. A worker who performs services primarily for one company is economically dependent on that company.

Prong B — outside the usual course of business OR off-premises

The product or service the employer organised to deliver constitutes the usual course of business; work performed in the usual course of business is employment. The premises where work is done is the employer’s place of business: a construction site is the usual place of business for contractors, the point of sales for salespeople. Remote work for a software firm is in the usual course of business even when performed from the worker’s home, so prong B is usually the prong that catches a misclassified remote worker.

Prong C — independently established trade or business

A business licence alone is not the marker. The marker is whether the business of the worker could exist independent of the particular employer. An independent contractor customarily does work for others, advertises, provides own tools, submits proposals or bids on projects, sets own fees, and is liable for the performance of the work. Crucially, the Alaska Department of Labor is explicit: “Written contracts with employees that state they are independent contractors responsible for their own taxes are null and void. Such contracts are not sufficient to alter the real status between the employer and the worker.”

03 All Three Prongs, No Exceptions

Alaska’s ABC test is strict in the same family as California’s AB5 standard. A worker who satisfies prongs A and C but fails prong B (because the work falls in the firm’s usual course of business) is an employee for Alaska UI. The 20-factor common-law test from Alabama or the federal IRS rule won’t save the classification. Run prong B first on every Alaska 1099 you’re considering — it’s the prong that catches the most out-of-state employers.

Why does Alaska workers’ compensation use a different test?

The Alaska Workers’ Compensation Act (AS 23.30) carries its own definition of “employee” at AS 23.30.395 and applies a right-of-control test, similar to the federal IRS common-law test, NOT the ABC test. A worker who passes ABC for unemployment insurance can still be deemed an employee for workers’ comp purposes, with separate financial exposure: a civil penalty of up to three times the unpaid premium plus uncovered-injury liability.

The right-of-control test asks one core question: did the hiring party reserve the right to direct the means and method of the work, even if it didn’t exercise that right? It’s a narrower test than the ABC standard, but it can find employment where ABC does not, because the right-of-control test does not require the worker’s trade to be outside the firm’s usual course of business. A high-control engagement that satisfies prongs B and C of ABC (independent trade, off-premises work) can still fail right-of-control if the contract reserves direction over how the work gets done.

What this costs an employer who gets it wrong

  • Civil penalty up to 3x the unpaid premium, calculated on full payroll the employer would have reported had the worker been properly classified, including in-kind payments (AS 23.30)
  • Stop-work orders for uninsured employers
  • Personal liability for the injury itself, if the worker was injured on the job during the look-back period and never carried under a policy
  • Loss of statutory immunity from civil suit, since the workers’ comp bargain (premium for immunity from tort liability) was never paid

The cost of getting workers’ comp classification wrong is open-ended. A single uncovered head injury can blow past six figures, even for a small firm. What is the $50 / 24-day Alaska safe-harbour gotcha?

Even where work passes prong B (outside the firm’s usual course of business), Alaska statute AS 23.20.526(a)(3) still pulls the worker into UI coverage if cash remuneration in the calendar quarter is $50 or more AND the worker is “regularly employed” to do the work. Regularly employed means working some portion of the day on each of 24 days within the calendar quarter, or the preceding calendar quarter.

The safe-harbour in 526(a)(3) was meant to exempt true one-off, small-dollar work from UI reporting. In practice it’s a trap. A consultant who worked for the firm in Q1 (say 30 days, on a different scope) and then does $200 of additional one-off advisory in Q2 still gets pulled into UI coverage for Q2 because the prior-quarter regular-employment threshold was already crossed. The 24-day count is on a quarterly basis and the look-back to the prior quarter applies even where the Q2 engagement itself is short.

An employer who has used the same individual as a regular freelance contributor cannot easily shift that person to a one-off small-dollar engagement and claim the safe-harbour. The Alaska Department of Labor reads regular-employment status forward across the quarter boundary. If you want the safe-harbour, the relationship has to genuinely change. Source: AS 23.20.526(a)(3); Alaska Department of Labor and Workforce Development guidance.

What does misclassifying an Alaska worker actually cost?

Stacked liability across four categories. A misclassified $60,000-per-year Alaska worker, audited and re-classified after 3 years on 1099, exposes the employer to roughly $70,000 to $130,000 per worker in back federal payroll tax, back Alaska UI contributions, FLSA double damages, and workers’ comp civil penalty, before legal fees and any uncovered-injury liability.

Why Alaska is harder than Alabama, not easier

On first glance, Alaska looks simpler than Alabama: no state personal income tax, so no state withholding track to add. But the unemployment insurance test is significantly stricter. Alabama applies the IRS 20-factor common-law test for UI and tax. Alaska applies the ABC test, which is a higher bar to clear for any worker performing services inside the hiring firm’s usual course of business. A specialist software consultant who is comfortably 1099 in Alabama is at material risk of reclassification in Alaska under prong B. How does the 2026 federal rule change interact with the Alaska test?

On 27 February 2026, the federal Department of Labor issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking to rescind the 2024 ‘totality of the circumstances’ FLSA test and restore the 2021 economic reality test. The proposal affects federal FLSA wage and hour analysis only. Alaska’s ABC test under AS 23.20.525 is unaffected because it sits in state statute, not federal regulation.

The federal proposal matters for FLSA overtime exposure, which is one of the four stacked liability categories above. If the proposed rule lands, the federal threshold for finding a worker is an ‘employee’ for FLSA overtime purposes tightens slightly compared with the 2024 rule. The Alaska analysis under the ABC test runs on its own statutory track, so a worker who is an employee for Alaska UI can still be analysed under the new federal economic-reality test for FLSA and reach a different conclusion in the edge cases.

Practical effect for an Alaska hire

For the vast majority of full-time-equivalent roles, all three Alaska tests reach the same answer: employee. The divergence sits in the edge cases, the highly-specialised consultant who works from home, uses their own tools, sets their own hours, and is paid by deliverable. That profile can pass right-of-control (workers’ comp) and pass economic-reality (FLSA) but still fail prong B of the Alaska ABC test if the work falls in the firm’s usual course of business. The conservative approach is the answer with the broader coverage, which in Alaska is almost always ‘employee’ under ABC.
