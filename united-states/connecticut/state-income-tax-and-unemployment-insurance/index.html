How does Connecticut state income tax and unemployment insurance work in 2026?

Connecticut runs a 7-bracket progressive state income tax topping out at 6.99 percent, layers an employee-paid Connecticut Paid Leave contribution of 0.5 percent on every dollar of wages up to the Social Security wage base, and asks employers for 1.9 percent unemployment insurance on a $27,000 wage base in the new-employer year, the highest-base SUI rate in New England.

Connecticut state payroll in 2026: state income tax runs a seven-band progressive ladder from 2.00 percent to 6.99 percent under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-700, after the 2024 Lamont budget cut the lowest brackets from 3.00 and 5.00 percent. Unemployment Insurance costs 1.9 percent on the first $27,000 of wages for new employers, down from 2.2 percent in 2025 under Public Acts 21-200 and 22-67, with an experience-rated band of 1.1 percent to 9.9 percent capped at 8.9 percent for 2026 via a 1.125 rate adjustment. Connecticut Paid Leave is employee-paid at 0.5 percent of wages up to the Social Security wage base of $184,500, for a maximum employee contribution of $922.50 per year. Every new hire completes Form CT-W4 selecting one of five withholding codes (A, B, C, D, or F); no allowance count, no exemption count. Quarterly returns run on Form UC-2 plus UC-5A with the Connecticut Department of Labor and Form CT-941 with the Department of Revenue Services, filed through ReEmployCT and myconneCT respectively.

What is the Connecticut state income tax rate in 2026?

Connecticut charges a seven-band progressive income tax from 2.00 percent to 6.99 percent, with the top rate hitting taxable income above $500,000 for single filers and above $1,000,000 for joint filers. The full ladder runs 2.00, 4.50, 5.50, 6.00, 6.50, 6.90, and 6.99 percent under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-700. The lowest two brackets dropped from 3.00 to 2.00 and from 5.00 to 4.50 percent on 1 January 2024 under House Bill 6941, the largest income tax cut in Connecticut history.

The 2024 lowest-bracket cut

House Bill 6941, signed by Governor Lamont as part of the FY2024-2025 biennial budget, dropped the bottom two brackets in the first Connecticut income tax reduction since the mid-1990s. The 3.00 percent rate on the first $10,000 of single income (or $20,000 joint) became 2.00 percent. The 5.00 percent rate on income from $10,000 to $50,000 single (or $20,000 to $100,000 joint) became 4.50 percent. The Department of Revenue Services estimated approximately one million Connecticut tax filers benefited from the cut, capped at single filers earning under $150,000 and joint filers earning under $300,000. Source: Office of Governor Lamont, press release December 2023.

The 3 percent phase-out add-back

Higher-income Connecticut filers face an unusual mechanic: the benefit of the lowest 2.00 percent (formerly 3 percent) rate is recaptured for taxpayers above certain CT AGI thresholds. The DRS publishes graduated 2 percent Phase-Out Add-Back Tables in Informational Publication 2026(1) that vary by withholding code. The mechanic effectively retires the lowest-bracket benefit for higher earners, so a $400,000 single filer pays an effective marginal rate close to the next bracket up rather than enjoying the full lowest-bracket dollars. Employers do not calculate the phase-out at source; the employee's selected withholding code determines whether the Code D high-income variant applies. Source: DRS IP 2026(1) Connecticut Employer's Tax Guide.

Personal exemption phase-out

Connecticut allows a personal exemption of up to $15,000 for single filers and $24,000 for married filing jointly, phasing out by $1,000 for every $1,000 of CT AGI above the threshold ($30,000 single, $48,000 MFJ). The exemption is fully eliminated at CT AGI of $30,000 plus $15,000 = $45,000 for single filers, and at $48,000 plus $24,000 = $72,000 for MFJ. A $90,000 Connecticut employee therefore gets zero personal exemption and is taxed on the full taxable income from dollar one. This is one of the structural reasons Connecticut effective rates run higher than the headline ladder suggests for middle-to-upper-middle earners. Source: Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-702 and DRS IP 2026(1).

What does a Connecticut employer pay in unemployment insurance (UI / SUTA)?

New Connecticut employers pay 1.9 percent on the first $27,000 of each employee’s annual wages, capped at $513 per employee per year. After three full calendar years of benefit-charge experience, the rate moves into an experience-rated band of 1.1 percent to 9.9 percent, with the 2026 maximum charged rate held to 8.9 percent via a statutory 1.125 rate adjustment introduced under Public Acts 21-200 and 22-67. The $27,000 wage base rose from $26,100 in 2025 and is the highest in New England, a counterpoint to states like Massachusetts ($15,000) and Rhode Island ($29,200).

The CT DOL calculates each employer’s experience rate annually from a reserve-account formula: cumulative contributions paid in, minus benefits paid out to former employees, divided by average taxable payroll. The system was reformed under Public Acts 21-200 (2021) and 22-67 (2022) in a tripartite business-labor-government effort to restore Trust Fund solvency after the 2020-2021 pandemic UI claim surge drained reserves. The reforms expanded the wage base (climbing from $15,000 historically toward $30,000 by 2030), reduced the maximum charged rate, and introduced the 1.125 rate adjustment for 2026. A 1.0 percent fund solvency tax applies on top of the experience rate. Authority: CT DOL Unemployment Insurance Tax page, accessed 26 May 2026.

The wage-base trajectory

Connecticut’s UI wage base climbed from $15,000 (pre-2024) to $25,000 (2024), $26,100 (2025), and $27,000 (2026), and is scheduled to keep stepping toward $30,000 over the next several years. The mechanic is deliberate: a higher wage base lets the state collect more on the same payroll without raising headline rates, which gives Connecticut political room to keep rates lower than peer high-cost states. For a fully-loaded Connecticut employee earning $80,000, the entire annual UI bill at the new-employer rate is reached in early April. After that, UI is zero on every paycheque for the remainder of the year. The whole annual UI cost is concentrated in Q1 and early Q2, not spread evenly. Source: Public Acts 21-200 and 22-67, accessed 26 May 2026.

FUTA credit interaction

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) is 6.0 percent on the first $7,000. A Connecticut employer that pays state UI in full and on time receives a FUTA credit of 5.4 percent, leaving an effective FUTA of 0.6 percent ($42 per employee per year). Connecticut was subject to FUTA credit reduction during the early 2010s after the state UI Trust Fund carried outstanding federal loans following the 2008 recession. The current Trust Fund balance, restored under the PA 21-200 / 22-67 reforms, has cleared the loan position. Verify the current-year status via IRS Schedule A (Form 940) each January. Late or partial state UI payment also erodes the federal credit independently of the loan-based reduction. Source: IRS Topic 759 and Form 940 Schedule A, accessed 26 May 2026.

What is Connecticut Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFMLA) and who pays it?

Connecticut Paid Leave is an employee-paid payroll tax funding the state’s family and medical leave benefit, withheld at 0.5 percent of all wages up to the federal Social Security wage base of $184,500 for 2026, for a maximum annual employee contribution of $922.50. Connecticut is one of nine US states (with California, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Washington, and the District of Columbia) operating a state-run paid family and medical leave programme, and the only state where the programme is funded entirely by employee contributions with no employer matching contribution.

What CT Paid Leave actually pays out

The programme funds up to 12 weeks of paid leave in a 12-month period, with an additional 2 weeks available for incapacitation related to pregnancy. The 2026 maximum weekly benefit is $1,016.40, calculated as 60 times the Connecticut minimum wage of $16.94 per hour. Benefits are wage-replacement at 95 percent of the employee’s base weekly earnings up to 40 times the state minimum wage, plus 60 percent of base weekly earnings between that floor and the cap. Qualifying reasons include the employee’s own serious health condition, bonding with a new child, caring for a seriously ill family member, military family exigency, and safe leave related to family violence or sexual assault. The programme is administered by the Connecticut Paid Leave Authority in partnership with Aflac as the third-party administrator. Source: CT Paid Leave Authority and Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-49g, accessed 26 May 2026.

The "employee pays it all" structure

Unlike Massachusetts (where the employer pays approximately 60 percent of the contribution on the medical side) or California SDI (where SDI funds both DI and PFL through a combined employee-paid line), Connecticut Paid Leave is funded 100 percent by employees. The 0.5 percent shows up as a separate line on the payslip and reduces gross-to-net for every Connecticut hire from the first paycheque. For a $200,000 Connecticut employee, the annual PFMLA contribution is capped at $922.50 because the Social Security wage base of $184,500 sits below the salary. For an $80,000 employee, it is $400 a year. The employer is the withholding agent, remitting quarterly to the CT Paid Leave Authority, but bears no programme cost. Source: CT Paid Leave Authority FAQ, accessed 26 May 2026.

Private plan opt-out

Connecticut employers can opt out of the state programme by offering a private plan that provides benefits equal to or greater than the statutory benefit, approved by the Paid Leave Authority. The private-plan route is structurally identical to the New York PFL private-plan model: the employer must demonstrate parity on benefit duration, weekly maximum, qualifying reasons, and job protection, plus contribute equivalent funding. Most Connecticut employers stay with the state plan because the administrative overhead of running a private plan rarely beats the 0.5 percent contribution rate. Teamed runs the state plan for every Connecticut hire by default. Source: CT Paid Leave Authority private plan guidance, accessed 26 May 2026.

What is Form CT-W4 and how does Connecticut withholding work?

Form CT-W4 is the Employee’s Withholding Certificate, the state equivalent of the federal Form W-4 but built on a Connecticut-specific withholding code system rather than allowance counts. Every Connecticut new hire selects one of five withholding codes: A (married filing separately or married filing jointly with both spouses working), B (head of household), C (married filing jointly, spouse not working), D (married filing jointly, both spouses working, high combined income), or F (single). If the employee does not file a CT-W4, the employer must withhold at the highest rate (Code F default), which is the most aggressive combination on either of the two DRS-published withholding tables.

The mechanics differ from the post-2020 federal W-4 in two important ways. Connecticut never adopted the allowance system in the first place and the state withholding model is built around filing status plus household-income code selection, not exemption counting. The CT-W4 asks for:

  • Withholding code selection — A, B, C, D, or F, mapped to the employee’s expected filing status and (for married filers) combined household income. A married joint filer with a working spouse generally selects Code A; the same filer with a non-working spouse selects Code C; a high-combined-income two-earner couple selects Code D for accelerated withholding.
  • Additional dollar amount per pay period — a flat-dollar over-withholding line for employees who want to over-pay to cover side income or capital gains the CT-W4 cannot otherwise capture.
  • Reduced dollar amount per pay period — a flat-dollar reduction for employees claiming Connecticut tax credits (property tax credit, EITC) that should reduce withholding below the table rate.
  • Exempt status — employees who expect zero Connecticut income tax liability for the year (typically because they expect the personal exemption to wipe out all CT AGI) can claim exempt and have zero state tax withheld.

The employer looks up the gross taxable wage on the Connecticut Withholding Tables using the selected code and pay frequency (weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly, monthly), applies the table figure, and adjusts by any CT-W4 additional or reduced dollar amount. Both Method I (table lookup) and Method II (exact calculation) are published annually in DRS Informational Publication 2026(1), the Connecticut Employer’s Tax Guide.

Code D and the high-income trap

The most-missed mechanic on the CT-W4 is the Code D requirement. Married couples filing jointly with two working spouses and a high combined income (typically above $100,000 combined) must select Code D rather than Code A to avoid systematic under-withholding. The CT-W4 worksheet walks through the calculation, but most employees default to Code A on autopilot. The result is a refund-or-bill surprise at tax-filing time. Connecticut DRS publishes IP 2026(7) Is My Connecticut Withholding Correct? specifically to help employees verify their code selection mid-year. Source: DRS IP 2026(1) and IP 2026(7), accessed 26 May 2026.

The "highest rate" default

An employee with no CT-W4 on file is treated under Code F (single, no exemption, no credits), which is the most aggressive withholding combination on the Connecticut table. For a $90,000 Connecticut employee that’s roughly $5,100 a year withheld, against $4,200 if the same employee filed a CT-W4 claiming the correct code. The gap accumulates across pay periods and the employee gets a refund instead of accurate take-home. Source: DRS IP 2026(1), accessed 26 May 2026.

How does a Connecticut employer file payroll tax returns (UC-2, UC-5A, CT-941)?

Connecticut uses a quarterly return cadence with a separate-agency filing model. Every Connecticut employer files Form UC-2 (Employer Contribution Return) and Form UC-5A (per-employee wage detail) with the Connecticut Department of Labor by the last day of the month after each quarter, plus Form CT-941 (Connecticut Quarterly Reconciliation of Withholding) with the Department of Revenue Services on the same cadence. UI filings run through ReEmployCT; income tax withholding filings run through myconneCT. Income tax deposits follow the federal Form 941 cadence (next-banking-day, semi-weekly, or monthly) based on the employer’s lookback liability.

Connecticut splits payroll-tax filings across two state agencies. UI sits with the Department of Labor (ReEmployCT portal); income tax withholding sits with the Department of Revenue Services (myconneCT portal). Paid Leave contributions sit with the Connecticut Paid Leave Authority (CT Paid Leave portal). Three portals, three quarterly returns. The Q1 calendar is the most demanding because UI is concentrated in Q1 by wage-base mechanics and PFMLA reaches its annual cap in the second half for high earners.

Income tax deposit cadence

Connecticut mirrors the federal Form 941 deposit cadence on income tax withholding. Employers with a $50,000-or-less lookback liability deposit monthly; above $50,000, semi-weekly. The next-banking-day rule applies once a single deposit hits $100,000. The CT DRS publishes the lookback-period notice in November for the next calendar year. The deposit voucher is Form CT-WH, paid electronically through myconneCT for all but the smallest employers. Source: DRS IP 2026(1) Connecticut Employer’s Tax Guide, accessed 26 May 2026.

Zero-filing rule

An employer with no Connecticut wages for a quarter still files zero returns on UC-2, UC-5A, and CT-941, otherwise the CT DOL and DRS flag missing returns and start the delinquency clock. All three returns are required even in a zero quarter if the employer is still registered. Source: CT DOL Knowledge Base and DRS IP 2026(1), accessed 26 May 2026.

Does Connecticut’s ABC test affect payroll tax obligations?

Yes, but only for unemployment insurance. The Connecticut Department of Labor applies a three-prong ABC test under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii) to determine whether a worker is an employee for UI purposes. A worker misclassified as an independent contractor under the ABC test is retroactively an employee for UI contribution purposes, meaning the employer owes back UI contributions, fund solvency tax, interest, and penalties for the misclassification period. Connecticut income tax (W-2 versus 1099 status) uses the federal common-law / IRS 20-factor test, and workers’ compensation uses a separate test under the Workers’ Compensation Commission, so the same worker can be classified differently across the three agencies.

The ABC test for Connecticut UI asks three questions about the worker, and all three must be satisfied for the worker to be classified as a contractor:

  1. Prong A — control and direction: the worker is free from direction and control in the performance of the service, both under the contract of hire and in fact.
  2. Prong B — outside usual course or off premises: the service is performed either outside the usual course of the employer’s business or outside all the places of business of the enterprise. Connecticut’s Prong B is more employer-friendly than California’s because the test is satisfied if either condition is met; California requires the work to be outside the usual course full stop.
  3. Prong C — independent business: the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business of the same nature as the service performed.

The hiring entity bears the burden of proving all three prongs. Failure on any one prong reclassifies the worker as an employee for UI purposes. The CT DOL Unemployment Compensation Audit Section is the primary enforcement body; audits typically follow a claim filed by a former contractor whose contract ended and who could not collect UI. Back contributions reach back four years per the standard limitations period under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-273, plus 1.0 percent per month interest and civil penalties for wilful misclassification. Source: CT DOL Employer Employee Relationship for CT Unemployment Taxes, accessed 26 May 2026.

The three-agencies, three-tests reality

A Connecticut worker can sit in three different classification buckets depending on which state agency is asking. CT DOL applies the ABC test for unemployment compensation. The Connecticut Department of Revenue Services follows the IRS common-law test (20 factors) for income tax withholding and W-2 versus 1099 issuance, which is consistent with the federal position. The Connecticut Workers’ Compensation Commission applies a separate multi-factor test that emphasises right-to-control and is generally more contractor-friendly than the ABC test. A worker may be correctly issued a 1099 for federal and state income tax purposes but still be subject to UI contributions because Prong B of the ABC test cannot be satisfied. Source: CT DOL FAQs on worker misclassification, accessed 26 May 2026.

The federal DOL 2024 rule overlay

The US Department of Labor’s 2024 final rule on independent contractor classification under the Fair Labor Standards Act applies to Connecticut workers for federal FLSA purposes (minimum wage, overtime, and joint-employer analysis), but does not override Connecticut’s state-level ABC test for UI. The two tests can produce different classification results for the same worker. The federal rule is more nuanced than the ABC test (six-factor economic reality analysis) but generally lands in a similar place for service-industry workers. Source: US DOL final rule effective 11 March 2024 and Harris Beach Murtha employer guidance, accessed 26 May 2026.
