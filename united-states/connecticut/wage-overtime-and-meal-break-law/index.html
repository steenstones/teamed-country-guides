How does Connecticut wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

Connecticut runs a $16.94 state minimum wage from 1 January 2026 (indexed to the federal Employment Cost Index under Public Act 19-4), defaults to weekly or every-other-week pay frequency under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-71b, and mandates a 30-minute meal break for any shift of 7.5 consecutive hours or more under § 31-51ii. Overtime is federal-FLSA only: 1.5x over 40 hours in a workweek, no daily trigger. The combination of weekly-pay default and a 7.5-hour meal-break trigger is unusual enough that national payroll engines configured for the federal floor will fail Connecticut audits on schedule mechanics, not on the wage rate.

Connecticut wage and hour law in 2026 stacks three rules that don’t map cleanly onto a federal-only payroll setup. The state minimum wage is $16.94 per hour from 1 January 2026, up 3.6 percent from 2025’s $16.35 under Public Act 19-4’s annual Employment Cost Index indexation (Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-58). Tipped staff sit lower: $8.23 per hour for hotel and restaurant service employees (tip credit $8.71) and $6.38 per hour for bartenders (tip credit $10.56), with a weekly written tip-credit attestation required from each employee. Overtime is federal-FLSA only under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-76c: 1.5x the regular rate over 40 hours in a workweek, with no daily overtime trigger and no consecutive-shift trigger (unlike Colorado or California). The unusual rule is the meal break: Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-51ii requires 30 consecutive minutes for any shift of 7.5 consecutive hours or more, taken after the first 2 hours and before the last 2 hours of the shift. Connecticut is one of only about eight US states that mandate adult meal breaks at all. And the pay frequency sits at the strict end: Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-71b defaults to weekly or every two weeks; semi-monthly or monthly cadences need express CT DOL approval on application. End of pay period to payday is capped at 8 days. Get the schedule mechanics wrong and the wage-claim exposure is criminal under § 31-71g, not just civil: any single underpayment over $2,000 is a class D felony.

What is Connecticut’s minimum wage in 2026?

Connecticut’s state minimum wage in 2026 is $16.94 per hour, effective 1 January 2026, set by Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-58 and indexed annually to the federal Employment Cost Index under Public Act 19-4. That’s a 3.6 percent increase on 2025’s $16.35 rate, calculated against the ECI change for the 12 months ending 30 June 2025. Tipped employees sit lower: $8.23 per hour for hotel and restaurant service staff and $6.38 per hour for bartenders, with the difference made up by tips. Connecticut sits in the top tier of US state minimum wages alongside Washington, California and Massachusetts; no local city ordinance overrides it.

Three rules that follow from the wage stack:

  • Annual reset on 1 January, announced by 15 October. Public Act 19-4 obliges the CT DOL Commissioner to review the federal Employment Cost Index by 15 October each year and announce the following January’s rate at that point. The 2026 rate was confirmed by Governor Lamont in September 2025 at $16.94. Payroll engines configured against last year’s minimum without an October-November review cycle will under-pay every shift from the first January payroll.
  • Tip credit needs weekly written attestation. CT DOL requires a tipped employee to sign a weekly statement confirming that tips earned that week, combined with the cash wage, met the state minimum. The statement is not optional. A tip-credit claim without contemporaneous weekly attestations defaults to the full $16.94 owed for every hour worked, recoverable as a wage claim with potential double damages under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-72.
  • No local minimums to track. Unlike California (San Francisco $19.36, West Hollywood $19.65) or Colorado (Denver $19.29, Boulder $16.82), Connecticut preempts municipal wage ordinances. A multi-state payroll team configuring Connecticut needs the state rate only; the local-jurisdiction patchwork doesn’t bite here.

The ECI indexation cycle

Connecticut’s minimum wage moved from a legislatively-set schedule to ECI indexation in October 2023, when the schedule under Public Act 19-4 reached its $15.69 floor and switched to automatic annual adjustment. The 2024 indexation gave $15.69, 2025 gave $16.35, and 2026 gave $16.94. The mechanic is simple: take last year’s rate, apply the ECI percentage change for the 12 months ending the previous 30 June, round to the nearest cent. The 3.6 percent increase for 2026 reflects ECI wage growth running well above headline inflation, which the legislature picked deliberately so the floor would keep pace with private-sector wage trends rather than just CPI. The state floor is now the second-fastest mover in the country after Washington. When does a Connecticut employer have to pay overtime?

A Connecticut employer pays overtime at 1.5x the regular rate for any hours worked over 40 in a workweek, under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-76c. There is no daily overtime trigger, no consecutive-shift trigger, and no 7th-day premium. Connecticut overtime tracks federal FLSA verbatim, which makes it operationally simpler than Colorado (daily-12 plus consecutive-12) or California (daily-8 plus 7th-day double-time). Overtime applies to all non-exempt employees regardless of pay basis (hourly, salary, piece-rate, commission), with the regular rate calculated from all remuneration in the workweek divided by hours worked.

Exempt salary threshold

Connecticut applies the federal FLSA exempt-salary threshold without a state-level uplift. The current floor sits at $684 per week ($35,568 per year) for executive, administrative, and professional exemptions under 29 CFR Part 541. The US Department of Labor’s 2024 proposed increases were partially struck down by federal court in late 2024, and the threshold reverted to the 2019 figure. Connecticut has no state-specific exempt floor, so the federal number is the operative one. An employee paid $36,000 per year on a manager title is exempt in Connecticut on the salary basis alone, provided the duties test under Part 541 is met. Misclassification still creates wage exposure: an “exempt” employee who fails the duties test is owed overtime on every workweek over 40 hours for the 2-year claim look-back (3 years if willful). Source: 29 CFR Part 541; CT DOL Wage and Hour Division; Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-76c. Accessed 26 May 2026.

The regular-rate calculation matters

Connecticut courts have followed federal FLSA case law on the “regular rate” calculation: all non-discretionary remuneration earned in the workweek (cash wages, non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, commission, on-call pay) divided by total hours worked. A non-discretionary attendance bonus that pushes a $20-per-hour employee’s effective rate to $21 in a given week means the overtime rate for that week is $31.50, not $30. Payroll engines that calculate overtime on base rate only without recapture of bonus and commission allocations under-pay by 5-10 percent on weeks with bonus payouts. The fix is automated regular-rate recalculation at week close, not at base-rate setup.

How do Connecticut meal breaks work under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-51ii?

Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-51ii requires a 30 consecutive-minute meal break for any employee who works 7.5 consecutive hours or more. The break must be given after the first 2 hours and before the last 2 hours of the shift, and the employee must be fully relieved of duty for the period to be unpaid. There is no separate rest-break requirement under Connecticut law. The 7.5-hour trigger is stricter than the federal default (FLSA mandates no meal break at all), and Connecticut sits in a small group of about eight US states that legislate adult meal breaks. An employer that provides 30 minutes or more of total paid rest or meal time within a 7.5-hour work period satisfies the rule.

A 30-minute consecutive break has to be scheduled after hour 2 and before the last 2 hours of the shift. National payroll engines configured to the federal floor don’t auto-schedule the break or auto-deduct the time; that’s a Connecticut-specific configuration step.

What “fully relieved of duty” means in practice

The 30-minute meal break only stays unpaid if the employee is completely off the clock and free from all work obligations. A receptionist who covers the phone during a meal break is on duty and the time reverts to paid working hours. A retail associate asked to handle one customer transaction during the 30 minutes is on duty and the time reverts to paid. The reversion is automatic: payroll auto-deductions for the unpaid slot get reversed at pay-period close, and the time counts toward weekly hours worked for overtime purposes. A pattern of recorded meal breaks shorter than 30 consecutive minutes or with documented duty performed during the break is a CT DOL flag and a wage-claim risk under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-72.

The exemptions are narrow and specific

Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-51ii(c) lists six exemption pathways, and the operational ones for most employers are: the nature-of-work exemption (continuous-process operations where the worker cannot be relieved, common in healthcare and 24-hour manufacturing); the small-shift exemption (fewer than five employees on a shift at a single location); and the collective bargaining agreement exemption (a CBA may waive the meal-break rule if expressly stated). The exemption that catches employers most often is the “collective bargaining” carve-out, which only works for properly negotiated CBAs and not for individual employment agreements no matter what the contract says. A worker who signs an employment contract waiving the meal break still has a statutory right under § 31-51ii(a) because individual contractual waiver is not one of the exempted pathways. The waiver is unenforceable; the wage-claim exposure for the unpaid time is the same as if no waiver had been signed.

The 2026 warehouse layer

On 3 March 2026, Governor Lamont signed Senate Bill 298 introducing a quota-disclosure regime for warehouse distribution centres with 250 or more employees at a single Connecticut site or 1,000 or more across multiple Connecticut sites. The law takes effect 1 July 2026 and explicitly prohibits quotas that prevent compliance with the § 31-51ii meal-period rule or that interfere with bathroom use (including reasonable travel time to and from facilities). Covered employers must give a written description of any productivity quota to each current employee by 1 August 2026, with civil penalties of $1,000 for a first violation, $2,000 for a second, and $3,000 for each subsequent breach. Private right of action plus state Attorney General enforcement. Outside the warehouse sector the § 31-51ii rule operates as before; inside it, the operational lift is documentation of the quota policy and a system audit confirming the quota cannot fire during scheduled meal time. Source: Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-51ii; Public Act 25-XX (Senate Bill 298), signed 3 March 2026; CT DOL Wage and Hour. Accessed 26 May 2026.

How often must Connecticut employers pay wages?

Under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-71b, Connecticut employers must pay wages weekly or every two weeks by default, on a regular pay day designated in advance. The end of the pay period must be no more than 8 days before the corresponding payday, and if the regular pay day falls on a non-work day, payment must happen on the preceding work day. Less-frequent cadences (semi-monthly or monthly) are permitted only with express CT DOL approval on written application, and an employer can never lawfully pay less frequently than monthly. Final pay on involuntary discharge is owed by the end of the next business day under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-71c; voluntary quits are owed final wages on the next regular payday under § 31-71c(a).

Connecticut is one of only a handful of US states with a default weekly-or-bi-weekly pay frequency at the statute level. Most US states allow semi-monthly (twice a month) as a default without state approval; the Connecticut DOL maintains an application process for semi-monthly or monthly cadences and grants approvals selectively (typically for executive payrolls, professional services partnerships, or specific union-negotiated arrangements). A multi-state employer running a national semi-monthly payroll cannot simply add Connecticut headcount to that cadence without first filing the application and waiting for approval.

Five practical rules for any Connecticut payroll:

  • Bi-weekly is the operational default. Most Connecticut employers run bi-weekly (every 14 days, 26 pay periods per year) rather than weekly, because it aligns cleanly with the federal Form 941 deposit cadence and the CT DRS Form CT-941 quarterly reconciliation. Bi-weekly is fully compliant with § 31-71b without any CT DOL application.
  • Semi-monthly needs DOL approval before the first run. An employer migrating from bi-weekly to semi-monthly (twice a month) needs to file an application with the CT DOL Wage and Workplace Standards Division and receive written approval before the first semi-monthly payroll. Running semi-monthly without approval is a class D felony exposure under § 31-71g if any underpayment over $2,000 results.
  • The 8-day rule is the operative limit. A bi-weekly pay period that closes on a Saturday must pay by the following Sunday at the latest (8 days). A semi-monthly schedule running 1-15 and 16-end-of-month with payday on the 20th and 5th of the following month falls inside the 8-day window for both periods; semi-monthly schedules with longer lag are non-compliant.
  • Final pay on involuntary discharge is next-business-day. An employee terminated on Monday is owed all earned wages (including accrued vacation if the employer’s policy treats vacation as earned wages) by close of business Tuesday. The final pay rule is far stricter than most US states, where 7-day or next-regular-payday is typical for involuntary discharge. The 24-hour clock starts at separation, not at the next pay-period close.
  • Criminal exposure on payment violations. Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-71g makes any wage-payment violation a class D felony if the total unpaid wages exceed $2,000, with fines of $2,000 to $5,000 per offense plus potential imprisonment. The criminal route is rarely pursued in practice, but it sits available to the Attorney General for egregious cases and elevates the seriousness of Connecticut wage compliance compared with the civil-only regime in most states.

For multi-state employers, the operational pattern that works is: run Connecticut headcount on bi-weekly from day one, file the semi-monthly application only if a specific business reason justifies it (executive payroll, partnership distributions), and never assume the national pay-frequency cadence transfers without scrutiny. Source: Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-71b (pay frequency); § 31-71c (final pay); § 31-71g (criminal penalty); CT DOL Wage Payment Laws guidance, updated December 2023. Accessed 26 May 2026.

How should multi-state employers handle Connecticut wage-and-hour law?

Treat Connecticut as a state where the wage calculation tracks federal FLSA but the scheduling mechanics and payment rules don’t. Run Connecticut headcount on bi-weekly pay frequency by default under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-71b, configure shift schedules to auto-trigger a 30-minute meal break for any shift of 7.5 consecutive hours under § 31-51ii, and build a 24-hour final-pay process for involuntary discharges under § 31-71c. The wage rate ($16.94/hr in 2026) and overtime calculation (federal-FLSA weekly-40) won’t catch a national payroll out; the pay-cadence default, the meal-break trigger, the tipped-employee weekly attestation, and the next-business-day final-pay rule will.

Five operational rules for multi-state US employers with Connecticut headcount:

  1. Default Connecticut headcount to bi-weekly pay. A semi-monthly cadence used elsewhere in the US doesn’t transfer to Connecticut without express CT DOL approval. Bi-weekly (every 14 days) is fully compliant under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-71b and aligns with federal Form 941 deposit cadence. If business reasons require semi-monthly, file the application with the CT DOL Wage and Workplace Standards Division before the first Connecticut payroll runs and document the approval letter in the employee file.
  2. Auto-schedule meal breaks for shifts of 7.5 consecutive hours or more. Configure the time-and-attendance system to flag any Connecticut employee scheduled for 7.5 hours or more and auto-insert a 30-minute meal-break block in the middle of the shift (after the first 2 hours, before the last 2). If the time-clock records less than 30 consecutive minutes for the break, auto-reverse the unpaid deduction and flag the entry for audit. Document the meal-break offer in the schedule, not just the deduction in the wage record.
  3. Build the 24-hour final-pay process for involuntary discharges. Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-71c gives the employer until the end of the next business day after discharge to deliver all earned wages, accrued vacation, and any other owed compensation. A multi-state payroll provider running monthly off-cycle payroll runs has to be told that Connecticut involuntary discharges trigger an off-cycle payment within 24 hours. The standard “final pay on next regular payday” default is non-compliant in Connecticut for involuntary separations.
  4. Maintain weekly tip-credit attestations for any tipped employees. If the Connecticut payroll includes hotel, restaurant, or bartending staff paid at the $8.23 or $6.38 tipped minimum, the employer needs each tipped employee to sign a weekly statement confirming tips brought their effective wage to $16.94 or more. The statement is not optional and not annual; it is weekly. Missed weekly attestations default the wage claim to the full $16.94 rate for every tipped hour worked, recoverable with double damages under § 31-72.
  5. Add a Connecticut-specific addendum to the national handbook. Document the bi-weekly pay default (or semi-monthly with DOL approval letter referenced), the 7.5-hour meal-break trigger and 30-minute break process, the federal-FLSA overtime calculation (weekly-40 only), the tip-credit weekly attestation requirement, the 24-hour final-pay rule for involuntary separations, the warehouse-quota disclosure obligation if applicable, and the criminal-felony exposure under § 31-71g for wage-payment violations over $2,000.
