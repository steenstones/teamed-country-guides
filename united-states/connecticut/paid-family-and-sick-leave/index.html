Which paid-leave laws apply to Connecticut employees in 2026?

Three statutes apply: CT PFMLA (paid family and medical), CT Paid Sick Leave under Public Act 24-8, and federal FMLA at 75-plus employees within 75 miles. A single Connecticut pregnancy can trigger all three at once. The compliance question is how they stack and which employer-headcount threshold flips when.

Connecticut is one of nine US jurisdictions with both a state-mandated paid sick leave statute and a state-mandated paid family and medical leave insurance programme on the books (California, Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Washington, plus the District of Columbia). The Connecticut configuration is distinctive on two points. PFMLA is 100 percent employee-funded, with no employer matching contribution. And Connecticut Paid Sick Leave just went through its biggest expansion since the original 2011 statute, with the old "50-plus service worker" carve-out replaced by phased near-universal coverage under Public Act 24-8. A multi-state employer who only ships a generic FMLA policy into Connecticut is short on three dimensions: no paid sick leave bank, no wage-replacement framework for family and medical leave, and no familiarity with the PA 24-8 phase-in schedule that is still tightening through 2027.

The two state statutes do different work. How much paid sick leave does Connecticut require in 2026?

1 hour of paid sick leave per 30 hours worked, capped at 40 hours per year, for every Connecticut employer with 11 or more employees from 1 January 2026. The threshold drops to every employer regardless of size on 1 January 2027. Public Act 24-8 replaced the old "50-plus service worker" rule with phased near-universal coverage. The accrual rule is the floor; an employer may front-load 40 hours at the start of each benefit year and skip accrual tracking, which is the cleaner administration path for salaried workforces.

Connecticut's original 2011 paid sick leave statute (the first state-level mandate in the United States) only applied to employers with 50 or more "service workers" in specific narrow occupational categories. Public Act 24-8, signed by Governor Lamont in May 2024, removed that carve-out and rebuilt the coverage on a three-step phase-in calendar tied to employer headcount.

The PA 24-8 coverage phase-in

  • 1 January 2025: coverage expanded from 50-plus "service workers" to every employer with 25 or more employees across all occupations.
  • 1 January 2026: threshold dropped to 11 or more employees. This is the live threshold for the 2026 compliance year.
  • 1 January 2027: threshold drops to every employer regardless of size. Connecticut becomes a near-universal-coverage sick-leave state, joining California, Colorado, New Mexico, and a handful of others.

Accrual, cap, carryover

  • Accrual rate: 1 hour for every 30 hours worked, beginning on the first day of employment. No probationary delay.
  • Annual cap: 40 hours per benefit year. Once the employee has accrued 40 hours, accrual pauses until they use some.
  • Carryover: unused hours carry over year to year, but the cap on the accrued balance remains 40 hours. The employee never holds more than 40 paid sick hours at any moment under the base programme.
  • Front-loading alternative: the employer may grant 40 hours at the start of each benefit year and skip accrual tracking. Front-loaded leave does not need to carry over, which simplifies administration but the employee's mid-year balance can be higher than under accrual.
  • Pay-out at separation: Connecticut does not require pay-out of unused paid sick leave at termination. Unused hours are forfeited unless the employer's policy or PTO bank rules say otherwise.

What Connecticut Paid Sick Leave covers

  • Employee's own mental or physical illness, injury, or health condition
  • Diagnosis, treatment, or preventive care for the employee or a family member
  • Care for a family member with an illness, injury, or health condition
  • Mental health wellness days, including therapy, counselling, or grief-related needs (added by PA 24-8 amendments)
  • Bereavement, funeral arrangements, and related needs of a deceased family member (added by PA 24-8 amendments)
  • Safe leave related to family violence, sexual assault, or stalking, for the employee or a family member
  • Closure of the employee's workplace or a family member's school or place of care by order of a public official due to a public health emergency

The seasonal-worker carve-out

Employees who work fewer than 120 days in a calendar year are exempt from Connecticut Paid Sick Leave under PA 24-8. The carve-out is narrow and matters mostly for genuine summer-season and short-term-project workers; standard part-time employees who work a regular schedule across the calendar year are covered. The 120-day test runs by calendar year, not by employer-tenure year.

Written notice and recordkeeping

Employers must provide written notice of paid sick leave rights to each employee by 1 January 2025, or at the date of hire for any employee starting after that date. Records of hours accrued and hours used must be retained for three years. The CT DOL Wage and Workplace Standards Division is the enforcement body; employers who fail to provide accrued sick leave or who retaliate against an employee for using it face civil penalties plus the employee's private right of action.

What is Connecticut Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFMLA) and who pays it?

Connecticut Paid Leave (CT PFML / PFMLA) gives eligible workers up to 12 weeks of paid family or medical leave per 12-month period, with an additional 2 weeks for pregnancy incapacitation, at a maximum weekly benefit of $1,016.40 in 2026. It is funded by a 0.5 percent employee-paid payroll contribution on wages up to the federal Social Security wage base of $184,500, capped at $922.50 per employee per year. Connecticut is the only US state where the paid family and medical leave programme is funded 100 percent by employee contributions; there is no employer match.

Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-49e et seq. created the Connecticut Paid Leave programme. Contributions began on 1 January 2021; benefits became claimable on 1 January 2022. The programme is administered by the Connecticut Paid Leave Authority (CTPLA) in partnership with Aflac as the third-party claim administrator. As of 30 April 2026, CT Paid Leave has approved more than 302,000 applications and distributed more than $1.65 billion in benefits to Connecticut workers since launch.

The five qualifying reasons

  • Bonding with a new child by birth, adoption, or placement in care, within 12 months of arrival
  • Family care for a seriously ill family member (Connecticut uses an expansive definition that covers spouse, domestic partner, child, parent, parent-in-law, grandparent, grandchild, sibling, and any person whose close association with the employee is the equivalent of a family relationship)
  • Own serious health condition, including pregnancy and childbirth recovery, plus the additional 2-week extension for medically certified pregnancy incapacitation
  • Military qualifying exigency arising from a family member's call to active military duty
  • Safe leave for the employee or a family member subject to family violence or sexual assault

Wage replacement: progressive, capped, paid by the state

The PFMLA wage-replacement formula is progressive. An employee receives 95 percent of their base weekly earnings up to 40 times the Connecticut minimum wage ($16.94 per hour in 2026, so 40 × $16.94 = $677.60 a week), and then 60 percent of base weekly earnings above that floor, up to the cap of 60 times the state minimum wage ($1,016.40 a week in 2026). The maximum weekly benefit was $981.00 in 2025 and rises with each Connecticut minimum wage step. The cap is recalculated annually by the CT Paid Leave Authority; check the Authority's current rate notice before publishing the figure to candidates.

Eligibility

An employee qualifies for the PFMLA wage-replacement benefit if they have earned at least $2,325 in subject earnings during the highest-earning quarter of the base period and are currently employed (or have been employed in the previous 12 weeks) with a Connecticut employer. The base period is the first four of the five most recently completed calendar quarters before the benefit-application date. Most full-time and substantial part-time Connecticut workers clear the threshold within their first few months on payroll. The wage-replacement-eligibility test is separate from the job-protection threshold tied to the Connecticut Family and Medical Leave Act, which mirrors the federal FMLA 12-month service / 1,000-hour test.

The "employee pays it all" structure

Unlike Massachusetts (where the employer pays approximately 60 percent of the contribution on the medical-leave side) or California SDI / PFL (which is technically a combined employee-paid line but with separate state-disability and paid-family components), Connecticut Paid Leave is funded entirely by employee contributions with zero employer matching obligation. The 0.5 percent shows up as a separate line on the payslip and reduces gross-to-net for every Connecticut hire from the first paycheque. For a $200,000 Connecticut employee, the annual PFMLA contribution is capped at $922.50 because the Social Security wage base of $184,500 sits below the salary. For an $80,000 employee, it is $400 a year. The employer is the withholding agent, remitting quarterly to the CT Paid Leave Authority, but bears no programme cost. This is structurally unique in the United States.

How a PFMLA claim runs operationally

An employee files a claim through the CT Paid Leave portal at ctpaidleave.org, providing medical or family-event documentation appropriate to the qualifying reason. Aflac, the third-party administrator, adjudicates the claim and issues benefit payments directly to the employee's bank account. The employer's payroll obligation during a PFMLA absence is benefit continuation only; the state insurance fund handles the wage replacement, so the employer's cash flow during a leave is unaffected except for any voluntary employer top-up policy. The employer is notified of the claim and confirms the employee's wage history and dates of employment, but does not adjudicate or deny the claim.

PFMLA contribution mechanics, the quarterly remittance schedule, and the interaction with state income tax withholding are also covered on the sibling Connecticut state income tax and unemployment insurance page. Cross-link rather than re-explain.

How did Public Act 24-8 change Connecticut Paid Sick Leave?

Public Act 24-8 ended the old 50-plus service-worker carve-out and replaced it with phased near-universal coverage of every Connecticut occupation. Coverage now hits 25-plus employees on 1 January 2025, 11-plus employees on 1 January 2026, and every employer regardless of size on 1 January 2027. The Act also added bereavement, mental health, and family-care reasons; required written notice to employees; and set a three-year recordkeeping rule. Most competitor content still cites the pre-2025 framework and is materially out of date.

Signed by Governor Lamont on 21 May 2024, PA 24-8 is the most significant rework of Connecticut paid sick leave since the original 2011 statute. The old law was narrow: it applied only to employers with 50 or more "service workers" in a defined list of low-wage retail and service occupations (cooks, retail clerks, hairdressers, hotel staff, security guards, and similar). White-collar professionals, technology workers, and most office staff were not covered, and small employers in any sector were exempt. The new framework removes both carve-outs and re-anchors coverage on employer headcount alone.

What "11 or more employees" actually means in 2026

The 11-employee threshold for the 2026 compliance year is counted at the Connecticut-employee level, not the global headcount. A 5,000-person multinational with 8 Connecticut employees is not covered in 2026 (the threshold has not been triggered for Connecticut specifically), but the same employer becomes covered on 1 January 2027 when the all-employers rule activates. A 12-person Connecticut employer that grows to 11 employees mid-year becomes covered the following 1 January, not retroactively. The headcount is measured by the average number of employees worked per week during the calendar year, based on payroll records.

Mental health and bereavement, the 2024 additions

PA 24-8 explicitly added mental health wellness days and bereavement leave to the list of covered uses. An employee can use accrued paid sick leave for a therapy appointment, a grief-related day off after a family death, or a counselling session, without needing to characterise it as a physical-illness day. The shift recognises mental health absences as legitimate uses of the sick-leave bank and aligns Connecticut with the broader trend in US state paid-leave law (California, Colorado, Illinois, and Maryland have made similar amendments since 2023). It also reduces the friction of using sick leave for absences that previously felt awkward to label.

The written-notice requirement

Every Connecticut employer covered by the statute must give each employee written notice of their paid sick leave rights, either at the date of hire or by 1 January 2025 (whichever is later). The notice must explain the accrual rate, the annual cap, the qualifying reasons, the carryover rule, and the prohibition on retaliation. The CT DOL Wage and Workplace Standards Division publishes a model notice that employers can adopt verbatim or adapt. Failure to provide the notice is a separate statutory violation from any failure to provide the leave itself, with its own civil penalty.

How do Connecticut Paid Sick Leave, PFMLA, and FMLA stack for a single pregnancy?

At a Connecticut employer with 75-plus employees and a 12-month-tenure employee with a complicated pregnancy, the paid and protected leave stack reaches roughly 14 weeks of state wage-replacement plus 40 hours of paid sick time plus the federal FMLA backdrop. Connecticut Paid Sick Leave covers the early appointments and acute episodes, PFMLA provides 12 weeks (14 with pregnancy incapacitation) of paid leave, and federal FMLA runs concurrently with PFMLA where both apply. Knowing how the clocks layer is the difference between competitive policy and an avoidable complaint to the CT Paid Leave Authority.

Three statutes, three eligibility tests, three duration clocks. The interaction is the compliance work, and most multi-state employers ship a one-page leave policy that does not account for the Connecticut-specific stacking pattern.

The four phases of a complicated pregnancy in Connecticut

  1. Prenatal appointments and early-pregnancy episodes (months 1-6): Connecticut Paid Sick Leave covers the routine prenatal-visit half-days, the early-trimester nausea episodes, the urgent-care visits. The 40-hour bank is typically enough for an uncomplicated pregnancy; it begins to feel tight in the second trimester for an employee with significant symptoms.
  2. Bedrest or hospitalisation before delivery (variable, e.g. weeks 28-40): if a healthcare provider certifies the employee as medically unable to perform job functions, the PFMLA medical-leave clock starts. For a complicated pregnancy, the 12-week base plus 2-week incapacitation extension creates a 14-week PFMLA ceiling. At a 75-plus-employee employer (the Connecticut FMLA threshold), federal FMLA runs concurrently with PFMLA for the first 12 of those weeks, exhausting the federal entitlement in parallel.
  3. Delivery and immediate postpartum recovery (weeks 40-46): the medical-leave portion of PFMLA continues to run, covering the medically certified recovery period. Paid Sick Leave remains available for episode-by-episode use as needed. The 14-week PFMLA cap may or may not be reached depending on the medical course of the pregnancy.
  4. Bonding leave (post-recovery): if PFMLA weeks remain after medical recovery, they are available for bonding within the 12-month window from the qualifying event. If FMLA was exhausted concurrently with PFMLA medical leave, there is no separate FMLA-bonding entitlement left; the federal 12-week clock is one bank, not two. State-funded bonding through PFMLA continues to the 12-week PFMLA cap.

The maximum-stack scenario

An employer with 80 Connecticut employees, an employee with 18 months of tenure, certified pregnancy incapacitation, and the full 14-week PFMLA extension. Roughly: 40 hours of Paid Sick Leave used episodically across the first two trimesters, then 14 weeks of PFMLA (12 of which run concurrently with FMLA) for medical leave and bonding combined. Total protected and largely paid leave: roughly 14 weeks of PFMLA at up to $1,016.40 per week, plus the 40-hour sick bank, against a federal FMLA backdrop that the PFMLA period exhausts in parallel. The employer's payroll obligation during the PFMLA period is benefit continuation only; the CT Paid Leave Authority pays the wages.

The CT FMLA overlay (often missed)

Connecticut has its own state-level FMLA statute (Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-51kk et seq.) that overlays federal FMLA at slightly different thresholds. The Connecticut FMLA applies to employers with 75 or more employees (versus 50 for federal FMLA) and uses a 1,000-hour-of-service eligibility test (versus 1,250 hours federal). The job-protected leave entitlement under the Connecticut statute is 16 weeks in a 24-month period, versus 12 weeks in a 12-month period under federal FMLA. CT FMLA runs alongside PFMLA where both apply and provides the job-protection right that PFMLA itself does not embed. The PFMLA pays the wages; the CT FMLA protects the job.

Can a Connecticut employer offer a private PFMLA plan instead?

Yes. An employer may opt out of state PFMLA by offering a private plan approved by the Connecticut Paid Leave Authority that equals or exceeds every state-plan benefit on every dimension. The private plan must demonstrate parity on benefit duration, weekly maximum, qualifying reasons, employee-cost share, and grievance process. Approval is required from the CTPLA; renewal applies on an annual cycle. The opt-out structure is identical to the New York PFL private-plan model. For most Connecticut employers the state plan wins on total cost of administration.

Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-49n governs the opt-out. An employer who wants to skip the state programme must file a private-plan application with the CTPLA, demonstrating that the private plan's benefits, eligibility, employee-cost share, and grievance process meet or exceed the state-plan equivalent on every line. Plans typically take the form of fully insured policies underwritten by carriers that have built CT PFMLA-compliant products (Aflac itself writes private-plan policies, as do MetLife, The Hartford, Unum, and Symetra as of 2026).

The opt-out cost trade-off

  • State plan: 0.5 percent of wages up to the Social Security wage base, capped at $922.50 per employee per year. Funded entirely by the employee. Fixed rate, no underwriting, no claims-experience volatility. The employer's only cost is administrative (withholding, quarterly remittance, claim coordination).
  • Private plan: underwritten premium based on the employer's demographic and claims profile. For a young, healthy workforce with low expected claim volume, the underwritten employee-contribution rate can come in below 0.5 percent. For an older workforce or one with high expected pregnancy or serious-health-condition exposure, the underwritten rate can come in higher, and the employer typically still cannot exceed the state-plan employee contribution.
  • Administrative load: private plan requires the carrier's claim-intake process, the employer's coordination with the carrier for medical certification and reinstatement, and the annual reapproval filing with the CTPLA. State plan requires only quarterly contribution remittance through the CT Paid Leave employer portal and confirmation of wage history when an employee files a claim.
  • Employee-experience risk: employees compare a private-plan claim experience against the state plan their friends at other Connecticut employers are using. Slower or more restrictive claim adjudication on the private side becomes a recruiting friction.

Annual reapproval discipline

Private-plan approval is annual. Each year the employer must file a renewal application demonstrating that the plan remains compliant with the current state-plan benefits, which can change as the CTPLA updates the maximum weekly benefit or the wage-replacement formula. Failure to renew on time pushes the employer back into the state plan and triggers premium-collection back to the date of lapsed approval. Build the renewal calendar into the payroll year early.

When the private-plan route makes sense

The private-plan route works best for larger, stable Connecticut workforces (100-plus employees) with a multi-year track record of below-average claim incidence and the operational capacity to manage a carrier relationship. For smaller employers, the state plan's simplicity and fixed rate almost always wins on total cost of administration. Multi-state employers running Connecticut as one piece of a larger US payroll typically use the state plan to avoid building Connecticut-specific carrier-administration tooling. As of April 2026, approximately 150,000 Connecticut employers are registered with the state plan against a small number of approved private plans.

The PFMLA 0.5 percent is straightforward as a payroll line; the real work is the PA 24-8 written-notice obligation, the 11-plus-employee Paid Sick Leave threshold for 2026 that broadens again to every employer in 2027, and the PFMLA-CT FMLA concurrency on complicated pregnancies. We capture the headcount-and-tenure tracker on the employee record so the right threshold surfaces on the leave-decision screen, not on the quarter close.
A note from Tom Price-Daniel

Connecticut paid leave is the most employee-funded and the most policy-mobile in the country.
0.5 percent PFMLA with no employer match, Public Act 24-8 phasing in to universal sick-leave coverage by 1 January 2027, and a CT FMLA overlay most multi-state employers do not know exists.
The discipline is tracking the 11-plus-employee threshold for 2026, the PFMLA wage-base cap mid-year for high earners, and the CT FMLA 1,000-hour job-protection test, all on one employee record, every payroll close.
