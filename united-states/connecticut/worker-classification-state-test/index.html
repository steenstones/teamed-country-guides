How does Connecticut’s ABC test for worker classification work in 2026?

Connecticut runs three different worker-classification tests under three different agencies. The ABC test under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii) controls unemployment insurance. Workers’ compensation runs on a multi-factor right-to-control test under § 31-275. State income tax withholding follows the federal IRS common-law factors. The same worker can be an employee for UI and a contractor for income tax. Prong B is the structural risk, and Connecticut’s Prong B is disjunctive: EITHER outside the usual course of business OR outside all the places of business of the enterprise.

Stat. § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii), administered by the Connecticut Department of Labor. Workers’ compensation applies a separate multi-factor right-to-control test under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-275, administered by the Workers’ Compensation Commission. State income tax withholding follows the federal IRS common-law / 20-factor test for W-2 versus 1099 issuance, administered by the Connecticut Department of Revenue Services. The same worker can be reclassified by one agency and not another. The structural risk sits in Prong B of the UI test: the worker must perform service either outside the usual course of the employer’s business OR outside all the places of business of the enterprise. Connecticut’s disjunctive Prong B is more employer-friendly than California’s strict conjunctive Prong B under Cal. Lab Code § 2775. Misclassification under the UI test exposes the employer to back contributions, fund solvency tax, 1.0 percent per month interest, and civil penalties under § 31-225a, on a four-year limitations period per § 31-273. Which worker classification test does Connecticut use?

Three tests, three agencies. The ABC test under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii) applies to unemployment insurance. A separate multi-factor right-to-control test under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-275 applies to workers’ compensation. The federal IRS common-law / 20-factor test applies to state income tax withholding through the Department of Revenue Services.

Connecticut sits in the strict-ABC family on unemployment insurance, alongside California and Massachusetts, but it is the only state in that family to use a disjunctive Prong B. The state moved into the ABC family decades before California codified AB5 in 2019: the ABC structure has been the UI standard in Connecticut since the mid-twentieth century unemployment compensation framework, reflecting a long-standing policy choice to make workers presumptively employees for the UI safety net while leaving the income tax and workers’ comp analyses on other tests.

What are the three prongs of Connecticut’s ABC test?

All three prongs must be met for contractor status under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii). Prong A: the worker is free from direction and control in performing the service. Prong B: the service is performed either outside the usual course of the employer’s business OR outside all the places of business of the enterprise. Prong C: the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade of the same nature as the service performed.

ProngStatutory text (paraphrased from § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii))What it actually tests
A The worker is free from direction and control in the performance of the service, both under the contract of hire and in fact. Who controls how the work is done. Both the written contract AND the day-to-day reality have to read ‘independent’.
B The service is performed either outside the usual course of the business for which the service is performed OR outside all the places of business of the enterprise. The disjunctive prong. Connecticut allows EITHER condition to satisfy B, unlike California which demands ‘outside the usual course’ on its own.
C The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business of the same nature as the service performed. Real, ongoing, public-facing business of their own. Other clients, marketing, business licence, separate insurance.

Why Connecticut’s disjunctive Prong B matters

Connecticut’s Prong B is the structural difference between this state and California. The California version of Prong B (Cal. Lab Code § 2775(b)(1)(B)) requires the work to be outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business, full stop. The Connecticut version allows the same contractor relationship to satisfy Prong B by being performed either outside the usual course or outside all the places of business of the enterprise. A software developer hired by a software company in California fails Prong B every time. The same engagement in Connecticut may satisfy Prong B if the developer works from a separate office, their own home studio, or a coworking space that is not one of the employer’s places of business. The CT DOL Unemployment Compensation Audit Section reads the ‘places of business’ clause narrowly, focusing on whether the employer maintains any physical premises where the contractor regularly performs the service.

The corollary is that fully-remote contractor engagements have a structurally easier path through Connecticut’s Prong B than they do through California’s. The narrow path still requires Prongs A and C to hold: freedom from direction and control in fact (not just on paper), and a customarily independent trade with other clients, marketing, and its own business presence. Failing A or C still reclassifies the worker as a CT UI employee regardless of how clearly Prong B is satisfied. Source: Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii); CT DOL Knowledge Base on Employer Employee Relationship, accessed 26 May 2026.

03 Three Prongs, Two Routes

Connecticut’s Prong B is disjunctive. The contractor passes B by working EITHER outside the usual course of the employer’s business OR outside all of its places of business. Fully-remote contractor engagements have a structural path through Prong B that California closes off. Prongs A and C still have to hold, and the burden of proof on all three sits on the hiring entity.

Can the same worker be classified differently across CT DOL, CT WCC, and CT DRS?

Yes. Connecticut’s three-agency structure is the headline trap for multi-state employers. The Department of Labor applies the ABC test for UI. The Workers’ Compensation Commission applies a multi-factor right-to-control test under § 31-275. The Department of Revenue Services aligns with federal IRS common-law factors for W-2 versus 1099 status. A worker can be a contractor for income tax, an employee for UI, and either for workers’ comp depending on the facts.

The three-agency divergence is not a theoretical curiosity. It is the day-to-day reality of how Connecticut audits and enforces classification. Most multi-state employers think of classification as a single decision driven by the IRS factors. In Connecticut, classification is three decisions on three tests, each enforced by a separate agency with separate audit triggers and separate exposure.

The DOL reclassification that triggers nothing else

A common pattern: the CT DOL Unemployment Compensation Audit Section reclassifies a contractor as an employee for UI purposes after a benefits claim. The same worker continues to file taxes as a 1099 contractor with the CT DRS, because the DRS follows federal common-law factors and the worker passed the IRS analysis. The employer ends up owing four years of back UI contributions to one agency and zero back PIT withholding to another, on the same engagement. The Workers’ Compensation Commission may never look at the engagement at all, unless the worker is injured on the job. The three-agency structure means a classification call has to be made three times, once on each test, and the worst-case exposure stacks across the three agencies if all three reclassify. Source: Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 31-222, 31-275, 31-225a; CT DOL Knowledge Base, accessed 26 May 2026.

How does Connecticut’s ABC test compare to California, Massachusetts, and other states?

Connecticut sits in the strict-ABC family for UI alongside California and Massachusetts but uses a more employer-friendly disjunctive Prong B. Most other US states (Colorado, Arkansas, Arizona, Alabama, New York, Texas, Florida) apply the IRS common-law test for UI and never trigger ABC analysis at all. The CT structure is closest to New Jersey on the disjunctive reading.

The US worker classification map is structurally three groups for state unemployment insurance. The strict ABC group (California, Massachusetts, and a handful of others) presumes employment and demands proof of all three prongs. The common-law group (most of the US, including Colorado, Arkansas, Arizona, Alabama, New York, Texas, Florida, Illinois) applies the IRS multi-factor analysis and reaches probabilistic answers. Connecticut occupies a middle position: ABC structure with all three prongs required, but with a disjunctive Prong B that is meaningfully wider than California’s.

The conversion trap for multi-state employers

A familiar audit pattern: a software company in Texas hires a contractor developer, renews the engagement for three years, then opens a remote-first team and re-engages the same person at a Connecticut address. The Texas engagement passes the IRS 20-factor analysis comfortably. The Connecticut re-engagement triggers ABC analysis at CT DOL the moment the worker files for unemployment after the contract ends. Prong A and Prong C are usually defensible if the contractor genuinely runs an independent business. Prong B turns on whether the developer regularly performs the service at one of the employer’s places of business. If the employer has no Connecticut office and the developer works from home, Prong B’s ‘outside all places of business’ clause is the route through. If the employer has a Hartford or Stamford office and the developer regularly works there, Prong B fails on both readings and the engagement is an employee for UI from day one.

Connecticut’s disjunctive Prong B is a real difference that affects the structural outcome, but it does not turn the state into a contractor playground. The ABC framework still presumes employment and still asks the hiring entity to prove all three prongs. The federal US DOL final rule on independent contractor classification under FLSA, effective 11 March 2024, layers a separate economic-reality analysis on top for federal wage-and-hour purposes; that test runs even when state ABC analysis succeeds. Source: Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222; Cal. Lab Code § 2775; Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 149 § 148B; US DOL final rule, accessed 26 May 2026.

What does Connecticut UI misclassification actually cost?

Stacked liability under three statutes. A misclassified $80,000-per-year Connecticut worker, reclassified by CT DOL after three years on 1099, exposes the hiring entity to back UI contributions on the first $27,000 of wages each year, the 1.0 percent fund solvency tax, 1.0 percent per month interest, and civil penalties under § 31-225a, on a four-year lookback per § 31-273. Add back state PIT withholding via CT DRS if DRS also reclassifies, plus workers’ comp gap under the WCC right-to-control test, plus federal FLSA exposure under the US DOL 2024 final rule.

Connecticut stacks misclassification liability more narrowly than California because the strict statutory civil penalty under § 31-225a covers failure to register and pay UI taxes, with interest and back contributions, rather than the open-ended PAGA-style private-plaintiff structure California uses. Why the four-year lookback is the headline number

The CT DOL Unemployment Compensation Audit Section reaches back four years per Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-273 to recover back contributions, fund solvency tax, interest, and civil penalties. The lookback runs from the date of audit, not the date of the original misclassification. Most audits begin after a former contractor files for unemployment and cannot collect, so the typical timeline is two to four years of accumulated exposure by the time the audit notice arrives. A small Connecticut team of four contractors at $80,000 each, audited after the third year, stacks into a $25,000 to $50,000 audit assessment before interest and penalty. Civil penalty escalation under § 31-225a for wilful misclassification or repeat findings adds material exposure on top.

Plaintiffs' firms in Connecticut do not have a PAGA equivalent to convert misclassification into a private class action with statutory penalties. Federal FLSA class actions are the parallel private-plaintiff path: misclassified workers can sue for unpaid overtime and minimum-wage gap under the economic-reality test post-2024, with liquidated damages and attorney’s fees. CT DOL audits and federal FLSA suits move on separate tracks but both reach back over the same misclassification window. Source: Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 31-225a, 31-273; US DOL final rule on independent contractor classification under FLSA, effective 11 March 2024.

How does the 2024 federal US DOL contractor rule interact with Connecticut’s ABC test?

The US DOL final rule on independent contractor classification under FLSA, effective 11 March 2024, applies an economic-reality test for federal wage-and-hour purposes. It does not override Connecticut’s state ABC test for UI. Federal FLSA reads economic-reality factors; Connecticut UI still demands proof of all three ABC prongs. A worker can satisfy one and fail the other.

The 2024 federal rule replaced the prior employer-friendlier 2021 rule and returned the FLSA classification analysis to the multi-factor economic-reality test the courts had applied for decades. Six factors are weighed, none controlling: opportunity for profit or loss, investments by the worker and the employer, degree of permanence of the work relationship, nature and degree of control, whether the work performed is integral to the employer’s business, and skill and initiative. The test reaches a probabilistic answer balanced across the six factors.

For a Connecticut engagement, the federal economic-reality test runs in parallel to the state ABC test. A contractor who satisfies the economic-reality test for federal FLSA purposes (no overtime entitlement, no minimum-wage protection under federal law) can still fail Connecticut’s ABC test for state UI purposes if Prong B does not hold. The reverse pattern (passes Connecticut ABC, fails federal economic-reality) is rarer because the Connecticut analysis demands all three prongs and the federal analysis weighs factors, but it is structurally possible. The two tests apply to two different statutes (FLSA wage-and-hour versus CT unemployment compensation) and are enforced by two different agencies (US DOL Wage and Hour Division versus CT DOL Unemployment Compensation Audit Section). One test passing does not insulate against the other.

The audit pattern most multi-state employers walk into

A pattern that recurs in 2026 audits: an employer relies on the 2024 economic-reality test to defend a federal FLSA position for a remote Connecticut contractor, and assumes the same analysis covers state UI. The CT DOL Unemployment Compensation Audit Section opens an audit, applies the ABC test, and reclassifies on Prong B if the contractor regularly worked at a Connecticut location of the employer. The economic-reality analysis does not appear in the CT DOL audit file. The Connecticut answer is governed entirely by § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii), not by federal regulations. Treat the two tests as separate exposures and run both at the contract stage. Source: US DOL final rule on independent contractor classification under FLSA, effective 11 March 2024; Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222.
