If you send an out-of-state employee into Alabama for what you think is a quick project, day 31 of attendance retroactively triggers Alabama withholding back to day 1. Not just from day 31. The full year.

On a $90,000 salaried hire, that is roughly $3,500 of back-withholding plus penalties and interest, all owed by the employer who missed the count. From 1 January 2026.

Most US employers look at Alabama’s headline numbers (2/4/5 percent income tax, $216 SUTA cap per employee) and assume the state is a soft landing compared to California. The headline rates are soft. The mechanics are not.

This page covers the income-tax bands and federal-tax deduction quirk, the SUTA base rate plus the assessments that stack on top, the A-4 / A-6 / A-1 / A-3 filing cadence, and the 30-day nonresident safe harbour that bites on day 31.

What is Alabama’s state income tax in 2026?

You withhold Alabama income tax in three bands: 2 percent on the first $500, 4 percent on the next $2,500, and 5 percent above $3,000 for a single filer. Married-filing-jointly doubles the dollar thresholds.

Because the top band starts at $3,000, almost every full-time hire hits the 5 percent marginal rate on their first paycheque.

Marcus is a developer at a Birmingham startup, base $95,000. The headline reads 5 percent. The cash impact is closer to 3.5 to 4 percent, because Alabama lets him deduct his federal income tax paid from his state taxable income. That deduction is the quiet feature that makes Alabama’s rates feel lower than the chart suggests.

The federal-tax deduction quirk

Alabama is one of a handful of states that lets employees deduct federal income tax paid from their state taxable income. The deduction softens the headline 5 percent rate meaningfully for higher earners.

SUTA: how Alabama’s base rate hides the real cost

As a new Alabama employer you pay 2.7 percent SUTA on the first $8,000 of each employee’s annual wages, capped at $216 per employee per year.

After two full calendar years of measurable experience, your actual cost lands in the 0.20 to 6.80 percent band. That is the State’s base contribution rate plus special and shared-cost assessments stacked on top.

Latoya is a sales rep based out of Huntsville. Year one, her employer pays $216 of SUTA on her wages. Year three, if the company has filed three claims from former employees, that same SUTA line could read $544. Same employee. Different reserve ratio.

Alabama mails an annual rate notice by 31 January each year. The rate is set by the employer’s reserve ratio, the historical balance of contributions paid in versus benefits paid out to former employees. A high-turnover employer drifts toward the top of the band. A stable employer with few claims settles near the floor.

Special and shared-cost assessments

The State’s published 0.59 to 6.19 percent is the base contribution rate. Alabama layers special assessments and shared-cost assessments on top year by year. For forecasting actual loaded payroll cost, plan on the 0.20 to 6.80 percent stacked range. That is what you remit in full.

Teamed books the assessment delta as a line item on the invoice, so the difference between base and stacked rate is auditable, not buried.

FUTA credit interaction

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) is 6 percent on the first $7,000. An Alabama employer that pays state SUTA in full and on time receives a FUTA credit of 5.4 percent, leaving an effective FUTA of 0.6 percent ($42 per employee per year).

Late or partial SUTA payment erodes the credit. Teamed books both lines together so the credit never gets lost.

Form A-4: every Alabama new hire fills one in

Form A-4 is Alabama’s Employee’s Withholding Exemption Certificate, the state equivalent of the federal W-4. Every new Alabama hire completes one on day one.

The current version (A4_0425) was revised April 2025. A Spanish version is available. Employees file a revised A-4 within 10 days of any decrease in exemptions.

When Marcus joins the Birmingham startup, he signs his A-4 digitally on day one. The form sets his withholding from his first paycheque. If he gets married six months in, he files a fresh A-4 to update the rate. There is no annual refresh requirement otherwise.

A-4 captures the data Alabama needs to set the right withholding rate from day one:

  • Filing status: single, married filing jointly, married filing separately, or head of family.
  • Personal exemptions claimed, which lower the withholding base.
  • Dependents, which Alabama treats separately from personal exemptions.
  • Additional withholding, for employees who want a flat dollar amount held back on top of the calculated rate.

The form stays on file with the employer until the employee submits a revised one. Divorce, a death in the family, or someone else claiming a dependent all trigger an update. Outside those events, no refresh is required.

Teamed’s onboarding flow collects Form A-4 alongside Form I-9 and the federal W-4 in a single digital signing session, then files the data straight into payroll. The employee never sees a paper form.

How does an Alabama employer file withholding (A-1, A-6, A-3)?

Three forms cover the withholding lifecycle: Form A-6 monthly if you withheld more than $1,000 in either the first or second month of a quarter, otherwise Form A-1 quarterly. Form A-3 reconciles the full tax year and is due 31 January.

All filings run through My Alabama Taxes (MAT) at myalabamataxes.alabama.gov.

The Birmingham startup employing Marcus crosses the $1,000 threshold by month two. From that point it files Form A-6 monthly, due the 15th of the following month. A smaller employer with two part-time hires might stay on Form A-1 quarterly for years.

Electronic-filing thresholds

Any single payment of $750 or more must be filed electronically through MAT. Employers submitting 10 or more W-2s (or 1099s with Alabama withholding) must e-file the W-2 set together with Form A-3 by 31 January of the following year. A paper W-2 batch above the threshold is rejected outright.

Zero-filing rule

A quarterly filer with zero withholding for the period still files a zero Form A-1, otherwise the system flags a missing return and starts the delinquency clock. A monthly filer with a zero month doesn’t file that month.

The 30-day safe harbour catches every out-of-state employer

From 1 January 2026, an out-of-state employee working in Alabama for 30 or fewer days in a calendar year is exempt from Alabama income tax withholding.

Day 31 retroactively triggers withholding back to day 1 for the full calendar year. The home state has to offer a similar exclusion or have no income tax for the safe harbour to apply at all.

Jamal is a remote worker in Mobile, payrolled out of his employer’s Atlanta office. Two-day site visits each month sit comfortably inside the 30-day window. A six-week project on the waterfront breaks it, and the employer owes Alabama tax on all of Jamal’s 2026 Alabama work, not just the 12 days after day 30.

Three conditions all have to be true for the safe harbour:

  1. The nonresident performs duties in Alabama for 30 or fewer days during the calendar year.
  2. The nonresident performs employment duties in more than one state during the calendar year.
  3. The nonresident’s home state has a similar exclusion, has no income tax, or the income is exempt for federal purposes.

Professional athletes, professional entertainers, and public figures are excluded from the safe harbour by name. Day 31 triggers full retroactive Alabama withholding plus an employer obligation to file the missed A-6 or A-1 returns. Track the day count from arrival, not from when work starts.

Employer protection if you get the count wrong

The act includes a penalty and interest waiver for employers who relied in good faith on either a time-and-attendance system that tracks employee location daily, or the employee’s annual written estimate of Alabama days.

The waiver doesn’t cover collusion or actual knowledge of fraud. Teamed’s payroll keeps a daily location ledger for every multi-state hire so the waiver always applies.

The 2026 overtime change: $1,000 deduction, not a payroll exclusion

The 2024 full-exclusion experiment ended 30 June 2025. From tax year 2026 to 2028, Alabama allows employees a $1,000 individual deduction for qualified overtime compensation on their personal return (Act 2026-604).

It is a deduction the employee claims on Form 40 the following spring. It is not an employer-side exclusion. Payroll withholding on overtime continues at the normal marginal rate.

Marcus works ten hours of overtime at the Birmingham startup. Payroll calculates his federal FLSA overtime at time-and-a-half, withholds Alabama tax on the full overtime line, and pays him net. When he files his 2026 Form 40, he deducts up to $1,000 of that overtime from his taxable income and gets a refund on the difference.

The headline difference matters for payroll, not just for the personal return:

  • Old rule (Oct 2024 to Jun 2025, expired): Act 2024-437. Overtime wages excluded from Alabama gross income at source. Payroll withheld zero state tax on the overtime portion. Employer reported aggregate exempt-overtime totals on each A-6 and A-1, with employee counts. W-2 Box 14 showed “EX OT WAGES”.
  • New rule (tax years 2026 to 2028): Act 2026-604. Employees deduct up to $1,000 of qualified overtime on their individual return. Payroll continues to withhold Alabama income tax on the full overtime wage. No special employer reporting requirement on A-6 or A-1.

Don’t under-withhold by analogy to the old rule. The overtime line on the payslip is taxable to Alabama at the normal marginal rate. Employees recover the deduction when they file the following spring.

Federal Fair Labor Standards Act still defines ‘overtime’

Alabama follows the federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) definition: non-exempt employees earn 1.5 times regular rate for hours over 40 in a workweek. Alabama does not add a state-level overtime premium or a daily-overtime rule. The state deduction keys to the FLSA-calculated overtime portion, not a workweek-by-workweek pay-rate uplift.

Which Alabama cities add local occupational taxes?

A handful of Alabama cities run their own employer-withheld occupational tax on top of state and federal. The biggest is Birmingham at 1 percent of gross wages for work performed inside city limits.

Other cities (Bessemer, Gadsden, Auburn, Opelika, and Macon County among them) levy rates in the 0.5 to 2 percent range, each with its own portal and due date.

Marcus, working inside Birmingham city limits, has an extra 1 percent withheld for the city, on top of his state 5 percent marginal. Latoya in Huntsville pays no city occupational tax at all, because Huntsville does not run one. The city of work is what matters, not the city of residence.

Three things to know about Alabama local occupational tax:

  1. Cities, not the state, run collections. The Alabama Department of Revenue does not administer city occupational tax. Each city sets its own form, portal, and due date.
  2. It is based on where work is performed, not where the employee lives. A remote employee in unincorporated Jefferson County who never sets foot in Birmingham owes nothing. A Birmingham employee who lives in Mountain Brook owes the full 1 percent.
  3. It is a withholding obligation on the employer. Birmingham Ordinance 20-38 (2020) made the employer the collection agent. Late remittance accrues penalties and interest at the city’s rate.

The canonical rate list is maintained by the Alabama League of Municipalities at almonline.org/TaxRates.aspx. Teamed’s payroll engine geo-codes each work location and applies the right city withholding automatically.
