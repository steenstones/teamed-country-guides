How does Alabama wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

Alabama has no state minimum wage, no state overtime statute, and no mandated meal or rest breaks for adult workers. Federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) does the work: $7.25 per hour, 1.5 times regular rate over 40 hours per workweek. The one Alabama-only rule sits in child labour, a documented 30-minute meal break for 14 and 15 year olds after 5 consecutive hours.

Alabama wage and hour law in 2026 is federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) law in practice. Minimum wage is $7.25 per hour (federal floor; Alabama has no state minimum and Alabama Code § 25-7-41 bars cities from setting a higher one). Overtime is 1.5 times the regular rate for hours over 40 in a workweek for non-exempt employees. Alabama law mandates no meal break and no rest break for adult workers; if you offer rest periods under 20 minutes, they count as paid hours worked. The one Alabama-only rule: a documented 30-minute meal break for minors aged 14 to 15 who work more than 5 consecutive hours, enforced by the Alabama Department of Labor with civil penalties of $300 to $5,000 per violation.

What is Alabama’s minimum wage in 2026?

Alabama’s minimum wage in 2026 is $7.25 per hour, the federal Fair Labor Standards Act floor. Alabama has no state minimum wage statute. Alabama Code § 25-7-41 (2016) preempts cities and counties from setting a higher rate, so Birmingham, Mobile, and Montgomery all pay the federal $7.25.

Three practical consequences flow from a federal-only floor:

  • Tipped employees can be paid a cash wage of $2.13 per hour if tips bring total earnings to at least $7.25 per hour each pay period. If tips fall short, the employer makes up the difference. This is the federal FLSA Section 3(m) tip credit, unchanged in Alabama.
  • Full-time employees on the minimum wage gross $15,080 per year at 40 hours per week. That number is the anchor for most onboarding paperwork, including SUTA wage-base calculations (Alabama’s $8,000 SUTA base hits in the first 21 weeks at minimum wage).
  • City wage rules don’t exist. A Birmingham employer pays the same hourly floor as a rural Bibb County employer. The only city-level wage-related layer is Birmingham’s 1 percent occupational tax (see the local-tax page), and that’s a deduction, not a floor.

For multi-state employers used to California ($16+ statewide, $20+ in some cities), New York ($15+ statewide, $16+ in NYC and Westchester), or Washington ($16.66), Alabama is the federal floor without exceptions. Quote employees a competitive offer based on local market rates, not the statutory minimum, because the statutory minimum sits well below typical Alabama market wages for any role above entry-level retail or food service. Source: Alabama Department of Labor; Alabama Code § 25-7-41; U.S. Department of Labor, Wage and Hour Division. Accessed 21 May 2026.

The 2016 city preemption

Alabama’s § 25-7-41 was passed in February 2016, days after Birmingham’s city council voted to raise the local minimum to $10.10 per hour. The state statute voided the Birmingham ordinance retroactively and barred future local action. It also blocks paid-leave mandates, predictive-scheduling ordinances, and benefits-related local employment laws. Alabama is unusual in the comprehensiveness of the preemption; it’s a one-floor state by design.

When does an Alabama employer have to pay overtime?

An Alabama employer pays overtime under the federal Fair Labor Standards Act: 1.5 times the regular rate of pay for every hour over 40 in a workweek, for non-exempt employees. Alabama has no daily overtime rule, no double-time rule, and no state-level salary basis above the federal $684 per week threshold for exempt status.

The workweek is the unit of measurement. An employee who works 12 hours on Monday and 4 hours each on Tuesday through Friday hits 28 hours in the week and earns zero overtime. The same employee working 9 hours per day Monday to Friday hits 45 hours and earns 5 hours at 1.5 times rate. Define the workweek once, in writing, and don’t change it to avoid overtime; doing so is a per-se FLSA violation.

Exempt vs non-exempt classification

To be exempt from overtime in Alabama (and the rest of the country), an employee must meet both a salary basis test and a duties test:

  • Salary basis: at least $684 per week ($35,568 per year), paid as a fixed salary not reduced by quality or quantity of work.
  • Duties test: primary duty must be executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales as defined in 29 CFR Part 541. Job title alone doesn’t establish exemption.

A salaried “manager” making $50,000 per year who spends 90 percent of the day stocking shelves alongside hourly staff fails the duties test and is owed overtime. This is the single largest FLSA litigation category nationally. The Alabama overtime tax line (different rule, often confused)

Don’t confuse Alabama’s overtime tax treatment with the FLSA overtime payment rule. The 2024 full state-income-tax exclusion on overtime wages (Act 2024-437) expired 30 June 2025. From tax years 2026 through 2028, Alabama allows employees a $1,000 individual deduction for qualified overtime on Form 40 (Act 2026-604). Payroll continues to withhold Alabama state income tax on the full overtime wage, and the FLSA payment rule is unchanged. The state income-tax page covers the deduction mechanics in full. Source: U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division; Alabama Department of Revenue, Overtime Pay Exemption (Amended). Accessed 21 May 2026.

Do Alabama employers have to give meal or rest breaks?

No. Alabama law does not require employers to provide meal breaks or rest breaks to adult workers. Federal FLSA also doesn’t mandate breaks for adults. If you do offer rest breaks of less than 20 minutes, federal law counts them as paid hours worked. Bona fide meal periods of 30 minutes or more, where the employee is fully relieved of duty, can be unpaid.

The federal default rules govern what happens when you do choose to offer breaks:

  • Short rest breaks (5 to 20 minutes): 29 CFR 785.18 treats these as compensable working time. If you give an employee a 15-minute paid coffee break, that 15 minutes counts toward the 40-hour overtime threshold.
  • Meal periods (30+ minutes): 29 CFR 785.19 allows these to be unpaid, provided the employee is fully relieved from duty for the purpose of eating. An employee required to stay at their desk and answer the phone during a 30-minute lunch is “engaged to wait” and the time is compensable.
  • Nursing mothers: the federal PUMP Act (Section 7(r) of FLSA, extended by the 2022 act) requires reasonable break time and a private non-bathroom space to express breast milk, for one year after the child’s birth. This rule applies in Alabama and is non-negotiable for any FLSA-covered employer.

Most Alabama employers offer a 30-minute unpaid lunch and one or two 10-15 minute paid breaks per 8-hour shift, by policy not statute. The structure has to be documented in the employee handbook and applied consistently; ad-hoc “take a break when you need one” phrasing creates wage-and-hour exposure when an employee later claims they worked through unpaid time. The contrast with multi-state employees

If your business operates in California, an Alabama policy won’t cover your Sacramento engineer. California requires a 30-minute meal period before the 5th hour and a second one before the 10th, plus 10-minute rest periods every 4 hours, with one hour of premium pay owed for each missed meal or rest. Oregon, Washington, Colorado, Illinois, and a dozen others have similar carve-outs. Alabama is the floor; the high-water mark drives the policy for any employee in a stricter state. Source: U.S. Department of Labor, Fact Sheet #22; 29 CFR Part 785. Accessed 21 May 2026.

What is Alabama’s 30-minute meal break rule for minors?

Alabama’s child labour law requires a documented 30-minute meal break for any 14 or 15 year old employed for more than 5 consecutive hours. The break has to be uninterrupted; rest periods shorter than 30 minutes do not interrupt the 5-hour count. Failure to document carries a civil penalty of $300 to $5,000 per violation, plus possible prosecution by the Alabama Department of Labor.

Daily and weekly hour caps for 14-15 year olds

The meal break rule sits inside a broader cap on hours for the same age group:

  • School-day cap: no more than 3 hours on a day when public school is in session
  • Non-school-day cap: no more than 8 hours per day
  • School-week cap: no more than 18 hours per week when public schools are in session
  • Non-school-week cap: no more than 40 hours per week when school is out
  • Time-of-day: no work before 7am or after 7pm during the school year (extended to 9pm from 1 June through Labour Day)

A 15 year old summer hire working full-time at a Mobile restaurant could legally pull a 40-hour week, with each shift over 5 hours triggering the 30-minute documented meal break. A 14 year old at the same restaurant during the school year is capped at 3 hours per school day and 18 hours per week, and the 5-hour meal-break trigger rarely applies in that pattern. Is on-call or waiting time paid in Alabama?

Federal FLSA controls this in Alabama. The test is “engaged to wait” (paid) vs “waiting to be engaged” (not paid). An employee at the workplace waiting for the next task is on the clock. An employee at home, free to use the time, who carries a phone in case they’re called in, is off the clock. The factual line is whether the employee can effectively use the time for their own purposes.

Three patterns Alabama employers see most often:

  • On-premises wait time: a delivery driver between runs reading a book in the warehouse breakroom. Engaged to wait. Compensable.
  • On-call at home with restrictions: an IT engineer required to stay within 15 minutes of the office, sober, and answer calls within 5 minutes. The restriction effectively prevents personal use. Likely compensable, depending on the call frequency and response window.
  • On-call at home, free to live normally: a service technician carrying a phone and expected to take one or two calls per week. Free to socialise, sleep, run errands. Not compensable for the wait time, only for the actual call response.

The federal Wage and Hour Division’s Fact Sheet #22 lists the controlling tests. Sleep time, travel time, training time, and meeting time each have their own rules; the page that matters operationally for Alabama employers is the one inside the federal handbook, not the state-side. Travel time, training time, meeting time

Three more FLSA-controlled patterns that catch employers out:

  • Travel time: ordinary commute is not compensable. Travel between job sites during the workday is compensable. Out-of-town overnight travel is compensable during normal working hours, even on weekends, if the travel cuts across the workday.
  • Training time: compensable unless all four of these are true: outside normal hours, voluntary, not directly job-related, no productive work performed.
  • Meeting time: attendance is compensable if mandatory or held during normal hours. Voluntary off-hours meetings with no job-related agenda are not compensable.

How should multi-state employers handle Alabama wage-and-hour law?

Apply the highest applicable standard for each employee, by employee location. Alabama is the federal floor; California, New York, Oregon, Washington, Colorado, Illinois, and Massachusetts each layer state-specific obligations on top. A single national handbook that promises California-style meal breaks to an Alabama employee is fine (you can always exceed the floor). A handbook that promises only the Alabama floor to a California employee is a wage-and-hour claim waiting to happen.

Three operational rules for multi-state US employers:

  1. Maintain a state matrix, not a single policy. Document each state where you employ someone, with that state’s minimum wage, overtime trigger, meal-break rule, rest-break rule, sick-leave accrual, and paid-leave entitlement. Re-check annually; state minimum wages typically reset 1 January.
  2. Default to the strictest state for shared benefits. If your remote-first policy offers paid sick leave, set the accrual at the highest state mandate your workforce sits in (currently Colorado at 48 hours per year, or California at 40 hours plus front-loading rules). Avoid promising less than what one of your employees is statutorily owed.
  3. Tag each employee’s work location in payroll. The wage-and-hour rule that applies is the rule of the state where the employee physically performs the work, not the state of incorporation. A New York payroll for a remote employee living and working in Birmingham follows Alabama wage-and-hour law (and Alabama state tax, and Birmingham 1 percent occupational tax). The 30-day safe harbour from Act 2025-334 carves a narrow exception for short-stay nonresidents; everything past 30 days lives under Alabama rules.

For most early-stage US employers, the practical move is a single national handbook that defaults to the strictest applicable state for any benefit, plus a state-specific addendum for jurisdictions with hard mandates (California, New York, Massachusetts, Washington, Oregon, Colorado, Illinois). Alabama doesn’t need an addendum, only inclusion in the federal-floor baseline.
