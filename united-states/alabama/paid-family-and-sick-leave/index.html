Does Alabama require paid family or sick leave in 2026?

No. Alabama has no state paid family leave law and no state paid sick leave law. Federal FMLA gives 12 weeks unpaid at 50+ employees. Everything else is voluntary, which is the burden and the opportunity.

Alabama in 2026 has no state paid family leave law and no state paid sick leave law. The only mandatory leave floor is federal FMLA (29 U.S.C. § 2601), which provides 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period for employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. An employee qualifies after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked. Below the 50-employee threshold, federal FMLA does not apply, and Alabama has no equivalent state floor. Everything else (paid parental, paid sick, paid bereavement, paid PTO) is voluntary, designed by the employer. Federal Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA, June 2023), USERRA for military duty, and Alabama’s Section 12-16-8.1 jury duty pay protection apply on top regardless of headcount.

Does Alabama require paid family leave?

No. Alabama has no state paid family leave law. Nine US states plus DC do require it (California, Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Washington, and Washington DC); Alabama is not one of them. There is no Alabama state insurance tax for PFL, no state PFL programme to fund, and no state PFL claim process for employees.

For an Alabama employer, hiring in Alabama removes the state-level PFL withholding line that adds 0.5 to 1.0 percent of wages in California, New York, or Washington. There is no equivalent payroll deduction, no state PFL bond schedule, and no state agency claim portal to maintain. The cost line simply does not exist on an Alabama payslip.

What this means for talent: an Alabama employee comparing job offers against friends in California or New York will not have access to a state PFL programme to fall back on for the birth of a child or the care of a parent. The employer’s voluntary parental-leave policy carries the full weight of that retention conversation, and the gap between what your offer letter says and what the California employer down the LinkedIn search results offers is fully visible to the candidate.

Does Alabama require paid sick leave?

No. Alabama has no state paid sick leave law. Eighteen US states and dozens of cities require accrued paid sick time (California, Colorado, Connecticut, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Washington, plus DC); Alabama is not one of them. The federal floor is also nothing for the private sector. Sick days at an Alabama job are whatever the offer letter says they are.

This makes the offer-letter sick-day line load-bearing for retention. SHRM’s 2025 benefits survey shows the median Alabama private-sector employer offers 5 paid sick days per year, accrued at roughly one hour per 30 hours worked. Competitive employers in knowledge-work sectors (tech, professional services, healthcare admin) typically go to 7 to 10 sick days, often consolidated into a single PTO bank with vacation rather than tracked separately.

One nuance: although there is no Alabama state PSL, an Alabama employee may still qualify for unpaid leave under federal FMLA if the absence is for a serious health condition (defined as inpatient care or continuing treatment by a healthcare provider, lasting more than three consecutive days) and the employer is FMLA-covered. A bad case of flu generally does not qualify; pneumonia hospitalisation typically does. Job protection during a serious-health-condition absence is the point, not the pay.

What does federal FMLA give Alabama employees?

Federal FMLA gives qualifying Alabama employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period, with group health coverage continued at the employer’s normal contribution. It applies only to employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. The employee qualifies after 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked in the preceding 12 months.

The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 is the federal floor for unpaid, job-protected leave. It overrides nothing in Alabama because Alabama has no competing state floor. The 50-employee threshold counts every US employee, not just Alabama ones, which catches multi-state employers off guard.

Five qualifying reasons trigger FMLA leave:

  • Birth and care of a newborn, within 12 months of birth
  • Placement of a child with the employee for adoption or for a placement under state child-placement law, within 12 months of placement
  • Care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition
  • The employee’s own serious health condition that makes them unable to perform the job
  • Qualifying military exigency arising from a family member’s active duty in the Armed Forces; up to 26 weeks for military caregiver leave

The 50-employee threshold counts your whole US workforce

An employer with 30 Alabama employees and 25 Texas employees crosses the 50-employee FMLA threshold even though neither state alone gets there. The DOL counts every employee on the US payroll for any 20 or more weeks of the current or preceding year. The 75-mile-radius rule then determines which specific worksites are covered, which matters for fully remote employers whose employees are geographically dispersed.

What FMLA does not require

  • Pay during leave. FMLA leave is unpaid by statute. An employee may substitute accrued PTO; the employer may require it. There is no FMLA wage-replacement benefit.
  • FMLA leave for employers under 50. A 20-employee Alabama company has no FMLA obligation and Alabama has no mini-FMLA. Below the threshold, both parental leave and serious-illness leave are entirely voluntary.
  • FMLA leave for the first 12 months. A 3-month tenure employee with a new baby has no FMLA right to job-protected leave; the employer may grant unpaid leave voluntarily and may terminate at any time, subject only to the federal anti-discrimination floor.

Source: US Department of Labor, FMLA Final Rule (29 CFR Part 825), accessed 21 May 2026.

What protections apply for pregnancy and disability in Alabama?

Federal law applies; Alabama adds nothing. The Pregnancy Discrimination Act (PDA, 1978) treats pregnancy like any other temporary disability for employer leave and benefits. The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA), effective 27 June 2023, requires reasonable accommodation for pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions, applying to employers with 15 or more employees. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) covers pregnancy-related disabilities such as gestational diabetes or severe preeclampsia.

The practical reading for an Alabama employer at 15 to 50 employees: PWFA covers reasonable accommodation (modified duties, schedule changes, time off for medical appointments, lactation breaks), but FMLA does not yet cover the 12 weeks of job-protected leave. The accommodation conversation under PWFA may include voluntary unpaid leave as the accommodation, but the employer has no statutory obligation to hold the job open beyond the accommodation period.

At 50+ employees the package stacks: PWFA accommodation during the pregnancy, FMLA for the birth and bonding, ADA for any post-delivery serious complications. The three federal laws together resemble what a single state PFL programme covers in California or New York, except every line is unpaid unless the employer voluntarily layers pay on top.

What this means for offer letters

The most retention-critical voluntary line on an Alabama offer letter is paid parental leave. The federal floor (12 weeks unpaid at FMLA-covered employers, accommodation under PWFA at 15+) leaves a wide gap that voluntary policy fills. Mid-market Alabama employers commonly offer 6 to 8 weeks of paid maternity leave and 2 to 4 weeks of paid paternity / partner leave; top-quartile knowledge-work employers offer 12 to 16 weeks paid for the primary caregiver regardless of gender. Source: SHRM 2025 Employee Benefits Survey, US EEOC PWFA guidance, accessed 21 May 2026.

What about military leave and jury duty in Alabama?

Federal USERRA protects civilian jobs for service members; Alabama’s Section 31-2-13, Code of Alabama 1975 mirrors USERRA for state active duty. Jury duty is mandatory and Alabama’s Section 12-16-8.1 requires full-time employers to continue regular pay during service. None of this is a state PFL or PSL programme; these are narrow, separate carve-outs.

USERRA and Alabama military leave

The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA, 38 U.S.C. § 4301 et seq.) provides civilian reemployment rights for service members called to active duty, with a cumulative cap of five years of military absence. The employer pays nothing during the leave itself, but health-plan coverage must continue (employee may pay up to 102 percent of the full premium for COBRA-style continuation). On return, the employee is reinstated to the position they would have held had they not left, the so-called escalator principle.

Alabama’s Section 31-2-13 adds state-active-duty protection (National Guard or State Defense Force called by the Governor): same job-protection logic, no statutory pay, no length cap for state active duty specifically. State and federal protections stack; the employee gets whichever is more favourable for the specific call-up.

Jury duty in Alabama

Section 12-16-8 makes jury service mandatory; an employee cannot be discharged or threatened for serving. Section 12-16-8.1 goes further for full-time employees: the employer must continue normal pay during jury service and cannot require the employee to use accrued vacation, sick, or PTO. The employer may offset the pay by whatever per-diem the court pays the juror. Part-time and temporary employees are protected from discharge but not from lost wages.

Most Alabama employers extend the pay protection to all employees regardless of full-time status, on the principle that a complicated jury-pay rule is more administrative cost than the saved wages are worth. Source: Code of Alabama 1975, Sections 31-2-13 and 12-16-8.1, accessed 21 May 2026.

What about employers with fewer than 50 employees in Alabama?

Below the federal FMLA threshold, an Alabama employer has no mandatory leave law to comply with at all, with three narrow exceptions: PWFA accommodation at 15+ employees, USERRA reemployment for service members, and Section 12-16-8.1 jury duty pay. Everything else is voluntary, including paid parental leave, paid sick leave, paid bereavement, and unpaid time off for a child’s school visit.

The good news is the absence of compliance friction: no state PFL claim portal, no accrued-PSL ledger to maintain, no termination clock to manage during a protected leave at single-digit headcount. The bad news is the absence of employer cover: the small Alabama employer is exposed to the full retention cost of a competitor offering a meaningful voluntary leave package.

The pattern we see at Teamed for clients hiring their first three to ten people in Alabama is to write a short, clear voluntary leave policy that punches above the median: 8 weeks paid parental leave, 8 paid sick days per year, 3 paid bereavement days, full pay during jury duty. The total cost is roughly 1.5 to 2.5 percent of payroll spread across the workforce, and it makes the difference between a candidate accepting your offer and accepting an offer from a California-headquartered competitor with state PFL benefits stacked on top.

The headcount tipping point

An Alabama employer hits the FMLA 50-employee threshold gradually as the US headcount grows, often without noticing. The trigger is 20 or more weeks of 50+ employees in either the current or preceding calendar year. Once tripped, the FMLA obligation continues for the rest of the current calendar year and the full following calendar year, even if headcount drops back below 50. The lookback rule catches employers who hire seasonally or scale through funding rounds; the FMLA obligation often outlasts the headcount that triggered it.
