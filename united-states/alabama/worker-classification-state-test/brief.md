## Verified data (sources at bottom)

### Alabama's test for worker classification

Alabama uses the **IRS 20-factor common-law test**, codified in **Act 2021-226 (HB 408)**, effective **1 July 2021**. The act:
- Mandates Alabama Department of Labor and Alabama Department of Revenue use the IRS 20-factor test
- Applies to: wage/hour determinations, unemployment insurance, state tax classifications
- **Explicitly excludes workers' compensation** — that statute still uses the older "right of control" test (Code of Alabama §25-5-1 et seq.)
- Codifies the federal **Section 530 safe harbor** (Revenue Act 1978) if employer had (a) reasonable basis, (b) issued 1099s annually, (c) consistent treatment of similar workers

### Statutory citations

- **Code of Alabama §25-4-7** — defines "employee" for unemployment compensation as master-servant relationship; independent contractor = self-employed individual meeting IRS definition, not an employee
- **Code of Alabama §25-5-1 et seq.** — Workers' Compensation, still applies "right of control" test
- **Act 2021-226 (HB 408)** — codifies 20-factor common-law test for unemployment/tax
- **Act 2025 SB86 (Portable Benefits Act)** — voluntary portable benefits accounts for independent contractors, effective **31 December 2025**. Critical: contributions by hiring parties to portable benefit accounts **are not evidence of an employment relationship**

### The 20 common-law factors (IRS Rev. Rul. 87-41, adopted by Alabama)

Grouped into three categories:

**Behavioural control** (right to direct how work is done):
1. Instructions — does the firm tell worker when, where, how to work?
2. Training — does the firm train the worker in its methods?
3. Integration — are worker's services integrated into the firm's business?
4. Services rendered personally — must worker do the work personally?
5. Hiring, supervising, paying assistants — does the firm or worker?
6. Continuing relationship — recurring engagement or one-off?
7. Set hours of work — does firm set hours?
8. Full time required — must worker devote full time to firm?
9. Doing work on employer's premises — at firm's office or worker's?
10. Order or sequence set — does firm dictate work order?

**Financial control**:
11. Oral or written reports — required by firm?
12. Payment method — hourly/weekly (employee) vs by project (contractor)?
13. Expenses — who pays travel and business expenses?
14. Tools and materials — who furnishes them?
15. Significant investment — does worker have own facilities/equipment?
16. Profit or loss — can worker realise profit or suffer loss?

**Relationship of parties**:
17. Working for more than one firm at a time — exclusive or not?
18. Making service available to general public — does worker advertise?
19. Right to discharge — can firm fire at will?
20. Right to terminate — can worker quit without contractual breach?

### Misclassification penalties — Alabama exposure

Misclassifying an employee as a 1099 contractor in Alabama exposes the employer to **stacked liability** across at least five categories:

1. **Federal payroll tax** (IRS): unpaid employer FICA (6.2% SS + 1.45% Medicare = 7.65%), unpaid employee FICA (recovered from employer if unable to recover from worker), FUTA (0.6% after credit on first $7,000), plus interest and penalties under IRC §3509 (reduced rates if unintentional) or full liability if intentional
2. **Alabama state income tax withholding** (Code of Alabama §25-4-7, §40-18-71): unpaid withholding from worker's wages, plus penalty and interest under Alabama Department of Revenue rules
3. **Alabama unemployment insurance (SUTA)**: unpaid contributions at the new-employer rate of **2.7% on the first $8,000** of wages per worker per year — up to **$216 per worker per year** in unpaid SUTA, plus penalty and interest; the employer may also lose the FUTA credit of 5.4% (taking FUTA from 0.6% to a full 6.0%)
4. **Federal FLSA back wages and overtime**: 2-year lookback (3 years if wilful), plus **liquidated damages equal to back wages** (double damages by default)
5. **Workers' compensation premium and coverage gap**: missed premium plus potential personal liability for any uncovered injury sustained by the misclassified worker during the look-back period; workers' comp uses the **right-of-control test**, which can find employment even where Alabama's tax test does not

### Recent changes that move the math (2025-2026)

- **31 December 2025 — Portable Benefits Act (SB86) live**: Alabama becomes the first US state offering a tax-advantaged voluntary benefits scheme for genuine independent contractors. Hiring parties may contribute to contractor benefit accounts (health, disability, retirement) AND deduct 100% of contributions on the Alabama corporate return WITHOUT the contributions counting as evidence of employment. Independent contractors deduct contributions as adjustment to income on personal Alabama return. Key protection: **participation cannot be used as a factor in worker classification determination**.
- **27 February 2026 — federal DOL Notice of Proposed Rulemaking** to rescind the 2024 "totality of circumstances" rule and restore the 2021 "economic reality" test for FLSA classification. This is a federal proposal, not yet final; Alabama state law (the 20-factor common-law test) is unaffected, but the federal floor may shift mid-2026.

### Cost example for an employer

A misclassified Alabama employee earning $60,000 per year over 3 years, treated as 1099 then re-classified by audit:

- Unpaid employer FICA: $60,000 x 7.65% x 3 = **$13,770**
- Unpaid SUTA: $216 x 3 = **$648**
- Federal FLSA back wages and overtime (assuming 8 hours weekly OT at 1.5x): variable, could exceed **$30,000 over 3 years** if non-exempt
- FLSA liquidated damages: equal to back wages, **another $30,000**
- IRC §3509 reduced rate (unintentional): roughly 1.5% of wages = ~$900 per year on FICA portion; full rate (intentional) doubles to 3% (rules vary)
- Federal income tax not withheld: variable
- Alabama state withholding back, penalty, interest: 2-5% of wages
- Workers' comp premium gap: variable by risk class

Conservative back-of-envelope total exposure: **$60,000 to $100,000 per misclassified worker over 3 years**, before legal fees.

## Conflict / uncertainty log

- The IRS published a 20-factor list in Rev. Rul. 87-41 (1987) but later consolidated into three categories (behavioural, financial, relationship) in modern guidance. Alabama law refers to the "20-factor common-law test" by name (per Act 2021-226 and Alabama Department of Labor); the three-category framing is how the IRS currently presents it. The page should present both — the 20 factors as the statutory hook (Alabama Act 2021-226), grouped into the three IRS categories for readability.
- No conflict on Act 2021-226 effective date (1 July 2021) or workers' comp carve-out.
