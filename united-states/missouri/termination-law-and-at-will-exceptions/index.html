Missouri follows the at-will doctrine: either party may end employment at any time, for any lawful reason, or for no reason. Missouri courts interpret the public-policy exception narrowly, limiting it to four established categories.

At-will employment in Missouri means you can terminate without cause, without documentation, and without advance notice, as long as you don't cross one of the recognised exceptions. The Missouri Department of Labor confirms this default rule for private-sector employment.

The doctrine runs in both directions. An employee may resign at any time without giving notice, and the employer cannot condition the final paycheck on notice served or transition tasks completed. The paycheck is due on the day of dismissal for involuntary separations regardless of how the termination is handled.

Missouri courts have consistently applied the at-will rule in favour of employers when the four recognised exceptions are not in play. The absence of a state-mandated notice period, combined with the immediate final pay obligation, means your compliance window opens the moment you decide to act, not before it.

Two Missouri-specific obligations attach to every involuntary separation regardless of the at-will doctrine: the immediate final pay rule and the Service Letter Statute. Both apply even when the termination is entirely at-will. Understanding them before the separation is the practical work.

What are the exceptions to at-will employment in Missouri?

Missouri recognises a narrow public-policy exception confirmed in Fleshner v. Pepose Vision Institute, 304 S.W.3d 81 (Mo. banc 2010), covering four fact patterns. Statutory MHRA protections apply separately at 6 or more employees.

1. The Fleshner public-policy exception

The Missouri Supreme Court in Fleshner v. Pepose Vision Institute (2010) confirmed the public-policy exception and mapped its four permissible categories. A termination is wrongful under Missouri law when the employee was fired for:

  • Refusing to perform an illegal act at the employer's direction;
  • Reporting a violation of law or regulation to an appropriate authority;
  • Exercising a right conferred by statute (such as filing a workers' compensation claim or asserting a wage entitlement); or
  • Performing a public duty (such as jury service or reporting for military duty).

Fleshner is notable for what it does not include. Missouri courts have declined to extend the exception to at-will firings based on employee lifestyle, off-duty conduct, or general "unfairness" grounds that some other states recognise. The exception is deliberately narrow: the termination must be tied to one of those four categories to succeed as a wrongful-discharge claim.

The practical implication: if your Missouri termination decision was made for performance, restructuring, or business reasons, and the employee was not engaged in any of the four protected activities at the time, Missouri's public-policy exception is not in play. Document the business reason clearly, and you have a defensible record if a wrongful-discharge claim is filed.

2. Implied contract

Missouri courts will find an implied employment contract if handbook language, consistent practice, or oral promises suggest that employment can only be ended for cause. The single most effective protection is a clear at-will disclaimer in every offer letter and employee handbook. But Missouri courts look at the totality of communications, not just the disclaimer in isolation. If your managers routinely promise job security or your handbook describes a multi-step progressive-discipline process without a clear carve-out, those representations can create an obligation that limits at-will termination.

Run your Missouri handbook language through employment counsel before deploying it to a new hire. A disclaimer that reads as boilerplate alongside substantive "for cause" procedures sends a mixed signal Missouri courts have enforced against employers.

3. Missouri Human Rights Act (MHRA)

RSMo § 213 et seq. prohibits termination on the basis of race, colour, religion, national origin, ancestry, sex, disability, or age (40 and over). The MHRA threshold is 6 employees, well below the 15-employee federal floor for Title VII, the ADA, and GINA. A Missouri company with 7 employees faces state anti-discrimination obligations before any of those federal statutes has triggered.

When is the final paycheck due after termination in Missouri?

Under RSMo § 290.110, wages are due immediately upon involuntary termination. For voluntary resignation, the final paycheck is due on the next regular payday. Unpaid wages after a written demand carry a penalty of up to 60 days of additional wages.

The immediate-payment rule on involuntary dismissal is the first thing that catches out-of-state employers in Missouri. Many states set the final paycheck deadline as the next regular payday for all separations. Missouri draws a sharp distinction: if you fire someone, their wages are due that day. If they resign, the next payday applies.

The penalty for non-compliance is significant. If the employer fails to pay without cause, the employee may send a certified-mail demand. If wages remain unpaid for seven days after that demand, Missouri courts can award up to 60 additional days of wages at the same daily rate as the underpayment. On a $5,000 monthly salary, the exposure after seven days of non-payment following a demand is up to $10,000 in penalty wages alone.

What counts as wages for final pay purposes: all earned wages through the last day of work, including accrued overtime under the FLSA. Missouri law does not require employers to pay out accrued vacation or PTO on termination unless a company policy or contract says otherwise. Employers cannot deduct unreturned equipment costs or other offsets from the final paycheck without a signed written authorisation from the employee or a court order.

Missouri Service Letter Statute
RSMo § 290.140
Applies 2026
The obligation most out-of-state employers miss

Any involuntarily separated employee with 90 or more days of service may request a written service letter. On request, you must provide it within a reasonable time, stating: the nature of the work performed, the duration of service, and the true reason for the dismissal. False or misleading statements in a service letter expose the employer to actual and punitive damages under Missouri law.

What is Missouri's Service Letter Statute and what does it require?

RSMo § 290.140 requires Missouri employers to provide a truthful written service letter to any employee with 90 or more days of service who requests one after involuntary separation. False statements in the letter carry liability for actual and punitive damages.

Missouri's Service Letter Statute is one of the most distinctive features of Missouri employment law and one of the least known outside the state. No equivalent obligation exists under federal law, and few other states have a comparable requirement. The statute applies to any involuntary separation, regardless of the reason for dismissal, provided the employee has been in the employer's service for at least 90 days.

What the letter must contain

The service letter must set out three things: the nature of the work or service performed by the employee; the length or duration of service; and the true reason or cause for separation. Each element is mandatory. A letter that provides two of the three, or that gives a vague or euphemistic reason, does not satisfy the statute.

The "true reason" requirement is where employers create liability. Giving a general "mutual agreement" or "end of assignment" reason when the actual dismissal was for performance or misconduct is the kind of mismatch Missouri courts have found sufficient for a service letter claim. The reason in the letter does not have to be favourable to the employee, but it must be accurate.

When does the obligation trigger

The statute is request-driven: the employee must ask for the letter in writing. There is no obligation to provide a service letter proactively. Once a written request is received, the employer must respond within a reasonable time (not specified by statute, but courts have interpreted "reasonable" to mean weeks, not months). The request must come from the separated employee directly; third parties cannot make the request on the employee's behalf.

What happens if the letter is false or misleading

An employer who provides a service letter containing a false statement faces a civil claim for damages, including punitive damages if the court finds the false statement was made with actual malice. This creates a genuine exposure risk on dismissals where the documented reason and the verbal reason diverged, or where the employer wants to characterise a performance-based dismissal more charitably than the facts support.

The safest approach: document the real reason for the dismissal accurately in your HR system before the separation, and confirm that the service letter, if requested, can faithfully reproduce that documented reason. Does Missouri require advance notice of termination?

No. Missouri imposes no statutory advance notice requirement for at-will employees. Neither employers nor employees are legally obligated to provide prior notice before ending the employment relationship.

Missouri's absence of a notice requirement applies in both directions. You can act on the day you decide to terminate. An employee can resign without two weeks' notice and the employer cannot penalise the resignation or withhold the final paycheck because notice wasn't given.

Three sources can override the default. First, any employment contract or collective bargaining agreement that specifies a notice period becomes contractually binding. Second, if your handbook or offer letter describes a notice process or implies one will be given, those representations can constitute an implied contract under Missouri case law. Third, the federal WARN Act requires 60 days' advance written notice for mass layoffs of 50 or more employees, for covered employers with 100 or more workers. Missouri has no separate state-level WARN equivalent.

For most individual Missouri terminations, the timeline is immediate. What creates compliance pressure is not notice but the final pay timing (due the same day for involuntary dismissals) and the Service Letter obligation (triggered by a written employee request post-separation).

Is severance pay required in Missouri?

No. Missouri does not require severance pay by statute. It is a business decision, voluntary unless stated in a contract, policy, or collective bargaining agreement.

Severance in Missouri is entirely at the employer's discretion. Employers use it to secure a release of claims, to support longer-tenured employees through a transition, or as part of a consistent reduction-in-force programme. There is no statutory floor or formula.

One statutory constraint applies when the separation agreement seeks to waive legal claims from an employee aged 40 or over. The Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA) requires: a written waiver in plain language; at least 21 days for the employee to consider it; a 7-day revocation period after signing; and specific language referencing the employee's ADEA rights. A waiver that skips any of these requirements is unenforceable for age discrimination claims, regardless of how the severance amount is documented.

If you offer severance, treat consistency as a risk management tool, not just good practice. Offering severance to some employees but not others in similar circumstances under the MHRA's 6-employee threshold can support a disparate-treatment claim if the gap tracks a protected characteristic.

Note on service letters and settlement: if you settle a disputed service letter claim and the settlement agreement includes a severance payment, the settlement does not supersede the employee's right to a truthful service letter under RSMo § 290.140. Missouri courts have found that the statute's public-policy underpinning survives contractual settlement of the damages claim itself.

How does the Missouri Human Rights Act affect termination decisions?

The MHRA applies to any employer with 6 or more employees, prohibiting termination based on race, colour, religion, national origin, ancestry, sex, disability, or age. That threshold triggers before Title VII, the ADA, or the ADEA applies under federal law.

The MHRA's 6-employee threshold is the figure most employers building distributed Missouri teams underestimate. Under federal law, Title VII and the ADA require 15 employees before anti-discrimination obligations attach. The ADEA requires 20. But with your 6th Missouri employee, state anti-discrimination law is fully operative and covers the same protected classes as those federal statutes.

For termination decisions specifically, the MHRA requires that no adverse employment action, including dismissal, be motivated in whole or in part by a protected characteristic. Missouri courts have interpreted "in part" broadly: a mixed-motive termination where business reasons and discriminatory animus both contributed to the decision can support a MHRA claim even if the business reason alone would have justified the dismissal.

Pre-termination documentation

Before any Missouri dismissal, confirm in writing that: the reason for termination is not tied to a MHRA-protected class; the employee has not recently engaged in a protected activity (filed a complaint, requested accommodation, served on jury duty); and the performance or business rationale was applied consistently to employees in comparable roles. The MCHR uses a 180-day filing window from the discriminatory act, so documentation assembled after the separation is less credible than contemporaneous records.

WARN Act and mass layoffs

If your Missouri operation is scaling down and you're laying off 50 or more employees from a single Missouri site, and your total US headcount is 100 or more, the federal WARN Act requires 60 days' written notice to employees, the Missouri Division of Employment Security, and the chief elected official of the affected municipality. Missouri has no separate state WARN law. The federal statute controls. An EOR relationship does not reduce the employer's WARN obligations; the number of workers affected at the worksite is what matters, not the EOR structure.

How do you terminate a Missouri employee without legal risk?

Document the business reason, process the final paycheck on the day of dismissal, prepare for a potential Service Letter request if the employee has 90 or more days of service, and confirm the decision doesn't implicate any of the four Fleshner categories or MHRA protected class. That's the Missouri compliance checklist.

Missouri's at-will doctrine is an asset in an employer-friendly state, but it doesn't eliminate compliance obligations. Every involuntary separation carries three tasks: same-day final pay, MHRA documentation, and potential Service Letter preparation. Handle all three, and your exposure is low.

Pre-termination checklist

Before you act: confirm the termination reason is not rooted in a MHRA protected class; check whether the employee has recently filed a wage complaint, requested reasonable accommodation, or reported a legal violation (all Fleshner triggers); verify your handbook language doesn't create an implied for-cause obligation the termination process didn't follow; and note whether the employee's tenure exceeds 90 days, which activates the Service Letter obligation on request.

Day of termination

Issue a written notice confirming the termination date. The final paycheck is due on the same day, not the next payday. Collect company equipment, revoke system access, and document what was communicated and when. Missouri law doesn't prescribe a specific form for the termination notice, but a written record closes most factual disputes in later proceedings.

Do not condition the final paycheck on equipment return, exit interview completion, or signing a separation agreement. Final wages earned through the last day of work are due immediately and unconditionally.

Post-separation: Service Letter readiness

After the separation, brief your HR administrator that a Service Letter request may arrive. Prepare the letter content in advance: nature of work, dates of employment, and the documented reason for dismissal. The letter should match the documented record exactly. If the internal documentation and the service letter differ, the discrepancy is exhibit one in a service letter claim.
