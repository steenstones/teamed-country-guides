Missouri is not a single-test state. The test you apply depends on the purpose of the determination and the industry the worker is in.

A misclassified $95,000-a-year Missouri contractor, reclassified after three years on a 1099, costs you $24,000 to $42,000 in back state withholding at 4.7 percent, back SUI premiums on the $9,000 wage base, workers’ comp back premium, and federal payroll-tax exposure. Per worker. Before legal fees.

Construction misclassification adds $50 per worker per day in civil penalties under RSMo § 285.515, plus criminal exposure as a Class A misdemeanour. Two years of daily penalties on a five-worker crew reaches $182,500.

This page covers the IRS common-law test Missouri uses for tax withholding, the ABC test the Division of Employment Security applies for state unemployment, the Construction Worker Independent Contractor Act, the workers’ comp reading, and what an audit costs across the three enforcement agencies.

Which worker classification test does Missouri use?

Pricing is Zero FX, no currency mark-up, no hidden conversion margin.Missouri runs different tests for different statutory purposes. There is no single ABC test covering all industries. State income-tax withholding and workers’ compensation follow the IRS 20-factor common-law test, which centres on the right to control how (not just whether) the work is performed.

State unemployment insurance uses an ABC test administered by the Missouri Division of Employment Security (DES) under RSMo § 288.034(12). The employer has to prove all three prongs to keep the worker classified as an independent contractor for UI purposes.

Construction work is governed by the Construction Worker Independent Contractor Act (RSMo §§ 285.500–285.515), which creates a separate nine-factor test, a presumption of employment, and criminal penalties for wilful misclassification.

Darnell is a Kansas City-based data engineer who consults for a fintech on a project basis. He owns his hardware, sets his own hours, works for four other clients, and invoices monthly. The IRS common-law analysis reads contractor across all three control families. The DES ABC test reaches the same answer. The construction statutes do not apply.

For most professional and knowledge-work roles, the Missouri and federal tests align. A contractor who passes the IRS 20-factor analysis cleanly will typically pass the DES ABC test and the workers’ comp reading as well, because all three look primarily at behavioural control.

The break comes in two places: the DES ABC test for state UI (which adds an independent-business prong that the pure IRS analysis does not require in the same way), and the Construction Worker Independent Contractor Act, which is its own regime entirely.

The IRS common-law test: what Missouri looks at for tax withholding

Missouri income-tax withholding follows the IRS 20-factor right-of-control analysis. No single factor decides. The pattern of facts decides. The central question is whether the hiring entity has the right to control not just the result of the work, but the means and methods by which it is accomplished.

The IRS groups the 20 factors into three families: behavioural control (who directs how and when the work is done, what tools and training apply), financial control (who provides equipment, who bears unreimbursed expenses, who can earn a profit or loss, how payment is structured), and relationship of the parties (written contract terms, employee-style benefits, permanence, whether the work is integral to the hiring entity’s business).

Missouri workers’ compensation reframes the same IRS analysis into a ten-factor test, but the underlying question is the same: who controls the work? The ten-factor reading is the Missouri Division of Workers’ Compensation’s practical lens on the same evidence pool.

Why the right-of-control question is the primary Missouri question

Missouri DOR auditors and the courts have consistently centred their analysis on whether the hiring entity has the right to control the worker, even if that right is never exercised. An employer who does not actually supervise a contractor day-to-day but reserves the right to do so in the contract will still fail the common-law test.

Darnell’s engagement is clean: he sets his own hours, works at his own home office, uses his own equipment, sets his own rates, and markets his services to four other fintech clients independently. The written MSA contains no supervision clause. The financial control and relationship prongs both read contractor. Missouri DOR will reach the same answer as the IRS.

Change one variable: Darnell is told he must attend the Kansas City office every Tuesday, must use the fintech’s development environment and coding standards, and must report to the engineering manager for daily standups. Those three facts shift the behavioural control reading, and the financial control reading starts to look hybrid. The right-of-control question has changed, even if the written contract still says “independent contractor.”

The DES ABC test for Missouri unemployment insurance

Missouri’s Division of Employment Security applies an ABC test under RSMo § 288.034(12) for state unemployment insurance. The hiring entity has to prove all three prongs. Fail any one, and the worker is an employee for SUI purposes.

Prong A: the worker is free from the employer’s control and direction in connection with the service, both under the contract and in fact.

Prong B: the service is performed outside the usual course of the employer’s business.

Prong C: the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business.

Prong B is the prong that most Missouri knowledge-work arrangements quietly fail. A tech company hiring a contract software developer fails Prong B, because software development IS the usual course of the tech company’s business. The contractor passes Prongs A and C cleanly and still owes state UI as an employee.

ABC prongWhat it testsCommon failure mode
Prong A: control Freedom from direction and control, both in the contract and in day-to-day fact Required attendance at client offices; fixed daily hours; mandatory tool and method compliance; supervisor check-ins built into the MSA
Prong B: outside usual course The service is outside the hiring entity’s normal trade or business A marketing agency hiring a freelance copywriter. A software firm hiring contract developers. A financial advisory hiring a contract analyst. All fail Prong B.
Prong C: independent business Worker is customarily engaged in their own trade or business, independent of any one client Single-client contractor, no separate LLC, no other active engagements, no public marketing of services. Prong C fails even if Prongs A and B pass.

Prong B is the prong most Missouri employers overlook

The IRS common-law test does not require that the service be “outside the usual course” of the business. The DES ABC test does. This creates a structural gap between the Missouri DOR reading (common-law, consistent with federal) and the DES reading (ABC, which adds Prong B).

A Missouri fintech hiring Darnell as a data engineer: if data engineering is the core of what the fintech does, Prong B fails. The IRS analysis may still come out contractor. The DES analysis reaches employee for SUI purposes. Same person, same engagement, two answers from two agencies on the same facts.

Missouri SUI wage base for 2026 is $9,000 (down from $9,500 in 2025). New-employer rate is 2.376 percent. Three years of back SUI premiums at 2.376 percent on a $9,000 wage base runs to approximately $641 before interest and civil penalties.

Pass the IRS test for tax. Still fail Prong B of the DES ABC test for unemployment. Same worker, same engagement, different Missouri agencies, two different answers.

Construction work runs under a separate statute with criminal penalties

The Missouri Construction Worker Independent Contractor Act (RSMo §§ 285.500–285.515) governs any worker performing construction services. The default is employee. The hiring party has to satisfy a nine-factor test to rebut the presumption.

Misclassifying a construction worker under the Act is a Class A misdemeanour and carries a civil penalty of $50 per misclassified worker per day. A five-worker crew misclassified for 500 working days produces $125,000 in civil penalties alone, before criminal prosecution, back payroll tax, and workers’ comp exposure.

The nine factors include: having a separate place of business, operating under a separate business name, owning or leasing their own equipment, having their own tools and materials, operating under a written contract that describes the service and payment, bearing financial risk for completing the job, operating independent of the hiring contractor’s supervision, holding required licences and certificates, and maintaining their own workers’ compensation and liability insurance.

Marcus runs a small electrical sub-contracting firm in Springfield. He is incorporated, holds a Missouri master electrician licence, carries his own liability and workers’ comp insurance, owns his equipment, works to a fixed-price scope for three different GCs simultaneously, and operates under a written sub-contractor agreement with each. He passes all nine factors. His workers are his own employees. If Marcus had no other active GC relationships, used the GC’s tools, and worked under daily direction from the site foreman, several factors would fail and the construction-employee presumption would not be rebutted.

Criminal exposure is real and prosecuted

RSMo § 285.515 makes wilful misclassification of a construction worker a Class A misdemeanour. Missouri prosecutors have used this provision. The civil penalty of $50 per worker per day runs concurrently with any criminal proceedings. The practical exposure for a mid-sized GC who books a framing crew on 1099 for two years is: Class A misdemeanour proceedings, $50 per worker per day civil penalty (up to $36,500 per worker per year), back payroll tax, back workers’ comp premium, and back state UI contributions.

One GC, one crew, two years: the penalty arithmetic

Three framers working 250 days per year, 1099’d for two years: civil penalty at $50/worker/day = $75,000 in civil penalties before any criminal proceedings. Back employer FICA at 7.65 percent on $150,000 in wages (three workers, $50k each, one year) = $11,475 per year, $22,950 over two years. Workers’ comp back premium at framing-industry NCCI rates (typically 15-25 percent of wages for framers) = $22,500 to $37,500 per year. Total exposure: $120,000 to $173,000, excluding legal fees, criminal defence costs, and state UI back premiums.

Missouri workers’ compensation uses a ten-factor reframe of the IRS test

Missouri workers’ compensation coverage is mandatory for employers with five or more employees in most industries (construction requires coverage from one employee). The Missouri Division of Workers’ Compensation applies a ten-factor test derived from the IRS common-law analysis.

The ten factors ask whether the worker controls their own methods, operates their own business, works for multiple clients, bears their own costs and risks, provides their own equipment, determines the time and place of work, is not integral to the hiring entity’s core operations, is paid by the job rather than by the hour, has no continuing employment relationship, and performs work that is not the primary business of the hiring entity.

Coverage for construction employers runs from employee one. A GC with three workers building a residential addition has to have workers’ comp. A tech company with three employees working remotely in Missouri has to reach five employees before the mandatory coverage threshold triggers.

The workers’ comp reading matters most when a worker is injured. At that point the Division reads back through the engagement file and applies the ten-factor analysis to determine whether the injured person was an employee (covered by the required policy) or a genuine independent contractor (not covered). An employer who has been booking a misclassified worker on 1099 faces the back-premium exposure, loses the independent-contractor defence, and becomes directly liable for the full workers’ comp claim.

Missouri sits in the mixed-test camp, between ABC states and pure common-law states

Missouri is mixed. The non-construction, non-UI reading follows the IRS common-law analysis, which is relatively business-friendly. The DES ABC test adds Prong B, which catches knowledge-work roles that the common-law test would classify as contractor. The Construction Worker Independent Contractor Act adds criminal exposure that no IRS analysis anticipates.

California, Massachusetts, and New Jersey run strict ABC tests for most industries. Texas, Florida, and Georgia run pure common-law. Missouri is closer to the common-law states for tech and professional services, then layers on an ABC test for UI and a construction-specific criminal regime that rivals any ABC-state penalty structure.

Two structural points anchor the Missouri picture for US multi-state employers:

  • The DES ABC Prong B gap. Any engagement where the contractor’s work is the same kind of work the hiring entity sells will fail Prong B for state UI. The IRS analysis says nothing about this. The DES does. Document the Prong B rationale in every Missouri engagement file.
  • The Construction Act criminal exposure. Missouri is one of a handful of states where construction misclassification is explicitly criminal. The $50/worker/day civil penalty is additive to criminal proceedings, not a substitute for them. The practical deterrent is significant. GCs who treat the construction-subcontracting line as a cost-management tool rather than a genuine independent enterprise face real risk.

Section 530 and the federal reasonable-basis defence

Missouri has no state statute that mirrors or displaces the federal Section 530 safe harbour. An employer who genuinely believed, and had industry practice or prior IRS guidance to support, that its contractor classification was correct may still cap the federal payroll-tax exposure under the reasonable-basis defence. Section 530 does not touch Missouri state-side withholding, SUI back premiums, workers’ comp back premium, or local earnings tax. Those run on Missouri statutes with no equivalent defence.

What a Missouri audit typically looks like

Most Missouri audits start through one of three triggers: a former contractor files a state UI claim with DES, a workplace injury generates a workers’ comp claim that the Division reviews, or the Department of Revenue selects an employer for a routine withholding audit. Once any one agency opens a determination, the reclassification tends to flow to the others through inter-agency data sharing.
