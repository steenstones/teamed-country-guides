Key facts at a glance

Missouri's minimum wage is $15.00/hour from 1st January 2026 (Prop A). Overtime is owed at 1.5x regular rate for any hours above 40 in a workweek (no daily trigger). No adult meal-break statute; federal paid-break rules apply instead. Tipped employees earn a minimum of $7.50/hour, with the employer required to top up to $15.00.

All rates are provisional. Missouri's 2025 legislature attempted to repeal or defer Prop A. The $15.00 rate is current law as of the date of this review but must be re-verified before payroll runs.

What is Missouri's minimum wage in 2026?

$15.00 per hour from 1st January 2026. Missouri voters passed Proposition A in November 2024, setting a two-step schedule: $13.75 for 2025, then $15.00 for 2026.

$15.00 / hour from Jan 2026
Prop A, 2024 ballot

Proposition A (2024) was approved by Missouri voters with approximately 57% in favour. The ballot measure replaced the prior annual CPI-indexed schedule with a fixed two-year ramp, then requires future annual increases tied to CPI from 2027. Missouri's higher rate governs where the employer is covered by both state and federal law; the federal floor of $7.25/hour applies only where the state exemption applies.

Legislative note

Missouri's 2025 legislative session included proposals to delay or reduce the Prop A $15.00 step. As of the date of this review, those proposals did not become law. Verify the current rate against Missouri Department of Labor before each payroll cycle.

Small business exemption

Retail or service businesses with annual gross revenue under $500,000 are exempt from the Missouri state minimum wage under RSMo 290.502. Those businesses must still comply with the federal $7.25/hour floor under the FLSA if they meet the FLSA's inter-state commerce tests. In practice, most employers of remote workers will be subject to the federal floor regardless of the state exemption.

Tipped employees

Missouri allows a tip credit. The tipped minimum wage is $7.50/hour (50% of the standard minimum wage), applicable to employees who customarily and regularly receive tips exceeding $20/month. Your obligation as employer: ensure every tipped employee's combined hourly wage and tips equal at least $15.00/hour for each hour worked in the pay period. Any shortfall must be made up in that same paycheck.

How does overtime work in Missouri?

Overtime is owed at 1.5x the regular rate for all hours worked beyond 40 in a single workweek. Missouri has no daily overtime trigger and no requirement to pay a premium for weekend or public holiday work unless the 40-hour threshold is reached.

RSMo 290.505 tracks the federal FLSA structure. The workweek is any fixed, regularly recurring period of 168 consecutive hours (7 consecutive 24-hour periods). You set the workweek definition when the worker is hired; you cannot change it retroactively to reduce overtime liability.

Regular rate of pay

Overtime is calculated at 1.5x the "regular rate," which is not simply the hourly wage. The regular rate must include all remuneration for employment except statutory exclusions: shift differentials, production bonuses, and non-discretionary bonuses must be factored in. Discretionary bonuses, holiday gifts under $25, overtime premiums already paid, and employer benefit contributions are excluded from the regular rate.

For salaried non-exempt employees, divide weekly salary by hours actually worked to arrive at the regular rate, then pay 0.5x that rate for each overtime hour (the salary already compensates the straight-time portion).

Who is exempt?

Missouri follows the FLSA white-collar exemptions. An employee is exempt from overtime if they meet all three of:

  • Salary level: paid at least $684/week ($35,568/year) on a salary or fee basis (the federal threshold reinstated after the 2024 court decision)
  • Salary basis: receives a predetermined, fixed salary not subject to reduction based on quality or quantity of work
  • Duties test: primary duties qualify as Executive, Administrative, Professional, Outside Sales, or Computer Employee under FLSA definitions
Federal overtime threshold note

The DOL's 2024 rule increasing the salary threshold to $43,888 (July 2024) and $58,656 (January 2025) was vacated by a federal court in November 2024. The reinstated threshold is $684/week. This is a fast-moving area; confirm the current federal level before relying on any exemption.

No daily overtime in Missouri

Unlike California, Nevada, and a handful of other states, Missouri imposes no daily overtime requirement. An employee who works 12 hours on Monday and nothing for the rest of the week owes no overtime under Missouri or federal law. The count resets each workweek.

Does Missouri require meal breaks or rest periods?

No. Missouri does not require employers to provide meal breaks or rest periods for employees aged 16 and over. The obligation to give (and pay for) breaks comes from federal FLSA rules and your employment contracts, not state law.

Missouri's Division of Labor Standards confirms there is no adult meal-break statute under Missouri law. This is a common source of payroll error when HR teams assume state law matches California's mandatory 30-minute unpaid meal break after 5 hours, or assume a universal break mandate applies. It does not in Missouri.

Federal FLSA rules that do apply

The FLSA does not require meal or rest breaks either, but it does govern how breaks must be paid when you provide them:

  • Short rest breaks (20 minutes or fewer): must be counted as compensable time and included in workweek hours for overtime purposes.
  • Bona fide meal periods (30 minutes or more): need not be paid, provided the employee is completely relieved of duties. If the employee must remain on-call and cannot use the break freely, it becomes compensable time.
Practical tip

Even without a legal mandate, most Missouri employers provide a 30-minute unpaid lunch break. Include break policy in your offer letter. If a role requires the employee to monitor systems or respond to queries during lunch, that time is compensable; track it or eliminate the on-call element to preserve unpaid status.

Minors (under 16)

Missouri's child labour provisions in RSMo 294.030 do require a rest break for minor employees. Workers under 16 must receive a 30-minute break after every 5 consecutive hours of work. This break may be unpaid.

Final pay and wage reduction rules in Missouri

Missouri requires all wages to be paid at termination. If an employee makes a certified-mail demand, you have 7 days to pay or face liability for up to 60 days' additional wages as a penalty.

Under RSMo 290.110, "wages" at termination includes all earned but unpaid wages, accrued vacation if your policy renders it payable on separation, and commissions that have been earned under the terms of your commission plan. Missouri courts interpret this broadly in favour of employees where the plan language is ambiguous.

Wage reduction notice

If you need to reduce a current employee's wage rate, Missouri requires 30 days' advance written notice before the reduction takes effect. Failure to give notice triggers a liability of $50 per employee for the violation. Issue the notice, document its delivery, and retain a copy.

07 Days to pay after certified demand

Employee sends certified mail demand for unpaid wages. You have 7 days. Miss it and Missouri law authorises a penalty of up to 60 additional days' wages. No grace period, no de minimis defence.

Payroll frequency

Missouri imposes no mandatory payroll frequency for most private employers. Biweekly or semi-monthly cycles are standard. Whatever frequency you choose, document it in the offer letter and apply it consistently; changing pay schedules without notice can be treated as an unlawful wage delay.
