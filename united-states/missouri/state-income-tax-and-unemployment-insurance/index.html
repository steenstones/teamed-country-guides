What is Missouri's state income tax rate for employer withholding in 2026?

Missouri's top income tax rate is 4.7% in 2026. Eight progressive brackets apply, starting at 0% on the first ~$1,313 of Missouri taxable income.

Missouri is on a glide path under SB 3 (2022). The top rate was 4.95% in 2023, dropped to 4.8% for 2024, and settled at 4.7% for 2025 and 2026. Further reductions to 4.5% are written into law but require revenue triggers: net general revenue must beat the prior three-year high by $175 million. Those triggers have not been met; 4.7% is the operative rate for 2026.

For employer withholding, you apply Missouri's eight brackets against each employee's annualised Missouri taxable wage, then divide back to the payroll period. Standard deductions reduce the base: $15,750 for single filers, $31,500 for married filing jointly. A supplemental wage method (flat 4.7%) is available for bonuses and commissions.

One notable relief for high-earning hires: Missouri allows a 100% capital gains subtraction from Missouri adjusted gross income effective 1st January 2025. Employees with stock compensation benefit disproportionately. Worth flagging on offer letters for software and finance roles.

Withholding is reported and paid quarterly via the Missouri Department of Revenue's online portal at dor.mo.gov. The annual reconciliation form is MO W-3.

What is Missouri's unemployment insurance tax rate and wage base for 2026?

Missouri's UI taxable wage base dropped to $9,000 for 2026, down $500 from 2025. New employers pay 2.376%.

$9,000
MO UI wage base 2026
Down $500 from 2025. Trust Fund drove the cut

Missouri's Unemployment Compensation Trust Fund balance triggers annual wage base adjustments. The base can drop by $500 or rise by $1,000, within a floor of $7,000 and ceiling of $13,000. The 2026 decrease means the maximum annual UI cost per new employee at 2.376% is $214, down from roughly $226 in 2025.

Experience rates run from 0.0% to 6.0% (excluding surcharges). Employers become eligible after two full calendar years of UI liability. Two overlays affect your actual rate: the Contribution Rate Adjustment (CRA), which can add 10%, 20%, or 30% to your rate if the Trust Fund is low, and the maximum rate surcharge of up to 1.5% for employers stuck at the maximum rate for multiple consecutive years.

An additional 0.020% Automation Adjustment applies to all taxable wages (effective 2023), earmarked to Missouri's Unemployment Automation Fund. This sits outside the 0%–6% range calculation.

All UI filings go through uinteract.labor.mo.gov, Missouri's online employer portal. Quarterly reports are due within one month of each quarter-end. Late filing attracts interest and loss of the good-standing rate protection.

Do Missouri employers need to withhold local earnings taxes?

Yes, if any employee works in Kansas City or St. Louis city. Both levy a 1% earnings tax that employers must withhold and remit separately from state tax.

Kansas City and St. Louis city are the only two Missouri jurisdictions with an earnings tax. The rate is 1% in both cases. Employers with workers inside either city boundary must register separately with the relevant city revenue office and withhold accordingly. The Kansas City tax applies to both residents and non-residents earning wages within KC limits. The St. Louis city tax applies to wages earned within the city boundary; employees in suburban St. Louis County (Chesterfield, Clayton, Creve Coeur) are not subject.

Both taxes renew via five-year voter approval under Missouri's Proposition A (2010). They were most recently renewed and are not at risk of repeal in the near term.

For out-of-state companies without employees in KC or STL city limits, local earnings tax is a non-issue. The vast majority of Missouri remote workers will have no local earnings tax obligation. If your first Missouri hire is in Kansas City, Teamed handles the KC registration and withholding automatically; you don't set up a separate payroll account.

One gotcha: the KC earnings tax applies to workers earning wages within city limits even if they're employed by a company headquartered elsewhere. A contractor incorrectly classified as a vendor who actually works from a KC office is still subject to the earnings tax. Misclassification in KC carries the same $50–$1,000 daily penalty as state law, applied per worker.

What are Missouri's wage, overtime, and worker classification rules?

Missouri's minimum wage is $15.00 per hour from 1st January 2026, up from $13.75 in 2025. Overtime kicks in at 40 hours per week at 1.5x the regular rate.

Missouri uses the IRS 20-Factor Test to determine worker classification, not the ABC test used by California and New Jersey. The primary factor is the right of control: whether the hiring party can direct how (not just whether) the work is done. Misclassification penalties run from $50 to $1,000 per day per worker under RSMo § 288.045. For a team of five contractors reclassified after an audit, that exposure compounds fast.

At-will employment applies with standard carve-outs: employment contracts, collective bargaining agreements, and the public policy exception (employees who file wage complaints or assert legal rights are protected from retaliation). Missouri's MHRA (Missouri Human Rights Act) covers race, sex, disability, national origin, religion, and ancestry discrimination.

Termination pay rules are strict on timing. Final wages are due at the point of separation. If an employee sends a written demand via certified mail and wages remain unpaid for seven days, Missouri courts can award up to 60 additional days of wages as a penalty. This is not a theoretical risk in wage dispute filings.

Tipped employees receive a minimum cash wage of $7.50 per hour, with the employer obligated to bring total compensation to $15.00 through tips. Employers with annual gross revenue under $500,000 are exempt from Missouri's state minimum wage law (but must still comply with federal FLSA minimums, currently $7.25).

What do out-of-state companies need to know before hiring in Missouri?

Missouri's glide-path tax cuts attract talent, but the KC and St. Louis local earnings taxes, plus the UI wage base reduction, are the two numbers most payroll setups get wrong in the first quarter.

Three things that catch first-time Missouri employers off guard. First, the UI wage base fell to $9,000 for 2026. Any quote or model built on the 2025 figure of $9,500 overstates your UI cost by roughly $12 per employee. Small delta, but it multiplies across a team and it's an easy check Teamed runs at every renewal.

Second, Kansas City's 1% earnings tax is a registration requirement, not just a withholding line. If you have a Missouri employee working in KC and you're not registered with the KC Revenue Division, you're running an unregistered payroll exposure. Most national payroll processors set this up correctly when prompted, but it's often missed on initial setup for companies new to Missouri.

Third, the flat-tax proposals circulating in the Missouri legislature (including Governor Kehoe's five-year plan to repeal individual income tax) are not yet law. Plan for 4.7% until legislation is enacted and signed. Teamed monitors Missouri legislative sessions and pushes rate updates to your payroll configuration the day a change becomes effective. You don't track the General Assembly; we do.

Missouri has no state paid family leave programme and no state disability insurance mandate. The payroll tax layer is cleaner than California, New York, or Washington, but the local earnings tax in two major cities adds a registration step that out-of-state companies regularly skip.

Frequently asked questions about Missouri employer taxes

What is Missouri's state income tax rate for employers in 2026?

Missouri uses eight progressive brackets in 2026, from 0% on the first ~$1,313 to 4.7% on income above ~$9,191. This is the third reduction under SB 3 (2022); the rate was 4.95% in 2023, 4.8% in 2024, and 4.7% from 2025 onward. Further cuts to 4.5% are contingent on revenue triggers not yet met. Employers apply these brackets against each employee's annualised Missouri taxable wages for withholding purposes.

What is Missouri's unemployment insurance wage base in 2026?

Missouri's UI taxable wage base is $9,000 per employee for 2026, down $500 from the 2025 figure of $9,500. The state adjusts the base annually based on the Unemployment Compensation Trust Fund balance. The floor is $7,000 and the ceiling is $13,000. At the new employer rate of 2.376%, maximum annual UI cost per employee is $214.08 in 2026.

What is the new employer UI rate in Missouri for 2026?

New employers in Missouri are assigned 2.376% for 2026 (the higher of the industry-average rate for their division or 2.7%). Nonprofit 501(c)(3) organisations are assigned 1.0%. After two full calendar years of UI liability, employers qualify for experience-rated assessment between 0.0% and 6.0%. The Contribution Rate Adjustment and maximum rate surcharge can push effective rates higher depending on Trust Fund balance.

Does Missouri have a local earnings tax employers must withhold?

Yes. Kansas City and St. Louis city each levy a 1% earnings tax on wages earned within city limits. Employers with workers inside either boundary must register separately with the city and withhold accordingly. Suburban St. Louis County employees are not subject to the St. Louis city earnings tax. Both taxes operate on five-year voter renewal cycles under Missouri Proposition A (2010).
