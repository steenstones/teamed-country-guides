Does Nevada have state income tax?

No. Nevada's constitution prohibits income tax on natural persons. There is no state income tax withholding, no state income tax tables, and no state income tax reconciliation filing for Nevada employers.

The Nevada Constitution (Article 10, Section 1) bans income tax on individuals, and the Nevada legislature has never enacted a corporate income tax either. For employers, this means the payroll compliance stack is simpler than any other state with a wage-heavy workforce. You do not register a state withholding account, do not deduct state income tax from paychecks, and do not file a state version of the W-2 reconciliation.

The taxes Nevada does impose on employers are different in character. The Modified Business Tax (MBT) is a quarterly payroll tax on the employer side, not a withholding tax. Unemployment Insurance (UI) is a standard US employer-only tax on the first portion of wages. Neither appears on the employee's paycheck stub as a state deduction.

No state income tax is a genuine employer advantage in Nevada. Your payroll administration cost is lower, onboarding is faster, and out-of-state employers frequently underestimate how clean the Nevada payroll set-up is until they've run it.

What is Nevada's Modified Business Tax and how is it calculated?

The MBT is 1.17% on quarterly gross wages above $50,000, after deducting employer-paid health costs. Financial institutions pay 1.554% with no exemption.

Nevada's payroll tax (not income tax).

Every Nevada employer registered under the Unemployment Compensation Law (NRS 612) must file an MBT return quarterly, even if no tax is owed. The $50,000 quarterly exemption means smaller-payroll employers often owe nothing. A business with a single employee earning $60,000 a year pays $60,000 / 4 = $15,000 per quarter. That is well under the $50,000 threshold, so the MBT bill is zero. Once quarterly wages (after health deductions) exceed $50,000, only the amount above $50,000 is taxed.

Health benefit deductions are broad. Employers may deduct amounts paid for employee health insurance premiums, dental, and vision coverage, which can materially reduce the taxable wage base, particularly for employers offering rich benefits packages. Self-insured costs and other authorised health plan payments also qualify.

One 2026 change to factor in: the Nevada Department of Taxation resumed its MBT Wage Comparison program on 1st January 2026. The Department now cross-references gross wages reported on MBT returns against wage data submitted to DETR for UI purposes. If your MBT and UI returns report different wage figures without an explainable deduction, expect a follow-up inquiry from both agencies.

What are Nevada's unemployment insurance tax rates in 2026?

New Nevada employers pay 3.00% (2.95% base + 0.05% CEP surcharge) on the first $43,700 per employee per year. That's a maximum of $1,311 per new employee annually.

Nevada's $43,700 taxable wage base for 2026 is up from $41,800 in 2025. The base adjusts each year at two-thirds of Nevada's average annual wage (NRS 612). New employers keep the 3.00% combined rate for 14 to 17 calendar quarters, after which Nevada's Employment Security Division calculates an experience rate. Experienced-employer rates range from 0.30% to 5.40%. A well-run employer with low turnover can reach rates well below the national average.

The 0.05% Career Enhancement Program (CEP) surcharge funds workforce retraining and applies to all employers on top of the base UI rate. Quarterly UI returns are filed through Nevada's NUI employer portal (nui.nv.gov). The same MBT Wage Comparison program that launched January 2026 means your UI-reported wages and MBT-reported wages will be cross-checked. File both from the same source data.

At the new-employer rate, a Nevada hire at $80,000 salary costs a maximum of $1,311 in UI taxes for the year (3.00% x $43,700). That's below the US median for new-employer UI cost in states with a comparable wage base, primarily because Nevada has no state income tax employer component on top of it.

What are Nevada's minimum wage and overtime rules in 2026?

Nevada's minimum wage is $12.00 per hour for all employers, unified since July 2024. Daily overtime after 8 hours applies only to employees earning under $18.00 per hour.

The two-tier minimum wage Nevada operated from 2006 through 30th June 2024 is gone. Under the old structure, employers offering qualifying health insurance could pay $1.00 less per hour. A 2024 constitutional amendment ended the differential; every Nevada employee earns $12.00/hr or more regardless of benefits offered. Legacy HR compliance guides still reference the old $11/$12 split. Any offer letter citing a health-insurance-contingent wage rate is non-compliant as of 1st July 2024.

Nevada layers its own overtime rules (NRS 608.018) on top of federal FLSA. Weekly overtime applies after 40 hours at 1.5x for all employees. Daily overtime (1.5x after 8 hours in a workday) applies only to employees earning less than 1.5 times the state minimum wage ($18.00/hr threshold in 2026). High earners do not trigger Nevada's daily overtime rule; standard FLSA weekly overtime still applies to them.

Nevada defines a workday as any 24 consecutive hours under NRS 608.0126, not a calendar day. An employee whose shift runs 6pm to 3am the next day works a single 9-hour Nevada workday, with 1 hour at overtime rate, even though it spans midnight.

Rest and meal breaks are mandatory: 10 minutes for every 4 hours worked, and 30 minutes after 8 continuous hours.

What paid leave must Nevada employers provide?

Nevada SB 312 requires employers with 50 or more employees to provide 40 hours of paid leave per year, usable for any reason. There is no state PFML and no state disability insurance program.

SB 312 (now codified at NRS 608.0197) applies to private employers with 50 or more Nevada employees who have been in operation for more than two years. Employees accrue 0.01923 hours of paid leave for every hour worked, reaching 40 hours annually for a full-time employee. The leave can be used for any reason; employers cannot require employees to explain why they're taking it. Employees may use up to half their accrued balance to care for immediate family members.

Unused leave carries over up to 40 hours. Employers who frontload the full 40 hours at the start of the benefit year eliminate the carryover obligation entirely. AB 305 (effective 1st January 2026) caps the fee a healthcare provider may charge an employee for completing a federal FMLA certification form at $30. This affects how employers communicate the FMLA documentation process.

Nevada has no state paid family and medical leave programme and no state short-term disability insurance. Workers needing extended leave rely on federal FMLA (available at employers with 50 or more employees, the same threshold as SB 312) and any employer-provided benefits. Private employers are not required to offer paid holidays; holiday policy is contractual.

How do you terminate an employee in Nevada?

Nevada is at-will. You can end employment for any legal reason without notice. On discharge, all wages are due immediately, same day, under NRS 608.020.

Nevada's at-will rule allows either party to end employment for any reason, or no reason, as long as it's not an illegal one (discrimination, retaliation, etc.). Most private-sector employees in Nevada have no contractual notice period unless their offer letter or employment agreement specifies one. If you've written a notice clause into the contract, you're bound by it.

The most operationally demanding Nevada termination rule is the final paycheck timing. When you discharge an employee, all earned wages become due and payable on the same day as termination (NRS 608.020). This means you need a process to calculate and issue the final paycheck immediately, including accrued vacation or PTO if your policy makes it payable on exit. For a voluntary resignation, you have more flexibility: pay by the next regularly scheduled payday or within 7 days of the resignation, whichever is earlier (NRS 608.030).

Miss those deadlines and NRS 608.040 allows the employee to claim a penalty of up to 30 days' additional wages, a risk that can easily exceed the original paycheck amount. Nevada has no statutory severance requirement; any severance is contractual. How does Nevada classify independent contractors?

Nevada uses the NRS 608.0155 '123 test' for wage and hour purposes. Misclassification penalties run $2,500 per employee for the first offence and $5,000 per employee after that.

NRS 608.0155 creates a three-part test for independent contractor status under Nevada's wage and hour laws. To classify a worker as a contractor, all three must be satisfied: the person is free from direction and control over their work (by contract and in fact); the service falls outside the hirer's usual course of business or is performed away from the hirer's premises; and the person operates in an established independent trade or business of the same nature as the work performed.

One significant limitation: for claims arising under Nevada's Constitutional Minimum Wage Amendment, courts apply the harder federal economic realities test instead of the NRS 608.0155 three-part test. The economic realities test is more difficult for employers to satisfy. Courts look at the full economic relationship, not just the contract language and work location. A worker who passes the 608.0155 test for overtime purposes may still be an employee for minimum wage purposes under the same arrangement.

The Nevada Labor Commissioner enforces misclassification penalties at $2,500 for the first misclassified employee and $5,000 for each subsequent one. What do out-of-state companies most often get wrong about Nevada payroll?

Three things catch out-of-state employers: the MBT wage comparison cross-check that resumed January 2026, the eliminated two-tier minimum wage that still circulates in old HR guides, and the workers' comp cap change landing October 2026.

First, the MBT Wage Comparison program: as of 1st January 2026, the Nevada Department of Taxation cross-references MBT returns against UI wage reports. If your MBT payroll and your UI payroll don't reconcile each quarter, both agencies will notice. Employers who handle MBT and UI through different service providers or different departments often discover the gap at audit time rather than at filing time. Filing both from the same data source is the simplest fix.

Second, the two-tier minimum wage: Nevada eliminated the health-insurance differential on 1st July 2024. Employers who adopted the $11/hr lower tier before the change and didn't update their offer templates after it are non-compliant. Nevada's minimum wage is $12.00/hr for everyone, regardless of benefits offered. Check offer letters for any health-contingent wage language before bringing on Nevada hires.

Third, SB 317 workers' compensation: effective 1st October 2026, the payroll cap used to calculate workers' comp premiums rises from $36,000 to approximately $98,400 per employee. Higher-salary Nevada roles will see premium increases at their next policy renewal. Budget for this now if you're hiring technical or leadership roles in Nevada during 2026.
