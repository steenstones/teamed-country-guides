What is Nevada’s worker classification test and how does it work?

Nevada runs three tests in parallel: NRS 608.0155 three-part test for wage and hour, a six-factor ESD test for UI, and the IRS 20-factor test for federal tax. A contractor can pass one and fail another simultaneously.

Nevada is not California. Most out-of-state employers approach Nevada contracting thinking the state has a straightforward common-law test — and they're partly right. NRS 608.0155 is more contractor-friendly than California's ABC test.

But Nevada runs three tests simultaneously, not one. The NRS 608.0155 wage-and-hour test, the Nevada ESD six-factor UI test, and the IRS 20-factor federal tax test each reach independent conclusions. A contractor who passes the state wage test can still trigger UI contributions or federal FICA liability on the same engagement.

And the NRS 608.0155 test has a carve-out that most guides skip: it doesn’t protect you on minimum wage claims. Those go through the federal economic realities test, which is harder to satisfy.

SB 224 (2023) increased the civil penalty stack for misclassification. $2,500 for the first misclassified worker. $5,000 for every one after that.

Most states run either a common-law balancing test or an ABC presumption-of-employee test. Nevada runs a hybrid. The NRS 608.0155 test governs the bulk of Nevada employment law obligations, including overtime, paid leave, rest breaks, and final pay rules. But the legislature carved out minimum wage claims, which continue to answer to the federal economic realities test — a more worker-protective standard that looks at the full economic dependency of the relationship, not just the contract terms and work location.

The Nevada Employment Security Division (NESD/ESD) applies its own six-factor test for unemployment insurance purposes under NRS 612.085. The ESD test overlaps significantly with the NRS 608.0155 criteria but has its own analytical framework and its own enforcement track. An auditor from the Labor Commissioner (applying NRS 608.0155) and an auditor from DETR (applying NRS 612.085) looking at the same contractor relationship can reach different conclusions.

For federal FICA and FUTA, the IRS 20-factor common-law test applies regardless of Nevada state law. The IRS looks at behavioural control, financial control, and the type of relationship. Passing Nevada’s NRS 608.0155 test does not automatically mean federal payroll taxes do not apply.

The practical implication: when you classify someone as a Nevada contractor, you need to run the analysis under all three frameworks before the engagement starts, not just the state wage-and-hour test most compliance guides describe.

What are the three parts of Nevada’s NRS 608.0155 test?

All three parts must be satisfied. If any one fails, Nevada treats the worker as an employee for wage and hour purposes. The test is cumulative, not a balance-of-factors analysis.

PartStatutory requirement (NRS 608.0155)What it actually tests
1 The person is free from the control or direction in the performance of the service, both under the contract and in fact. Control over how the work gets done — both on paper and in the day-to-day working relationship. A worker who receives daily task lists, uses company tools, and has their hours set by the hirer fails Part 1 regardless of what the contract says.
2 The service is either outside the usual course of business of the employer, or the service is performed outside of all the places of business of the enterprise. This is an either/or: if the work is outside the hirer’s usual course of business, Part 2 passes regardless of where it’s performed. Alternatively, if the work is done entirely off the hirer’s premises (home office, the worker’s own studio, etc.), Part 2 also passes — even if the work is core to the business.
3 The person is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession or business of the same nature as that involved in the contract of service. The worker must operate a real, pre-existing independent business — not one created specifically for this engagement. Other clients, public marketing, a separate business presence, and a history of similar work for others all point toward Part 3 passing.

Part 2 is what makes Nevada different from California

California’s ABC test under AB5 requires that the work falls outside the usual course of business, full stop. There is no location alternative. A developer writing code for a software company fails California’s Prong B automatically, regardless of where they work.

Nevada’s NRS 608.0155 Part 2 is an either/or. That same developer, working fully remotely from their own home office, may satisfy Part 2 in Nevada even if the work is core to the hirer’s business. The “outside all places of business” branch creates a meaningful path through the test that California does not offer.

This distinction drives a genuine gap in contractor risk between Nevada and California operations. A remote software contractor who would be a mandatory employee in California under AB5 may be validly classified as a contractor in Nevada under NRS 608.0155, assuming Parts 1 and 3 also hold.

The minimum wage carve-out

NRS 608.0155 explicitly states that the presumption it creates does not apply to claims arising under Nevada’s Constitutional Minimum Wage Amendment. For those claims, courts apply the federal economic realities test, which looks at the full picture of economic dependence rather than the three-part structure. A worker who satisfies all three parts of NRS 608.0155 — and is therefore a contractor for overtime and leave purposes — can still be classified as an employee for minimum wage purposes under the same arrangement. The exposure is niche but real in low-rate classifications where the hourly equivalent of 1099 payments approaches minimum wage territory.

What “independently established” means in practice

Part 3 requires a business that exists independently of the engagement, not one set up specifically for the contract. The best evidence: multiple clients in the same field, public marketing or a business website, a registered trade name or LLC, separate professional insurance, and a record of prior similar engagements. A sole proprietor with one client and no public presence of their own fails Part 3 even with a clean contractor agreement in place. The agreement is not itself the business.

How does Nevada’s unemployment insurance classification test differ from NRS 608.0155?

The Nevada ESD applies a six-factor test under NRS 612.085, separate from the NRS 608.0155 wage-and-hour analysis. Both tests cover similar ground but the ESD test is a balancing framework, not a three-part conjunctive test.

NRS 612.085 sets the framework Nevada’s Employment Security Division uses to determine whether services are performed in “employment” for UI contribution purposes. The statute creates an exemption from UI “employment” only if the claimant can show that the individual: (1) was free from control or direction in the performance of services; (2) performed services outside the usual course of business or outside all places of business; (3) customarily engaged in an independently established trade or business; (4) had the legal right to terminate or leave the relationship without incurring legal liability; (5) incurred business expenses not reimbursed by the service recipient; and (6) had an investment in the tools, equipment, or facilities used.

The overlap with NRS 608.0155 is substantial (factors 1 through 3 mirror the three parts exactly), but the ESD test adds investment-in-the-business and expense-risk dimensions. A contractor who passes the wage-and-hour test but whose costs are fully reimbursed and who uses hirer-provided equipment may still be classified as an employee for UI purposes. The ESD runs its own audits independently from the Nevada Labor Commissioner.

For the practical employer, the safest position is one that satisfies both tests simultaneously. Where the engagement is designed to pass NRS 608.0155 through the “performed off hirer’s premises” route under Part 2, pay close attention to UI factors 5 and 6 — the contractor should carry real business expenses and use their own kit, not just work from a different location using hirer-provided systems.

How does the IRS 20-factor test apply to Nevada contractors?

The IRS 20-factor common-law test governs federal payroll tax (FICA and FUTA) for every Nevada employer. It operates independently of NRS 608.0155. Passing Nevada’s state test does not mean federal payroll taxes are off the table.

The IRS 20-factor test, set out in Revenue Ruling 87-41, groups facts across three dimensions: behavioural control (who directs how the work is done), financial control (who bears financial risk and investment), and the type of relationship (written contracts, benefits, permanency). No single factor is decisive. The pattern of facts across all 20 determines the answer.

Nevada employers who classify workers as contractors must satisfy the federal test independently. The most common points of divergence are:

  • Integration. If the contractor’s work is integral to the business’s operations, the IRS counts that as an employee indicator. Nevada’s Part 2 location alternative doesn’t change the federal integration analysis.
  • Continuing relationship. Multi-year contractor engagements with the same hirer signal employee status to the IRS, even where NRS 608.0155 would pass on the same facts.
  • Right to discharge. If you can end the relationship at will without legal consequence, that cuts toward employee under the IRS test.

The practical implication: a Nevada contractor engagement that satisfies NRS 608.0155 (including the off-premises route through Part 2) can still trigger FICA and FUTA as a matter of federal law. For roles that pass Nevada’s state test through the location branch, run the IRS 20-factor analysis as a second check. The federal liability in a mismatch case includes back FICA (employer and employee shares), FUTA, penalties, and interest.

Does construction work have different classification rules in Nevada?

Yes. Construction contractors in Nevada must hold a Nevada State Contractors Board (NSCB) licence under NRS 624. Unlicensed construction workers cannot be lawfully classified as independent contractors for that work regardless of how the NRS 608.0155 test scores.

NRS 624 requires a licence for any contractor who undertakes construction, alteration, repair, demolition, or improvement of any structure for a value exceeding $1,000. The licensing requirement is independent of the employment classification analysis. An unlicensed individual performing construction work for hire is not a lawful independent contractor under Nevada law, even if the NRS 608.0155 three-part test would otherwise be satisfied. The result is that misclassification exposure in construction combines the NRS 608.0155 civil penalties with NSCB enforcement, which includes its own civil penalties and licensing sanctions.

For general contractors and subcontractor relationships: the prime contractor has independent obligations to verify that subcontractors hold valid NSCB licences. A prime contractor who engages an unlicensed individual as a “subcontractor” faces both NSCB penalty exposure and the NRS 608.0155 misclassification liability as if the individual were an employee.

Nevada’s construction misclassification enforcement has historically been active. The state’s large tourism and commercial construction market creates a regular audit pattern around prevailing-wage and unlicensed-subcontractor issues. SB 224’s 2023 civil penalty enhancements apply equally to construction misclassification cases.

What are Nevada’s misclassification penalties after SB 224?

Nevada’s Labor Commissioner may impose $2,500 per misclassified worker for a first violation and $5,000 per misclassified worker for each subsequent violation. SB 224 (2023) enhanced the civil penalty framework and expanded enforcement tools.

Civil penalties per misclassified worker are the floor, not the ceiling. On top of civil penalties, a successful misclassification finding carries:

  • Back wages. Overtime premiums, meal-break penalties, and minimum-wage gaps for the period of misclassification, calculated from the date the relationship began. Nevada’s late-pay penalty (NRS 608.040) adds up to 30 days’ additional wages for final-pay violations that arose during the relationship.
  • UI back contributions. DETR can recover unpaid unemployment insurance employer contributions for the misclassified period, plus interest and penalties under NRS 612.
  • Workers’ comp exposure. An employer who did not carry workers’ comp for a misclassified worker faces the full premium gap plus penalties under NRS 616B.627 if the worker was injured during the engagement.
  • Federal payroll tax. Back FICA (both employer and employee shares if the employer cannot recover the employee portion), FUTA, and IRS penalties on top.

SB 224 (2023) added enhancements to the civil penalty structure for misclassification cases. The full final form of SB 224’s penalty provisions should be verified against the enrolled act, as enacted language may differ from the bill as introduced. The $2,500/$5,000 per-worker framework was already in place prior to SB 224 under NRS 608.0155; SB 224 layered additional enforcement mechanisms on top.

The Labour Commissioner can investigate on its own initiative, not just in response to worker complaints. Nevada has historically used payroll audit cross-referencing — the same mechanism that drives the 2026 MBT Wage Comparison program — to identify contractor-heavy payrolls for classification review.

How does Nevada’s contractor test compare to other US states?

Nevada sits in the middle of the spectrum — more permissive than California, stricter than pure common-law states like Texas and Florida. The location branch in Part 2 is the key differentiator that makes Nevada more contractor-accessible than most ABC-test states.

StateClassification standardKey characteristic
California ABC test (Cal. Lab. Code § 2775, AB5) Strictest in US. Employee presumption. Prong B kills most knowledge-work contractor arrangements.
Massachusetts ABC test (M.G.L. c. 149 § 148B) Three-part conjunctive. Prong B mirrors California, no location alternative. Mandatory treble damages on violation.
Nevada NRS 608.0155 three-part test (wage & hour) Part 2 either/or: outside usual course of business OR performed off hirer’s premises. More path than ABC states. No presumption of employee status.
Texas IRS common-law test (state and federal aligned) Balance-of-factors. No ABC test. No presumption. Historically contractor-friendly.
Florida Common-law control test (FS 448) Multi-factor control analysis. No presumption. No ABC structure. UI test is separate statutory framework.
New Jersey ABC test (N.J.S.A. 43:21-19(i)(6)(A)-(C)) ABC for UI. Prong B similar to California. Separate common-law test for some wage claims.

The practical takeaway for multi-state employers: the same remote contractor role that is unworkable in California (Prong B) and Massachusetts (Prong B, treble damages) may be properly classifiable in Nevada if the worker controls how they do the work, works off your premises, and runs an established independent business. The work being core to your business does not automatically fail Part 2 in Nevada if the off-premises branch applies.

What Nevada does not offer is the federal protection that some IRS-aligned states provide through Section 530 of the Revenue Act of 1978. Section 530 provides a safe harbour from federal payroll tax liability if a business relied on a long-standing, reasonable classification practice and treated workers consistently. Nevada’s state civil penalties are not subject to Section 530. Only the federal FICA/FUTA piece of a misclassification claim benefits from Section 530 analysis.

What are the most common Nevada contractor misclassification red flags?

Four patterns generate the bulk of Nevada misclassification findings: single-client contractors without an independent business, contractors performing work on hirer-controlled premises using hirer equipment, long-tenure engagements with no other clients, and construction relationships with unlicensed subcontractors.

Single-client contractor with no public business presence. If the worker has no other clients, no website, no trade name, no prior history of similar engagements for other hirers, and signed up specifically for your project, Part 3 of NRS 608.0155 fails. The absence of an “independently established” business predates the engagement. The Labour Commissioner looks for evidence the business existed and operated prior to your contract, not just evidence the worker signed a 1099 agreement.

Work performed on hirer’s premises using hirer equipment. NRS 608.0155 Part 2 offers the off-premises route as an either/or with the outside-usual-business route. If the work happens at your office, on your laptops, using your project management systems and your email accounts, the location branch of Part 2 fails. You then need to rely on the outside-usual-course-of-business branch, which returns you to the same analysis that California applies.

Long-term exclusive engagements. A contractor who has worked exclusively for your business for 18 months, has no other clients, and whose business would disappear if your contract ended faces significant Part 3 exposure. The IRS integration factor also reaches strongly toward employee on these facts. Duration alone doesn’t determine status, but duration combined with exclusivity is the pattern that draws auditor attention.

Construction with unlicensed subcontractors. Any construction engagement in Nevada where the subcontractor lacks an NSCB licence creates classification exposure regardless of how the NRS 608.0155 test scores. The licence is a statutory prerequisite for lawful contractor status in covered construction work, not an optional marker of independent business status.

Cross-state reclassification overhang. An out-of-state employer who previously classified a worker as a contractor in a common-law state (Texas, Florida) and is now engaging the same worker for Nevada-based work needs a fresh NRS 608.0155 analysis on the Nevada engagement. The prior classification doesn’t carry. The Nevada facts determine the Nevada answer.

Frequently asked questions about Nevada worker classification

What test does Nevada use to classify independent contractors in 2026?

Nevada applies three separate tests. For wage and hour (overtime, paid leave, rest breaks), NRS 608.0155 applies a three-part conjunctive test. For unemployment insurance contributions, the Nevada ESD applies a six-factor test under NRS 612.085. For federal payroll tax (FICA, FUTA), the IRS 20-factor common-law test applies regardless of how the state tests resolve. A contractor can pass one test and fail another on the same set of facts.

What are Nevada’s worker misclassification penalties?

The Nevada Labor Commissioner may impose civil penalties of $2,500 per misclassified worker for a first violation and $5,000 per misclassified worker for subsequent violations under NRS 608.0155. SB 224 (2023) enhanced the civil penalty framework. Back wages, UI contributions, workers’ comp premiums, and federal payroll tax liability layer on top.

Does Nevada’s three-part test cover all employment law claims?

No. NRS 608.0155 expressly excludes minimum wage claims under Nevada’s Constitutional Minimum Wage Amendment. Those claims use the federal economic realities test, which is harder to satisfy. Construction work is separately subject to NSCB licensing requirements under NRS 624, and an unlicensed construction worker cannot be a lawful contractor regardless of the NRS 608.0155 analysis.

How is Nevada’s contractor test different from California’s?

The key structural difference is in Part/Prong 2. California’s ABC test requires the work to fall outside the hirer’s usual course of business, full stop. Nevada’s NRS 608.0155 offers an alternative: the service may satisfy Part 2 if it is performed outside all of the hirer’s places of business, even if the work itself is core to the business. A remote developer working from home may pass Nevada’s Part 2 through the off-premises route while failing California’s Prong B on the same role.
