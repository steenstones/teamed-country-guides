Does Nevada require paid leave in 2026?

Yes — and it's broader than most US states. Nevada's SB 312 (NRS 608.0197) requires employers with 50 or more employees to let workers accrue up to 40 hours of paid leave per year — usable for any reason, with no documentation required. There is no state paid family and medical leave programme.

Nevada is the rare US state that mandates paid leave but makes it usable for any reason. That one word — any — is the source of most Nevada employer confusion. You're not tracking "sick hours" and "personal hours" separately; you're tracking a single paid-leave bank that employees spend however they choose.

The SB 312 obligation under NRS 608.0197 has been live since 1st January 2020. Employers with 50 or more Nevada employees who have been in business more than two years must run the accrual. New employers get a two-year runway before the obligation switches on.

What Nevada does not have is a state paid family and medical leave (PFML) programme. There's no state payroll deduction, no benefit portal, no state-funded wage replacement for a parent on parental leave. The 40-hour any-reason bank is it, below the federal FMLA floor of 12 weeks unpaid.

This page covers the SB 312 accrual mechanics, the FMLA overlay at 50 employees, the 2026 AB 305 update on FMLA certification costs, and the voluntary policy decisions that determine whether your Nevada offer letter is competitive.

What does Nevada's SB 312 require from employers?

Nevada's SB 312, codified at NRS 608.0197, requires any private employer with 50 or more Nevada employees in business for more than two years to let employees accrue paid leave at 0.01923 hours per hour worked — roughly 40 hours per year for a full-time employee.

The law is deliberately broad. Use is not restricted to illness or caregiving. An employee can take the leave for a dental appointment, a child's school event, a personal errand, or no stated reason at all. After the 90-day waiting period from hire, the employee requests the time and you process it. You cannot ask for a doctor's note or documentation.

Sofia is the HR director at a 65-person Las Vegas hospitality company. She has one Nevada employee who uses all 40 hours each year in small increments — a few hours here, a morning there — because the law allows any-reason use in partial-day increments. Sofia doesn't need to record a reason. She runs the accrual ledger and pays the leave when requested. That's the full obligation.

The two-year new-employer runway

An employer who has been in business for fewer than two years is exempt from SB 312, regardless of headcount. This gives genuinely new Nevada businesses a runway to build payroll systems before the accrual obligation switches on. The trigger date is two years from the employer's initial Nevada business registration, not two years from when it hit 50 employees.

A startup that registered in Nevada in Q1 2024 and grew to 60 employees by Q1 2025 was not yet covered under SB 312 until Q1 2026. From that point forward, the accrual obligation applies retroactively to the full workforce. Setting up the tracking system before the two-year mark, rather than scrambling at the deadline, is the standard practice Teamed recommends.

Does Nevada have a state paid family and medical leave programme?

No. Nevada has no state-funded paid family and medical leave (PFML) programme. There is no state payroll deduction, no Nevada PFML claim portal, and no state-funded wage replacement when an employee takes parental leave or leave for a serious health condition.

This is the sharpest divide in Nevada leave law. The 40-hour SB 312 bank covers any reason but runs out quickly — roughly five standard working days. A Nevada employee on parental leave beyond those five days is on unpaid leave (FMLA-protected if eligible) or whatever voluntary paid parental leave the employer funds.

Marcus is a software engineer at a 55-person Reno SaaS company. His partner is pregnant. He plans to take parental leave. Nevada's SB 312 gives him up to 40 hours of any-reason paid leave he can put toward that leave. After those 40 hours, if his employer hasn't established a voluntary paid parental leave policy, he's on unpaid FMLA leave (assuming he meets the 12-month / 1,250-hour eligibility requirement).

Compare that to California, where a PFL benefit replaces up to 60 to 70 percent of wages for 8 weeks funded through employee payroll deduction. Nevada has no equivalent. The voluntary parental-leave policy is entirely the employer's decision and cost.

What does federal FMLA give Nevada employees?

Federal FMLA gives qualifying Nevada employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per 12-month period. It applies to employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius, with the employee having at least 12 months of tenure and 1,250 hours worked in the preceding 12 months.

For Nevada, FMLA threshold and SB 312 threshold are identical at 50 employees. In practice, the moment a Nevada private employer crosses 50 employees and has been in business two years, both obligations switch on simultaneously. The leave laws stack rather than replace each other.

When Marcus uses his 40 hours of SB 312 any-reason leave during his parental leave, those hours can run concurrently with FMLA leave — the FMLA clock ticks while he's using the SB 312 paid hours. His job-protected FMLA period is not extended by the paid leave; the two run together.

AB 305: the 2026 FMLA certification fee cap

Assembly Bill 305, effective 1st January 2026, caps the fee a healthcare provider may charge a Nevada employee for completing a federal FMLA medical certification form at $30. Before AB 305, providers could charge whatever they chose for the paperwork, sometimes $75 to $150, creating a barrier for employees seeking leave certification.

For employers, what it means: is indirect. You are not required to pay the certification fee; the cap simply limits what the provider can charge the employee. Where employers have voluntarily reimbursed certification fees as part of a supportive leave policy, the $30 cap means the employer's cost drops correspondingly.

What applies to employers with fewer than 50 Nevada employees?

Below 50 Nevada employees, SB 312's any-reason paid leave obligation does not apply. Neither does federal FMLA. The leave picture at smaller Nevada employers is narrow: PWFA accommodation at 15 employees, USERRA reemployment rights for service members, and jury duty pay continuation under Nevada state law.

Everything else — paid parental leave, paid sick leave, paid bereavement, unpaid personal leave — is whatever the employer voluntarily writes into the offer letter or employee handbook.

This matters for Nevada's tech startup ecosystem. A 30-person Las Vegas software company has no SB 312 obligation, no FMLA obligation, and no state PFML obligation. The entire leave package is voluntary. The question is what competitive offer it takes to hire the same engineer who is weighing an offer at a 55-person competitor where SB 312 kicks in automatically.

Jury duty in Nevada

Nevada requires employers to continue an employee's regular wages during jury service. NRS 6.190 prohibits any employer from discharging, threatening, or otherwise retaliating against an employee for serving on a jury. Unlike several states where jury duty pay continuation is limited to full-time employees or capped at a number of days, Nevada's requirement applies broadly. Most Nevada employers handle this by continuing payroll normally during service and logging the absence separately so it does not draw down PTO or SB 312 leave.

What protections apply for pregnancy and disability in Nevada?

Federal law applies in Nevada and Nevada adds limited state-level protections on top. The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA, effective 27 June 2023) requires reasonable accommodation for pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions at any employer with 15 or more employees.

Nevada law also requires employers to provide reasonable accommodation for employees who are pregnant or have a condition related to pregnancy or childbirth, under NRS 613.4353. This mirrors the PWFA but applies under state law, meaning employees can bring claims under either or both.

The combined effect: a Nevada employer with 15 employees has both federal PWFA and Nevada state pregnancy accommodation obligations. Both cover the same ground, but having parallel routes for employees to enforce their rights (federal EEOC or Nevada Equal Rights Commission) means the practical compliance burden is the same.

What voluntary leave do competitive Nevada employers offer in 2026?

Nevada's SB 312 sets a floor of 40 hours any-reason paid leave per year at 50+ employees. Competitive Nevada employers treat that as the minimum and build on top with voluntary paid parental leave, supplemental sick days, and bereavement pay.

The Las Vegas and Reno tech markets have tightened. Engineers with remote-work options compare offers across states; a Nevada employer competes against Californian companies whose employees get state PFL on top of a voluntary package. The 40-hour SB 312 floor is a start, not a finish.

The PTO integration question

Nevada's SB 312 is silent on whether the any-reason leave must sit in a separate bucket or can be merged into a general PTO bank. The Nevada Labor Commissioner has confirmed that an employer may satisfy NRS 608.0197 by maintaining a single PTO bank, provided the bank accrues at least at the SB 312 rate and employees can use it for any reason without documentation.

Most multi-state employers operating in Nevada integrate SB 312 compliance into a single PTO policy rather than maintaining a parallel any-reason leave ledger. The practical test is that the PTO policy must meet or exceed the 0.01923-per-hour accrual rate and cannot impose any use restriction that would violate the any-reason provision.

What about military leave in Nevada?

Federal USERRA protects the civilian jobs of service members called to active duty. Nevada mirrors this protection for state active duty under NRS 412.139, extending job-protected leave for Nevada National Guard members called by the Governor.

USERRA reemployment rights apply at all employer sizes — there is no headcount threshold. The 5-year cumulative leave cap and escalator reinstatement principle apply to all Nevada employers regardless of whether they are SB 312-covered or FMLA-covered.
