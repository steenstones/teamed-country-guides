How does Nevada wage, overtime and meal-break law work in 2026?

A $12 minimum wage, daily 8-hour overtime for lower earners, mandatory meal and rest breaks, and no income tax on the payslip.

Nevada’s wage-and-hour rules catch most out-of-state employers in two places: the daily overtime trigger and the mandatory meal and rest break requirements. Neither exists under federal FLSA.

The state minimum wage is $12.00 per hour as of 1 July 2024, a single-tier rate following the 2022 constitutional amendment (AJR 10) that repealed the old two-tier health-insurance-linked structure. The rate is CPI-indexed annually from July; confirm the current figure in the Nevada Labor Commissioner’s annual bulletin.

Under NRS 608.018, employees earning less than 1.5 times the state minimum wage (currently below $18.00 per hour) are owed overtime at 1.5x for each hour over 8 in a single workday, in addition to the federal weekly-over-40 trigger. Nevada is one of only five US states with a daily overtime rule. Employees at or above the 1.5x floor follow federal FLSA only.

Under NRS 608.019, non-exempt employees are entitled to a 30-minute unpaid meal break for shifts of 8 or more continuous hours, and a 10-minute paid rest period for each 4 hours worked. Deny the rest break and you owe the 10 minutes as paid time. Deny the meal break and you have a wage claim on your hands.

What is Nevada’s minimum wage in 2026?

Nevada’s state minimum wage is $12.00 per hour from 1 July 2024. It supersedes the federal $7.25 floor in every situation.

The rate is set by the Nevada Labor Commissioner under the CPI-indexing authority granted by AJR 10, the 2022 constitutional amendment. A revised rate is published each year before the 1 July effective date. As of this writing the 2026 bulletin has not yet issued; confirm the current figure directly with the Nevada Office of the Labor Commissioner before the July payroll cycle.

The old two-tier system is gone. Before AJR 10, Nevada ran a lower tier ($9.50) for employers offering qualifying health benefits and a higher tier ($10.50) for employers who did not. That structure was repealed. Every Nevada employer pays the same floor.

Two things trip up national employers:

  • The CPI step is annual, from 1 July. Nevada does not fix the dollar amount years ahead the way Hawaii’s statute does. The rate changes each July. Build a July review into your annual payroll calendar and check the Labor Commissioner bulletin by mid-June.
  • No local layer. Las Vegas, Henderson, Reno, Sparks all use the state rate. There is no Clark County or Washoe County premium to track. One rate covers the whole state.

The daily OT threshold moves with the minimum wage

The daily overtime boundary sits at exactly 1.5 times the current minimum wage. At a $12.00 floor, that threshold is $18.00 per hour. When the CPI adjustment raises the minimum to, say, $12.50, the daily OT boundary rises to $18.75. Update both figures together every July.

Exempt salary floor

Nevada follows the federal exempt salary floor: $684 per week, or $35,568 per year, plus the federal duties test. The 2024 federal rule raising the floor to $43,888 was vacated by a Texas federal court in November 2024. Nevada has not legislated a higher independent floor like California’s $68,640. Audit exempt classifications every July when the minimum wage adjusts; the duties test is the more common failure mode than the salary floor itself.

The daily 8-hour overtime trigger

Non-exempt employees earning less than 1.5× the current state minimum wage are owed 1.5x pay for each hour worked beyond 8 in a single workday, in addition to the standard federal over-40 weekly trigger.

At the $12.00 minimum wage, that threshold is $18.00 per hour. Pay your hourly employee $16.50 and a 10-hour shift owes 2 hours of daily overtime on the day, regardless of what the rest of the week looks like.

Employees at or above the 1.5× threshold ($18.00+) follow federal FLSA only: weekly overtime over 40 hours, no daily trigger.

Does Nevada have daily overtime?

Yes. NRS 608.018 creates a daily 8-hour overtime trigger for non-exempt employees earning below 1.5 times the state minimum wage.

Nevada is one of only five US states with a daily overtime rule. The others are Alaska, California, Colorado, and Puerto Rico. Every other US state, plus the federal FLSA baseline, only triggers overtime at 40 hours in a workweek.

The daily-vs-weekly calculation order

On any day a below-threshold employee works more than 8 hours, you owe daily overtime on the excess. At the end of the workweek you also check weekly hours. You pay whichever figure produces more total overtime pay, not both. The employer cannot double-count daily premium hours toward the 40-hour weekly total and then add weekly overtime on top of daily overtime already paid.

Gabriela earns $15.00 per hour at a Las Vegas restaurant. She works Monday 10 hours, Tuesday 9 hours, Wednesday 8 hours, Thursday 8 hours, Friday 5 hours. Total: 40 hours. No weekly overtime. But Monday and Tuesday each exceed 8 hours, so she is owed daily overtime: 2 hours Monday plus 1 hour Tuesday, three hours at $22.50 each ($67.50). The weekly total did not trigger anything, but the daily rule did.

The bonus-week regular-rate trap

Gabriela picks up a non-discretionary $150 weekend set-up bonus for a 42-hour week. That bonus folds into her ‘regular rate’ for the week she earned it. Her overtime premium applies to the higher blended rate, not her $15.00 base. Run federal-default payroll software that calculates overtime on the base rate only and you owe the difference, plus the same amount again in liquidated damages.

The 1.5x threshold audit

Every July when the minimum wage adjusts, the daily OT threshold moves with it. An employee you classified as ‘above threshold’ at $17.50 per hour in June becomes subject to the daily trigger again if the minimum wage rises to $12.00 and the 1.5x line moves above $18.00. Set a calendar reminder to audit wage-tier classifications alongside the Labor Commissioner bulletin each year.

Are meal and rest breaks mandatory in Nevada?

Yes. NRS 608.019 mandates both. Nevada is stricter than federal FLSA and most US states on breaks.

A 30-minute unpaid meal break is required for any shift of 8 or more continuous hours. A 10-minute paid rest period is required for each 4 hours of work, or major fraction thereof. These are not optional.

The ‘fully relieved’ meal-break trap

A meal break is unpaid only if the employee is genuinely free of all work duties. A server who answers a table question during their 30-minute break converts that break to paid time under federal rule. A back-office worker who responds to a Slack message during lunch faces the same result. Auto-deducting 30 minutes per shift without verifying the employee was actually off duty is the most common Nevada wage-claim source after overtime miscalculation.

The rest-break premium

Unlike California, Nevada has no statutory ‘premium pay’ (one extra hour of pay) for a missed rest break. But a denied 10-minute break still counts as uncompensated work time. Deny it on every 4-hour block across a 30-person shift workforce for two years and the wage exposure adds up fast. Pay 10 minutes of unauthorised time per break you fail to give.

8-hour shift: 30-minute unpaid meal. Every 4 hours: 10-minute paid rest.

The PUMP Act applies across Nevada

The federal Providing Urgent Maternal Protections for Nursing Mothers Act requires reasonable unpaid break time and a private space, not a toilet stall, for nursing employees for one year after the child’s birth. Nevada has no parallel state rule, but the federal requirement applies to every FLSA-covered employer. Set a private lactation space before your first Nevada hire with nursing employees; retrofitting is harder than building it in.

How often do you pay Nevada employees?

Nevada requires wages be paid at least twice a month (semi-monthly) on regular paydays the employer designates in advance.

Pay owed for work performed through the first half of the month is due by the 15th day of the same month. Pay for the second half is due by the last day of the month. A bi-weekly (every two weeks) payroll cycle also satisfies the rule.

The immediate final-pay rule

Nevada’s immediate final-pay statute (NRS 608.020) is one of the strictest in the United States. When you dismiss an employee, all accrued wages are due on the spot, not on the next payday. For most EOR clients, ‘on the spot’ means same-business-day processing. Have a process in place before you need it; retrofitting a same-day payroll run during a separation conversation creates errors.

The 30-day continuing-wages penalty (NRS 608.040) compounds the exposure. Pay five days late on a $40,000-per-year employee and the daily rate ($40,000 / 365 = $109.59) accrues for up to 30 days, adding $3,287 to the cost of a payroll error. Teamed tracks final-pay deadlines automatically and triggers the same-day run at separation so this penalty never accrues.

How does tipping work under Nevada wage law?

Nevada allows no tip credit. Every tipped employee must receive the full $12.00 state minimum wage in direct cash wages, regardless of how much they earn in tips.

Tips belong entirely to the employee. Nevada law prohibits employers, managers, and supervisors from keeping any portion of tips, including tips from a mandatory service charge that goes to management rather than service staff.

The no-tip-credit rule is straightforward compared to states like Hawaii or Georgia. You pay the full minimum wage, tips are on top, and pooling is fine within the tipped-employee group. The payroll configuration is simpler; the labour cost per tipped role is higher than a tip-credit state.

Nevada versus other western states for wage and hour risk

Nevada sits between California and Texas on the complexity ladder. More rules than Texas, far fewer than California. The daily overtime trigger and break mandates are the two places a national employer gets caught.

Build your national policy with federal FLSA as the baseline. Add a Nevada addendum covering the daily 8-hour OT trigger (for employees below $18/hr), the NRS 608.019 break schedule, the no-tip-credit rule, and the immediate final-pay obligation. That covers the five most common Nevada wage claims.

Five things to get right before your first Nevada hire:

  1. Set the $12.00 floor from day one, and calendar the July CPI update. Every Nevada employee is on the same rate. Check the Labor Commissioner bulletin by mid-June each year.
  2. Check each employee’s hourly rate against the $18.00 daily OT threshold. Below it: daily 8-hour trigger applies. At or above it: federal weekly-only rule applies. Audit the list every July after the minimum-wage adjustment.
  3. Build the NRS 608.019 break schedule into your payroll system. 30-minute unpaid meal for 8+ hour shifts, 10-minute paid rest per 4-hour block. Auto-deduct of meal time without verifying the employee was fully relieved creates wage claims.
  4. Do not run a tip credit. No tip credit exists in Nevada. Every tipped hire starts at the full $12.00 floor. Configure payroll accordingly before the first paycheque.
  5. Set up a same-day final-pay process now. NRS 608.020 requires immediate payment on discharge. The 30-day continuing-wages penalty at NRS 608.040 makes late final pay expensive. A payroll system that can run a same-day run on a separation notice is not optional in Nevada.
